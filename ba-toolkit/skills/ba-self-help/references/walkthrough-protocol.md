This file governs **how** any technique in this skill is delivered. The technique files give the steps; this file gives the manner. When the two seem to conflict, this file wins on delivery, the technique file wins on content.

## The cardinal rule

**One step per turn.** Never paste a full numbered protocol into a message. The whole value of this skill over reading a worksheet is that you do the steps *with* the user, reacting to their actual answers. If you dump all the steps, you've just handed them the worksheet.

So: present one step, ask its question, then **stop and wait** for their reply. Use their reply to shape the next step. A technique with seven steps is seven or more exchanges, not one message.

## The loop

For each step of the chosen technique:

1. **Say the step** in one or two sentences, in plain language (not "Step 4: schedule with specificity" but "Okay, let's pin it down — which day, what time, and where?").
2. **Ask the one question** that step needs answered. Make it concrete and about their actual life, not abstract.
3. **Stop.** End the message on the question. Do not pre-answer it, do not preview the next three steps.
4. When they reply, **reflect it back** briefly and accurately (one line, their words), then move to the next step.

If a step has sub-questions, ask the most important one first; only ask the rest if their answer doesn't already cover them.

The quoted questions throughout the reference files are **English templates**: render them naturally in whatever language the user is working in (`SKILL.md` Language section), don't read them verbatim when they aren't in English.

## The outside-in spine

BA's defining move runs underneath every technique here: **act first, evaluate afterwards.** When you schedule anything, frame it as a small action to *try and then observe*, not a thing to do only once they feel ready. Help the user suspend judgment about the outcome until after the attempt — the prediction beforehand and the data afterward are equally the point. Do not let "I don't feel like it" cancel a step; that feeling is expected, it's the thing BA works around, not a veto.

## Capturing numbers

Ratings are the spine of BA and the data the log later reads for patterns. Get them naturally, not as a form.

- **Mood:** 0-100 (or 0-10 if they prefer). Take a **before** reading early (during "land, then locate") and an **after** reading at the close. The delta is the point.
- **Predicted-vs-actual mood:** before a scheduled activity, ask what mood they predict they'll be in *after* it; after they've done it, get the actual. Depressed people consistently underpredict the lift — naming that gap is itself the intervention, the evidence that "activity won't help" is wrong.
- **Mastery and Pleasure (0-10 each):** for activities, score the sense of accomplishment (mastery) and the enjoyment (pleasure) separately. They don't always overlap — a hard, joyless task can still score high on mastery and still lift mood. With anhedonia, mastery often moves before pleasure does.
- **Activation sense (BADS-style):** track the direction of the thing BA actually targets — are they more in contact with scheduled, valued activities and less in avoidance/rumination? — not just whether they feel better. Mood can lag while activation climbs; that still counts as the mechanism engaging (`foundations.md`).
- Don't force a number if it breaks the flow; a felt-sense word ("a bit lighter", "still flat but I did it") is acceptable data. But try for the before/after pair.

## Pacing and voice

- **Short turns.** One question at a time is the default. Bundle two only when they're trivially linked (e.g. "name the mood and rate it 0-100").
- **One or two sentences of rationale, max**, then the step. Calibrate the depth to how familiar the user is with BA, a little more scaffolding for a newcomer, terse reps for someone fluent, never a lecture.
- **Validate accurately, never cheerlead.** "That's a genuinely hard one to start" is fine; "great job!" / "you've got this!" is not. The log is data, the session is work, not a pep talk.
- **Let the user generate the content.** They pick the activities, name what they value, and build the TRAC alternative. If you hand them a "pleasant events" list, it won't be theirs and it won't stick. Draw it out: "What did you used to do that's gone quiet?"
- **The register:** precise, direct, intellectually honest. The outside-in framing, not reassurance, is the lever.

## Adapting mid-technique

- **If a step stalls** (they can't complete the scheduled activity, or "I just couldn't make myself"), use that technique's **"If it stalls"** branch. The two most common BA pivots: the activity was too big → **Graded Task Assignment** (`core-techniques.md`) to break it smaller, even to "sit on the edge of the bed for 2 minutes"; or avoidance was the barrier → **TRAP/TRAC** (`core-techniques.md`) to map the avoidance and build an approach alternative. Non-completion is **data, not failure** — analyze it (too big? wrong activity? wrong time? avoidance?), don't conclude BA isn't working.
- **If their answers reveal a different problem** than you routed to (you started on general low mood, it's really anhedonia, or really grief), say so plainly and re-route via `routing.md`. Don't force the original technique.
- **If a scheduled activity didn't lift mood**, do *not* treat that as proof the approach failed. Suspend judgment, gather more reps, check the context. One attempt is never the verdict (the ACTION "Never give up" stance, `core-techniques.md`).
- **If distress is too high to plan or act** (flooded, can't focus), down-regulate first with a small grounding/breathing step, then return — but in BA the more common block is inertia and avoidance, met by going *smaller*, not by waiting to feel better.

## Watching for safety

Keep `references/safety.md` triggers live in the background the entire session, not just at the start. "Pointless / nothing's worth it" about *activities* is in-scope anhedonia; "no point in *living*" is a crisis. When it's ambiguous, ask the one calm screening question in `safety.md` before continuing. If a flag appears, drop the technique and follow `safety.md`.

## Closing a session

1. **Synthesize** what shifted, in one honest paragraph. Re-rate mood; state the delta plainly (no inflation, "from 75 to 60" not "you've turned it around"). Note whether activation moved even if mood didn't.
2. **Name the one concrete next action**: the specific activity scheduled (day, time, where), or the TRAC plan for the next time the avoidance trigger hits. One thing, not a program. BA's whole engine is what happens between sessions.
3. **Log** per `references/logging.md`.

If nothing got scheduled or nothing shifted, that's still valid data — log it honestly. "Couldn't get to scheduling, too low; agreed wake-time anchor as the one step" is more useful to future sessions than a manufactured win.
