## How to use this file

These are the universal BA techniques, used across almost every presentation. The condition files (`depression.md`, `anhedonia.md`, etc.) assume these and add only the specialized layer, so they point back here by name and number. Deliver every one of them per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.** The steps below already carry the question to ask at each step, ask that, don't paraphrase the whole list.

The usual arc is **monitor → schedule → target the avoidance**: Activity Monitoring (1) before Activity Scheduling (5), with TRAP/TRAC (9) when avoidance is the engine and Graded Task Assignment (6) when even the smallest scheduled step won't go. The numbering matches the source catalog; the two BATA-specific techniques (Dabbling §17, Savoring §18) live in `anhedonia.md`.

The non-negotiable BA stance underneath all of it: **act first, evaluate afterwards**, and activities are chosen from *this* person's experience and values, never from a generic "pleasant events" list.

## Assessment & conceptualization

---

## 1 · Activity Monitoring

**Reach for it when:** the start of almost every BA piece of work. Before you schedule anything, you need to see what the user actually does hour to hour and how mood tracks it — which is often quite different from what they assume. First-line for everyone; the data picks the scheduling targets, not your guess.

**Walk it through:**
1. Set up the log. Ask: "For the next while — even just a day to start — can you jot what you're doing each hour, who's there, and your mood 0 to 100? Brief notes, it's a log not a journal." (If a week feels too much, one day or even tracking one activity is fine.)
2. Add the two scores. Ask: "For each thing, also rate Mastery 0 to 10 (sense of accomplishment) and Pleasure 0 to 10 (enjoyment)." (See 4.)
3. Review it together, data first. Ask: "Looking at it, when is mood lowest? Which activities lifted it, even a little? Which were followed by a dip?" Don't interpret before looking — let the pattern show.
4. Name the targets. Ask: "Given that, which moments or gaps look like the place to add something?"

**Clinical note:** if monitoring is too heavy (severe depression, low concentration), shrink it — one activity a day, or a 3-column version (activity / mood / time). Don't skip it to jump to scheduling; the monitoring *is* the foundation.

**If it stalls:** consistent non-completion is itself information (avoidance, burden, barriers) — treat it that way, not as failure. Do a quick version *in-session* together first to lower the initiation barrier and show how it works.

## 2 · Functional Analysis (ABC / TRAP mapping)

**Reach for it when:** you want to understand a specific avoidance behavior — what sets it off and what keeps it going. The core conceptualization move in Martell-style BA; ongoing throughout. Pairs naturally with TRAP/TRAC (9).

**Walk it through:**
1. Pick a concrete recent example. Ask: "Take one recent time you withdrew or didn't do something — what was the moment?" (Ideally one from the monitoring log.)
2. Antecedents. Ask: "What was going on just before — the situation, and what you felt or thought in that moment?"
3. The behavior. Ask: "What did you actually do, or not do?"
4. Consequences. Ask: "What happened right after — the immediate relief? And what did it cost you later?"
5. Find the function. Ask: "So what's this behavior *doing* for you in the moment — what does it let you avoid?" Keep it "what's the function", never "what's wrong with me" — the stance is collaborative and non-pathologizing.

**If it stalls:** if antecedents are hard to find, use the TRAP boxes (9) as a visual scaffold, and review the monitoring log for the recurring contexts before trying again.

## 3 · Values & Life-Areas Assessment (LAVA)

**Reach for it when:** the user can't name activities worth scheduling, or you need to anchor scheduling in what actually matters to them (the BATD approach). First-line for building the activity hierarchy; especially useful in anhedonia, grief, and severe amotivation. Values are "a compass, not a destination" — direction, not a finish line.

**Walk it through** *(LAVA: Life areas → Associated values → Value-based activities → Action plan)*:
1. Frame it. Say values are what kind of life they want to be living, and ask: "Set mood aside for a second — what kind of person, in what areas, do you want to be?"
2. Walk the life areas. Ask them to rate, for each that's relevant, **Importance 0-10** and **current Engagement 0-10**: relationships/family, romantic, friendships, education, work/career, hobbies/recreation, physical health/self-care, spirituality, community/volunteering, daily responsibilities. The **gap** between importance and engagement is the signal.
3. Pick the priorities. Ask: "Which 3 to 5 areas have the biggest gap *and* feel workable right now?"
4. Make them concrete. Ask: "For one of those, what's a specific, observable activity that expresses it?" — push for "eat a vegetable with dinner 3 nights" over "be healthier."
5. Set frequency. Ask: "How often, and for how long, this week?" These become the hierarchy (feed into 5).

**If it stalls:** if they can't name values, treat it as a clinical signal, not a comprehension gap (it can be avoidance, anhedonia, or identity disruption after loss). Smaller prompts: "What did you care about before this set in?" "What would someone who knows you well say matters to you?" In anhedonia, start with **Dabbling** (`anhedonia.md`) before values work — values may be out of reach when reward is blunted.

## Core activation

---

## 4 · Mastery & Pleasure ratings

**Reach for it when:** alongside monitoring and scheduling, to separate two different mood-lifting mechanisms. Especially useful when "nothing's enjoyable" — **mastery activities can lift mood without being enjoyable at all.**

**Walk it through:**
1. Teach the two axes. "**Mastery** is a sense of accomplishment — did finishing it give you any 'I did that', however small? It can be high even for something hard or unpleasant. **Pleasure** is just enjoyment." Ask: "Make sense as two separate dials?"
2. Score activities on both. Ask: "Rate that one Mastery 0 to 10 and Pleasure 0 to 10."
3. Read the pattern. Ask: "Across the day, is your life short on mastery, on pleasure, or both?"
4. Use it to choose targets. For blunted pleasure (anhedonia), prioritize **mastery** activities first; for low accomplishment, prioritize manageable productive tasks.
5. Predict then check. Before an activity get a predicted score; after, the actual. Depression reliably **underpredicts** — name the gap when it shows; it's the evidence against "it won't be worth it."

**Clinical note:** in BA the ratings reveal behavior-mood relationships; you're not arguing about whether something "should" be enjoyable, just gathering what is.

**If it stalls:** if scores are uniformly zero, severe anhedonia may be drowning the signal — move to the **Savoring** module (`anhedonia.md`).

## 5 · Activity Scheduling with a graded hierarchy

**Reach for it when:** the workhorse of BA, the central intervention once monitoring has a baseline. First-line for everyone after monitoring. **Specificity is the activation.**

**Walk it through:**
1. Generate candidates. Pull from the monitoring log (1) and values (3). Ask: "From what we've seen, what activities are worth trying — across a few of the areas that matter?"
2. Rank them easiest (1) to hardest (10). Ask: "Order a handful from most-doable to most-daunting — where does each sit?"
3. Schedule specifically. Ask: "Pick a low one. Which day, what time, where, who with?" — write "Tuesday 10am, 20-min walk, alone," not "walk more."
4. Start low, not aspirational. Begin with what they can **most reliably do**, not what they most want to achieve.
5. Set the outside-in instruction. "Do it and rate mood before and after — act first, judge after. The point is to try it and see, not to feel ready first."
6. Review next time. If it helped → continue, nudge up frequency/difficulty. If it didn't → **don't conclude BA failed** — analyze (too big? avoidance? wrong activity/time?) and modify.
7. Problem-solve barriers (8); build reliable activities into a routine (10).

**Critical distinction:** BA doesn't assume in advance which activities will lift mood — that's settled by *their* experience, not a curated list.

**If it stalls:** can't complete any scheduled activity → the rung is too high, drop to **Graded Task Assignment** (6). Avoidance is the barrier → **TRAP/TRAC** (9). A real external obstacle → **Problem-Solving** (8).

## 6 · Graded Task Assignment

**Reach for it when:** a task feels too big to start; severe depression, amotivation, low energy; the smallest thing on the hierarchy still feels unmanageable. The decomposition tool. **Every completed step is a reinforcement** — the goal is the next step, not the end state.

**Walk it through:**
1. Name the stuck task. Ask: "What's the thing you keep meaning to do but can't get started on?"
2. Break it down together. Ask: "What's the very first physical step, then the next?" — you provide the frame, they know what's doable.
3. Give the rationale. "We're starting tiny on purpose, to make success near-certain — each step counts."
4. Rehearse it mentally (link to 13). Ask: "Walk through doing the first step — when, where, any snag?"
5. Shrink anything shaky. If a step looks too hard in rehearsal, break it smaller before assigning.
6. Assign the smallest feasible first step. For severe amotivation this can be "sit on the edge of the bed for 2 minutes" instead of "go for a walk."
7. If they don't complete it, **reassign a *smaller* piece** — not the same one — before deciding what further breakdown is needed.

**If it stalls:** subdivide until a step is incontestably achievable; do the first step *in-session* to remove the initiation barrier entirely. For anhedonia, use **Dabbling** (`anhedonia.md`), built for impaired initiation.

## 7 · Behavioral Experiment (BA version)

**Reach for it when:** the user needs to *see* the behavior-mood link for themselves — especially under "nothing will help" / "I won't enjoy anything." A motivation-builder for the outside-in approach.

**Walk it through:**
1. Pick a target activity (from 2 or the hierarchy). Ask: "What's one activity we could test?"
2. Set it up as a test, not a verdict. "We can only know if this helps by trying it for a bit. This is an experiment, not a judgment."
3. Predict. Ask: "Before you do it, what mood do you predict you'll be in after, 0 to 100?" Lock the number.
4. Do it, suspending judgment. "While you do it, hold off evaluating — just do it, and we'll look after."
5. Record the actual. Ask: "Now it's done — actual mood 0 to 100, and anything you noticed?"
6. Compare predicted vs actual. If it helped → continue, increase frequency. If not → analyze and modify.

**Distinction from CBT:** here you're testing **functional relations** (does this behavior move mood?), not the accuracy of a belief.

**If it stalls:** check whether it was actually completed, whether something got in the way, or whether avoidance crept in mid-attempt — modify the design. Systematic avoidance → **TRAP/TRAC** (9).

## 8 · Problem-Solving for activity barriers

**Reach for it when:** a real external obstacle is blocking homework, or a concrete life problem is maintaining the reward deficit. **Targets activity barriers specifically** — not a catch-all, and *not* for when the barrier is avoidance (that's 9).

**Walk it through** *(five-step D'Zurilla & Goldfried)*:
1. Orientation. "This is solvable — let's treat it as a problem to crack, not a wall." Address hopelessness about solving if it's there.
2. Define it. Ask: "What exactly is the problem, in one sentence of facts, and what would 'solved' look like?"
3. Generate options. Ask: "Brainstorm possible solutions — quantity first, don't judge them yet. Give me several, even bad ones."
4. Decide. Ask: "Weigh the pros and cons — which is the best, or least-worst, to try?"
5. Implement & check. "Do it, watch what happens, adjust; and notice when it works."

**If it stalls:** first sort the barrier — **external** (use this) vs **avoidance** (use TRAP/TRAC 9, problem-solving aims at the wrong level for avoidance). If it's a skill gap (e.g. doesn't know how to start the conversation) → **Social Skills** (14).

## Targeting avoidance

---

## 9 · TRAP / TRAC

**Reach for it when:** any behavioral avoidance — the central avoidance-targeting heuristic in BA. First-line for spotting and pivoting out of avoidance; especially when the user can't say what they're avoiding.

**Walk it through** — *Step 1, find the TRAP:*
1. Introduce it on a concrete example:
   - **T = Trigger** — the situation or cue just before. Ask: "What happened right before you pulled back?"
   - **R = Response** — the *internal* state right after: emotion, thought, body sensation (this is the experience, **not** the behavior). Ask: "What did you feel or think in that instant?"
   - **AP = Avoidance Pattern** — what they did to escape. Ask: "And what did you actually do?"
2. Map it in three boxes (T → R → AP) with their example. (e.g. T: "partner criticized me" → R: "shame, tight chest, 'I'm a failure'" → AP: "went to bed all day".)
3. Cost-benefit. Ask: "In the moment, what did avoiding give you? What did it cost you later?"

*Step 2, build the TRAC:*
4. Same T and R, swap the AP for **AC = Alternative Coping** — a behavior that *approaches* instead of avoids. Ask: "Same trigger, same feeling — what's a small approach move instead?"
5. Make it concrete. Not "don't go to bed" but "text back that I'll come for an hour and leave when I want."
6. Assign it. Next time the trigger hits, run the TRAC. The therapeutic question pair to teach them: "What's the TRAP here?" and "So what gets me back on TRAC?"

**If it stalls:** if they can't find TRAPs, return to the monitoring log (1) for recurring patterns; map the diagram together across several examples before expecting them to do it solo.

## 10 · Routine Establishment

**Reach for it when:** daily structure has collapsed — erratic sleep, skipped meals, no shape to the day. A specific maintaining variable in depression, and the best **foundational** step before complex scheduling. First-line when structure is gone.

**Walk it through:**
1. Assess the disruption (from the monitoring log). Ask: "What's irregular right now — wake time, sleep, meals?"
2. Start with **wake-time**. Ask: "What's one consistent time you could get up, every day, even weekends?" — this single anchor sets up the rest of the day.
3. Add basic regularity. Ask: "What about a rough regular time for meals?" Build eating/sleeping/basic structure before layering activation goals.
4. Give the rationale. "Regularity stabilizes mood a bit on its own, and we can't fairly judge whether activities help until they sit in a steady-ish routine."
5. Once ~a week of basic regularity holds, layer specific activities (5) onto it.

**If it stalls:** sort whether the disruption is symptom-driven (anergy, hypersomnia) or avoidance-driven. Severe hypersomnia → start with *any* consistent timing, even if late. Avoidance-driven → **TRAP/TRAC** (9).

## 11 · Anti-Avoidance Assignments

**Reach for it when:** any behavioral avoidance you want to directly prescribe an approach to — social, task-based, or routine. Applies the TRAP/TRAC conceptualization to concrete homework; most intensive in the middle phase of the work.

**Walk it through:**
1. Identify the avoided situation (via functional analysis, 2). Ask: "What's the specific thing you keep avoiding?"
2. Understand the function. Ask: "What's the short-term relief, and the long-term cost?"
3. Map it with TRAP, then generate the approach with TRAC (9).
4. Grade the approach (6) so the first step is doable. Ask: "What's the smallest approach version of this?"
5. Test it as a behavioral experiment (7): predict, do, compare.
6. If routine disruption is in the mix, address that first (10) — it's often the most tractable entry.

**If it stalls:** if approaching produces distress well *beyond* expected discomfort, consider whether this is fear-driven avoidance needing **exposure** (clinician-guided, `safety.md`) rather than depression-driven avoidance — BA approach work addresses the latter.

## 12 · Rumination as avoidance (attending to experience)

**Reach for it when:** repetitive self-focused thinking ("why am I like this", "if only") is crowding out doing anything. BA treats rumination as a **cognitive avoidance behavior**, not a thought to argue with.

**Walk it through:**
1. Functional analysis of the rumination itself. Ask: "When does this thinking tend to start, and what does it pull you away from doing?" — focus on when/where, not the content.
2. Reframe it as avoidance. "This thinking gives a bit of relief but it's taking you out of contact with your actual life — that's what keeps the low going."
3. Explain why you won't argue the content. "We're not going to debate whether the thoughts are true — just notice when they show up and what comes before and after."
4. Spot the patterns. Ask: "Is it unanswerable 'why' questions, 'if only' replays, or self-pity loops?"
5. Turn "why" into "how." Ask: "'Why am I so alone?' → what's the 'how can I make contact with one person this week?' version?"
6. Train **attending to experience.** "During an activity, deliberately put attention on the outside world — colours, sounds, textures, where people are — and back off the inner commentary." (Like mindfulness, no formal meditation required.)
7. Run it through TRAP/TRAC (9): rumination is the AP; attending-to-experience is the AC.
8. Optional: use the *onset* of rumination as a cue to start a valued activity — turn the trigger into the intervention.

**If it stalls:** if rumination is extreme and doesn't budge with functional reframing, flag that Rumination-Focused CBT (which targets the thinking process directly) may be a better fit as an adjunct.

## Self-monitoring & self-management

---

## 13 · Behavioral / Mental Rehearsal

**Reach for it when:** before any homework the user anticipates struggling with, or has performance anxiety about. An adjunct to nearly every scheduling assignment, especially early.

**Walk it through:**
1. Surface the obstacles. Ask: "What might get in the way of doing this — time, energy, logistics, nerves?"
2. Mentally walk it through. Ask: "Picture yourself doing it step by step, like watching a video — talk me through it."
3. Find where it snags. Ask: "Where in that did it get hard?" Be specific about when/where.
4. Shrink anything too hard (link to 6) before assigning.
5. For social activities, role-play it: you play the other person, they practice in-session.
6. End on a concrete commitment. Ask: "So exactly when, where, and what will you do?" — saying it out loud increases follow-through.

**If it stalls:** if rehearsal surfaces so many obstacles the task seems impossible, the rung is too high — decompose further (6). If social anxiety is driving it, consider whether graded social skills work (14) is needed.

## 14 · Social Skills (in BA)

**Reach for it when:** prolonged withdrawal has eroded social skills the user used to have, or their social world doesn't reinforce non-depressed behavior. Assessed **individually**, not standardized; an adjunct applied when skills are the barrier to social reinforcement.

**Walk it through:**
1. Check it's warranted. Ask: "Has being withdrawn this long made some social things genuinely rusty — or do the skills feel intact and it's more avoidance?"
2. If warranted, pin the specific deficit (via functional analysis + self-report). Ask: "Which specific bit is hard — starting conversations, ending them, asking for something, saying no?"
3. Instruct directly in that skill, then **model** it.
4. Role-play it in-session; reinforce what they do well.
5. Assign graded real-world practice. Ask: "Where could you try that this week, somewhere low-stakes?"
6. Review: if social reinforcement increases, continue; if the environment stays unreinforcing despite good skills → **Contingency Management** (15).

**If it stalls:** if the environment fails to reinforce even skilled approach behavior, the problem is the environment's contingencies, not the skill — go to 15, don't keep re-running skills cycles.

## 15 · Contingency Management / Social Support Contracting

**Reach for it when:** the user's social world is inadvertently reinforcing depressed behavior (care and excusing when they're symptomatic; less attention when they're active). A BATD component; also a Martell adjunct.

**Walk it through:**
1. Check the contingency. Ask: "When you're really struggling, do people rally and take things off you — and when you're doing okay, does that attention quietly drop off?"
2. Find a willing support person (partner, friend, family).
3. Educate them on the BA rationale — frame it **behaviorally, not morally**: the aim is to reinforce activation, not to judge.
4. Set the asks: respond warmly when the user completes activation goals; gently *reduce* (not withdraw) the extra positive attention around depressed behavior.
5. Write a light "way to get an assist," not a formal contract — fit it to the relationship and culture.
6. Add a **self-reward contingency.** Ask: "What small enjoyable thing could you schedule *after* completing an activation goal?"

**If it stalls:** no support person available → lean on self-reward scheduling and the session itself as reinforcement. If their involvement causes tension, examine the social dynamics in functional analysis — the relationship may itself be a target.

## 16 · ACTION acronym (self-activation)

**Reach for it when:** the user has learned monitoring, scheduling, and TRAP/TRAC and is ready for a take-home decision framework — for any mood-drop or urge to avoid. Middle-to-late phase; builds autonomy.

**Walk it through** — teach this six-step sequence to run whenever they notice a dip or an urge to avoid:
- **A = Assess.** "Is what I'm doing right now avoidance? Am I doing it because I feel bad, or because I'd choose it regardless of mood?"
- **C = Choose.** Deliberately choose to self-activate **or** take a real, time-limited break. Either is fine *if chosen on purpose*; automatic avoidance is the thing to catch.
- **T = Try.** Try the chosen behavior.
- **I = Integrate.** If it helped, fold it into the routine.
- **O = Observe.** "Do I feel better after? Is this moving me toward what I value?"
- **N = Never give up.** Scientific stance — if it didn't help this once, try again or try something else. One attempt is never the verdict.

**If it stalls:** if they can't use ACTION solo, return to structured TRAP/TRAC worksheets in-session — ACTION needs the underlying skills already practiced.

## 19 · Relapse Prevention Plan

**Reach for it when:** later in the work, the hand-off from active sessions to self-management. The terminal technique; belongs in any complete BA course. **The user leads, you coach** — that transfer of ownership is deliberate.

**Walk it through:**
1. List early warning signs — *behavioral*, not just mood. Ask: "What are your specific early signs the low is creeping back — sleep changing, dropping certain people or activities, more rumination?"
2. List what worked. Ask: "Which activities most reliably lifted your mood during this — your own list?"
3. Build the protective routine. Ask: "What daily/weekly structure keeps those activation behaviors in?"
4. Plan for high-risk times. Ask: "What's coming — stressors, transitions, anniversaries, the dark months — and what's the plan for each?"
5. Hand them the ACTION acronym (16) for ongoing self-monitoring.
6. Write the if-then. Ask: "Finish this: 'If I notice [early warning sign], I will [specific activation behavior].'"
7. Let them run the planning; you coach from the side.

**If it stalls:** if they can't lead it, sort whether it's skills (review TRAP/TRAC) or anxiety about life without sessions (run a small behavioral experiment with planned brief re-contact after a month).
