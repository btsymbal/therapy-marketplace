## How to use this file

The medical-comorbidity layer — low mood alongside chronic pain, a medical illness, substance recovery, or new motherhood. It adds condition-specific adaptations on top of the universal toolkit; run the techniques from `core-techniques.md` by number, one step per turn (`walkthrough-protocol.md`). **Safety, read first:** several of these are clinician-guided. **Substance use** — BA addresses the depression component only, *not* the SUD; a full SUD program is required. **Postpartum** — wants clinician guidance, especially with any psychosis, severe suicidal ideation, or inability to care for the infant. Deliver only the self-help-safe layer and signpost (`safety.md`).

## Chronic pain: the boom-and-bust cycle

In chronic pain, a cycle parallels depression's avoidance cycle: **overactivity when pain is tolerable (boom) → pain flare and enforced rest (bust) → deconditioning, less activity, lower mood.** Activity becomes **pain-contingent** — driven by how the body feels moment to moment. BA's answer is **pacing**: consistent, graduated activity regardless of pain level, not activity gated by pain.

## Technique adaptations for chronic pain

- **Activity Scheduling (5):** shift from "pleasant activities" to **resumption of meaningful activities discontinued because of pain** — build the hierarchy around avoided functional activities (cooking, walking, socializing), not novel enjoyments. Ask: "What did the pain make you stop doing that mattered?"
- **Graded activity by time quota, not pain (6):** increases are based on **time/quota**, not pain. "Walk for 10 minutes" — not "walk until it hurts." This directly breaks pain-contingent responding.
- **Pacing principle:** a good day is **not** a license to over-do. The goal is consistent performance in a target range — not maxing out on good days and crashing on bad ones. Ask: "On a low-pain day, can you hold to the quota instead of bingeing on activity?"
- **Pain psychoeducation + guided disclosure:** the behavioral model of the pain-mood interaction; telling a trusted person about the pain experience can cut isolation and shame.
- **Catastrophizing as avoidance:** functional-analyze refusing activities because of *anticipated* pain, and run it through **TRAP/TRAC** (9).

## Substance use disorder

BA here is an **adjunct to the depression**, never the SUD treatment (`safety.md`). Self-help-safe adaptations:
- **Values Clarification (3):** emphasize substance-free activities and the life areas use has eroded.
- **Functional Analysis (2):** ABC-map the use — triggers, internal states before, and the function (escape, social reward, boredom).
- **Activity Scheduling (5):** schedule activities that **compete** with use and provide alternative reward, timed to high-risk periods.
- **Social Support Contracting (15):** reinforce abstinence-supporting behavior; reduce exposure to environments that reinforce use.

## Postpartum depression

Clinician-guided (`safety.md`). Supportive, self-help-safe pieces:
- **TRAP adapted to the new-mother context:** Triggers (infant-care demands, sleep loss, isolation, changed identity); Response (guilt, shame, worthlessness); Avoidance Patterns (not seeking support, stopping enjoyable activities, self-isolating).
- **"Good-enough mom" framing:** validate the difficulty while reducing the perfectionism that blocks activation.
- **Help-seeking as activation:** seeking support is itself a valued, activating behavior — name it as a target, not a weakness.
- **Culturally relevant activities:** scheduling has to fit the user's cultural norms around new motherhood.

## Cancer

- **Values Clarification (3):** activities take on particular urgency; values work helps prioritize meaningful engagement within physical limits.
- **Schedule around treatment:** accommodate appointment schedules, fatigue cycles, changing capacity.
- **Functional analysis (2):** identify how appointment anxiety, side effects, and existential worry function as triggers for avoidance.

## Post-stroke depression

Standard BA applies — **Activity Scheduling (5)** and **Functional Analysis (2)** are the core. Adapt session length and complexity for cognitive and physical changes after stroke. Short-term benefit is the best-supported finding; medium-term evidence is not established.

## HIV/AIDS (low-resource settings)

Delivered through stepped-care, lay-worker models with simplified materials in local language. Adaptations: broader content than standard BA (relaxation, social-network engagement, rumination management (12), psychosocial-stressor support); treat medical-treatment adherence as a valued activity in scheduling; adapt social-reinforcement components (15) to community norms.

## The honest nuance to say out loud

**Don't transfer depression-population effect sizes to medically ill populations.** A 2025 meta-analysis found **insufficient evidence overall for BA in people with non-communicable diseases** (small, heterogeneous effects), with post-stroke the best-supported subgroup. BA's effect can be attenuated by medical complexity, reduced physical capacity, and a different reinforcement landscape. For chronic pain specifically, set realistic expectations: BA helps **mood, disability, and catastrophizing** more than raw pain intensity — aim for movement and function, not for the pain to vanish. Optimal session counts in these comorbid contexts are largely unknown.
