## How to use this file

The grief layer — withdrawal and avoidance *after a loss* (a death, a breakup, the end of a role or career). It adds the grief-specific adaptations on top of the universal toolkit; run the techniques themselves from `core-techniques.md` by number, one step per turn (`walkthrough-protocol.md`). **Safety:** severe or complicated grief, and any suicidal thought, want clinician care — see `safety.md`.

## The BA read on grief

BA understands complicated grief as a three-process reinforcing cycle:
1. **Environmental disengagement** — the loss removes a primary source of reward, and the person withdraws from *other* sources too, compounding it.
2. **Avoidance of grief stimuli** — systematically avoiding the people, places, activities, and memories tied to the loss; short-term relief, but it blocks natural integration and cuts off access to positive memories.
3. **Rumination** — repetitive thinking about the loss, sustained by the brief relief it gives (another cognitive avoidance behavior).

The pattern: loss → reward deprivation → withdrawal → more deprivation → grief-depression cycle. BA's job is to **restore contact with surviving sources of reinforcement** and interrupt the avoidance/rumination that maintains the cycle — without pathologizing the grief itself.

## Technique application in grief

- **Activity Monitoring (1) — grief-adapted.** The diary reveals the full scope of behavioral contraction since the loss. Track specifically: avoided places tied to the person, conversations being dodged, activities once done *with* them and now abandoned. Ask: "What have you stopped doing since, that you used to do?"
- **Values Clarification (3) — grief-adapted.** After a major loss, especially a role loss, values work has to address three things: which valued areas the loss has stripped, which surviving areas can be reactivated, and whether new valued directions may emerge over time. Ask: "Which parts of life are still yours to step back into?" — and don't rush to (see Pacing).
- **Anti-Avoidance Assignments (11) — grief-adapted.** Target grief-*stimulus* avoidance specifically (places, photos, objects, conversations about the person). Graded approach, framed in BA terms — **not** "processing the grief" but restoring contact with a source of experience that is painful *and* meaningful. Approach it gradually (6).
- **Rumination-as-Avoidance (12).** Complicated grief is often sustained by "if only I'd called", "why did I say that", "what's the point now". Run the full attending-to-experience protocol; help them see the rumination as a TRAP (9) and build the attending-to-experience TRAC.

## Pacing: don't rush the re-scheduling

The one pacing rule that matters here: **premature scheduling of new activities, before the person has had space to grieve, can feel invalidating.** Grief is not a problem to activate away. Move at their pace — re-engaging surviving roles and gentle approach to avoided reminders, not a brisk activity program. The goal is restoring contact with life, including the painful-but-meaningful parts, not distraction from the loss.

## The honest nuance to say out loud

The evidence base for BA *specifically in grief* is **promising but limited** — small samples, limited trial data. For severe complicated grief / prolonged grief disorder, BA should be considered **alongside** specialized grief therapies (Complicated Grief Treatment, Prolonged Grief Disorder protocols), not as a replacement. If the grief is severe, signpost specialist care (`safety.md`).
