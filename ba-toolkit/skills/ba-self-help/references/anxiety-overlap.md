## How to use this file

The anxiety layer — for anxiety that is **comorbid with depression**, where the same avoidance serves both. It adds the overlap-specific notes on top of the universal toolkit; run the techniques from `core-techniques.md` by number, one step per turn (`walkthrough-protocol.md`).

**Scope, read first:** this file is for **comorbid** anxiety-and-depression. It is **not** for standalone primary anxiety disorders — panic, specific phobia, social anxiety, GAD, health anxiety without depressive withdrawal. For those, BA is not the established treatment and the user should be routed to cognitive/exposure work (a CBT skill), not run through BA. If someone arrives with pure panic/phobia/OCD, say so plainly and don't BA it (`routing.md`, `safety.md`).

## The shared mechanism: avoidance

BA and anxiety-focused CBT share one target: **avoidance.** In anxiety, avoidance maintains the disorder by preventing disconfirmation of feared outcomes. In depression, avoidance maintains it by preventing access to positive reinforcement. When the two are comorbid — common — the *same* avoidance behaviors do both jobs at once. That shared mechanism is why BA's approach work is conceptually applicable to the anxiety side of a comorbid picture: approaching, rather than avoiding, addresses both functions.

## What the evidence actually supports

Be precise about this:
- **Comorbid anxiety + depression:** BA is supported as transdiagnostic for the comorbid presentation; meta-analysis finds BA reduces anxiety symptoms versus inactive control (with no clear advantage over other active treatments).
- **Primary anxiety disorders:** a Cochrane-style review concluded it is "still unclear whether BA is effective as a treatment for anxiety disorders" on their own. One RCT found BA declined GAD symptoms more rapidly than exposure during treatment, but it needs replication.

The takeaway for routing: lean on BA for the anxiety *that travels with depression*; don't present it as a standalone anxiety treatment.

## Technique application in anxiety overlap

- **TRAP/TRAC for anxiety-avoidance (9).** The model maps cleanly: the **Trigger** is an anxiety cue, the **Response** is the fear experience, the **Avoidance Pattern** is whatever they do to escape the feared context. The TRAC builds the approach alternative. Ask: "What's the cue, what's the fear, and what do you do to get out of it?"
- **Graded approach activities (5, 6).** Build a hierarchy of approach activities and start low — same scheduling machinery, oriented toward the avoided situations.
- **Behavioral Experiments (7).** Use them to test the behavior-mood link and, in the comorbid frame, to gather evidence about what actually happens on approach.

## BA framing vs exposure framing

Keep the framing honest about which you're doing. In **pure BA**, graded approach activities are framed as **behavior-mood experiments** — "what can we schedule that moves you back into contact with your life?" — *not* as habituation to a feared stimulus. Exposure framing — "let's stay with the feared thing until anxiety drops" — is a different, clinician-guided treatment. For comorbid presentations the distinction may matter less in practice; for primary anxiety it matters a lot, and exposure (clinician-guided) is first-line there, not BA.

## The honest nuance to say out loud

**BA is not established as a standalone treatment for primary anxiety disorders.** It's conceptually applicable through the shared avoidance mechanism and helps the anxiety symptoms that ride along with depression — but for panic disorder, social anxiety, specific phobia, or GAD without comorbid depression, exposure-based therapy remains first-line. Be explicit with the user about this distinction rather than implying BA covers their anxiety on its own. (A specialized variant, CABA, exists for veterans with comorbid PTSD and mild TBI — clinician-guided, see `safety.md`.)
