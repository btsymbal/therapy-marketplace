## How to use this file

The anhedonia layer — when *wanting* and *liking* themselves are blunted, not just mood. This is BATA (Behavioral Activation Therapy for Anhedonia): standard BA plus two specialized modules, **Dabbling** and **Savoring**, delivered here in full walk-through form. It assumes the universal toolkit in `core-techniques.md` (monitoring, values, scheduling) and adds the anhedonia-specific work. Deliver every step per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: one step per turn, end on a question, wait. **Route here** (rather than `depression.md`) when *wanting* is gone — "I can't even look forward to anything", "I feel nothing", lost interest in what they used to love. **Safety:** anhedonia with medication-refractory depression wants a psychiatric consult alongside — see `safety.md`.

## Why standard BA may not be enough for anhedonia

Anhedonia is reward-system dysfunction across three components: **approach motivation** (wanting), **initial responsiveness to reward** (liking), and **reward learning** (updating expectations from outcomes). Standard activity scheduling assumes the person can want and enjoy at least a little; in marked anhedonia those capacities are blunted, so scheduling alone can stall. BATA adds explicit psychoeducation plus Dabbling and Savoring to target wanting, liking, and learning directly.

Two adaptations to the universal techniques in anhedonia:
- **Mastery before pleasure.** When pleasure is flat, prioritize **mastery** activities (4) — they lift mood without requiring enjoyment.
- **Track initiation, not just completion.** In Activity Monitoring (1), log *attempts to start*, not only finished activities — initiation is the thing that's impaired.
- **Values may need more scaffolding** (3) — anhedonia impairs introspective access to what one prefers; use the smaller prompts, and consider starting with Dabbling *before* values work.

## Anticipatory vs consummatory pleasure

Teach this distinction — it reframes the user's experience and sets up both modules:
- **Anticipatory pleasure (wanting):** looking forward to something. Often the *more* impaired of the two, and — usefully — anticipated pleasure is a strong predictor of improvement.
- **Consummatory pleasure (liking):** experiencing it in the moment. May have residual capacity that goes unnoticed without deliberate attention — which is what Savoring trains.

A practical move from this: track **predicted vs actual** enjoyment (4, 7). Anhedonia drives systematic *under*prediction; the gap, made visible, is both evidence and a learning signal.

## 17 · Dabbling

**Reach for it when:** anhedonia, impaired *initiation*, a narrow behavioral repertoire, "I can't think of anything I want to do." First-line in BATA. The aim is **starting and contact with novelty — not pleasure.**

**Walk it through:**
1. Normalize the initiation problem. "Anhedonia specifically makes it hard to *start* new things, not just to enjoy them. That's the symptom, not you failing." Ask: "Does that match how it feels?"
2. Assign brief **novel** activities. Ask: "What are a few small things you've never really tried — outside your usual set — you could dabble in this week?" Novelty is the active ingredient.
3. Set a curious, no-expectation stance. "We're gathering data about what might interest you. Pleasure isn't the goal here — trying is."
4. Keep them brief. Minutes, not hours — to keep the initiation barrier as low as possible.
5. Frame neutral/unsuccessful attempts as **data, not failure.** Ask: "Even if it did nothing for you — what did you notice while doing it (sensory, what you did, who was around)?"
6. Use what they noticed to inform later scheduling (5).

**If it stalls:** if even brief novel activities feel impossible, apply **Graded Task Assignment** (6) to shrink the first Dabbling step to almost nothing ("open the craft supplies for 30 seconds"). Reassure that *no pleasure during Dabbling is expected* and not a failure.

## 18 · Savoring

**Reach for it when:** anhedonia with blunted in-the-moment reward; applied **after** Dabbling has re-established some novel engagement. Trains consummatory (liking) capacity.

**Walk it through:**
1. Recall the wanting/liking distinction, and the point: "There may be more residual in-the-moment capacity than you notice without deliberately attending to it."
2. Pick an activity with any positive potential, and set the intention to attend. Ask: "Which activity has even a sliver of potential — and can you go into it deliberately paying attention?"
3. During it, attend across the senses. Ask afterward: "What came in through sight, sound, touch, taste, smell?"
4. Slow it down. "Deliberately slow the activity to lengthen contact with anything positive."
5. Record what was detected. Ask: "Afterward — was there *any* positive sensation, however mild or brief?"
6. Keep Savoring sequenced after Dabbling; don't lead with it.

**If it stalls:** if no positive signal shows after several Savoring attempts, return to Dabbling with different activity categories. If consummatory pleasure stays completely absent over weeks, flag that a psychiatric consult about the anhedonia is worth considering (`safety.md`).

## Reward-circuitry psychoeducation

Explaining anhedonia in plain neurobiological terms reduces self-blame. Points to convey (briefly, not as a lecture):
- Anhedonia involves changes in the brain's reward circuitry — the system for approach motivation, anticipation, and reward learning. It's a **symptom, not a character trait.**
- The blunting of pleasure is not permanent or a sign of who they are.
- BA targets this circuitry **through behavior**: scheduled contact with potentially rewarding activities gives the brain the learning opportunities it needs to recalibrate.
- Expected pleasure is often more impaired than in-the-moment enjoyment — so people frequently notice *more* during an activity than they predicted. That's exactly what monitoring and Savoring are designed to surface.

## The honest nuance to say out loud

The case for BATA over standard BA for anhedonia is **theoretically grounded and supported by neuroimaging — but not by a head-to-head clinical superiority trial.** There's no published RCT comparing BATA directly to standard BA. Don't oversell it as proven-better; offer it as a sensible, evidence-informed adaptation when wanting and liking are the blunted thing. And keep the general BA honesty: early reps may feel like nothing — that's predicted, not failure.
