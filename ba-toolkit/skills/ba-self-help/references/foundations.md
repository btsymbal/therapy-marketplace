## How to use this file

This is the **why** behind every technique in the skill — the behavioral model BA rests on. Use it as a lens and a source of one-or-two-sentence rationale, **not** a lecture to paste. The user rarely needs the theory delivered explicitly; they need the *stance* it produces — that doing comes before feeling, that avoidance is the engine, that they're not broken. Reach for a sentence from here when a technique needs its rationale, then run the technique (`walkthrough-protocol.md`).

## The RCPR model: depression as a reinforcement problem

BA is grounded in a **behavioral**, not cognitive, model of depression. The core idea (Ferster; Lewinsohn): depression sets in when a person's behavior stops reliably producing rewarding outcomes — a low rate of **response-contingent positive reinforcement** (RCPR). Life loses its sources of reward (a loss, a stressor, a move, an illness), the person does less, doing less removes still more reward, and the spiral tightens:

```
life stressor → mood drops → withdrawal/avoidance → fewer rewarding experiences → deeper depression → more withdrawal
```

The clinical punchline the user needs to feel: depressed behavior is a **reasonable response to a depleted reinforcement environment**, not a character flaw or a failure of willpower. They're not broken — they're in a cycle, and behavior is the way out. This non-pathologizing framing is itself therapeutic; lead with it.

## Avoidance as the central mechanism

The contemporary model puts **avoidance** — behavioral and cognitive — at the centre of how depression keeps itself going. When people feel bad they avoid: stay in bed, decline invitations, stop opening the laptop, ruminate instead of acting. Each avoidance gives **short-term relief** (that's the negative reinforcement that locks it in) while cutting off contact with the people and activities that could actually lift mood. Five kinds of avoidance to listen for:

1. **Social withdrawal** — declining contact, isolating.
2. **Non-social avoidance** — excessive bed rest, not doing tasks.
3. **Cognitive avoidance** — not addressing problems, dodging decisions.
4. **Distraction avoidance** — endless TV/scrolling, comfort eating.
5. **Emotional avoidance** — substance use to not feel.

**Rumination is an avoidance behavior** in this model — repetitive self-focused thinking removes the person from contact with their immediate environment and is maintained by the relief it briefly gives. That's why BA targets the *function* of rumination (what it lets the person avoid) rather than arguing with its content (`core-techniques.md` rumination-as-avoidance).

## The outside-in principle

BA's defining direction: **behavior before mood — "act opposite to your urge."** The person does not wait to feel motivated before acting; they act first and observe the mood change afterward. "Action precedes motivation," not the reverse. This is the single most important thing to transmit, and it's what separates BA from cognitive therapy: BA doesn't first try to change the belief that an activity is pointless — it schedules the activity and lets the experience be the evidence. Help the user **suspend judgment about the outcome until after the attempt**.

## The two protocol variants

Two well-evidenced flavours of BA. You don't pick one rigidly — you borrow from both — but knowing the difference sharpens technique choice:

| | **Martell-style BA** | **BATD / BATD-R** (Lejuez) |
|---|---|---|
| Core emphasis | Functional analysis of avoidance; TRAP/TRAC; flexible | Values-based activity hierarchy; behavioral contract |
| Structure | Semi-structured, case-driven | Structured: values → hierarchy → contract → monitor |
| Rumination | Explicitly targeted as cognitive avoidance | Not a primary focus |
| Activity selection | What historically provided reinforcement | Values across 10 life areas (LAVA) |

Neither uses a generic "pleasant events" list as the mechanism — **both are individualized.** Reach for the Martell tools (functional analysis, TRAP/TRAC, rumination work) when avoidance is the obvious engine; reach for the BATD tools (values/LAVA, the graded hierarchy, contracting) when the user needs structure and a values anchor to find activities at all.

## What the evidence says (one paragraph)

BA reliably beats inactive controls and is broadly **equivalent to full CBT and to antidepressant medication** — not inferior, not clearly superior. It works for severe depression, prevents relapse, and — the headline finding (COBRA trial, Lancet) — works even when delivered by briefly-trained non-specialists, which makes it the most *scalable* evidence-based depression treatment there is. NICE recommends it as first-line. Use this for confidence and honest framing, not as a sales pitch; pair it with the honest nuance that early activations may feel flat before the loop turns (`safety.md`).

## Measuring the mechanism: BADS

The **Behavioral Activation for Depression Scale (BADS)** tracks the BA *mechanism* itself, not just symptom relief. Four subscales:

1. **Activation** — how much the person is actively engaging in scheduled, valued activities.
2. **Avoidance / Rumination** — how much behavioral and cognitive avoidance, and rumination.
3. **Work / School Impairment** — avoidance of responsibilities.
4. **Social Impairment** — social withdrawal.

You won't administer the formal scale in self-help, but use its *logic* as the thing you're actually tracking session to session (a short 9-item form, BADS-SF, exists for exactly this): is activation climbing and avoidance/rumination falling? That can move **before** mood does — which is why "I did the walk but still feel flat" is the mechanism working, not failing. Capture that sense in the log (`logging.md`), and let it counter the user's read that nothing's changing.
