## Adaptations and clinical context

This is an **orienting reference**, not a walk-through. It's here so you can answer a user's question about IPT variants, or recognize when their situation points to a specialized form — and so you can speak sensibly about when IPT is chosen over or combined with other treatment. None of these are things to "run" as self-help; the problem-area files and `core-techniques.md` are where the actual work lives.

## IPT adaptations (variants of the same model)

All of these keep the core IPT model (the four problem areas, the cross-cutting techniques); they differ in population, format, or an added component.

- **IPT-A (adolescents, 12–18).** Standard structure adapted with developmental language and a "closeness circle" tool; parents attend 1–3 sessions only (to protect the alliance with the teen); peer relationships get weight alongside family; the sick role does **not** excuse missing school. One of only two evidence-based therapies for adolescent depression (with CBT). May be especially suited where there's high parent–child conflict or low family income.
- **IPT-G (group format).** Pre-group individual sessions set each member's formulation; the group becomes "a laboratory for experiments in communicating". Manualized for eating disorders (Wilfley); also used for PTSD and in low-resource settings.
- **Maintenance IPT (IPT-M).** Monthly sessions over a long period, under a *separate* contract after acute treatment, to prevent recurrence in people who've recovered. Important caveat from the source: IPT does **not** show strong relapse prevention once it *ends* — so someone at high recurrence risk needs continued care (IPT-M or medication), not a one-off. Combined medication + IPT-M is the most robustly supported recurrence-prevention strategy.
- **IPSRT (Interpersonal and Social Rhythm Therapy — bipolar).** Standard IPT plus social-rhythm theory: life events that disrupt daily rhythms (sleep-wake, routine) can trigger bipolar episodes. Adds the Social Rhythm Metric (tracking daily activities, social stimulation, mood/energy). **Adjunct to mood-stabilizing medication, not a standalone bipolar treatment** — and only for *stabilized* bipolar (see `safety.md`).
- **Perinatal IPT (IPT-P).** For pregnant and postpartum women; pregnancy/new-parenthood framed as a role transition (the primary focus). "Complicated pregnancy" is a sub-type of role transitions, not a separate area. Notable because IPT is often preferred perinatally when medication is something the person wants to avoid.
- **Group IPT for low-resource settings (WHO).** Delivered by trained lay facilitators after brief training — no professional clinician required; WHO mhGAP-endorsed; strong RCT evidence (rural Uganda). Three phases: inventory, making changes, planning for independent continuation.
- **Brief IPT (IPT-B)** — ~8 sessions for efficient delivery. **Family-Based IPT (FB-IPT, ages 8–12)** — splits individual and parent–child work.

If a user's situation maps onto one of these (an adolescent, a bipolar diagnosis, the perinatal period), name it and suggest they seek the specialized form with a clinician — don't try to deliver the adaptation here.

## When IPT is chosen over CBT

IPT and CBT are about equally effective for depression overall; the choice is guided by the person and the problem. **Lean toward IPT** when:
- The low mood is clearly tied to a recent interpersonal event (grief, a life transition, a relationship dispute) — a direct match between the method and the problem. This is the strongest signal, and it's exactly what this skill routes on.
- Pregnancy/postpartum when the person wants to avoid medication.
- The person is affect- and relationship-focused rather than thought-focused.
- They're uncomfortable with structured homework or skills practice.
- Adolescents with high parent–child conflict; low-income or interpersonally isolated presentations.
- PTSD with a sexual-trauma history (IPT, non-exposure, has specific support there — clinician-delivered).

**Lean toward CBT** (point the user there — the sibling `cbt-toolkit` exists) when:
- The problem is a specific phobia, panic, OCD, or health anxiety — exposure/cognitive work fits better.
- The distress runs through a clearly distorted *thought* that wants testing, rather than a relationship.
- Relapse prevention is the goal (CBT shows post-treatment prevention effects; IPT doesn't unless continued).
- Comorbid personality disorder is prominent; or they want structured skills practice and homework.

When the distress has **no interpersonal thread at all**, IPT is simply the wrong tool — say so and redirect (`routing.md`, `safety.md`).

## Combined treatment (IPT + medication)

Combined medication + IPT is consistently better than either alone for most depression outcomes. Medication is worth adding to IPT when: there's a history of severe symptoms that previously responded to an antidepressant; relapse on stopping medication before; no adequate improvement from psychotherapy alone after a fair trial; pronounced psychomotor symptoms; or the person prefers a combined approach. IPT may be preferred over medication in pregnancy/breastfeeding (a risk–benefit conversation) or by personal preference. These are decisions for the user and a prescriber — surface the consideration, don't make the call.

## The honest bottom line for this skill

This file describes a large, well-evidenced *clinical* treatment with many specialized forms. This skill offers a small, useful slice of it: running individual IPT techniques on a present interpersonal problem, as education and self-help. When a user's situation calls for one of the adaptations above, or for combined treatment, or for a clinician's judgment — name that, and treat this companion as a bridge to it, not a substitute (`safety.md`).
