This file is the foundation the routing and the problem-area files lean on. It explains *why* IPT works the way it does. You don't walk a user through this file as a technique — you draw on it to frame the work and to explain the model when it helps.

## The interpersonal model

IPT's central premise is bidirectional: disturbing life events and interpersonal problems can **precipitate or maintain** low mood, and low mood in turn **impairs interpersonal functioning**. The whole treatment turns on helping the person see this link and resolve a focal interpersonal problem — which lifts the mood with it.

Four change mechanisms operate at once: (1) enhancing social support; (2) decreasing interpersonal stress; (3) facilitating emotional processing; (4) improving interpersonal skills. IPT's distinctiveness is that it activates all four within a pragmatic, affectively-charged focus on **one central interpersonal problem** — not a diffuse exploration.

The practical upshot for this skill: keep the session anchored to a specific relationship or life change. When the user drifts to abstract rumination, gently bring it back to who and what. The mood is the symptom; the relationship is the lever.

## The medical-model framing and the sick role

IPT frames low mood as a common, treatable condition that is **not the person's fault** — "something the patient has, rather than a defining feature of who they are." In formal IPT, naming it and assigning a temporary "sick role" reduces self-blame and guilt, excuses some current pressure, and reframes the person's job as *getting well* and leaning on support.

In this self-help context, you are not diagnosing or assigning a clinical sick role. But the *spirit* is useful and worth carrying: depression makes ordinary things harder, and that is the illness talking, not a character flaw. "The low mood is making this harder than it would otherwise be — that's not you failing, that's how this works." Use it to lift self-blame, lightly, not to excuse the person from their life. (In adolescents, formal IPT is explicit that the sick role does *not* excuse missing school — the same caution against over-applying it holds.)

## The three-phase structure (and how this skill uses it)

Standard acute IPT runs **12–16 weekly sessions** in three phases. This skill is not a course of treatment, so it doesn't reproduce the phases — but knowing them tells you where a given piece of work sits, and lets you orient a user who *is* in or considering IPT.

- **Initial phase (sessions ~1–3).** Build alliance; assess; psychoeducation; conduct the interpersonal inventory; pick the problem area (one primary, two max); agree the formulation and a treatment contract. *In this skill:* the "land, locate, route, and state the focus" opening (`SKILL.md` steps 1–3) is a compressed echo of this — get the relationship, the temporal link, and name the area.
- **Middle phase (sessions ~4–13).** Each session opens with a brief mood check and the interval's interpersonal events, links them to the focal area, and applies the problem-area techniques. *In this skill:* this is where almost all the work happens — the technique walk-throughs in the problem-area files.
- **Termination phase (last 2–3 sessions).** Name the ending; review gains and credit the person; frame the ending itself as a positive role transition ("graduation"); consolidate; plan for relapse warning signs. *In this skill:* the close of a session (`walkthrough-protocol.md`) borrows the crediting and consolidation, in miniature.

One honest caveat to hold: IPT does **not**, on its own, show strong relapse-prevention effects after it ends (unlike CBT). For someone at real risk of recurrence, ongoing care matters — say so rather than implying a single session inoculates them.

## The interpersonal inventory

The interpersonal inventory is IPT's foundational assessment: a guided tour of the person's significant relationships, present and past. It's what reveals which problem area is most relevant. In a full course it spans the initial phase; in this skill you do a fast, light version while locating the problem.

What it covers:
- Current and past significant relationships — romantic, family, social, work.
- Communication patterns and style in each.
- Role expectations — what each party expects of the other.
- Satisfaction, dissatisfaction, and reciprocity.
- Who they confide in; where their support comes from.
- Major life events, the mood changes around them, and the timing.
- Recurring patterns across relationships.

**How to do the light version:** open non-directively — "Tell me about the people who matter most to you right now" — then, for the relationship in focus, get specific: how often they're in contact, what they do together, what they like and dislike about it, what they wish were different, how it's changed. For the cast that recurs across sessions, this is what feeds the "Key relationships" section of `CLAUDE.md` (`logging.md`).

## The case formulation

The formulation is a brief, explicit statement linking the low mood to a specific interpersonal problem area. It's the bridge from assessment to work, and it's shared openly with the person. The form:

> "Your low mood [began / has been kept going] around the time that [interpersonal event / problem]. As we work on [that event / problem], I'd expect the mood to ease with it."

In this skill, say a one-sentence version out loud when you route ("This sounds like grief that's got stuck — let's work on the mourning, and I'd expect the weight to shift as we do"). It tells the user *why* you're focusing where you are, and gives the session a spine. See `routing.md` for the rules on which area to name when more than one fits.

## What IPT is NOT

Knowing the boundaries keeps the technique honest — and several of these are exactly where IPT diverges from CBT, which matters because the sibling `cbt-toolkit` does the other thing:

- **No formal homework** in the CBT sense (no structured worksheets / assigned tasks). A "next move" in this skill is a real-world interpersonal step, not a homework sheet.
- **No challenging or restructuring of thoughts.** IPT does not test whether a thought is distorted or proportionate. When a balanced view emerges (e.g. a balanced inventory of old vs new role), it comes through noticing interpersonal reality, not disputing logic.
- **No transference interpretation.** Even where the therapeutic relationship is used as material (interpersonal deficits), it's treated as a real present-day relationship, not a re-enactment of the past. See `interpersonal-deficits.md`.
- **No trauma exposure, dream analysis, or systematic unconscious work.**
- **Present-focused, not developmental.** IPT works the here-and-now relationship, not childhood origins.
- **Not just empathy and support.** It has a defined focus and a technique repertoire — it is active and structured, not open-ended listening.

If the user actually needs the *other* thing (to test a clearly distorted thought, to work a phobia, to process trauma), say so and point them elsewhere (`safety.md`, `routing.md`). IPT is the right tool when the distress runs through a relationship or a life change.
