## What it is and when it applies

A role dispute is a conflict with an important person in which the two of you hold **differing, non-reciprocal expectations** about the relationship. It can be **overt** (open argument) or **covert** (cold, unspoken distance and resentment). Contexts: partner, parent, child, sibling, friend, boss. The low mood comes from the unresolved, ongoing conflict — or from the threat that a valued relationship might end.

**What makes disputes uniquely demanding:** unlike grief and transitions, which centre on the user's own needs, a dispute requires holding **both** people's needs and motives in mind at once. That makes it more complex and often slower to shift. Don't expect a single session to resolve a marriage.

**Safety note first:** if the dispute involves any risk of violence or abuse — toward the user or by them — that is a safety matter, not a renegotiation. Drop the technique and follow `safety.md`.

A dispute with someone who has **died** is grief, not this area (`grief.md`). A dispute that has already **ended** the relationship is a role transition (`role-transitions.md`).

## The three-stage model (identify the stage first)

The stage sets the goal. Working out the stage is your first task, because it changes what you're aiming for.

| Stage | What it is | The goal |
|---|---|---|
| **Renegotiation** | Communication hasn't stopped; the user lacks the skills or confidence to express themselves, or treats their own expectations as less important | Help them see their expectations are genuine and reasonable; encourage assertive expression |
| **Impasse** | Communication has stopped; the user feels renegotiation is impossible, hopeless about change | "Heat up" the cold dispute — encourage them to directly ask the other person what *they* expect; reopening a stopped conversation surfaces the contradictory expectations, even if it's temporarily tense |
| **Dissolution** | One or both want to end the relationship; reached only after renegotiation has genuinely been exhausted | Facilitate mourning the loss of the relationship — the dissolution itself becomes a role transition |

Ask what tells you the stage: "When this comes up now, do you still talk about it, or has it gone quiet?" "Do you feel there's any room to change it, or does it feel finished?"

## Goals

1. Help the user recognize the dispute exists and name what it's about.
2. Find options to renegotiate; decide on a plan.
3. Correct faulty expectations on both sides.
4. Improve communication enough to resolve the issues — and where full resolution isn't possible, help the user modify their expectations or communicate more adequately. Even a partial result counts.

## Technique catalog

Draws on `core-techniques.md`; one step per turn (`walkthrough-protocol.md`).

### 1. Communication analysis of the dispute

The primary tool — it makes the dispute's mechanics concrete. See `core-techniques.md` 4 for the full play-by-play. Specifically in dispute work:
1. Reconstruct a specific recent exchange where the dispute was live: "Tell me about the most recent time this tension came up. Walk me through exactly what happened."
2. Examine: what was expressed vs what was felt; assertion failures (felt one thing, said another); faulty assumptions about the other's intent; non-verbals that contradicted or amplified the words; missed chances to repair.
3. Reflect the pattern and plan forward: "Each time you raise it, you end up saying [X] when you really want to say [Y] — so what would saying [Y] sound like next time?"

### 2. Perspective-taking

**When best:** throughout — uniquely central here, because a dispute is inherently two-sided. Moves the user from a unilateral view to a fuller one.

**How:**
1. Once they've given their side, gently invite the other's: "Let's try this from [person]'s point of view. What might they have been feeling in that moment? What might they have needed?"
2. Don't invalidate the user's view — hold both: "Both can be true: you had good reason to react as you did, *and* they may have had their own reasons."
3. Use what they know about the person to build a plausible account: "You've said they shut down when they feel criticized — could that have been happening here?"
4. Test whether their expectations of the other are realistic given who that person is: "What do you know about how they handle this kind of thing?"

Hold the result as a *hypothesis*, not the truth — you're building a plausible read of the other person, not declaring what they "really" meant.

**If it stalls:** role play (`core-techniques.md` 7) — take the other person's role so the user feels how it looks from the other side.

### 3. Role play of dispute scenarios

**When best:** after communication analysis has found a pattern to change; when the user can't picture communicating differently; in the renegotiation stage when they need rehearsal. Use `core-techniques.md` 7, specifically:
- Play the other party as they'd likely behave, from the user's description.
- Give the user practice expressing their genuine needs assertively and clearly.
- Rehearse repair moves and recovering from escalation.
- Debrief what worked and what felt hard.

### 4. Decision analysis about the relationship's future

**When best:** at impasse, or nearing dissolution; when the user must decide whether to keep trying to renegotiate, accept the situation, or end it. See `core-techniques.md` 6. Specifically:
1. Generate options explicitly: "What are the realistic options? You could talk to them about what's not working. You could accept it as it is. You could decide it isn't sustainable. Any others?"
2. Evaluate each realistically — interpersonal and emotional consequences included.
3. **Do not guide toward stay-or-leave.** This is theirs. Your job is to help them weigh it, not to answer it.

### 5. Affect exploration around the dispute

**When best:** early, before communication analysis and decision work; when the user is too flooded to work, or conversely too cut-off and intellectualized to reach the feeling. Apply encouragement of affect (`core-techniques.md` 5) to the dispute: validate the feelings as genuine and reasonable before any action planning. Don't move to communication strategy while they're overwhelmed or emotionally numb.

## How a dispute session tends to go (by stage)

**Renegotiation:**
- Mood check + interval. "How did things go with [person] this week?"
- Communication analysis of a specific exchange.
- Validate the feelings; then explore more assertive communication.
- Role play it; debrief; plan the next real interaction.

**Impasse:**
- Acknowledge the hopelessness honestly.
- Explore what stopped the communication: "What do you think would happen if you raised it directly?"
- Gently challenge the impasse: "What if you simply asked them what they want from this relationship?"
- "Cold disputes need heating up" — help them take one step to reopen the conversation, even if it raises tension at first.

**Dissolution:**
- Verify renegotiation is genuinely exhausted — not just *felt* impossible.
- Facilitate mourning the relationship as it was, or as it was hoped to be.
- Move, as it fits, into role-transition work — the ending is a new role to navigate (`role-transitions.md`).

## If it doesn't work

- If it's volatile and the user is in danger: prioritize safety; this stops being a communication exercise (`safety.md`).
- If they can't summon any empathy for the other party: keep validating the user's feelings and return to perspective-taking gently over time — don't force it in one go.
- If it's clearly dissolution but they can't accept it: treat it like grief for the relationship, and move gradually to a role-transition frame.
- If nothing changes: reassess the stage (impasse often masquerades as renegotiation); consider whether a long-standing interpersonal-deficits pattern is blocking any movement (`interpersonal-deficits.md`).
