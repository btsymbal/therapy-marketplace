## Contents
The eight cross-cutting IPT techniques, used in every problem area. Each has: when it works best, how to do it step by step, and what to try if it doesn't work. The problem-area files (`grief.md`, `role-disputes.md`, `role-transitions.md`, `interpersonal-deficits.md`) call these by name and add their specialized layer.

5. Encouragement of affect

7. Role play
8. Psychoeducation

Deliver every one of these **one step per turn**, per `walkthrough-protocol.md`. The quoted questions are English templates — translate them to the user's language.

## 1. Nondirective exploration

**When it works best:** Opening of a session; the light interpersonal inventory; whenever you need to understand the user's experience without steering. Most important early, while the terrain is still being mapped.

**How to do it:**
1. Ask open questions that don't presuppose an answer: "Tell me about the people who matter most to you." "What's been happening with [person] lately?" "What's been on your mind since we last spoke?"
2. Follow the user's lead — let them choose what to develop.
3. Use attentive space after an open question; don't fill the pause prematurely.
4. Reflect back what you heard without interpreting: "So that conversation left you unsure where you stand with her."
5. Keep your curiosity genuine — don't fish for a predetermined answer.

**If it doesn't work** (they're concrete, stuck, or you need specifics): shift to direct elicitation (2).

---

## 2. Direct elicitation

**When it works best:** When open exploration yields too little; when you need a specific detail for a communication analysis (what exactly was said); when the user is skating past a feeling or topic.

**How to do it:**
1. Move from broad to specific: "What exactly did you say when she walked in?" "What were you feeling at that precise moment?" "What did you expect him to say back?"
2. Use closed or semi-closed questions for granular emotional and interactional detail.
3. Ask for an example, not a generality: "Can you give me a recent specific time this happened?"
4. One question at a time — don't stack three.
5. Return to open exploration once you have the detail.

**If it doesn't work:** feed it into communication analysis (4) — direct elicitation is often the tool that supplies the play-by-play.

---

## 3. Clarification

**When it works best:** When the account contains a contradiction or ambiguity (describing an angry scene while smiling; "it was fine" said tightly); when you're unsure what something meant; as an ongoing way to help the user see a pattern they can't see in themselves.

**How to do it:**
1. Name the inconsistency neutrally, without confronting: "You said the argument ended fine, but you seem quite upset telling me — help me understand that."
2. Reflect it back plainly: "Earlier he sounded very supportive, and now you're describing feeling alone with it. I want to get that right."
3. Ask them to elaborate or resolve it.
4. Work both levels: check your own accuracy, *and* help the user notice their own bias in how they're describing the situation.
5. Remember clarification is often therapeutic in itself — you don't always have to push to full resolution.

**If it doesn't work:** what looks like a contradiction is often an unnamed feeling — switch to encouragement of affect (5) to surface it first.

## 4. Communication analysis (the play-by-play)

This is the primary working technique of IPT middle-phase sessions — the tool that turns an abstract "we always argue" into something concrete and workable. Use it whenever the user describes a significant interpersonal exchange; it's central in role disputes but applies across all four areas.

**How to do it (this is many turns, one question each):**
1. **Get one specific, recent, emotionally charged exchange** — not "we always fight". "Tell me about the most recent conversation with [person] that felt important. The most recent one."
2. **Map the setting:** who was there, where, what time, what came just before.
3. **Reconstruct the dialogue sequentially — like a film script:** "What did you say first?" → "What did she say back?" → "Then what did you say?" Go line by line.
4. **Examine tone and non-verbals:** "What was your tone? Your body language? Hers?"
5. **Explore the feeling at each beat:** "What were you feeling when you said that?" "What were you feeling when she answered that way?"
6. **Compare stated vs intended vs perceived:** "What were you trying to say?" "What do you think she heard?" "What do you think she intended?"
7. **Name the problematic pattern:** look for a gap between what was felt and what was said (assertion failure), faulty assumptions about the other's intent, non-verbal leakage contradicting the words, missed chances to repair.
8. **Reflect the pattern back:** "It looks like each time this comes up, you say [X] when you actually want to say [Y], and she ends up feeling [Z] when you wanted [W]. Does that fit?"
9. **Plan the next interaction:** "Given what we just saw, how might you approach this conversation differently?"

Each charged exchange is a "biopsy of interpersonal functioning" — over repeated examinations the recurring pattern shows itself.

**If it doesn't work** (can't reconstruct it verbally, or naming the pattern isn't enough alone): switch to role play (7) — enact the exchange so it can be seen and an alternative rehearsed.

## 5. Encouragement of affect

This is what most distinguishes IPT from CBT. Use it throughout, in every area, and especially when the user intellectualizes, minimizes, or can't reach the feeling.

**The IPT view of affect:** feelings are **not obstacles or distortions** — they are interpersonal signals carrying a message about what the person needs. Painful feelings are not symptoms to be challenged or tested for proportionality (that's CBT). Anger, grief, guilt, relief — each is normal and informative. You validate it and help express it, you don't dispute it.

**How to do it:**
1. **Make space for the feeling:** "You mentioned the loss quite briefly — how did that actually feel?" "Take your time; I want to hear what that was like."
2. **Name the emotion** when they allude to it without naming it: "It sounds like there might have been anger there — is that right?"
3. **Validate it as normal:** "Given what you went through, that makes complete sense." Stress that feeling it or saying it doesn't make them weak.
4. **Elicit the full range, including the complicated ones** — anger, guilt, relief, ambivalence. In grief especially, validate the feelings people fear are "wrong" (anger at the person who died; relief; guilt about the relief).
5. **Let them experience it in the moment** — don't rush to problem-solve; hold the feeling with them before moving on.
6. **Link it to the relationship:** "What do you think that anger / sadness is telling you about what you need from [person]?"
7. **Don't challenge it.** You are not testing whether the feeling is proportionate; you are understanding its interpersonal message.

**If it doesn't work:** more targeted direct elicitation (2) about the emotional experience; or communication analysis (4), which tends to bring the affect up organically as the exchange is rebuilt.

## 6. Decision analysis

**When it works best:** Middle-phase, especially role disputes (renegotiate vs accept vs leave) and role transitions (what to do about a new demand). Whenever the user faces an interpersonal decision.

**The principle, non-negotiable:** you do **not** prescribe the solution and do **not** offer an opinion on what they should do. Decision analysis builds the person's own problem-solving and supports their autonomy. "Solutions are not offered, nor are opinions expressed about what the patient should do." This is exactly where IPT differs from more directive problem-solving — resist the pull to answer it for them.

**How to do it:**
1. **Name the decision:** "You've described feeling stuck about whether to [stay / raise it with your mother / take the job]. That's a real decision — let's think it through together."
2. **Generate options — theirs first:** "What are the different things you could do here?" Let them produce options before evaluating any. Add one only if there's a genuine omission: "Is there any other possibility we haven't named?"
3. **Evaluate each realistically:** "What might happen if you did that? Pros and cons? Best case? Worst case?"
4. **Explore the interpersonal consequences:** "How do you think [person] might respond to that?"
5. **Explore the felt response to each:** "When you imagine taking that path, what do you feel?"
6. **Hand the decision back:** don't say which is right. Reflect: "When you picture [option A] you seem calmer than with [option B] — what does that tell you?"
7. **Plan implementation** once *they've* decided: how to act on it, what obstacles to expect.

**If it doesn't work:** role play (7) to rehearse a chosen path; or encouragement of affect (5) if they're blocked from deciding because they can't reach what they actually feel.

## 7. Role play

**When it works best:** Middle-phase; rehearsing a hard conversation in a dispute; saying to someone who died what was never said (grief); practicing approaching people (interpersonal deficits); after a communication analysis has identified a pattern the user wants to change. It's one of IPT's more behavioural, practical moves.

**How to do it:**
1. **Set it up explicitly and get buy-in:** "I'd like to try something some people find helpful — would you be willing to act out how a conversation with [person] might go? I'll play [person]."
2. **Brief the scene:** "You're about to bring up [the topic] with your partner. Set it for me — where are you, what time, how's the day gone?"
3. **Run it** — take the other person's role, responding as that person likely would based on what the user has told you. Where useful, play them slightly more challenging than expected, to build resilience.
4. **Stay in role long enough** for a real rehearsal, but step out if the user gets distressed or confused about the exercise.
5. **Debrief:** "How did that feel? What did you notice about how you communicated? What worked? What would you change?"
6. **Demonstrate an alternative if useful:** briefly take the user's role to model one approach, then debrief — "what did you notice, what might you take from that?"
7. **Plan the real-world version:** "Given what we practiced, how might you approach the real conversation?"

**If the user is reluctant** (finds it artificial): normalize — "it can feel strange, but practicing often makes the words come more naturally" — and start with a brief, low-stakes scene. If resistance persists, drop back to communication analysis (4) without the enactment.

**If it doesn't work:** communication analysis (4) to work the pattern without acting it out; or decision analysis (6) to plan the conversation without rehearsing it.

## 8. Psychoeducation

**When it works best:** Early, when framing the work (the mood–relationship link, what to expect); and at any point the user doesn't understand why the session is shaped as it is.

**How to do it:**
1. **About the mood–relationship link:** "Low mood and our relationships feed each other. By working on what's happening with [person], we'd expect the mood to ease too."
2. **About the medical-model framing** (lightly, in self-help — see `model-and-structure.md`): "Depression makes ordinary things harder — that's the illness, not you failing." Use it to lift self-blame, not to excuse them from life.
3. **About what this is (and isn't):** be honest that this is an educational companion running IPT techniques, not a course of IPT treatment (`safety.md`). Set realistic expectations about what one sitting can do.
4. **Repeat as needed** — psychoeducation isn't a one-time event; return to the explanation when the user's response suggests they've lost the thread.

**If it doesn't work** (they reject the framing): explore their own understanding of what's going on before pushing the model; introduce framing gradually rather than insisting on it up front.
