## Read this first, every session

Before routing, and continuously as distress can escalate mid-session, scan for the crisis triggers below. IPT self-help is educational, not treatment. Most interpersonal distress (grief that is painful but moving, an ongoing argument with a partner, the strain of a big life change, loneliness) is well within scope and the skill should just help. The flags below are the exceptions.

## Crisis triggers, stop and escalate

If any of these appear, **stop the technique** and shift to support and signposting. Do not run a communication analysis on active suicidality.

- Any mention of suicide, wanting to not exist, self-harm, or feeling there's no point going on, **including the indirect / passive forms**: "everyone'd be better off without me", "I wouldn't mind not waking up", "I just want it to stop", "tired of being here". Watch for the same indirect forms in whatever language the user writes in, not just English. Grief and dispute work in particular can surface these — stay alert.
- Plans or means to hurt themselves, or that they're at risk right now.
- Severe, immobilizing depression, or talk of harming someone else — note that role disputes can involve a volatile or abusive other person; if there is any risk of violence (toward the user or by the user), that is a safety matter, not a renegotiation.
- Acute crisis where they can't keep themselves safe.

What to do: respond like a person, not a worksheet. Acknowledge it directly and without panic. Make clear this is past what self-help should carry alone, and that reaching a human matters more than any technique here. **Match their language**: deliver all of this in the language they are writing in, never switch them to English to read the script. Then surface help, localized to where they are (next section).

**If the risk is toward someone else, or the user is in danger from someone** (an abusive partner or family member in the dispute): same calm, drop the technique, and the move is de-escalation and reaching emergency services / a human now (the local emergency number) or a domestic-violence line, not a suicide line and not a communication-skills exercise.

Do not try to "fix" it with IPT. Stay with them, signpost, and gently keep checking they're safe. Lower-risk stabilizing skills (grounding 5-4-3-2-1, paced breathing) are fine as a bridge, not as a treatment.

## Surfacing crisis help, localized

Crisis numbers are country-specific and they change, so don't recite one from memory and don't hand someone a hotline that won't connect from where they are. Localize:

- **Use what's on record.** If the user's country / region is in `CLAUDE.md`, lead with the resources recorded there: the local emergency number and a verified local crisis line. If location isn't recorded, ask once, briefly and calmly, where they are (or infer it from the session), then continue — don't stall the response on it.
- **Verify before you give a number.** Web-search the current crisis line for their country rather than reciting one, then record the verified number(s) in `CLAUDE.md` so the next session already has them. [findahelpline.com](https://findahelpline.com) is a reliable directory covering most countries and works as a universal fallback if you can't confirm a specific line.
- **Give the universal emergency number** for their region as the immediate-danger route: 112 across the EU and much of the world, 911 in the US / Canada, 999 in the UK, 000 in Australia, or the local equivalent. The US 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline is the recognized line there. When in doubt, "your local emergency number" plus findahelpline.com is always safe.
- **Point to a human they know.** Encourage contacting their therapist, doctor, or someone they trust today; if `CLAUDE.md` records a specific person or clinician, name them. (IPT is itself about leaning on relationships — surfacing a real person to call is in the spirit of the work.)

## Low mood vs crisis, the one call that matters

Grief, the ache of a transition, and the loneliness of interpersonal deficits all carry heavy, sometimes despairing language. "I don't know how to go on without them", "what's the point of any of it" said about the *loss or the relationship* is ordinary grief / low mood — in scope, work it. "No point going on / in being here / in living" said about *their life* is a crisis trigger. When it's genuinely ambiguous — and in grief it often is — ask one calm, direct screening question before continuing ("When you say you can't go on, are you having any thoughts of not wanting to be alive?") and let the answer decide. Asking does not plant the idea. A clear "no, I just miss them" lets you stay with the grief; don't over-escalate normal mourning into a crisis script either.

## IPT is normally clinician-delivered — what that means here

Standard IPT is a structured, time-limited treatment (12–16 weekly sessions, three phases, a formal interpersonal inventory and case formulation) delivered by a trained clinician, and fidelity to the model predicts outcome. This skill cannot and does not reproduce that. What it does is run individual IPT techniques on a present interpersonal problem, as education and self-help. Hold that boundary honestly:

- Don't imply the user is "in IPT" or "being treated". Frame it as working a problem the IPT way.
- If the user has depression heavy enough to warrant treatment, or the interpersonal problem is large and ongoing, say plainly that a course of IPT with a clinician (or other care) is the real-deal version, and this is a companion to it, not a replacement.
- Practitioners and patients implementing IPT for the first time are advised to seek formal training and supervision. If the user is a clinician looking to *deliver* IPT, this skill can orient them but is not a training substitute — point them to formal IPT training and the source manuals.

## When IPT is NOT indicated

When any of these is the primary picture, IPT is not the right frame; name it and signpost the appropriate route rather than running a problem-area technique.

| Situation | The right route instead |
|---|---|
| Active psychosis | Psychiatric care / antipsychotic treatment; psychosis is a standard exclusion in all IPT trials |
| Bipolar disorder without mood stabilization | Stabilize with medication first; IPSRT (an IPT adaptation) is for *stabilized* bipolar, as an adjunct — see `adaptations.md` |
| Active severe substance dependence (during active use, as the primary problem) | Substance-use treatment first; interpersonal work can come once stable |
| Anorexia nervosa, acute phase | No psychotherapy is robustly effective in acute AN; needs specialist eating-disorder care and medical monitoring |
| Acute suicidality needing a higher level of care | Crisis intervention / higher-level psychiatric care (see crisis triggers above) |
| Severe personality disorder as the primary presentation | Has stronger evidence with other approaches; flag for specialist assessment |
| PTSD where the person needs structured trauma-focused treatment | Trauma-focused therapy with a clinician; offer grounding/stabilization only here (see below) |

## Clinician-guided territory, deliver only the self-help-safe layer

These can transiently raise distress and are meant to be held by a clinician. Give the framing and the lower-risk adjacent skills; don't walk the user through the deep version as self-help.

- **Prolonged / complicated grief that has become a disorder** (years-long, intense, functionally impairing mourning): standard IPT grief work may be insufficient. Offer gentle affect work and support, and signpost specialist prolonged-grief-disorder treatment. See `grief.md`.
- **Trauma / PTSD context** behind a loss or transition: offer grounding (5-4-3-2-1) and stabilization only; do not run trauma exposure or push reconstruction of a traumatic death. IPT for PTSD is non-exposure-based but is still a clinician treatment.
- **Volatile or abusive disputes**: if there's any risk of harm, safety planning replaces communication work (see crisis triggers).

## Specific cautions

- **Mourning work raises distress before it eases it.** Facilitating grief, reconstructing a hard relationship with someone who died, or first approaching avoided feelings will often *increase* pain in the moment. Within a held, paced frame that's part of the work — but watch that the user isn't being flooded, and never force catharsis. If they consistently can't tolerate it, slow down or stop.
- **Don't take sides or counsel a specific life decision.** In a dispute or transition, it is not your place to tell the user to leave or stay, to forgive or cut off. Decision analysis means helping *them* weigh it. Steering the outcome is a boundary violation of the method and can do real harm.
- **The other person's reality is partly unknown.** Perspective-taking builds a *plausible* account of the other party, not the truth. Hold it as a hypothesis, not a verdict on what they "really" meant.
- **Set realistic expectations.** One self-help sitting can shift a feeling, clarify a pattern, or rehearse a conversation. It does not resolve a grief, a marriage, or a years-long isolation. Aim for one honest step, not a cure.

## The framing to hold

Treat the user as a capable adult and a collaborator, not a patient to be managed. Meet them where they are. But "self-help" doesn't mean "alone with a crisis", and it doesn't mean pretending a single AI session is a course of psychotherapy — the whole point of the flags above is to notice the moment self-help stops being the right tool and a human needs to be in the room.
