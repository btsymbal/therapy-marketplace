## How to route

The user won't name a problem area, they'll name a feeling and a situation ("I can't stop crying since my dad died", "my wife and I just go in circles", "since the baby came I don't recognize my life", "I have no one to call"). Your job: from what they say plus the concrete interpersonal moment, identify which of the **four IPT problem areas** the low mood is tied to, then reach for that area's **first-line technique** and open its file. Pick one area to focus, not a menu — one primary, two maximum.

The locating question that does most of the work: **"When did the low mood start or get worse, and what was happening in your relationships around then?"** The interpersonal event with the strongest temporal link to the mood is usually the area.

Always do the safety scan (`safety.md`) and get a feeling + 0-100 intensity + a specific relationship and recent moment first (`SKILL.md` flow steps 1-2). For the model behind these areas, see `model-and-structure.md`.

## Front door: what the user says → problem area

| What they bring | Problem area | File |
|---|---|---|
| A significant person **died**, and the mourning has stalled, never started, gone on long past the acute period, or feels stuck / avoided | **Grief** (complicated bereavement) | `grief.md` |
| Ongoing conflict, tension, or cold distance with a specific important person (partner, parent, child, sibling, friend, boss) — they want different things and it isn't resolving | **Role dispute** | `role-disputes.md` |
| A major life change — wanted or unwanted — that they're struggling to adapt to: divorce, breakup, new baby, job loss / new job / promotion, retirement, moving / immigration, a diagnosis, empty nest, graduation, widowhood | **Role transition** | `role-transitions.md` |
| Long-standing loneliness and isolation, few or no close relationships, a history of relationships that don't last or are conflicted — and **no** specific recent event that set off the mood | **Interpersonal deficits** | `interpersonal-deficits.md` |
| A loss that is **not a death** (lost job, lost health, lost relationship, lost role) | Role transition, not grief | `role-transitions.md` |
| A dispute that has clearly **ended** the relationship (the breakup / estrangement already happened) and they're grieving it | Role transition (the dissolution is now a transition); may borrow grief techniques | `role-transitions.md` (+ `grief.md` for the mourning) |
| Distress with **no interpersonal thread** at all — a specific phobia, a body-symptom fixation, an isolated panic attack with no relational trigger | Not an IPT case | say so plainly; IPT works through relationships — a different approach (e.g. CBT) fits better |
| Generically "low" / "stuck" with no clear relationship or event named yet | Needs locating | ask the "when did it start, what was happening with people?" question, then route |
| Chronic low mood for as long as they can remember, no clear onset event (dysthymia-like) | No clear temporal link — see the selection rules below ("iatrogenic role transition") | `role-transitions.md` (tentative framing) |

If two rows fit, use the selection rules below to pick.

## Problem area → first-line techniques → file

The moves to reach for **first** in each area. The cross-cutting techniques (communication analysis, encouragement of affect, decision analysis, role play, clarification, exploration) live in `core-techniques.md`; each area file adds its specialized layer.

| Problem area | First-line technique(s) | Files |
|---|---|---|
| **Grief** | Facilitating mourning (catharsis) + reconstructing the relationship with the deceased (positive *and* negative) + encouragement of affect across the full range + re-establishing social support | `grief.md` + `core-techniques.md` |
| **Role dispute** | Identify the stage (renegotiation / impasse / dissolution) first → communication analysis of a specific exchange + perspective-taking + affect exploration + role play + decision analysis about the relationship's future | `role-disputes.md` + `core-techniques.md` |
| **Role transition** | Link the mood to the change + mourning the old role + a balanced inventory of old vs new role + building skills and social support in the new role | `role-transitions.md` + `core-techniques.md` |
| **Interpersonal deficits** | Review of past significant relationships for the recurring pattern + exploration of affect (fear / shame / anticipatory anxiety) + graduated social engagement with role play + reinforcing every step | `interpersonal-deficits.md` + `core-techniques.md` |

## The rules for picking the area when more than one fits

From Parts 1.5 and 8.4 of the source. Apply in order:

1. **Strongest temporal link.** Pick the area whose interpersonal event is most clearly tied in time to when the low mood started or got worse. "When did it begin, and what was happening with the people in your life then?"
2. **Most amenable to change, or most distressing.** If two areas compete on timing, pick the one most likely to actually shift (is this dispute resolvable? is this transition workable?) — or, alternatively, the one causing the most distress right now.
3. **Interpersonal deficits is the last choice.** Only select it when none of the other three fits — i.e. there is *no* identifiable acute interpersonal event, the person has genuinely sparse relationships, and a long-standing pattern of impoverished or conflicted connections. It is the hardest area to work and the one with the weakest outcomes; don't default to it just because someone is lonely *as a result of* a recent loss or transition (that's grief or transition).
4. **Transition vs dispute tiebreak.** When a role transition and a role dispute co-occur (common — e.g. a divorce is both), identify which factor primarily **initiated** or is **maintaining** the low mood, and lead with that.
5. **Dissolution becomes a transition.** When a dispute ends the relationship, the ending itself is a role transition; mourn it as such.
6. **Chronic depression with no clear event.** When the mood has no datable onset and no clear interpersonal trigger (dysthymia-like), the convention is the "iatrogenic role transition" — frame the entry into doing this work itself as the change to focus on. This is a tentative, single-source convention; use it only as a last resort and hold it lightly.

State your pick in one sentence and why ("This sounds most like a role transition — the low mood tracks the move, so let's work the loss of your old life and what's hard about the new one"), then start. Don't lecture them on the taxonomy.

## Multi-problem and re-routing

- Most sessions have one focal area; some genuinely straddle two (a death that also created a transition; a transition that sparked a dispute). Work one at a time — the most acute or most temporally-linked first. Name the other and offer to come back.
- If a walk-through reveals the real area is different from where you started (you opened on grief, but the stuck point is actually an unresolved dispute with a living sibling about the death), say so plainly and switch files. Don't finish the wrong technique for tidiness.
- When affect is too high to work (mid-grief wave, flooded with anger about the dispute), don't push into analysis or planning — stay with the feeling and validate it first (`core-techniques.md`, encouragement of affect), then return.
- **"Lonely" splits two ways.** Loneliness *after* a specific loss or change → grief or role transition (work the event). Loneliness as a *lifelong pattern* with no triggering event → interpersonal deficits. Ask which it is before routing — the difference is whether there's a "when did this start" event.
- **A dispute with someone who has died** is grief, not a role dispute — the unfinished conflict gets worked through reconstructing the relationship with the deceased (`grief.md`), not communication analysis with a living person.
