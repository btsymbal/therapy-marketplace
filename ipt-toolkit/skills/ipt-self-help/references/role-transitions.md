## What it is and when it applies

Role transitions is the broadest IPT problem area. It covers any major life change — wanted or unwanted, expected or sudden, positive or negative — that requires the person to take on a **new social role**. The low mood comes from missing or grieving the old role and struggling to adapt to the demands of the new one.

**Examples:** marriage, divorce, separation, a breakup, retirement, job loss, a new job or promotion, becoming a parent, empty nest, a diagnosis of chronic illness, immigration or moving, graduation, widowhood, the end of any significant role — or the *dissolution* of a relationship that was being disputed (`role-disputes.md`).

This is also where **non-death losses** go (lost job, lost health, lost relationship) — those are transitions, not grief (`grief.md` is only for the death of a person).

**Two special cases from the source, held lightly:**
- *Perinatal:* pregnancy and new parenthood are themselves role transitions — the primary frame for low mood there.
- *Chronic depression with no datable onset:* when no clear life event links to the mood (dysthymia-like), the "iatrogenic role transition" convention frames the entry into doing this work as the change to focus on. This is a tentative, single-source idea — a last resort, held loosely (`routing.md`).

## Goals

1. Mourn the old role.
2. Accept the loss of the old role.
3. Develop a realistic — not idealized — view of the new role.
4. Build the skills, social support, and self-esteem the new role needs.

## Technique catalog

Draws on `core-techniques.md`; one step per turn (`walkthrough-protocol.md`).

### 1. Drawing the link between the low mood and the change

**When best:** early — establishing the focus; the foundation for everything after.

**How:**
1. Draw the temporal link out loud: "Your low mood started / got worse around the time of [the change]. Does that fit?"
2. Explore what the old role gave them: "What did [the old role] give you? What were the best parts of it?"
3. Explore what's hard about the new one: "What feels hardest about being in this new place? What doesn't feel like you yet?"

### 2. Mourning the old role

**When best:** early-to-middle; especially when the change was *desired* or "should" be happy, so they feel they have no right to grieve it.

**How:**
1. Legitimize mourning even for a wanted change: "Even good transitions carry real losses. What do you miss about how things were?"
2. Draw out the full range — sadness, anger, fear, guilt, a sense of lost control.
3. Don't rush to silver linings — let the mourning be real first.
4. Validate the grief even when others don't get it ("but you *wanted* the divorce!").
5. Use encouragement of affect (`core-techniques.md` 5) across the session.

### 3. Balanced inventory of old and new role

**When best:** middle, once some mourning is underway; when they idealize the old role and catastrophize the new one. (Note: this addresses a depressive thinking pattern, but **through interpersonal reality, not cognitive restructuring** — you're not disputing the logic of their thoughts, you're helping them notice parts of their actual situation the low mood has hidden.)

**How:**
1. Review the positives **and** negatives of **both** roles.
2. Counter the pull to idealize the past: "You miss [the old job] — were there parts that were also frustrating or limiting?"
3. Counter the pull to catastrophize the present: "I'm hearing a lot that's hard about [the new role] — are there aspects that hold some potential too, even small ones?"
4. The aim is realism, not forced positivity — help them "see it as it is rather than as ideal".
5. Keep it interpersonal and observational; don't argue them out of a feeling.

### 4. Skill building for the new role

**When best:** middle-to-later; when the new role needs skills they don't yet have (parenting, a new workplace, socializing in a new context, self-care after a diagnosis).

**How:**
1. Identify what the role demands: "What does this new role ask of you that you haven't needed before?"
2. Notice which skills they already have (and may be discounting) and which are genuinely new.
3. Encourage acquisition — in IPT you don't *teach* the skill directly (unlike CBT); you help them identify and develop it, and point to resources.
4. Use decision analysis (`core-techniques.md` 6) to think through how to get the skill — a course, a group, a mentor, reading.
5. Credit each step as evidence of growing competence in the role.

### 5. Building social support in the new context

**When best:** middle-to-later; when the transition disrupted their network (divorce took the mutual friends; retirement took the workplace; immigration took the whole previous world).

**How:**
1. Map the support after the change: "Who's still in your life? Who's become less available? Who could be a new source of support?"
2. Explore what's blocked connection — practical barriers, emotional ones (grief, pride, shame), skill gaps.
3. Encourage gradual, concrete steps: "Is there one person you could reach out to this week?"
4. Review afterward: "What happened? How did it feel? What helped, what didn't?"

## How a transition session tends to go

**Earlier (mourning the old role):**
- Mood check + interval. "Since we last spoke, have there been moments you found yourself missing [the old role]? Tell me about one."
- Facilitate mourning; encourage affect; validate.
- Begin noticing both roles' positives and negatives, toward the balanced inventory.

**Later (building in the new role):**
- Mood check + interval. "How's [the new role] been going? What's felt better? What's still hard?"
- Skill-building; explore possible new social connections.
- Notice and credit growth — attribute the progress to the user's own capacities.

## If it doesn't work

- If they can't accept the loss after staying with it: return to mourning; and if the transition involved a death, consider whether grief (`grief.md`) is the truer frame.
- If they idealize the old role so hard that balance feels impossible: slow down, work the mourning more before introducing the balanced inventory — don't force it.
- If the new role is genuinely very hard with little upside (a major disability): focus on social support and realistic coping; don't minimize real losses or manufacture positives.
- If they lack the social skills to build support in the new context: consider whether interpersonal deficits is co-occurring (`interpersonal-deficits.md`), or use role play (`core-techniques.md` 7) to rehearse social approaches.
