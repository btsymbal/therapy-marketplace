## What it is and when it applies

In IPT, "grief" as a problem area means **specifically the death of a significant person**, and specifically mourning that has become complicated — delayed, inhibited, stalled, or excessive. Two boundaries matter:

- It is **not** for other kinds of loss (job, health, a relationship that ended, a role). Those are **role transitions** (`role-transitions.md`).
- It is **not** ordinary, healthy grief in the early period after a death. Normal mourning doesn't need an intervention.

You're in grief territory when the low mood's onset or persistence is tied to someone's death, and the mourning has stalled, never really began, or has run on and stayed disabling well past the acute period. The person may idealize the dead and be unable to hold ambivalent feelings; or avoid the subject entirely.

**A dispute with someone who has died** belongs here, not in `role-disputes.md` — the unfinished conflict is worked through reconstructing the relationship with the deceased, not communication analysis with a living person.

**Watch the boundary with prolonged grief disorder.** If the grief is years-long, intensely impairing, and unmoved by gentle work, that may be a disorder needing specialist treatment — offer support and signpost rather than pushing catharsis (`safety.md`).

## Goals

1. **Facilitate mourning** — help the person feel the loss and come to terms with it (the catharsis goal).
2. **Reconnect** — re-establish relationships and interests that the loss eclipsed (the social-reconnection goal).

Mourning usually comes first; reconnection grows as it eases. Many sessions sit in the first goal.

## Technique catalog

Each draws on the cross-cutting moves in `core-techniques.md`; deliver one step per turn (`walkthrough-protocol.md`).

### 1. Facilitating the mourning process (catharsis)

**When best:** mourning is blocked, delayed, or incomplete; the person avoids talking about the death or the person.

**How:**
1. Invite them to talk freely about the person: "Tell me about [them] — what were they like? How did you meet? What do you miss most?"
2. Walk the sequence around the death, at their pace: "Take me through what happened. Where were you? Who told you?"
3. Say plainly that feeling and speaking the pain doesn't mean weakness.
4. Follow their pace, but gently return to the topic if they consistently deflect.
5. Normalize grief's shape — non-linear, in waves, set off by unexpected reminders.

**If it stalls:** more targeted direct elicitation (`core-techniques.md` 2) — "What do you miss most about them today?" / "What's the hardest part of the day without them?" If grief is severe and prolonged and unmoved, consider that it may be prolonged grief disorder needing specialist care (`safety.md`).

### 2. Reconstructing the relationship with the deceased

**When best:** after mourning has begun; when the person idealizes the dead and can't mourn authentically; when complicated feelings (anger, guilt, relief) are blocking progress.

**How:**
1. Get the relationship in full — the good **and** the hard: "What was day-to-day life with them like? What was most rewarding? What was hardest?"
2. Gently open the complicated parts: "Were there times they frustrated you? Times you were angry, or let down?" Normalize that real relationships hold both.
3. Surface feelings that may never have been expressed — anger, guilt, relief, ambivalence.
4. Validate those feelings as normal: "It's very common to feel angry at someone we loved who died — and then guilty for feeling it."
5. A realistic, non-idealized picture is what makes authentic mourning possible; idealization keeps grief frozen.

**If it stalls:** encouragement of affect (`core-techniques.md` 5) aimed at the complicated emotions; clarification (3) when their account of the relationship contradicts itself.

### 3. Exploration of affect in grief

**When best:** throughout; especially when affect is constricted or only one narrow note of grief is showing.

**How:** use encouragement of affect (`core-techniques.md` 5) with deliberate attention to the full range — sadness, anger (at the person, the medical system, God, others), guilt ("what I should have said or done"), relief (after long illness or a conflicted bond), ambivalence, loneliness, longing. Validate each one explicitly. Saying it aloud, here, is the work.

### 4. Re-establishing social support and activities

**When best:** as mourning progresses (often later in the work); when they've withdrawn since the death; as the focus shifts from the past toward the future.

**How:**
1. Map the current support: "Who have you been in contact with since they died? Who have you been avoiding?"
2. Encourage reconnection with the people who matter; explore what's blocked it.
3. Invite others into remembering — connecting again doesn't require forgetting.
4. Encourage gradual re-engagement with things they used to enjoy, one at a time.
5. After an outing or contact, ask specifically: "What did you enjoy? What was hard? How did you feel afterward?"
6. As mourning eases, deliberately move the conversation from the deceased toward the relationships the person is now rebuilding.

**If it stalls:** decision analysis (`core-techniques.md` 6) to weigh options for reconnecting when they can't initiate; role play (7) to rehearse an anxious-making conversation (reaching out to a friend they've avoided).

## How a grief session tends to go

**Earlier (mourning-heavy):**
- Open with a mood check and what's happened since.
- Return to the loss: "Last time we were with what happened when they died. How have you been sitting with that this week?"
- Facilitate mourning, reconstruct the relationship, encourage affect.
- Notice what they avoid and approach it gently.

**Later (toward reconnection):**
- Open with mood + interval.
- Mark progress: "You said you were finally able to go through some of their things — what was that like?"
- Move toward reconnection: "As it's eased a little, what would you like your life to look like now?"
- Work the emerging activities and relationships.

## If it doesn't work

- If they refuse to discuss the death: don't force catharsis. Use clarification to understand the avoidance; hold the interpersonal focus gently across the conversation.
- If the grief is severe and prolonged (years-long, intense, impairing) and standard work isn't moving it: it may be prolonged grief disorder — signpost specialist treatment rather than pushing harder (`safety.md`).
- If another problem area is also keeping the mood down (the death also created a transition, or a family dispute about it): name it and consider whether to add a second focus (`routing.md`).
- If nothing shifts after staying with it: revisit the formulation — is this actually a long-standing interpersonal-deficits pattern that pre-dated the loss? (`interpersonal-deficits.md`).
