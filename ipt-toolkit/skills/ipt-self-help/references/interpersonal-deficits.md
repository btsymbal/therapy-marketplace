## What it is and when it applies

Interpersonal deficits is the problem area of **last resort**. Select it only when **none** of the other three fits — that is, when there is **no specific acute interpersonal event** (no death, no live dispute, no recent transition) linked to the low mood, **and** the person has a long-standing history of impoverished or conflicted relationships, social isolation, and difficulty making or keeping close connections. Some IPT manuals call it "interpersonal sensitivities" or "insecurities" to reduce stigma — that gentler framing is often better with the user.

**Who it fits:** people with few or no close relationships; who've never sustained a close bond; with a long history of conflicted relationships; who are severely isolated — and whose low mood has no identifiable acute interpersonal trigger.

**Why it's last resort:** it's the least well-defined of the four areas and the hardest to identify reliably, and the clinical consensus is that outcomes here are weaker than the other three. So don't reach for it just because someone is lonely — if the loneliness *followed* a loss or a change, that's grief or a role transition (work the event). Interpersonal deficits is specifically for loneliness as a **lifelong pattern with no triggering event**. (Note: the evidence on whether outcomes are genuinely worse here is actually mixed — one large study found no difference — so hold the "last resort" rule as a routing discipline, not a reason to be pessimistic with the user.)

This is also the one area where **past** relationships, not just current ones, are primary working material — because the recurring pattern is the target.

## Goals

1. Decrease social isolation.
2. Build new relationships, or improve existing ones.

## Technique catalog

Draws on `core-techniques.md`; one step per turn (`walkthrough-protocol.md`).

### 1. Review of past significant relationships

**When best:** early — unique to this area; the only problem area where historical relationships get extended focus, because the recurring pattern across them is what you're after.

**How:**
1. Extend the interpersonal inventory deep into the past: "You've said you haven't felt close to many people over the years. Let's look back — were there relationships that mattered more, even if they didn't last?"
2. Explore each in detail: positives and negatives, how communication went, what ended or limited it.
3. Actively hunt for recurring themes: "Looking across these — do you notice anything similar? Any pattern in how they tend to go?"
4. Reflect the pattern back without judgment: "It sounds like in several of these you ended up feeling [overlooked / overwhelmed / misunderstood] — is that right?"
5. The goal is to find whether a similar pattern recurs across relationships — that pattern becomes the focus of the work.

### 2. Exploration of affect in relationships

**When best:** throughout; when avoidance of relationships is driven by anxiety, fear of rejection, shame, or past hurt.

**How:**
1. Explore the fears that block connection: "What do you imagine would happen if you got closer to someone?" "What feels risky about reaching out?"
2. Validate the fears as understandable given their history: "Given what happened with [past relationship], it makes sense you'd be cautious."
3. Help them see that avoidance, while protective, also costs them — explore the cost compassionately.
4. Use encouragement of affect (`core-techniques.md` 5) with particular care around shame and anticipatory anxiety.

### 3. Using the relationship with you as a model

**When best:** specific to this area — the **only** IPT area where the working relationship itself becomes material — when a problematic interpersonal process shows up directly in the session (excessive dependency, hostility, repeated reassurance-seeking, withdrawing when a topic gets close).

**CRITICAL DISTINCTION:** this is **not** transference interpretation. The relationship with you is examined as a **real, present-day relationship**, not as a re-enactment of the past and not as symbolic material. You notice and name what's happening here-and-now; you do **not** interpret what it "means" about their childhood.

**How:**
1. When a pattern shows up in session, notice it and, when it's useful, name it plainly: "I notice when we get near [topic], you tend to [go quiet / change the subject]. Have you noticed that?"
2. Invite them to look at the working relationship itself: "You've said you feel like no one really gets you. What's it like being here with me — anything that's felt helpful, or not?"
3. Use their answer to spot the pattern: "You said you half-expect me to judge you. I wonder if that shows up in other relationships too?"
4. Aim to modify the pattern as it appears — the working relationship can be a template for others.
5. Stay in the present and on real interpersonal skills; do **not** interpret hidden meaning.

### 4. Social skills coaching and graduated engagement

**When best:** middle-to-later; when there's a concrete deficit in starting or keeping social contact; after the pattern has been identified.

**How:**
1. Suggest specific, low-stakes settings: "Where might you be around other people? A class, an event, an old acquaintance you've meant to contact?"
2. Encourage small steps over big leaps: "What's one small step toward being around people this week?"
3. Use role play (`core-techniques.md` 7) to rehearse specific approaches — starting a conversation, responding to an overture, reaching out to an old contact.
4. After each attempt, review: "What happened? What did you feel? What went well? What was hard? What did you enjoy, even a little?"
5. Actively reinforce the steps: "Reaching out when you're not sure you'll hear back takes real courage — that's worth naming."

### 5. Reinforcing positive interpersonal steps

**When best:** throughout, every session. At each step, explicitly acknowledge each move toward connection, however small — not generic praise, but interpersonally specific: "You reached out to [person] — that's the kind of thing that rebuilds a network over time." Attribute progress to the user's own capacity and agency.

## How a deficits session tends to go

**Earlier:**
- Mood check + interval.
- Continue the review of past relationships: "Last time you told me about [relationship] — I'd like to understand more about what happened there."
- Identify recurring patterns; reflect them back.
- Explore the affect around closeness and connection.

**Later:**
- Mood check + interval. "How did [the step you planned] go?"
- Troubleshoot what came up; reinforce what worked.
- If something surfaced in the working relationship itself, work it as a here-and-now sample.
- Plan the next small step of graduated engagement.

## If it doesn't work

- If nothing moves after staying with it: reassess whether another area actually fits better — a transition or a long-standing dispute that the inventory missed (`routing.md`).
- If response is minimal and the deficits look severe or personality-rooted: this is at the edge of what self-help can do — signpost a clinician and a structured approach (`safety.md`).
- If the working relationship itself becomes the obstacle (e.g. severe dependency that won't shift): this needs a clinician; be honest that a single companion session can't carry it.
