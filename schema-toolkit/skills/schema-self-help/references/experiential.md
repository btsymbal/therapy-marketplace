These are the emotional heart of Schema Therapy — they shift the *felt meaning* of a schema in a way logic alone can't. They are also the most activating techniques in the skill, normally delivered by a trained clinician. This skill **will walk the user through them** when routed here, but only inside the scaffolding below and in `safety.md`, and you say plainly — without refusing — that a schema therapist deepens this work and is worth seeking for heavy or trauma-linked material. Deliver every step per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: one beat per turn, end on a question, wait. Slower than any other technique.

## Before any experiential work

Non-negotiable, every time (full version in `safety.md`):

1. **Check contraindications.** Do not run these if acute suicide risk, active self-harm, substance dependence needing detox, untreated psychosis/mania, recent interpersonal violence, or severe un-groundable dissociation is present. Offer psychoeducation, flashcards, grounding, and signpost a clinician instead.
2. **Stabilize first.** Confirm they're grounded, not currently flooded or dissociating. If they are, ground (5-4-3-2-1, feet on the floor, name the room, paced breathing) and only proceed once they can both feel and think.
3. **Set the anchor.** Agree a stop word or a calm/safe image; tell them they can open their eyes and stop anytime; keep the present available ("you're here, safe, we're just visiting").
4. **Watch continuously** for dissociation/flooding (glassy detachment, "I'm not here", escalating panic). If it appears: stop, ground, stay present, don't re-enter the scene this session.
5. **Debrief is mandatory.** Always leave time to return to the present, talk it through, and re-rate. Never end inside an activated scene.

## 1 · Imagery rescripting

**Reach for it when:** a Vulnerable Child mode is at the root — abandonment, shame/defectiveness, emotional deprivation, mistrust — and cognition isn't moving the feeling. The most powerful experiential technique: it re-enters a charged early memory and changes what happens in it, so the old emotional meaning (shame, fear, "I'm alone") is overwritten with safety, protection, and care.

**Walk it through (one beat per turn; stay in present tense, sensory, slow):**
1. **Bridge from the present feeling.** With a current activation live, ask: "Close your eyes, stay with that feeling in your body — let it take you back. How old are you? Where are you?" (Let the affect choose the memory; don't pick one for them.)
2. **Enter the scene.** Ask for it in present tense, sensory: "What do you see, hear, who's there? Say it as if it's happening now."
3. **Amplify the felt reality** gently — the faces, the sounds, the temperature — enough to make it alive, not enough to flood. Check the body each turn.
4. **Find the child's need.** Ask: "What does this younger you need right now — protection? Someone to step in? To be comforted? To be taken out of here?"
5. **Bring in a protective figure.** First you/a safe other enters the scene and does what's needed — intervenes, confronts the person causing harm, protects and reassures the child. Ask: "Who could come in and stop this / hold you? Let them in — what do they do, what do they say?"
6. **Hand it to their Healthy Adult.** Invite the user's own adult self into the scene to take over caring for the child-self. Ask: "Now you, as you are now, step in. What do you do for that little one? What do they most need to hear from you?"
7. **Stabilize inside the image** — let a sense of safety and connection settle. Ask: "What's happening for the child now? Stay there a moment."
8. **Translate to the present.** Ask: "Bring that back to the situation that set this off today — what's different now?"
9. **Debrief fully** (mandatory): open eyes, come back to the room, re-rate the feeling and the schema belief, talk through what came up.

**If it stalls:** can't enter the image / it's abstract → start with a letter (`letter-writing.md`) or chair work (2) to access the material, then return. A Detached Protector blocks entry → name and validate the Protector first (`healthy-adult.md`). Can't bring in their own Healthy Adult → stay with you/a safe figure providing care this time; the user's adult can take over in a later round. Dissociation → stop, ground, don't re-enter (`safety.md`). Don't force a happy ending — an authentic partial shift beats a manufactured rescue.

## 2 · Chair work / mode dialogues

**Reach for it when:** modes are at war inside the user — a loud Punitive/Demanding Parent attacking a Vulnerable Child, a Detached Protector dominating, a Compliant Surrenderer that can't find its own voice. Chair work externalizes the modes into separate seats so they can be heard, challenged, and answered — with the Healthy Adult learning to respond.

**Walk it through (one move per turn; physical chairs help, but it works with imagined seats or "speaking as" each part):**
1. **Assign the chairs.** Name which modes are in the room and give each a seat. Ask: "Let's give the [critic] one chair and the [hurt part] another — which feels more present right now?"
2. **Voice the first mode fully.** Have them sit in (or speak as) one mode and say it in the first person, with its real charge. Ask: "Be the critic for a moment — say exactly what it says to you, out loud, 'you…'."
3. **Switch seats and respond** from the other mode. Ask: "Now move to the hurt part's chair. Hearing all that — what does it feel, what does it want to say back?"
4. **Bring in the Healthy Adult.** When a destructive exchange is clear, model and then coach a Healthy-Adult response that protects the Child and stands up to the critic. Ask: "If a wise, fair adult who was fully on your side heard the critic talk to you like that, what would they say to it?"
5. **Keep switching** to deepen it, with the Healthy Adult increasingly answering the parent and comforting the child.
6. **For a punitive critic specifically:** have the critic deliver one or two lines; move to the Vulnerable-Child chair and feel the impact; then build a two-sided card — critic's line on one side, Healthy-Adult counter on the other (links to `cognitive.md`).
7. **Hand authority to the Healthy Adult** and reduce your prompting as they generate their own responses.
8. **Debrief fully** (mandatory): come out of the parts, back to the room, re-rate, note what the Healthy Adult said that landed.

Keep first attempts short (~15-20 min of dialogue) and leave ample debrief time.

**If it stalls:** too flooded to switch chairs → drop the switching, use a single empty-chair "say it to them" without role-reversal. Detached Protector dominates so there's no contact → do imagery (1) or name/validate the Protector first (`healthy-adult.md`). Can't access a Healthy-Adult voice → ask "what would you say to a friend / a child this happened to?" and route the answer back. Critic gets louder and self-attack spikes toward self-harm → that's a `safety.md` flag, stop and follow it.

## 3 · Historical role play

**Reach for it when:** a present-day interpersonal pattern clearly traces to a specific past relationship/scene, and the user has enough emotional access (usually after some imagery work) to re-enter it behaviorally and re-understand it. Distinct from imagery in being enacted and interpersonal rather than purely internal.

**Walk it through (four phases, slowly, one beat per turn):**
1. **Phase 1 — Schema activation.** Re-enact the original scene roughly as it happened, with you voicing the other person from the user's account. Ask: "Set the scene — who said what? Let's replay it; you be you, I'll be them."
2. **Phase 2 — Meaning reattribution.** Reverse roles: the user plays the other person to explore that person's state/motivation. Ask: "Now be them. What was going on for them — fear, their own limits, their own schema?" (This is for understanding, not excusing harm.)
3. **Phase 3 — Guided rescripting.** Pause; identify the modes that were active then; model a Healthy-Adult response to the situation. Ask: "Knowing what you know now, what did that younger you actually need to say or have happen?"
4. **Phase 4 — Client-enacted rescripting.** Replay with the user responding from their own Healthy Adult — saying or doing what they couldn't then.
5. **Debrief fully** (mandatory): out of role, re-rate, and connect it to the present-day version of the pattern.

**If it stalls:** gets flooded → return to imagery (1) with its protective-figure step first. Perspective-taking in Phase 2 is too hard → do two-chair (2) for each perspective before enacting. It surfaces severe trauma → stabilize and signpost a clinician (`safety.md`); don't push enactment of major trauma in self-help.
