Letter writing is one of the three main emotion-focused techniques (alongside imagery and chair work). It's more contained than live imagery — the page holds the feeling — which makes it a good on-ramp when imagery feels too abstract, and a good consolidation after experiential work. Types A and C reach into raw early material and carry the experiential scaffolding in `safety.md` (stabilize, watch for flooding, debrief); Types B and D are gentler. Deliver per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: build the letter with them in stages, don't have them write the whole thing in one go while you wait silently.

## The four types

- **Type A — Vulnerable Child → caregiver:** from the younger self to the person who caused harm; what was hard, what was needed. **Never sent.**
- **Type B — Coping-mode letter:** written *from* a coping mode (the Detached Protector, the Compliant Surrenderer) to surface its function and voice. A self-awareness tool.
- **Type C — Angry Child letter:** the anger the child couldn't express, to the person who caused harm; often read aloud from an "angry chair". **Never sent.**
- **Type D — Healthy Adult letter:** written after experiential work to consolidate a rescripting or grow self-compassion — to the younger self, or to oneself now.

## The never-send rule

For **Type A and Type C**, establish up front, before a word is written, that the letter is **never to be sent**. It is for processing the emotion, not for delivery to the real person. Say this explicitly and confirm they're clear. Sending these letters is not the goal and can cause real-world harm; the work is internal. (Type D may sometimes be shared, e.g. a compassionate letter to oneself kept as a flashcard, but A and C are categorically not-sent.)

## 1 · Type A — Vulnerable Child to caregiver

**Reach for it when:** there's grief and unmet need toward a caregiver — emotional deprivation, abandonment, not being protected — and the user needs to give the younger self a voice. Gentler entry to the Vulnerable Child than live imagery.

**Walk it through (build in stages, one prompt per turn):**
1. Set it up: confirm the **never-send** rule, and that they'll write *as the younger self* to the caregiver.
2. Prompt one: "What was hard for you back then — tell them, in the child's voice." (Let them write a few lines; respond to what they wrote, don't rush on.)
3. Prompt two: "What did you need from them that you didn't get?"
4. Prompt three: "What do you wish they had done differently?"
5. They can start in session and finish at home. Next time, have them **read it aloud** to you (or out loud to themselves) — reading is where the affect lands.
6. Optional bridge to imagery: as they read, the younger self can be met with Healthy-Adult/limited-reparenting care (`experiential.md` 1, `healthy-adult.md`).
7. Debrief and re-rate.

**If it stalls:** writing feels too structured/cold → switch to a voice-recorded message instead. Too much emotional distance to reach the child → do a short imagery bridge first (`experiential.md`), then write. Flooding → stop, ground, debrief (`safety.md`).

## 2 · Type B — Coping-mode letter

**Reach for it when:** a coping mode runs the show and the user can't see it clearly (the wall that goes up, the part that always says yes). Writing *as* the mode externalizes it and reveals its protective logic — useful before trying to get past it.

**Walk it through:**
1. Name the coping mode to write from (`core-model.md`): Detached Protector, Compliant Surrenderer, Self-Soother, Overcompensator.
2. Prompt: "Write as that part — 'My job is to…'. What are you protecting them from? How do you do it?"
3. Prompt: "What are you afraid would happen if you stepped aside?"
4. Read it back; reflect what the mode is guarding (almost always a Vulnerable Child).
5. Bridge: thank the mode for its job, and ask what the Healthy Adult would want it to know (`healthy-adult.md`).

**If it stalls:** they can't separate from the mode enough to write as it → do mode identification first (`assessment.md` 2). It just intellectualizes → ask what the mode *feels*, not just what it does.

## 3 · Type C — Angry Child letter

**Reach for it when:** there's legitimate anger at someone who caused harm that was never safe to express — and surrender/numbing has kept it buried. Gives the Angry Child a sanctioned voice. Carries the full experiential scaffolding.

**Walk it through:**
1. Confirm the **never-send** rule and that this is the *child's* raw anger — no editing for fairness, no caretaking the other person.
2. Prompt: "Tell them what they did and how it made you feel — no holding back, this is just for you."
3. Prompt: "Tell them what they had no right to do."
4. Have them **read it aloud**, ideally from an "angry chair" (`experiential.md` 2), to externalize and release it.
5. Afterward, bring the Healthy Adult in to stand with the child (not to scold the anger). Debrief and re-rate.

**If it stalls:** anger won't come (numbing/Detached Protector, or a Punitive Parent forbidding anger) → name that block first; the inability *is* the material. Anger tips into self-attack or revenge planning → redirect to the Healthy Adult and check `safety.md` (anger toward others is a flag if it's a real plan).

## 4 · Type D — Healthy Adult letter

**Reach for it when:** consolidating after experiential or cognitive work, or building self-compassion directly. The most self-help-safe letter — and a good standalone exercise.

**Walk it through:**
1. Choose the recipient: the younger self, or oneself in a recent hard moment.
2. Prompt: "Write to that part of you the way a wise, fully-on-your-side adult would — what does it most need to hear?"
3. Prompt: "What do you understand now that it couldn't, back then?"
4. Prompt: "What do you want it to know about how you'll have its back going forward?"
5. Read it aloud; notice the felt response. Consider keeping it (or a line from it) as a flashcard (`cognitive.md`).

**If it stalls:** can't find the compassionate voice → "what would you write to a child / a friend this happened to?" and turn it back on themselves. It comes out as another demand ("you should be stronger") → that's a Demanding Parent wearing the costume; name it and rewrite from genuine care (`cognitive.md` 3).
