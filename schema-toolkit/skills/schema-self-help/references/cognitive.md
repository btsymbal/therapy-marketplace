These three are the self-help-safe cognitive and between-session layer — no contraindications, usable from early on, and the bridge that carries in-session shifts into daily life. Important sequencing (`core-model.md`): schema-level restructuring (3) lands *after* coping modes are softened and the emotional charge is worked experientially — don't lead with logic on a hot schema while a Detached Protector is fully up. Flashcards (1) and the diary (2) can run from the first session. Deliver each per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: one step per turn, end on a question, wait.

---

## 1 · Schema flashcards

**Reach for it when:** the user needs something to hold onto *between* sessions and in the moment a schema fires — especially abandonment spikes, shame flares, or craving. A flashcard is a short, pre-written statement that reconnects them to the Healthy-Adult view when they're too activated to generate it. It doubles as a transitional anchor ("a link to the calmer version of me").

**Walk it through (build it with them, one part at a time):**
1. Anchor it to a real activation. Ask: "Think of the last time this schema grabbed you — what were you feeling and thinking?" (Get it specific; the card is for *that* moment.)
2. **Acknowledge the feeling** (part a). Draft together: "Right now I feel ___ and I'm believing ___."
3. **Name the schema and its origin** (part b). "I recognize this is my ___ schema, which I learned when ___. It's an old feeling, not the present fact."
4. **Reality-test it** (part c). Ask: "What's the evidence this old belief isn't the truth of *this* situation?" Put the strongest one or two lines on the card.
5. **Healthy alternative + action** (part d). Ask: "So what's the truer thing to tell yourself, and one thing to do instead?" Write it in their words.
6. Decide the carry. Ask: "Where will you keep this so it's there when you need it — phone note, wallet, lock screen?" For abandonment schemas, a physical transitional object alongside it can help.
7. Plan to review it next session and refine the wording.

**If it stalls:** they can't produce a counter in the heat → that's expected, which is exactly why it's pre-written when calm. Card doesn't get read during distress → shrink it to one line, or pair it with a physical cue; explore (gently) what blocks reading it (often a Detached Protector). Card feels hollow → it's probably ahead of the emotional work; do an experiential piece first (`experiential.md`), then the card consolidates it.

## 2 · Schema diary / monitoring form

**Reach for it when:** the user is comfortable enough with schema/mode language to track patterns in daily life (usually after some identification work). It captures a triggering episode in nine elements so that, across entries, the recurring schemas, triggers, and overreactions become visible. More structured than a flashcard; it's the self-monitoring backbone.

**Walk it through (the nine elements; in session do it on one real episode, one element per turn, then hand it over as a between-session tool):**
1. **Trigger** — "What happened? Just the facts."
2. **Emotions** — "What did you feel, and how strong (0-100)?"
3. **Thoughts** — "What went through your mind?"
4. **Behaviors** — "What did you actually do?"
5. **Schema(s)** — "Which schema(s) does this fit?" (`core-model.md`)
6. **Healthy view** — "What would your Healthy Adult say about it?"
7. **Realistic concern** — "What part of this is a *genuine* problem worth attention?"
8. **Overreaction** — "What part of your response went beyond that realistic concern — the schema's amplification?"
9. **Healthy behavior** — "What would a Healthy-Adult response have looked like?"

The 7/8 split (realistic concern vs. overreaction) is the heart of it: it separates the kernel of truth from the schema's distortion without invalidating either.

**If it stalls:** nine elements feel like too much → drop to flashcards (1) first, or just track trigger → schema → overreaction. Monitoring itself gets avoided → explore what it activates (often shame or a Demanding Parent saying "you should be over this"); name that mode rather than pushing the form. They keep logging the same loop with no shift → that's the signal to move to experiential (`experiential.md`) or restructuring (3).

## 3 · Schema-level cognitive restructuring

**Reach for it when:** a schema belief is driving the distress and the user is regulated enough to examine it — *and* the coping modes aren't fully blocking contact. Differs from ordinary thought-challenging: it targets the **core schema** ("I'm unlovable", "I have to be perfect or I'm worthless"), not a surface automatic thought, and it's anchored in the felt experience from the experiential work, not done as a dry debate.

**Walk it through:**
1. Pin the **core schema belief**, not the surface thought. Ask: "Under that thought, what's the deeper thing it says about you?" Rate belief 0-100%.
2. **Pros and cons of holding the schema.** Ask: "What has believing this protected you from or done for you? And what has it cost you?" (Honors why it formed before challenging it.)
3. **Test its validity, Socratically.** Ask in turn: "What genuinely supports it? What doesn't fit — including things you'd normally wave away? Was there ever a time it wasn't true?"
4. **Schema dialogue.** Have them voice both sides alternately: "Say the schema's line out loud… now answer it from the wiser part." (One line each, back and forth — a written or spoken two-voice exchange.)
5. **For a Demanding/Critical Parent — rewrite the rules.** (a) Write the critic's actual "commandments" verbatim ("never rest", "never need anyone"). (b) Draft contrasting **Healthy-Adult guidelines** emphasizing balance and self-compassion. (c) Decide where to display them. (d) Ask: "How does the new version feel to read?"
6. **Anchor it in the felt work and re-rate.** Tie the new belief back to a moment from imagery/chair work where it felt true, not just sounded true. Re-rate belief and feeling.

**If it stalls:** belief won't move with logic alone → that's the signal cognition is ahead of emotion; go to imagery rescripting or chair work (`experiential.md`) to shift the emotional meaning first, then return. "I know it's not true but I still feel it" → same pivot to experiential. A Punitive/Demanding Parent keeps re-asserting → externalize it in chair work (`experiential.md`) rather than out-arguing it in your head. The belief might be partly *true* (a real, current problem) → don't gaslight it; separate the realistic kernel (diary element 7) and problem-solve that part.
