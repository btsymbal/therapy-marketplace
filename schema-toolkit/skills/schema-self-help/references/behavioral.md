This is where schema change becomes schema *action* — replacing the schema-driven move (avoid, comply, overcompensate) with a Healthy-Adult one in real life, and gathering evidence that the feared outcome doesn't follow. It's Phase-3 work and a critical principle of the model applies (`core-model.md`): behavioral pattern-breaking lands *after* the coping modes have been softened through experiential work — push behavior change too early, while a Detached Protector or Punitive Parent is fully up, and it either won't happen or becomes one more thing to fail at. Deliver per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: one step per turn, end on a question, wait.

## 1 · Behavioral pattern-breaking

**Reach for it when:** the user understands the schema and the mode, the charge is somewhat softened, and the work now is to *do the thing the schema has always stopped them doing* — say no, ask for the need, stay instead of fleeing, let someone close, leave the bad situation. The behavior is chosen to fit the Healthy Adult and to violate the schema's prediction.

**Walk it through:**
1. **Label the schema and the specific trigger** tied to the behavior. Ask: "Which schema is driving this, and what situation sets the old behavior off?" (e.g. Subjugation → "when someone asks a favour, I always say yes".)
2. **Name the old coping move and the new Healthy-Adult alternative.** Ask: "What do you normally do — and what would the Healthy-Adult version do instead?" Make the new behavior concrete and small enough to actually attempt.
3. **Surface the schema's prediction.** Ask: "If you did the new thing, what does the schema say will happen?" (e.g. "they'll be angry and leave".) This is the bet you're testing.
4. **Rate expected distress 0-10** before the task. Ask: "How hard does that feel to do, 0-10?"
5. **Do it** — in session if possible (make the call, draft the message, practice the sentence), otherwise as a clearly-defined homework task.
6. **Rate actual distress 0-10** after, and capture what *actually* happened. Ask: "How did it really go — and how distressing was it, 0-10?"
7. **Compare expected vs. actual** and draw the conclusion against the schema. Ask: "The schema predicted ___ — what actually happened, and what does that say about the old rule?"
8. **Use Healthy-Adult self-dialogue** through it (before/during/after), and bring the result back to review — including into the `cognitive.md` restructuring and the `schema-log` Follow-up.

**If it stalls:** they avoid the task → don't push the behavior; the avoidance points back to an active coping mode or too-high charge — return to experiential work (`experiential.md`) or empathic confrontation of the avoidance (`healthy-adult.md`) first, then retry smaller. Feared outcome partly *did* happen → use it as real data (often "it was survivable, and I coped"), and problem-solve the realistic part rather than treating it as proof of the schema. A Demanding Parent turns the task into a perfectionistic test → shrink it, and reframe success as *attempting*, not flawless execution.

## Embedded behavioral techniques

Schema Therapy folds in standard behavioral methods once the experiential ground is laid. Reach for the one that fits the schema, and run it the same way — small, predicted, tested, reviewed:

- **Assertiveness training** — for Subjugation, Self-Sacrifice, Approval-Seeking, the Compliant Surrenderer. Practice naming a need / saying no, starting low-stakes.
- **Graded steps / exposure** — for Vulnerability to Harm, avoidance-driven schemas, the Detached Protector's withdrawal. Build a small-to-bigger ladder of approach behaviors.
- **Relaxation / arousal down-regulation** — as a coping skill for the anxious/vulnerable schemas (and see `safety.md` on grounding when flooded).
- **Anger management / expression** — for the Angry Child and Overcompensator: channel the legitimate signal under the anger into an assertive Healthy-Adult action rather than an outburst or a freeze.

For each, the schema-therapy difference is that the behavior is explicitly framed as the **Healthy Adult breaking the schema's rule** and the result is fed back to weaken the schema — not just symptom reduction in isolation. Record any homework task's result in the session's `schema-log` Follow-up (`logging.md`).
