## How to use this file

These tools cut across all six conversational maps (`core-maps.md`) and don't belong to any single one. Reach for them throughout: the seven question types are a versatile toolkit for any re-authoring conversation; **Absent-But-Implicit** is the most direct route to a counter-story when no unique outcomes will surface; **double listening** is the underlying stance that makes both work. Deliver every one per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one question per turn, decentered, follow their words, wait.**

## The seven narrative question types (Roth & Epston)

Sallyann Roth and David Epston described seven cross-cutting question types used across all the maps. Think of them as a sequence you can move through to take a single exception and grow it into a preferred identity with a future — or as a grab-bag to reach into when a conversation needs a particular kind of opening.

| # | Type | What it does | Example questions |
|---|------|--------------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Unique Outcome** | Find an exception to the dominant story | *"Have there been times you were able to rebel against it?"* / *"Did you give busyness the slip to come here today?"* |
| 2 | **Unique Account** | Make meaning of the exception with a grammar of agency | *"How were you able to defy its prescriptions?"* / *"How did you protest against its pushing you around?"* |
| 3 | **Unique Redescription** | Develop a new self-understanding from the account | *"What does this tell you about yourself that you hadn't known?"* / *"Who might confirm this newly developing picture of you?"* |
| 4 | **Unique Possibility** | Speculate on futures the new self-description opens | *"Where do you think you'll go next, now you've started this?"* / *"Is this a direction you see yourself taking?"* |
| 5 | **Experience of Experience** | See themselves through a significant other's eyes; historicize | *"What do you think I'm appreciating about you as I hear how you've been leaving busyness behind?"* / *"What would your grandmother say she saw in you here?"* |
| 6 | **Preference** | Ensure *their* values, not yours, guide the work | *"Is this your preference or not?"* / *"Do you see this as a good or a bad thing for you?"* |
| 7 | **Consulting Your Consultants** | Elevate them from patient to expert consultant on their own life | *"Given everything you've come through, what have you learned that you'd want others facing this to know?"* |

Notes on use:
- **Preference questions (6) are a safety rail** — sprinkle them in to keep the work owned by the client, never assume an outcome is "good" until they've said so. This is the same move as Stage 3 of the Statement of Position Map (`core-maps.md` Map 1).
- **Consulting-your-consultants (7)** is especially powerful in substance use, chronic illness, and eating disorders, where lived experience becomes authority (`substance-use.md`, `collective-practices.md` on archives of resistance).
- These pair naturally with the **two landscapes** (`walkthrough-protocol.md`): 1-2 live mostly in the Landscape of Action, 3-7 in the Landscape of Identity.

## The Absent-But-Implicit technique

**Reach for it when:** the person expresses distress, pain, or suffering — and especially when **no unique outcomes will surface** by any other route. This is the most direct path to a counter-story when every other avenue feels exhausted. Powerful in trauma, grief, depression, and profound hopelessness (`trauma.md`, `grief.md`, `depression.md`).

**The rationale (Derrida, via Carey, Walther & Russell 2009):** we make sense of what something *is* only by contrast with what it is *not*. You can only experience "isolation" if you already hold an idea of "connection"; only feel "despair" if you carry some knowledge of "hope". What is **absent but implicit** in an expression of pain reveals what the person most deeply values. The technique "offers a way to identify hope even when there is a sense of overwhelming despair".

**Walk it through:**
1. Listen to the distress and ask yourself: *what contrast does this imply? What value, connection, or hope does this pain reveal the person already holds?* (This is double listening, below.)
2. Reflect that implied value back as a question, tentatively: *"It sounds like this loss hurts so much because [connection / fairness / being a good parent] matters deeply to you — would that be right?"*
3. Inquire into its history: *"Do you remember when that first became important to you?"*
4. Trace a history of action, knowledge, skills, and values around it — past moments of protest, resistance, or preference, scaffolded from concrete to abstract (`core-maps.md` Map 6).
5. Connect them to figures (real or fictional, past or present) who shared or supported that value (`core-maps.md` Map 3).
6. Link the story across time, from past stands to the present.
7. Project forward: *"What step could you take that's consistent with what you've always valued here?"*

**Scaffolding questions (verbatim from Carey et al. 2009):** *"Who would have known about this and would have appreciated what you were standing for back then?"* / *"Can you think of others who share your view about what matters here?"* / *"What's the link between the protests you made as a child and what you've been doing more recently?"*

**If it stalls:** move to **Re-Membering** (`core-maps.md` Map 3) to find figures whose values align with the implied commitment; once the implied value gives a direction, go back to **Unique Outcomes** (Map 5) and search there; use **Scaffolding** (Map 6) to climb from the implicit value to future-oriented possibility questions.

## Double listening

Not a separate technique so much as the stance underneath all of the above — and it's worth naming on its own because it's easy to lose. Every account of a problem carries two stories at once:

- the **explicit** story: the pain, the problem, the distress, the defeat;
- the **implicit** story: what that pain reveals the person values, hopes for, or has been standing for.

Listen on both tracks simultaneously. Don't only track the problem and then "look for positives" afterward — hear the counter-story *in* the problem-story as it's told. When someone says "I feel like I've failed everyone", the explicit story is failure; the implicit story is that their responsibility to others matters enormously to them. Your next question comes from the implicit track: *"It sounds like being there for people is something you hold yourself to — where did that commitment come from?"*

Double listening is what keeps externalizing from becoming bleak-proof negativity, what surfaces unique outcomes that the person had dismissed, and what powers Absent-But-Implicit. Keep it running the whole session.
