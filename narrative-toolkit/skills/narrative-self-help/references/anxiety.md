## How to use this file

The anxiety layer: worry, dread, "what-if" spirals, phobic fear, and the problem-saturated story "I'm an anxious person, that's just me". NT externalizes anxiety as an entity with a name, a history, and social-cultural roots — freeing the person from the identity position "I *am* anxious". Assumes the six maps in `core-maps.md`. Deliver per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one question per turn, decentered, wait.** If distress is acute (mid-spiral, can't think), stabilize first (`safety.md`) before any re-authoring.

## 1 · Externalizing "the Fear" / "the Worry" / "the What-If Spiral"

**Reach for it when:** the start of nearly all anxiety work. Naming the anxiety as separate is itself relieving.
**Walk it through** (`core-maps.md` Map 1):
1. Negotiate a name in their language — "the Worry", "the What-If Spiral", "the Constant Hum", a personified "the Worry Monster" (esp. with younger people, `children-adolescents.md`). Ask: *"If the worry were a character that visits, what would you call it?"*
2. Map its effects: *"What does the Spiral get you to avoid? What futures does it insist on showing you? What % of your choices does it run?"*
3. Map counter-influence: *"When has it told you to bail and you stayed anyway?"*
4. Note the cultural fuel: *"What messages about [danger / failure / not coping] feed it?"*

**If it stalls:** can't name it → use imagery or have them describe what it "sounds like"; feels total → relative-influence percentages to show it varies ("never 100%").

## 2 · The Statement of Position Map for anxiety

**Reach for it when:** you want the full four-stage structure (`core-maps.md` Map 1) — it's the workhorse for anxiety. The pivotal stages are 3 and 4: the person taking a position on the Fear's effects and justifying it from their values. Don't tell them the Fear is bad; let them decide.
**Walk it through:** Stage 1 name → Stage 2 effects + counter-influence → **Stage 3** *"Is the way the Fear narrows your life okay with you, or not?"* → **Stage 4** *"What does that answer say about the life you're trying to live?"* The position they take *against* the Fear, grounded in their own values, is the lever — it converts dread into a stand.

**If it stalls:** they're ambivalent (the Fear "keeps me safe") → honor that honestly, externalize the *cost-benefit* ("what does it charge you for that safety?"); don't argue them out of it.

## 3 · Unique outcomes and re-authoring

**Reach for it when:** any moment the Fear didn't get the last word — the entry to re-authoring (`core-maps.md` Maps 5 → 2).
**Walk it through:**
1. Find one: *"Was there a time the Worry tried to stop you and you went ahead anyway?"* / *"A moment you handled uncertainty better than it predicted?"*
2. Elaborate across both landscapes; draw out the quality they showed.
3. Link exceptions into a history; let them title the alternative story ("the person who walks toward things").

**If it stalls:** none surface → widen the timeframe, ask about partial resistance, or use **Absent-But-Implicit** (`cross-cutting-tools.md`) — the dread implies something they care about protecting; find that.

## School / test anxiety

A group NT protocol (psychoeducation on NT premises + externalizing "test anxiety" + the Statement of Position Map) reduced test anxiety in one quasi-experimental study (Peker et al. 2026, N=22, Turkish 8th-graders — situational, non-clinical). For a student user: externalize "the Test Panic", map what it does the night before and in the exam room, find moments it didn't run the show, and build the "I can sit with nerves and still perform" story. The **two-circles method** (Ayashiro 2021) externalizes a teacher-student conflict using two concentric circles — useful framing if the anxiety is relational.

## Honest evidence

Preliminary at best. **No adult RCT for any DSM anxiety disorder** using White/Epston NT. Support is a single quasi-experimental test-anxiety study (N=22) — plus an important **negative finding**: NT alone did *not* reduce preschool separation anxiety in one 2023 study (play therapy did). A conceptual paper proposes externalizing metaphors for anxiety in high-functioning autism (McGuinty et al. 2011). Offer the work as a meaningful reframe, not a proven treatment; keep `safety.md` live.
