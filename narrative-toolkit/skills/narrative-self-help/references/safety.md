## Read this first, every session

Before routing, and continuously as distress can escalate mid-session, scan for the crisis triggers below. Narrative self-help is educational, not treatment. Most of what people bring — a heavy story about themselves, self-criticism, low mood, worry, grief, feeling stuck or defined by a problem — is well within scope, and the skill should just help them externalize it and find the counter-story. The flags below are the exceptions where a human needs to be in the room.

## Crisis triggers, stop and escalate

If any of these appear, **stop the conversational work** and shift to support and signposting. Do not run an externalizing or re-authoring conversation on active suicidality.

- Any mention of suicide, wanting to not exist, self-harm, or feeling there's no point going on, **including the indirect / passive forms**: "everyone'd be better off without me", "I wouldn't mind not waking up", "tired of being here". Watch for the same indirect forms in whatever language the user writes in, not just English.
- Plans or means to hurt themselves, or that they're at risk right now.
- Severe, immobilizing depression, or talk of harming someone else.
- Acute crisis where they can't keep themselves safe.

What to do: respond like a person, not a worksheet. Acknowledge it directly and without panic. Make clear this is past what self-help should carry alone, and that reaching a human matters more than any narrative work here. **Match their language**: deliver all of this in the language they are writing in, never switch them to English to read the script. Then surface help, localized to where they are (next section).

**If the risk is toward someone else** (not themselves): same calm, drop the technique, but the move is de-escalation and reaching emergency services / a human now (the local emergency number), not a suicide line. (See also the accountability caution below — externalizing must never become a way to offload responsibility for harm.)

Do not try to "re-author" your way out of a crisis. Stay with them, signpost, and gently keep checking they're safe. Lower-risk stabilizing skills (grounding 5-4-3-2-1, paced breathing) are fine as a bridge, not as a treatment.

## Surfacing crisis help, localized

Crisis numbers are country-specific and they change, so don't recite one from memory and don't hand someone a hotline that won't connect from where they are. Localize:

- **Use what's on record.** If the user's country / region is in `CLAUDE.md`, lead with the resources recorded there: the local emergency number and a verified local crisis line. If location isn't recorded, ask once, briefly and calmly, where they are (or infer it from the session), then continue, don't stall the response on it.
- **Verify before you give a number.** Web-search the current crisis line for their country rather than reciting one, then record the verified number(s) in `CLAUDE.md` so the next session already has them. [findahelpline.com](https://findahelpline.com) is a reliable directory covering most countries and works as a universal fallback if you can't confirm a specific line.
- **Give the universal emergency number** for their region as the immediate-danger route: 112 across the EU and much of the world, 911 in the US / Canada, 999 in the UK, 000 in Australia, or the local equivalent. In the US the Suicide & Crisis Lifeline is **988** (call or text, 24/7, free, confidential). When in doubt, "your local emergency number" plus findahelpline.com is always safe.
- **Point to a human they know.** Encourage contacting their therapist, doctor, or someone they trust today; if `CLAUDE.md` records a specific person or clinician, name them. (This doubles as a "club of life" member — see `core-maps.md` Map 3 — but in a crisis the move is contact, not conversation.)

## Low mood vs crisis, the one call that matters

"Pointless / nothing's worth it / what's the point" about activities or effort is anhedonia, ordinary depression, in scope — externalize "the Depression" and look for unique outcomes (`depression.md`). "No point going on / in being here / in living" is about their life — that is a crisis trigger. When it's genuinely ambiguous, ask one calm, direct screening question before routing ("When you say pointless, are you having any thoughts of not wanting to be alive?") and let the answer decide. Asking does not plant the idea. A clear "no, just flat" lets you de-escalate and treat it as low mood; don't over-escalate ordinary depression into a crisis script either.

## Clinician-/specialist-guided work, deliver only the safe layer

These want a trained clinician or specialist and can transiently raise distress. **Do not walk the user through them as self-help.** When the routed problem is one of these, give the framing and the safe adjacent moves, say plainly that the full work wants a professional, and offer the lower-risk layer instead.

- **Trauma / PTSD (White/Epston NT)** (`trauma.md`), clinician-guided. NT for trauma does **not** revisit the traumatic event; it externalizes the trauma's *effects* on identity and relationships and uses Absent-But-Implicit, re-membering, and witnessing. Even so, trauma work should be clinician-guided — Erbes et al. 2014 is a single small pilot. Offer grounding/stabilization and the framing; do not run trauma processing as self-help.
- **Narrative Exposure Therapy (NET)** (`net.md`), a **different, manualized, trauma-exposure protocol** that only shares the word "narrative" with this skill. NET involves sustained in-sensu trauma exposure and must be delivered only by trained NET practitioners. **Never run NET, never present NET's evidence as NT's, and route anyone seeking it to a trained clinician.**
- **Eating disorders (anorexia / bulimia)** (`eating-disorders.md`), specialist CBT-style and medical monitoring required; NT's evidence here is rated "insufficient for conclusions". Offer the externalizing framing ("Ana"/"Mia" as separate) and signpost specialist assessment; do not run it as standalone treatment.
- **Active suicidality with identity work** — the Ellis (2022) case recommends NT *integrated with* DBT (for safety and emotion regulation) for clients with suicidality. NT alone is not a crisis intervention; follow the crisis section above.

## Narrative-specific cautions

- **Externalizing must preserve accountability.** Separating the person from the problem is powerful, but where there is violence, abuse, or harm to others, externalizing must never become an excuse that offloads responsibility ("the Anger made me do it"). Keep the person accountable for actions while still externalizing the problem; always assess power and safety dynamics first. This is a hard rule, not a stylistic one.
- **Stay decentered — don't impose the story.** The risk in re-authoring is the helper getting excited and writing the client's preferred story *for* them. Over-enthusiasm and leading questions re-center you and produce a thin, borrowed story that won't hold. Ask, stay curious, let them author the meaning.
- **Don't pathologize, but don't deny reality either.** NT refuses diagnostic framing on principle. Honor that — but a real, solvable problem or a genuine risk is not a "story to re-author". Name reality plainly when it's reality (a real deadline, a real loss, a real danger).
- **Honest evidence.** NT's quantitative evidence base is thin and NT itself resists the medical-model evidence hierarchy. Don't oversell outcomes; offer the work as a meaningful way to relate to one's story, not a guaranteed cure.

## The framing to hold

Treat the user as the author and expert on their own life, a collaborator, not a patient to be managed — that *is* the narrative stance. Meet them where they are. But "self-help" doesn't mean "alone with a crisis", and "anti-diagnostic" doesn't mean ignoring danger: the whole point of the flags above is to notice the moment self-help stops being the right tool and a human needs to be in the room.
