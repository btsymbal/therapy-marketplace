## How to use this file

These six conversational maps (White, *Maps of Narrative Practice*, 2007) are the core of Narrative Therapy — the transdiagnostic catalog used across almost every problem. The domain files (`depression.md`, `grief.md`, etc.) assume these and add only the specialized layer, so they point back here by name. They are **structured inquiry frameworks, not rigid procedures** — any session may draw on several, and Map 6 (Scaffolding) underpins all of them.

Deliver every one per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one question per turn, decentered, follow their words, wait.** The questions below are templates — ask the one that fits, in the user's language, and let *them* author the meaning. The single most important rule: **the person is not the problem; the problem is the problem.** Externalizing (Map 1) is almost always where you start.

## Map 1 · Externalizing Conversations (Statement of Position)

**Reach for it when:** the foundational move — nearly every presenting problem, and especially powerful when the person is fused with the problem ("I *am* a failure / an anxious person / an addict"), in shame, or in a family where everyone blames or is blamed. It makes everything else possible: you cannot find an exception to a problem someone believes they *are*. Sustained as a *stance* throughout therapy, not a one-time step. **Caution:** with violence or harm, externalizing must preserve accountability and never become an excuse — assess power and safety first (`safety.md`).

**Walk it through** — White's four-stage Statement of Position Map, one question per turn:

**Stage 1 — Negotiate an experience-near name.**
1. Invite them to name the problem as a separate entity, in their own language — don't impose a clinical label. Use personification and metaphor ("the Fog", "the What-If Spiral", "the Bully", "Ana"). Ask: *"If this difficulty had a name, what would we call it?"* / *"Some people name the problem so they can see it as separate from themselves — what might fit for you?"* / *"What does it do when it's at its strongest?"*

**Stage 2 — Map its effects (relative-influence questions).**
2. Explore how the problem affects their relationships, daily life, thoughts, work, identity — and the cultural messages that feed it. Ask: *"Where in your life does [the Fear] have the strongest grip?"* / *"When did it first come into your life?"* / *"How has it tried to get you to see yourself?"* / *"What messages about [success / bodies / worth] give it fuel?"*
3. Then map their counter-influence over it: *"When you resisted its demands, what made that possible?"* / *"What did you do that forced it to back down?"* (These often surface the first unique outcomes — see Map 5.)

**Stage 3 — Evaluate the effects (position questions).**
4. Invite them to take a position — do **not** tell them the effects are bad. This is the move that preserves their autonomy and generates agency. Ask: *"Are these effects okay with you, or not?"* / *"Is this a good thing or a bad thing for you?"* / *"How do you feel about what it's been doing?"*

**Stage 4 — Justify the evaluation.**
5. Explore *why* they hold that position, connecting to values and the life they want. Ask: *"What does your being unhappy about this say about what you value?"* / *"What kind of life are you trying to build that this problem gets in the way of?"* / *"Whose interests does this problem serve?"*

**Deepening (personification):** *"What does the problem want for your future?"* / *"How does it trick you into its way of thinking?"* / *"What promises has it made — has it kept them?"*

**If it stalls:** problem still feels inherent ("it's just me") → try a different name, a more vivid metaphor, smaller and more concrete questions, or non-verbal methods (drawing); a little humor/playfulness can unstick it. No counter-influence surfaces → widen the timeframe to childhood, shrink the question ("was there even one brief moment?"), use relative-influence percentages ("what % of the time does it win?"), or go to **Absent-But-Implicit** (`cross-cutting-tools.md`). Keep **double listening** running — the counter-story is inside the problem-story.

## Map 2 · Re-Authoring Conversations

**Reach for it when:** after some externalizing has separated problem from person, and whenever any unique outcome surfaces. This is the primary ongoing vehicle of the middle and later work — how isolated exceptions get built into a thick, historically grounded alternative story. A single unique outcome lacks power; the work is linking exceptions into "a history over time, or a sequence with a theme".

**Walk it through:**
1. Identify a unique outcome (action-, intention-, feeling-, or relational-based — see Map 5).
2. **Landscape of Action** questions — establish concrete context: *"What happened? Where were you, who else was there? What steps did you take to make that possible? What did you actually do?"*
3. **Landscape of Identity** questions — draw meaning, tentatively, never imposing: *"What does that say about you as a person? About what you value? What quality did you show in that moment? What does it reveal about what matters to you?"*
4. **Historicize** — link to similar past events: *"Were there other times, maybe further back, when something like this showed up?"*
5. Connect multiple such events across time to give the emerging story a history.
6. Invite them to name it: *"If this story had a title, what would it be?"*
7. Keep thickening by applying both landscapes to further events.

*Stance:* stay **decentered** — avoid excessive enthusiasm (it centers you) and avoid imposing conclusions about what an outcome means. They attribute the meaning; your questions just open the door.

**If it stalls:** use **double listening** and **Absent-But-Implicit** (`cross-cutting-tools.md`); widen the timeframe to childhood; use experience-of-experience questions (*"who in your life wouldn't be surprised you did that — what would they say?"*); use "consulting your consultants" questions (`cross-cutting-tools.md`) to move them from patient to expert.

## Map 3 · Re-Membering Conversations

**Reach for it when:** (1) a significant positive figure is mentioned — ask what they'd think of the user's current developments; (2) the user describes an ability — trace who modeled it; (3) hopelessness/unworthiness — find a figure who saw good in them; (4) **grief** — the deceased continues as a member of the "club of life", a non-pathologizing alternative to "letting go" (`grief.md`); (5) trauma where abusive figures hold too much "membership weight".

The rationale is White's "club of life": identity is a social construction with a *membership* of significant figures. The hyphen in "re-membering" is deliberate — this is active revision of who holds what status in your life, not passive recall. Members can be living or deceased, known or unknown, real or fictional (a historical figure, a book or film character). Some are "VIP members"; some can be downgraded.

**Walk it through** — Part 1, the figure's contribution (one question per turn):
1. *"What has this person contributed to your life?"*
2. *"What did they do that made a difference?"*
3. *"Why do you think they took an interest in you?"*
4. *"What did you appreciate about them?"*
5. *"What did *you* contribute to *their* life?"* (the reciprocal direction is often neglected — don't skip it)
6. *"What did they appreciate about you?"*
7. *"How did their appreciation become part of how you see yourself?"*

Part 2 (in group/community form) — outsider-witness retelling (Map 4). Part 3 — the person responds to what they heard, connecting it to their values and what's now possible. (Solo: you can act as witness, or they imagine the figure witnessing — see Map 4.)

**If it stalls:** no real positive figures → admired public/historical figures, book or film characters count; negative figures dominate (abuse) → focus on *downgrading* their membership weight and finding even one minor figure who saw good in them; in grief → *"What would they want for you now? What's your responsibility to keep alive?"*

## Map 4 · Definitional Ceremonies / Outsider-Witness

**Reach for it when:** the preferred story needs **social confirmation** to feel real — a story that stays only in the person's head lacks the sense of being witnessed and recognized. Problems isolate; witnessing reconnects and makes identity claims public. Used as a culminating or midpoint practice. (White built this deliberately as an alternative to reflecting teams, to avoid reproducing pathologizing, expert-positioned, problem-focused talk.)

**Full structure (group form):** Stage 1 — the focal person is interviewed while witnesses listen silently. Stage 2 — witnesses are interviewed using the four categories below while the focal person listens. Stage 3 — the focal person responds to what resonated/surprised. Stage 4 — conversation continues with the enriched material.

**The four-category witness response** (this is the structure — keep witnesses to *these*, not advice or applause):
1. **Expression** — *"Which particular words or phrases struck you as they spoke?"*
2. **Image** — *"What image came to mind? What picture did their words paint?"*
3. **Resonance** — *"Why those words — what did they connect with that's important in your own life?"* (witnesses situate it in *their* experience, not comment on the focal person)
4. **Transport** — *"What's it been like to be connected to this story? How has it shifted your thinking or sense of what's possible?"*

Witnesses do **not** offer: applause, advice, storytelling that takes over, or imposed "shoulds".

**Self-help / solo adaptation** (the full group format usually isn't available here — see `collective-practices.md` for group setups): act as the witness yourself, responding through the four categories with genuine, situated resonance (not praise); or have the user *imagine* a chosen figure (real or fictional) witnessing their story and answer the four categories as that figure; or write a therapeutic letter from you-as-witness (`documents.md`); or have them name real people who could witness it and plan to share it. Always flag when a real community of witnesses would add something this can't.

**If it stalls:** start with the imaginal/letter form before any real-audience sharing; keep it to one unique outcome; if witnessing feels exposing, make a counter-document instead (`documents.md`) as a private record first.

## Map 5 · Unique Outcomes

**Reach for it when:** any time a contradiction to the problem-saturated story appears — even briefly, even something the user dismisses as a fluke. Unique outcomes (also "sparkling moments", "exceptions") are the **entry points into re-authoring** (Map 2). Most powerful encountered mid-externalizing, when mapping the person's counter-influence over the problem.

**Types** (any of these counts): **action-based** (something they did), **intention-based** (a desire/plan to act differently), **feeling-based** (an emotion contradicting the problem), **relational** (an unexpected connection).

**Walk it through:**
1. Listen for what they minimize or gloss over — unique outcomes hide in dismissed instances.
2. When one appears, pause and name it: *"Wait — you said once you managed to ___. Can we stop there?"*
3. Ask finding-questions: *"Was there a time [the Fear] tried to stop you and you went ahead anyway?"* / *"A recent moment you handled things differently than the problem usually dictates?"* / *"Have you ever rebelled against it, even a little?"*
4. Elaborate across **both landscapes** (Map 2) — action then identity.
5. Build temporal depth by linking it to other exceptions across history.

**If none surface:** externalize first to create space, then ask when the problem was *absent*; widen the timeframe to childhood; ask about *partial* resistance (*"times it had less grip?"*); use **Absent-But-Implicit** (`cross-cutting-tools.md`) — the premise is that exceptions are always present even in a heavily problem-saturated story; the challenge is perceptual, not existential. Don't over-enthuse when one appears — stay curious.

## Map 6 · Scaffolding Conversations

**Reach for it when:** implicitly, always — it's the sequencing principle under all six maps. Explicitly, when the person is stuck at an abstract level, can't get from experience to meaning, or can't move from description toward future agency. White adapted Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development: bridge "what a person can do unaided" and "what they can do with support" through small, sequenced steps.

**Walk it through** — five levels of inquiry (distancing tasks). Always move **low to high, never skip**:
1. **Low-level distancing** — immediate, concrete experience: *"What happened? Tell me about that moment."*
2. **Medium** — connecting events, comparing: *"What does that event mean to you? How are these two related?"*
3. **Medium-high** — reflecting on patterns: *"What have you learned from these? What does this pattern tell you about yourself?"*
4. **High** — concepts about life/identity: *"Given what you've learned, what do you want for your life?"*
5. **Very high** — future-oriented action: *"What changes would you start, to move toward that preferred future?"*

**If it stalls:** drop back a level — make sure the foundation is solid before climbing. The near-to-far principle means never jumping; if they're giving abstract answers to a concrete (Level 1) question, gently ask for more specific description before going up.
