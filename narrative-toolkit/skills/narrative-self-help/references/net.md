## Why this file exists

People searching for "narrative therapy for trauma" frequently mean — or land on — **Narrative Exposure Therapy (NET)**, which shares only the word "narrative" with the White/Epston Narrative Therapy this skill is built on. This file exists to make the distinction unmistakable, to route anyone seeking NET to a trained clinician, and to **prevent NET's much stronger evidence base from being mis-attributed to NT.** This skill does **not** deliver NET. If a user wants NET, the correct response is information + a referral, not a session.

## NET is not White/Epston NT — the category distinction

Conflating their evidence bases is a category error. They differ on nearly every axis:

| Dimension | White/Epston NT (this skill) | NET (Schauer, Neuner & Elbert) |
|---|---|---|
| Theoretical base | Social constructionism, poststructuralism | Trauma-focused CBT + testimony approach |
| Target | Identity/meaning across many problems | PTSD specifically |
| Protocol | No fixed protocol; six maps as a flexible framework | Manualized, 4–12 sessions of ~90 min |
| Diagnostic stance | Explicitly anti-diagnostic; refuses DSM framing | Uses DSM/ICD PTSD diagnosis |
| Core tool | Externalizing; re-authoring | The "lifeline" (rope + flowers/stones/candles) |
| Core mechanism | Identity re-authoring via preferred story | In-sensu trauma **exposure** + testimony |
| Output | Letters, certificates, documents | A written testimony (potential legal use) |
| Population | General clinical; family/community | War refugees, torture and disaster survivors |
| RCT evidence | Very sparse | 24+ RCTs; Tier-1 meta-analyses |
| Guideline recognition | None documented | NICE, APA, ISTSS, VA/DoD |

## What NET actually is

The core technique is the **lifeline**: a rope laid on the floor representing the person's life chronologically. Flowers mark positive events; stones mark traumatic events; candles mark deaths of significant others. The therapist walks the chronology with the client across multiple sessions, spending **in-sensu exposure** time on each traumatic event until emotional activation decreases, producing a written testimony the client keeps (and which can be used for advocacy or legal purposes). This is sustained, structured trauma exposure — the opposite of White/Epston NT's deliberate choice *not* to revisit the event (`trauma.md`).

## Evidence and variants

- **NET:** 24 RCTs, N≈1,391; medium effect sizes (SMD 0.30–0.49) for PTSD vs. controls; recognized by NICE, APA, ISTSS, VA/DoD (Raghuraman et al. 2020; Lely et al. 2019).
- **KIDNET:** child-adapted (ages 7+); same lifeline/exposure; 3 RCTs; 17% vs. 70% meeting PTSD criteria at 12-month follow-up (Ruf et al. 2010).
- **FORNET:** for people with both trauma history and a history of perpetrating violence; adds sticks for violent acts; 2 RCTs; lay-counselor feasibility shown in the DRC (Köbach et al. 2015).
- **NET for intellectual disabilities:** an underpowered RCT pilot (N=15, Mayer et al. 2023) with a large observed effect.

**These effect sizes belong to NET only — never present them as evidence for the Narrative Therapy this skill delivers.**

## The hard boundary

NET must be delivered **only by trained NET practitioners**. It involves prolonged trauma exposure and can transiently increase distress; it is **not a self-help protocol** and this skill will not run it. If a user asks for NET, the lifeline, or trauma-exposure work: explain the distinction briefly, do not attempt it, and route them to a clinician trained in NET (see `safety.md`). For NT's own (clinician-guided) approach to trauma — which works on the trauma's *effects*, not the event — see `trauma.md`.
