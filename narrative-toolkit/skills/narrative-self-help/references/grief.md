## How to use this file

The grief and bereavement layer. NT's orientation is **continuing bonds**, not "letting go": the goal is not to sever the relationship with the person who died but to **re-member** them as an ongoing member of the "club of life", in a modified form. This is a profound relief for many people who've been told they should be "moving on". Assumes the six maps in `core-maps.md`; **Re-Membering (Map 3) is the core technique here** and often where you lead, rather than externalizing. Deliver per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one question per turn, decentered, gentle, wait.** Keep `safety.md` live — complicated grief with suicidality is a crisis, not a re-authoring session.

## The continuing-bonds reframe

Offer it lightly, as a possibility, early: many people carry an unspoken instruction to "let go" and feel they're failing at grief. NT says the opposite — that the relationship continues, changed, and that keeping the person present in your life is healthy, not pathological. Don't impose it; ask whether it fits: *"Some people find grief eases not by letting the person go but by finding a new way to keep them in their life — does that land for you, or not?"*

## 1 · Re-Membering the person who died

**Reach for it when:** the core of grief work (`core-maps.md` Map 3). Move in both directions — what they gave the person *and* what the person gave them; the reciprocal direction is often the most healing and the most neglected.
**Walk it through (one question per turn):**
1. *"What did they contribute to your life — what did they do that made a difference?"*
2. *"What did you appreciate most about them?"*
3. *"What did *you* contribute to *their* life? What did they appreciate about you?"*
4. *"How did being loved by them become part of how you see yourself?"*
5. Bring it into the present: *"What would they want for you now? What's your responsibility to keep alive on their behalf?"*
6. Give the continuing relationship a shape: *"What's one way you could keep them as a member of your life going forward?"*

**If it stalls:** the relationship was painful or ambivalent → don't force warmth; you can still re-member honestly (what was true, what you keep, what you set down), and you can *downgrade* a harmful figure's weight (`core-maps.md` Map 3).

## 2 · Outsider witnessing the bond

**Reach for it when:** the bond and the preferred memory of the person want to be witnessed and made real (`core-maps.md` Map 4). In self-help: act as witness yourself through the four categories (expression, image, resonance, transport) with genuine, situated resonance — not condolence; or have them imagine someone who also loved the person witnessing their account; or write a letter that witnesses the bond (`documents.md`). Real group ceremonies (e.g. a bereaved-family group) add what solo can't — signpost when relevant (`collective-practices.md`).

## 3 · Tree of Life and documents

**Reach for it when:** the person wants something tangible to hold the bond. The **Tree of Life** (`collective-practices.md`) places important people (living *and* deceased) on the leaves and gifts received from them on the fruits — a structured, non-retraumatizing way to honor the person within the whole of one's life. A **letter to the person who died**, or a **counter-document** recording what they gave and what continues, anchors the continuing bond (`documents.md`). A six-session group pilot (Lordello & Silva 2025) combined Tree of Life, Re-membering, and Outsider Witnessing for bereaved families.

## Honest evidence

Preliminary — one pilot group study (Lordello & Silva 2025, no control group). Offer the continuing-bonds reframe and re-membering as a meaningful, non-pathologizing way to grieve, not a measured treatment. Keep `safety.md` live for complicated or suicidal grief.
