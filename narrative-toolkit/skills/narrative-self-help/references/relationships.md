## How to use this file

The family and couples layer. NT's foundational move — externalizing a *shared* problem so it sits outside everyone rather than inside one blamed person — maps directly onto relationship work and is its single biggest gift here: it converts "you're the problem" into "the Conflict is the problem, and we're on the same side against it". In self-help the user is usually **one member** of the couple/family working on their part of the pattern; coach them to externalize the *relational* problem, not the other person. Assumes the six maps in `core-maps.md`. Deliver per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one question per turn, decentered, wait.** **Safety:** where there is abuse, coercion, or violence, externalizing must **never** dissolve accountability or keep someone in danger — see `safety.md` first.

## 1 · Externalizing the shared / relational problem

**Reach for it when:** the start of the work — the couple/family is locked in mutual blame. Externalize the pattern itself (`core-maps.md` Map 1).
**Walk it through:**
1. Name the relational problem as a third thing — "the Distance", "the Conflict Cycle", "the Silence that grows between us". Ask: *"If the thing that comes between you two had a name — not you, not them, the pattern itself — what would you call it?"*
2. Map its effects on the relationship and how it recruits each person into roles.
3. Map counter-influence: *"When has the Conflict Cycle tried to start and one of you stepped out of it?"*
4. Position it jointly (even if only one is present): *"Is the way the Distance runs things okay with you?"*

**If it stalls:** the user keeps re-blaming the partner → gently redirect to the *pattern* as the target ("what does the Cycle get *you* to do?"); name that they can only work their own side of it.

## 2 · Unique outcomes in the shared history

**Reach for it when:** the relationship's preferred story has gone missing under the problem. Mine the shared history for exceptions (`core-maps.md` Maps 5 → 2).
**Walk it through:** *"When did the two of you last beat the Distance, even for an evening? What did each of you do? What does that say about what this relationship is capable of?"* Build those moments into a counter-story of the relationship — its values, what it's for — and let them title it.

**If it stalls:** no positive moments accessible → widen to the start of the relationship ("what first drew you together?"), or use **Absent-But-Implicit** (`cross-cutting-tools.md`) — the hurt implies what they wish the relationship still held.

## 3 · Re-authoring the relationship; family witnessing

**Reach for it when:** building the alternative relational story and giving it standing. Use **Re-Authoring** (`core-maps.md` Map 2) on the relationship as the unit. **Therapeutic letters** between members, or to the relationship, thicken it (`documents.md`). In families, **Definitional Ceremonies with family members as witnesses** (`core-maps.md` Map 4) and **Re-Membering** of significant ancestors can re-author the family story. In self-help, the user can write to their partner/family, or imagine a trusted figure witnessing the relationship's preferred story.

## Honest evidence

Honest framing: **NT is *not* among the well-established couple therapies** (Carr 2018; Doss et al. 2021; Owen et al. 2023 — three independent reviews). Family NT rests on case vignettes and practitioner consensus, with no controlled trials specific to it. Two quasi-experimental studies of women with low marital satisfaction (N=30 each, Iran) showed *mixed* results. Offer externalizing-the-shared-problem as a genuinely useful reframe, not an evidence-based couple treatment; suggest a couples/family therapist for serious or entrenched distress, and follow `safety.md` where there's any abuse or danger.
