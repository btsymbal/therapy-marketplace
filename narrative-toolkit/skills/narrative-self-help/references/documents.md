## How to use this file

Documents are how Narrative Therapy makes a preferred story **durable** — the written word "remains intact, to be revised, reedited, and rewritten", outlasting the session and the memory of it. Every therapeutic document is "a storage site for valued aspects of a person's identity". In self-help this is one of the most natural NT moves to do solo: the user can write, or co-write with you, a letter / certificate / counter-document that anchors what came into view. Record what's been made in `CLAUDE.md` (`logging.md`) so it can be re-read and built on. Co-write per the spirit of `walkthrough-protocol.md` — draw the words out of *them*; their phrasing is the point.

## Why documents work

A preferred story that lives only in conversation is fragile. Written down, it becomes something the person can return to, re-read, and re-experience — "in re-reading documents, people often have the experience of remaking and re-experiencing the meaning located in them". Documents also resist the deficit-based official records (medical notes, school reports, diagnoses) that often carry an institutional, problem-saturated account of the person — they form an **"archive of resistance"** to those records (`collective-practices.md`).

## 1 · Therapeutic letters — the ten types

White & Epston's ten letter types (use the one that fits the moment):

- **Letters of invitation** — to a reluctant family member; highlight their importance, give positive reasons to engage, share the new narrative.
- **Redundancy letters** — relieve someone of a role (e.g. a caregiver) as the person becomes more independent through their revised story.
- **Letters of prediction** — the therapist writes the person's goals/hopes as if already underway; seeing the story written helps bring it to pass.
- **Counter-referral letters** — communicate progress and the new narrative to a referrer (GP, school), written *to* the family/client and copied to the referrer.
- **Letters of reference** — counter a troublesome narrative imposed by an important figure (teacher, doctor) with lived evidence for the new story.
- **Letters for special occasions** — prepare the person for a stressful upcoming event (court, a family gathering, an interview) by outlining the desired outcome.
- **Brief letters** — short, warm reminders of progress and that they can re-apply the re-authoring themselves.
- **Letters as narrative** — the most common type: a post-session summary recapping the conversation, highlighting unique outcomes, opening space for the alternative story (see section 2).
- **Self-stories** — authentic autobiographies the *person* writes after re-authoring, to share as testimony.
- **Counter-documents** — certificates, declarations, awards that visually remind the person of success and counteract negative official records (section 3).

## 2 · The session-summary letter (how to write one)

The most common and most useful in self-help. **It is not templated** — Moules (2002): "The findings do not create a template… nor a rigid form." The approach is "art-like". This is practitioner heuristic, not a validated procedure.

*Method (Ingamells):* write it soon after the session while fresh; mind-map the key ideas before reading notes; aim for roughly **40% of the letter to be in the person's own words** (a heuristic, not a rule); arrange their phrases to best tell the counter-story; cut anything that doesn't move the story forward.

*Content elements:*
1. **Editorial-rights disclaimer** — say this is your version and invite them to correct anything.
2. **Acknowledgement** — name what they shared; acknowledge the problem's effects without foregrounding them.
3. **Unique outcomes** — name specific moments they showed a value, quality, or alternative-story movement.
4. **The alternative story** — what those moments say about who they are and what they value.
5. **Reflective questions** — open, curious questions that carry the work into the gap before next time.
6. **A warm, brief close.**

*Tone (Moules 2002):* **acknowledge suffering before commendations** — "people need to know their pain has been recognized" first; use **questions more than statements** ("questions are the vehicles of breath"); never let praise "obscure the recognition of suffering". Don't cheerlead — this is the decentered stance in written form.

## 3 · Counter-documents and certificates

Counter-documents highlight the person's story and subordinate the problem story — "the history of capacities, abilities, values, and commitments of the person". Forms include certificates, declarations, awards, songs, poems. Examples:
- "Certificate of Achievement: Standing Up to Perfectionism"
- "Declaration of Independence from Self-Criticism" (or "from Ana", `eating-disorders.md`)
- "Award for Courage in Facing the Fog"

*How to construct:* identify the achievement; use the person's own language; put their name prominently; reference the qualities it demonstrates; use ceremonial/formal language so it feels official; involve them in the design. These are especially powerful for children (`children-adolescents.md`) and as tangible artifacts at a witnessing/ceremony (`collective-practices.md`).

## 4 · Letters from the future

An adaptation of prediction letters, written from an imagined future vantage:
- a **personal accomplishment letter** to be read after a big event;
- a **"rainy day" letter** written from a future moment of doing well, to be opened in a hard time;
- an **"older, wiser self" letter** from an imagined future self to the present one.

These are a gentle, evocative self-help move when the preferred story needs a voice from ahead.

## Honest evidence

Widely used in NT practice, but Moules (2002) is explicit: "there is no empirical evidence or formal evaluative studies to support the benefit of therapeutic letters." The popular "a letter is worth three sessions" claim derives from an informal, unpublished 1995 survey cited third-hand — **not** peer-reviewed evidence; don't repeat it as fact. Offer letters and documents as a meaningful, story-thickening practice, honestly framed.
