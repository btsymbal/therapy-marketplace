## How to use this file

The child and adolescent layer. In a self-help setting the user is usually a **parent (or an older teen working on their own stuff)**, not a young child directly — frame the techniques as something a parent can do *with* their child, or that an adolescent can do for themselves. NT adapts well to children because externalizing can ride on a child's natural imaginative play. Assumes the six maps in `core-maps.md` (especially Externalizing and Scaffolding). Deliver per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one question per turn, wait.** **Safety:** child safeguarding concerns, abuse, or a child's suicidality need professional involvement immediately — see `safety.md`.

## 1 · Playful externalizing in the child's own imagery

**Reach for it when:** a child's behavioral problem or fear — the foundational move, made playful (`core-maps.md` Map 1). The metaphor should come from the *child's* world, not an adult label.
**Walk it through (coach the parent, or the teen):**
1. Let the child name the problem as a character — "the Worry Monster", "the Grump", "Mr. Mischief", "Sneaky Poo" (White's classic for encopresis). Ask the child: *"If this thing that makes the worry come had a name, what would it be? What does it look like?"*
2. Map what the character does and *when it doesn't win*: *"When did you boss the Worry Monster back? How did you do that?"*
3. Recruit the child as the hero against the externalized problem, and a "helper" character who's on their side.
4. Celebrate counter-influence with genuine curiosity (not adult applause), and consider a certificate (`documents.md`).

**If it stalls:** too abstract for the child → go fully into play/drawing (below); resistance → make the problem a bit ridiculous, let the child boss it around.

## 2 · Multi-sensory methods (drawing, play, clay)

**Reach for it when:** the child is young (age 4+) or verbal questioning isn't landing. Externalizing through **drawing, clay, play, and story characters** lets a child put the problem outside themselves literally. Have them draw the Worry Monster, sculpt it, then talk to the drawing about when they've beaten it. Theme-development happens over multiple sessions; chronological narrative becomes more accessible around age 6+.

## 3 · Cinema Narrative Therapy

**Reach for it when:** a child responds to films more than to talk (Turns & Macey 2016). Animated films with a **clear external villain** (the adversary is outside the hero) are well-suited: use the film's externalized antagonist as a projective entry point, then apply standard externalizing/re-authoring questions to the child's own situation *through* the film's story. Ask: *"Who's the [villain] in your story, and when have you been like [the hero] who stood up to it?"*

## 4 · Scaffolding with children

**Reach for it when:** always — but especially explicitly with children, since the research (Ramey et al. 2010) found therapists and children move through scaffolding levels in sequence and change happens at the level of language. Keep questions **near-to-far** (`core-maps.md` Map 6): start with what concretely happened before asking what it means. Drop a level the moment answers go blank.

## Honest evidence and safety

Preliminary. A Danish CAMHS Narrative Family Therapy pre-post study (N=48, no control) found symptom reduction across diverse presentations; an autism pilot (Cashin et al. 2012, N=10, no control) found improvement; a single test-anxiety quasi-experiment (N=22). No controlled trials for most child conditions, and NT alone did *not* reduce preschool separation anxiety in one 2023 study. Offer the playful framing as genuinely useful for a parent, not a proven treatment, and route safeguarding/clinical concerns to professionals (`safety.md`).
