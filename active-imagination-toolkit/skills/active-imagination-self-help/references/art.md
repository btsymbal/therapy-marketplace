## How to use this file

**Visual art / painting / mandala** is the most historically documented modality in Jung's own
practice — he directed patients to paint their dreams and fantasies. It's specifically useful when
**imagery is present but dialogue feels premature**, when verbal defenses are strong, or when somatic
or traumatic intrusions need an external container the image can hold. Image-making bypasses verbal
defenses, so it's a good door when words won't catch. Rests on `foundations.md`; delivered per
`walkthrough-protocol.md`: one step per turn, wait. Keep `safety.md` live.

**Note on the clinical variant.** *Therapist-held* image-making — where the analyst holds and works
with the image on behalf of a patient with early trauma and insufficient ego-structure — is a
clinician modality, not self-practice (`clinical-presentations.md`, `safety.md`). The self-practice
forms below are for stable users. Artistic skill is irrelevant throughout; the process is the point,
not the product.

## 1 · Painting / drawing the image

**When & where it works best:** Imagery is surfacing but isn't ready for dialogue; verbal work has
stalled; the user wants to externalize a mood or figure onto a surface where it can be met.

**Walk it through (one step per turn):**
1. **Gather any medium.** Paper, pencil, watercolor, pastel, charcoal — whatever's to hand. Ask:
   "What have you got to make marks with? It honestly doesn't matter what."
2. **Enter the inward state and anchor.** Dual-awareness anchor (`foundations.md`); relaxed
   alertness. Ask: "Let an inner image surface — don't try to create something. What's there?"
3. **Externalize it (Phase 2→3).** Ask: "Open your eyes and start putting it down — 'create first,
   criticize second.' Don't evaluate it while you make it. What's going onto the page?"
4. **Follow where the image leads.** Ask: "Add what the image itself seems to call for — extend a
   line, change a color. Don't impose a composition. Where does it want to go?"
5. **Stop when it feels complete**, not when a timer runs out. Ask: "Does it feel finished, or is
   there more it wants?"
6. **Examine it.** Ask: "Look at what you made. What do you notice? Stay descriptive — resist
   jumping to what it 'means' yet." (You can bridge to written dialogue with a strong element, as in
   the mandala procedure below.)
7. **Ethical confrontation (Phase 4).** Ask: "What does this image's content ask of you? One
   concrete thing." Record/log (`logging.md`); keep the image as part of the record.

## 2 · The mandala

**When & where it works best:** Checking the **current state of the self** ("How am I right now?"),
or as a **session closer** after difficult material — the circle is contained and self-organizing.
Tracking a **mandala series** across time is a documented method for following individuation
(`logging.md`).

**Walk it through (one step per turn):**
1. **Prepare.** Square paper, pencil, something round to trace or a compass, coloring implements.
   Mark a center point. Ask: "Ready, with a center point marked?"
2. **Draw the circle.** Ask: "Draw a large circle around the center — the 'magic circle'. Done?"
3. **Sit with the question.** Ask: "Quietly hold one question: 'How am I now?' Don't answer in
   words — just hold it."
4. **Fill it spontaneously (Phase 2→3).** Ask: "Begin filling the circle from the center outward —
   shapes, lines, symbols, colors, whatever comes. Don't plan the composition. What's appearing?"
5. **Work in silence to completion.** Ask: "Keep going until it feels complete. Tell me when it's
   done."
6. **Dialogue with a significant symbol.** Ask: "Pick the element that pulls at you most. Ask it,
   in writing: 'What would you like to communicate to me?' — then wait." (This crosses into
   `writing.md`; run the surprise test.)
7. **Photograph it to begin a series**, and apply Phase 4. If they're in therapy, the mandala is
   worth sharing with the therapist. Record/log; note the series in `CLAUDE.md` (`logging.md`).

## If it doesn't work, try

- **No inner image arrives:** choose colors intuitively from current mood or body-feeling and make
  **abstract marks** without trying to make anything recognizable. The physical act of mark-making
  often catalyzes symbolic material that mental focusing won't.
- **Still nothing, or the surface stays dead:** move to `sandplay.md` (tactile rather than
  visual-kinesthetic entry), or hold the feeling-state per `foundations.md` and wait.
- **Strong/disturbing material surfaces and overwhelms:** the mandala/closing form can contain it —
  but if it floods, stop and ground (`safety.md`). For trauma-linked imagery without a stable
  ego-structure, this wants the therapist-held variant, i.e. a clinician
  (`clinical-presentations.md`).
