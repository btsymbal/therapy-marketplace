## How to use this file

This file holds the **boundary**: who can safely do this alone, who can't, and what the analyst
provides that solo practice structurally can't. Use it whenever you're unsure whether to run a
session at all, or whether to keep going. It complements `safety.md` (the hard stops) with the softer
judgment about readiness. The honest baseline, repeated from `safety.md`: active imagination is
**primarily a later-stage analytic practice**, it "cannot be read from a book," and the self-help
layer is a real but bounded subset for stable people.

## When clinical supervision is essential

Supervision is essential when **any** of the following is true: an absolute contraindication is
present (psychosis, unstabilized borderline structure, active infantile affects, disrupted symbolic
capacity); trauma is prominent; numinous or overwhelming material is present; the person is in
early-stage work without analytic groundwork; or any of the three failure modes (dissolution,
domination, inflation) is showing. In these cases the self-help layer is psychoeducation,
stabilization, and a clear, kind hand-off — not the technique.

## The four functions an analyst serves that self-practice can't

Name these honestly so the user understands what they're working without:

1. **Readiness assessment** — judging whether ego-structure, symbolic capacity, and tolerance of
   aloneness are sufficient. Historically, apparent cases of active imagination have concealed
   something else entirely (e.g. a delusional transference); a person can't reliably make that
   assessment of themselves.
2. **Guided introduction and framing of the attitude** — transmitting the "as if" stance as a lived
   experience inside a relationship, not just an idea read off a page.
3. **Reception and amplification** — receiving the figures and themes and contextualizing them in the
   person's history, complexes, and situation.
4. **Detecting and redirecting fruitless soliloquy** — distinguishing genuine active imagination from
   complex-driven repetition (the absence of surprise). The self-practitioner is structurally least
   able to catch this in themselves — which is exactly why the **surprise test** (`foundations.md`)
   and honest logging (`logging.md`) matter so much in self-practice, and why persistent stalling is a
   signal to bring in an analyst.

## When self-practice is feasible

Self-directed active imagination is possible for **psychologically stable** people. What a stable
self-practitioner needs:

- A **stable life structure** — no acute crisis; stable housing, relationships, work.
- An existing **journaling or reflective practice** (a recording habit).
- Familiarity with the **four-phase scaffold** and the **"as if" stance** as conceptual anchors
  (`foundations.md`).
- A **grounding ritual** before and after sessions.
- A known **exit protocol** for overwhelming sessions (`safety.md`).
- A willingness to **bring material to a therapist or analyst** if something persistently disturbing
  or confusing emerges.

Even for stable practitioners, **writing and visual** are the most accessible modalities; **movement**
benefits from relational holding, **music** needs more specialized setup, and **sandplay's** proper
form assumes a trained therapist (`sandplay.md`). Match ambition to readiness.

## Session structure for self-practice

- **Duration:** 20-45 minutes. Longer is not better; beginners shorter still (`visual.md`).
- **Environment:** quiet, private, uninterruptible.
- **Recording:** immediately after every session — date, modality, figures encountered, the
  ethical-confrontation commitment. A session without recording is a session whose material is lost
  (`logging.md`, `foundations.md`).
- **Cadence:** let integration keep pace with engagement. If Phase 4 commitments are piling up
  unacted-on, slow down — that backlog is itself a sign the work is outrunning the integration.

## How active imagination differs from derivative practices

Hold these distinctions so you don't quietly drift into an easier, different practice and call it
active imagination:

- **Internal Family Systems (IFS):** parts-dialogue with a similar internal-figure orientation, but
  more relational, more accessible for self-work, and **not** requiring interpretation of
  symbolic-archetypal content — lighter on ego-structure prerequisites. (Comparison is Tier-3 only.)
- **EMDR imagery:** imagery inside a **directive, trauma-processing** protocol with bilateral
  stimulation — trauma-specific, not individuation-focused.
- **Expressive arts therapy:** art-making in a **more structured, therapist-held** frame; can be a
  stepping-stone toward active imagination for someone not yet ready for full ego-lowering.
- **Guided imagery / visualization:** the ego **follows a script** provided by someone else; the
  content is **not autonomous.** Active imagination requires the content to be autonomous and
  **surprising** — that's the line that actually separates them (`foundations.md`, the surprise test).
