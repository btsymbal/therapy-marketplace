## How to use this file

**Visual / inner theater** is the most direct form of active imagination and the one Jung
practiced himself. It's the first-line modality for people who are visual in their natural register
(they see images, think in pictures). It rests entirely on `foundations.md` (the four phases, the
"as if" stance) and is delivered per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on an
invitation, wait.** Keep `safety.md` live — dual awareness and the surprise test run the whole time;
if dual awareness collapses, exit immediately.

Appropriate for stable individuals with a well-functioning ego. Beginners: 15-20 minutes is
plenty; experienced practitioners go longer. If any absolute contraindication is present, this is
not the modality (`safety.md`).

## 1 · Inner theater (the core visual procedure)

**When & where it works best:** A vivid or charged dream image, a lingering mood or unexplained
affect, or a bodily sensation that carries symbolic weight. When no image is available, the user
can imaginatively enter a personally significant landscape and watch what appears. Don't manufacture
an image for the occasion.

**Walk it through (one step per turn):**
1. **Select the entry image** from spontaneous material — a dream fragment, an unexpected emotion, a
   recurring fantasy. Ask: "What image, figure, or feeling is asking for attention right now? Don't
   invent one — what's actually there?" If nothing visual, name the dominant feeling-state and use
   that as the door.
2. **Anchor the stance and settle.** Do the dual-awareness anchor (`foundations.md`). Ask them to
   settle somewhere quiet, eyes closed, aiming for **relaxed alertness** — not drowsy, not
   analyzing. Ask: "Are you settled, and can you say the anchor — 'I'm here, I can return and exit
   any time'?"
3. **Hold the image and let it move on its own (Phase 2).** Ask: "Hold the image without analyzing
   or steering it. Just watch — does it start to move, shift, or change by itself? Tell me what it
   does." Do not let them direct it.
4. **Address the figure directly (Phase 3).** When a figure appears: "Ask it, in your own words —
   who are you? what do you want from me? why are you here? — then wait, and tell me what it
   actually says or does. Don't answer for it."
5. **Check dual awareness.** Ask: "Are you still both in the scene and watching yourself in it — or
   does it feel like you've become the figure?" If the observer is gone → exit protocol (`safety.md`).
6. **Run the surprise test.** If the response was predictable, name it: "That sounds like what you'd
   expect — ask again and let it surprise you, or sit in silence until something you didn't author
   comes."
7. **Continue to a natural stop.** Ask: "Does the exchange feel complete, or is there more it wants
   to say?" Don't cut off mid-exchange arbitrarily; don't force it to continue past its own end.
8. **Come back and ground.** Open eyes; brief grounding (feet on the floor, name five objects).
9. **Record immediately (`logging.md`).** Ask them to write or sketch the whole session before it
   fades — figures, what was said, what they felt.
10. **Ethical confrontation (Phase 4).** Ask: "What does this ask of you in waking life? Name one
    concrete thing you'll do or change in response — however small." Pin it.

## 2 · Continuing a recurring dream or nightmare

**When & where it works best:** A recurring dream or nightmare, for a psychologically stable
person — **not** during acute nightmare disorder, and not without clinical oversight if it's
trauma-linked (`clinical-presentations.md`, `safety.md`). The move is to enter the dream **from its
ending point** and let it continue consciously, beyond where it habitually stops.

**Walk it through (one step per turn):**
1. **Find the ending.** Ask: "Where does the dream always stop — what's the last thing that happens
   before you wake or it cuts off?"
2. **Anchor and re-enter.** Do the stance anchor; eyes closed, relaxed alertness. Ask: "Can you
   return to that exact moment and just be there, watching, without forcing what comes next?"
3. **Let it continue (Phase 3).** Ask: "Now let it go on past where it usually ends — don't script
   it, follow it. What happens next? Can you turn and face the pursuer / open the door / meet what's
   there?" Wait for autonomous movement.
4. **Engage what's there.** Ask the figure or force its question as in procedure 1, step 4.
5. **Dual-awareness + surprise checks**, as above. If it floods, exit (`safety.md`).
6. **Natural stop, ground, record, Phase 4**, as in procedure 1, steps 7-10.

## If it doesn't work, try

- **Imagery stalls / nothing comes:** don't force it. Drop to the underlying feeling-state and hold
  *that* — "stay with the tension itself; if you stay long enough, it tends to form an image." Wait.
- **It feels ego-directed (you/they are scripting the figure):** stop entirely. Ask the figure a
  genuinely open question, then fall silent and wait. Predictable = the **domination** failure mode
  (`safety.md`), not progress.
- **Overwhelm / dual awareness destabilizes:** exit immediately via the grounding protocol
  (`safety.md`). Do not "work through" it.
- **Persistent stall over several sessions:** try `writing.md` as a parallel door, or `music.md` as
  a catalyst for stalled imagery. If it keeps stalling, that's a readiness signal — see
  `clinical-vs-self-guided.md`.
