## How to use this file

These are the self-help-safe Compassionate Mind Training (CMT) practices — the
transdiagnostic core, used across almost every problem. They assume the model in
`model.md` (three circles, tricky brain, "not your fault") and follow the ladder there:
SRB before imagery, imagery before self-practice. The condition files
(`shame-self-criticism.md`, `depression.md`, etc.) point back here by name and add only the
specialized sequencing. Deliver every one per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one cue per turn,
invite don't demand, end on a question, wait.** The steps below carry the cue to give at
each step — guide that, don't paste the whole list.

The clinician-only practices (chairwork, feared-compassion work, imagery rescripting) are
**not** here — they live in `advanced-clinician.md`, documented for signposting only.

## 1 · Soothing Rhythm Breathing (SRB)

**Reach for it when:** the foundation, every session, before any imagery or deeper work.
It directly activates the parasympathetic ("rest") system via the breath, and for anyone
with a very loud threat system it comes before everything else. Must be reliably accessible
before you climb the ladder.

**Walk it through** (target ~6 breaths per minute, the body's natural resonance rate):
1. "Sit comfortably, feet flat on the floor. Let your weight settle into the chair."
2. "Let a slightly friendly expression come to your face — just the faintest softening at
   the corners of the mouth." (A real affiliative cue, not a performance.)
3. "Breathe down into the belly, not the chest. Try about 3 seconds in, a small pause, then
   about 3 seconds out — slow and even." (~10-second cycle. A longer out-breath — say 4 in,
   5-6 out — soothes some people more; offer it if the even count doesn't land.)
4. "As you breathe, notice the weight of your body, your feet on the floor."
5. "When the mind wanders — it will — just bring it back, gently, no telling-off."
6. Optional: "If it helps, hold a smooth object and let your attention rest on it."
After a minute or so, check in: "What's the body doing now, compared to when we started?"

**If it stalls / backfires:**
- Breathing triggers panic or dissociation → stop immediately; return to a natural breath;
  ground first with tactile anchors (feet, hands, an object), then try SRB more gradually.
- Breath focus is anxiety-provoking generally → start with box breathing (4 in, hold 4, 4
  out, hold 4) for more cognitive control before SRB.
- History of breathlessness / respiratory illness → gentle abdominal breathing with no
  count target, attention on relaxation rather than rate.
- SRB is fine but imagery still triggers threat → don't advance; consolidate SRB as daily
  practice for a week or two before adding imagery.

## 2 · Safe / Soothing Place imagery

**Reach for it when:** the first imagery rung, right after SRB is established. Builds
soothing-system access through a multi-sensory imagined place. The CFT twist — *the place is
glad you're here* — targets the affiliative system directly and sets it apart from a generic
CBT/EMDR safe place.

**Walk it through:**
1. "Let's settle with about a minute of the breathing first." (Establish SRB.)
2. "Now let an image of a calming place come — real, imagined, or abstract. A beach, a
   forest, a room, a landscape, or just a quality. Don't force it; let it arrive."
3. "What can you feel there? Warmth of sun, texture underfoot, a breeze, a soft surface?"
4. "What can you hear? Waves, a fire, leaves, birds — or quiet?"
5. "Any scent in the air there?"
6. "Let your face soften as you rest in it."
7. "Now, gently — see if you can sense that this place is *glad* you're here, that it
   welcomes you with no conditions." (The reciprocity step. Skip it for trauma histories
   until the place is well-established — it can stir attachment material too soon.)
8. "Rest here a minute or two. When you're ready, come back slowly." Check the felt sense.

**If it stalls / backfires:**
- Vivid pictures are hard → drop the visual; describe what the place *feels / sounds /
  smells* like instead. Imagery is multi-sensory, not just visual.
- Intrusive thoughts enter → acknowledge without judgment and return, as in mindfulness.
- Trauma history → stay in the sensory grounding (step 3) and don't progress to reciprocity
  (step 7) until safeness is solid.
- No safe place can be found at all → drop to **Compassionate Colors** (3) as an easier,
  more abstract on-ramp.

## 3 · Compassionate Colors

**Reach for it when:** an early, abstract imagery warm-up — especially when figurative
imagery (places, people) is too hard or activating. Bypasses evaluation by using pure
sensory engagement.

**Walk it through:**
1. Establish SRB.
2. "Let a color come to mind that feels like compassion or warmth or kindness — don't pick
   it, just notice what arises."
3. "Imagine that color around you, like a gentle light or mist."
4. "On each in-breath, let a little of it enter through the chest, around the heart."
5. "As you breathe out, let it spread gently through the body."
6. "Let the color carry qualities — wisdom, strength, warmth, complete kindness."
7. "Keep that faint friendly expression as you hold it."
8. "And hold in mind that this color is here *for* you — to support and strengthen you."

**If it stalls / backfires:**
- No color comes → start with warmth as a felt sensation (imagine holding something warm, or
  warm light on the face) rather than a visualized color.
- Use it as a bridge to the Safe Place (2) or the Compassionate Other (4) once abstract
  imagery feels comfortable.

## 4 · Compassionate Other / Ideal Compassionate Image

**Reach for it when:** generating the felt experience of being compassionately cared *for* —
the central practice for anyone with little reliable internal model of being genuinely cared
about. Especially powerful for social anxiety ("a felt sense of being listened to without
words"). A bridge to self-compassion: receiving care from the image uses the same machinery.

**Walk it through:**
1. Establish SRB (~60s). "Sit upright and open, let a friendly expression come."
2. "Bring to mind what an *ideal* compassionate figure would be like — someone (or
   something) with: deep wisdom that understands suffering and your difficulties; real
   strength and courage, not coldness — able to be with pain without turning away; a caring
   motivation aimed at *you*, not pity but a genuine wish for you to flourish; and warmth in
   how it is with you."
3. "Let an image form — it can be human (any age, gender), an animal, light or warmth, or
   something symbolic. Don't force a particular figure; let it arise."
4. "Imagine its voice — the tone, the pace. Notice its expression toward you, the effect of
   its presence."
5. "Now let it direct compassion toward you — as warmth, light, a kind gaze — for maybe ten
   seconds. Notice what that does in the body."
6. "Let yourself know: this image will never criticise you, never judge you. Let it gently
   fade, and come back to the breath."

When the user has no positive attachment memories to draw on: begin with abstract warmth
(light, warmth in the chest) rather than a personified figure, and build the image across
several short sittings. Normalize it as a deliberate reparative act — *creating what wasn't
provided*, not a deficit.

**If it stalls / backfires:**
- Imagery activates threat / backdraft → pause; return to SRB; validate that warmth often
  reopens old attachment wounds (expected, not failure); look at what specifically fired;
  start from abstract symbols (warmth, light) rather than a figure; build it slowly across
  sessions. (Deeper FBR work is clinician-only — `safety.md`.)
- No felt response despite understanding → attend to *any* subtle bodily shift, however
  small, and amplify that rather than the picture.
- Psychosis or dissociation present → do **not** use personified imagery; SRB and safe place
  only, very gradually (`psychosis-personality.md`, clinician-guided).

## 5 · Compassionate Self

**Reach for it when:** the "active agent" of CMT — the internalized compassionate identity
used in letter writing and in applying compassion to a specific difficulty. Follows the
imagery rungs (2-4), once there's a felt sense of compassionate qualities to draw on. Used
across every condition.

The four qualities, built one at a time:
- **Wisdom** — understanding life and suffering; that difficulties aren't one's fault; the
  capacity to hold complexity without collapsing.
- **Strength** — confidence and the courage to understand without judgment; tolerating
  hardship. Not coldness or rigidity.
- **Warmth** — genuine kindness toward self and others; a kind expression, a gentle tone.
- **Caring-commitment / responsibility** — commitment to work through difficulties;
  rejecting blame, but not responsibility.

**Walk it through:**
1. Establish SRB.
2. "Let your body take a posture that holds these qualities — upright but not rigid, open
   but grounded."
3. "Let a gently compassionate expression come."
4. "Now, one at a time: recall a moment — any moment, however small — when you showed
   *wisdom*. … a time you showed *strength*. … a time you felt real *warmth*. … a time you
   acted with genuine *care*." (One per turn; draw out their concrete examples.)
5. "Let those qualities gather into a felt sense of a compassionate self — not a perfect
   self, a self genuinely committed to understanding and kindness."
6. "Notice any shift in the body. That's the embodied compassionate self."
7. Useful metaphor: "method acting" — inhabit the character, don't analyse it from outside.

**If it stalls / backfires:**
- "I'm not like that" → they're not pretending to be someone else, but accessing qualities
  all humans have, however unused. Ask for any tiny moment of genuine kindness.
- The identity collapses under distress → drop back to the **Compassionate Other** (4) —
  being cared for *by* an image — before generating it from the inside.
- Feels fraudulent / performative → normalize: the qualities are *practiced*, not performed;
  they grow through repetition, not sincerity.

## 6 · Compassion Flowing Out (to others)

**Reach for it when:** opening the **self → others** flow — usually the least threatening,
and a good bridge for someone who resists self-compassion but can feel kindness toward
others.

**Walk it through:**
1. Establish SRB.
2. "Bring up a specific moment of feeling genuinely kind toward a person or an animal — not
   during a crisis, an ordinary warm moment."
3. "Focus on the wish to help that was there, and the feeling of kindness itself."
4. "Notice any physical warmth, softening, or opening in the body."
5. "Picture yourself becoming a little calmer, wiser, stronger as you hold this kindness."
6. "Rest in a genuine wish for them to be free of suffering."
7. Optional loving-kindness phrases: "May you be well. May you be happy. May you be free
   from suffering."

**If it stalls / backfires:**
- Can't recall any kind moment toward a person → start with an animal, or even a plant —
  any genuine caring. "Other" can be very broad.
- Move to receiving (7) and then self-compassion only once this flow is accessible.

## 7 · Compassion Flowing In (receiving)

**Reach for it when:** the **others → self** flow — receiving care. Often the hardest flow
for high-shame people; approach it only after flowing-out (6) is accessible.

**Walk it through:**
1. "Recall a specific moment of receiving genuine kindness — a quiet, low-stakes one, not a
   crisis."
2. Adopt the compassionate posture and expression from (5).
3. "Spend a moment on the other person's words and tone in that moment."
4. "Now their genuine feeling toward you — their care, their warmth."
5. "Now the whole felt experience of receiving that care. Let it land."
6. "Let any gratitude or warmth grow; keep the soft expression."

For someone with no usable positive memory: use imagined scenes; the **Compassionate Other**
(4) is the bridge — receiving the imagined figure's care is the same mechanism.

**If it stalls / backfires:**
- Tears or distress → not failure; for many people this is the first time they've attended
  to being cared for. SRB to stabilize, then return.
- Can't accept that anyone genuinely cared → gently name it as a fear of *receiving*
  compassion (an FBR); slow down, don't override (`model.md` 4, `safety.md`).

## 8 · Compassionate Letter Writing

**Reach for it when:** extending the compassionate self into daily life, between sessions.
A self-help-safe consolidation used from mid-work onward, once there's some compassionate-
self or compassionate-other to write from.

**Walk it through:**
1. "Settle with SRB and connect with the compassionate self (or the compassionate figure)
   before you start."
2. "Write a letter *from* that compassionate part *to* the part of you that's struggling."
3. "Begin with validation — name what's hard, and why it makes sense given your history."
4. "Express understanding of where it comes from: 'Of course you feel this way — here's
   why…'."
5. "Offer gentle encouragement toward what matters — what would moving through this with
   kindness look like?"
6. "Keep the tone warm and supportive. No 'shoulds', no 'why don't you', no advice-giving —
   the compassionate voice encourages, it doesn't instruct."
7. "Read it back, aloud if you can. Keep it, and re-read it in hard moments."

When resistance is present: write as if from a trusted other (a mentor, a fictional
compassionate figure) rather than from inside, and move toward an internal voice over time.

**If it stalls / backfires:**
- The writing turns self-critical or advice-giving → read it aloud, notice the tone, return
  to the **Compassionate Self** (5) to re-establish the quality, then resume.
- Writing activates rather than soothes → speak the letter into a recording instead, or keep
  it very short.

## 9 · Compassionate Mindfulness

**Reach for it when:** an early rung and a steady companion throughout — bringing *kind,
warm* attention to experience rather than neutral observation. The point isn't detachment;
it's building the compassionate stance the imagery work then operates from.

**Walk it through** (use any standard practice — mindful breathing, leaves on a stream,
mindful walking — but with CFT's warmer framing): guide it one cue at a time, and where a
standard script says "observe without judgment", say instead **"attend to this with
kindness, with warmth."** When the mind wanders, the return itself is done gently — that
*is* the practice, and doing it without self-criticism is the skill.

**If it stalls / backfires:**
- Standard mindfulness is triggering (common in trauma) → don't start with formal breath
  mindfulness. SRB first, then safe place, then very brief (1-2 min) mindfulness with a
  clear exit.
- Self-criticism about "being bad at it" → use it directly: redirecting *without* self-
  attack is the whole point.

## 10 · Compassionate Kitbag

**Reach for it when:** very high threat reactivity, where standard imagery floods the person.
Physical objects with soothing sensory qualities act as anchors that carry a soothing state
out of the session — a body-first bridge before imagery or cognitive work is possible.
Developed for personality-disorder and complex-trauma populations
(`psychosis-personality.md`), but useful for anyone who can't yet hold an image.

**Walk it through:**
1. Together, identify small physical objects with soothing sensory properties — a texture, a
   scent, a temperature, a weight.
2. Pair each object with a soothing or compassionate state during practice (hold the object
   while doing SRB or safe place), so the object and the state link up.
3. The user carries the objects between sessions.
4. When threat fires, the object is a physical bridge back toward the soothing state —
   engage the body through the object *before* attempting imagery or thinking.

**If it stalls / backfires:** if even the object can't shift a flooded state, return to the
most basic grounding (feet on floor, naming five things in the room) and SRB; build the
object-state pairing slowly over several sittings.
