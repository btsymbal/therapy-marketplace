## How to use this file

The techniques in this skill are not a standalone toolkit — **they are expressions of this
model.** Getting the model across (warmly, briefly, conversationally) is the de-shaming
move that makes every later practice possible, and it is the universal entry to every
session before any technique. Deliver it the way you deliver everything here
(`walkthrough-protocol.md`): not as a lecture, but built bottom-up from the user's own
example, a piece at a time, checking it lands. You rarely need all of it in one session —
use the parts that de-shame *their* specific difficulty.

## 1 · Three emotion systems (the three circles)

**Reach for it when:** the start of almost any piece of work; whenever the user treats
their distress as evidence they're broken or defective rather than a system doing its job.

The human brain runs three evolved emotion systems, each built for a different purpose:

1. **Threat / protection system** — fast survival responses: anxiety, anger, disgust,
   fight / flight / freeze / submit, and the negativity bias. Built to protect us. It
   can't easily tell a real threat from an imagined one, or the past from the present —
   which is why a memory or a self-critical thought sets it off as if the danger were here
   now.
2. **Drive / seeking system** — excitement, wanting, achieving, pursuing rewards.
   Essential and energising, but without a counterbalance it tips into perfectionism,
   compulsive overwork, or addiction.
3. **Soothing / contentment / affiliative system** — peacefulness, connectedness, and a
   sense of **safeness** (different from "safety", which is vigilant monitoring for the
   absence of threat). This is the brain's natural threat regulator. It's activated not by
   achievement but by *affiliative cues*: warmth, kindness, a gentle voice, a kind face —
   from others, and eventually from oneself.

**The core idea:** psychological distress builds when the threat system is chronically
*over*active and the soothing system is chronically *under*active — often because early
life provided little experience of being genuinely soothed. CFT's whole aim is to **build
the soothing system** to a functional level — not to suppress threat or pump up drive.

**Walk it through:** draw or describe the three circles with the user, then map *their*
current state onto them. Ask: "If these are your three systems right now, which is big and
loud, which has shrunk?" Almost always the threat circle is large and the soothing circle
small. Land the point: this isn't a character flaw, it's an imbalance we can rebalance with
practice. The work ahead is growing that third circle.

**If it stalls:** if the abstract model doesn't land, build it from their own moment —
"when your boss criticised the report, which system fired?" — and skip the diagram.

## 2 · The tricky brain and "not your fault"

**Reach for it when:** the user is ashamed of their own mind — of ruminating, catastrophising,
self-attacking, replaying humiliations.

Our "new brain" (imagination, planning, self-awareness, the ability to think about
ourselves) sits on top of an "old brain" of basic emotions and drives we share with other
animals. The combination creates loops no other species suffers: we can ruminate about
threats that aren't here, imagine future catastrophes, and build self-critical narratives —
and the old-brain threat system reacts to all of it as if it were real. **We didn't design
this brain, and we didn't choose the early environment that shaped it.**

This is the central CFT move: **de-shaming.** "The way your mind works — the loops, the
self-attack — is a product of evolution, your genes, and experiences you didn't pick.
*This is not your fault.*" It is not an excuse (you're still responsible for working on
your difficulties), but it lifts the second layer of suffering: the shame about having the
difficulty in the first place.

**Walk it through:** name the specific loop they're caught in, then frame it
evolutionarily: "Of course your mind does this — it's a tricky brain none of us designed.
Given your history, it makes complete sense it learned to work this way." Watch their
response: relief means it landed; "but it *is* my fault" is itself the self-critic and a
cue toward `shame-self-criticism.md`.

**If it stalls:** if "not your fault" is rejected outright, don't argue it — that rejection
is often a fear/block/resistance (see 4) and a sign to slow down, not to push.

## 3 · Compassion: two psychologies, three flows

**Reach for it when:** the user equates compassion with weakness, softness, or letting
themselves off the hook.

CFT defines compassion as two things together: (1) **sensitivity** to suffering — in self
and others; (2) a **commitment** to do something about it. So compassion is not softness or
avoidance — it requires *courage* (to turn toward pain) and *wisdom* (to know what helps).
This reframe matters for clients who think self-compassion means going easy on themselves.

Compassion flows in three directions, each cultivated separately:
1. **Self → others** — giving compassion to other people.
2. **Others → self** — receiving compassion from others.
3. **Self → self** — self-compassion.

A person can be blocked in any or all three. For most high-shame people, **self-compassion
is the most blocked flow** and is best approached *last*, after the other two have opened a
little. Don't push self-compassion on someone who can't yet give it to others or receive it.

**Walk it through:** ask which flow feels possible and which feels impossible. "Can you feel
warmth toward someone you care about? Can you let someone's care land on you? Can you turn
any of that toward yourself?" The answers route the three-flows work in `core-cmt.md` (6-8).

## 4 · Fears, blocks, and resistances (FBRs)

**Reach for it when:** compassion practices fall flat, or the user actively dismisses or
avoids warmth.

The soothing system is wired to early attachment. If warmth and closeness were historically
tied to pain, abuse, or unavailability, then **affiliative cues themselves become threat
signals** — and offering compassion to such a person can trigger "backdraft": a surge of
grief, fear, anger, or numbness. This is not resistance to treatment; it *is* the treatment
target, and it's expected.

Three forms:
- **Fears** — "compassion is weakness", "self-compassion is self-indulgent", "if I open up
  it'll overwhelm me".
- **Blocks** — simply can't access the compassionate feeling in the moment, even wanting to.
- **Resistances** — "I don't see the point; I don't deserve it."

**The key stance:** an FBR has *wisdom* in it — it was a protective strategy that made sense
in context. You de-shame it ("of course warmth feels dangerous, given what it cost you"),
you don't override it. A fear of compassion can't be argued away with more compassion —
that's the paradox. In self-help you **notice, name, normalize, and slow down**; the deeper
FBR *intervention* is clinician-guided (`safety.md`, `advanced-clinician.md`).

**Walk it through:** when warmth triggers threat, say so plainly and kindly: "Something in
you flinched at that — that's really common, and it's not a failure. Can we slow down and be
curious about it?" Then step down the ladder.

## 5 · The ladder of compassion

**Reach for it when:** sequencing any session — deciding what to do first and what is too
soon.

CMT practices are ordered from most accessible to most challenging, and the order is a
clinical rule, not a suggestion:

1. Noticing moments of compassion in daily life
2. Mindfulness
3. **Soothing Rhythm Breathing (SRB)**
4. Safe / soothing place imagery
5. Compassionate other / ideal compassionate image
6. Compassionate self
7. Letter writing and (clinician-only) chairwork
8. (Clinician-only) imagery rescripting for trauma

**SRB must be reliably established before imagery; imagery before self-practice.** Each rung
is a prerequisite for the next. On a hard day, the user steps *down* a rung rather than
pushing through — returning to SRB is success, not regression. Don't rush someone up the
ladder to reach a "better" technique; a well-established lower rung beats a flooded higher
one.

**Walk it through:** locate where the user reliably is, work one rung above it, and name the
ladder out loud so stepping down later doesn't feel like failure: "If this gets to be too
much, we just drop back to the breathing — that's not going backwards, that's how this
works."
