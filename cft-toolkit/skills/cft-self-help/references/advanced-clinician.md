## How to use this file

These three are core CFT techniques, but they are **clinician-guided** — designed to be
delivered by a trained CFT therapist, in person, with stabilization protocols, because they
can produce overwhelming distress, dissociation, or fragmentation. **Do not walk the user
through them as self-help.** This file exists so you can (a) recognise when a problem is
pointing toward them, (b) explain *what* they are and *why* they help, in plain warm
language, so the user understands the path ahead, and (c) signpost to a CFT-trained
clinician while offering the safe adjacent CMT practice instead. This mirrors how the CBT
sibling skill handles ERP and trauma exposure: psychoeducation and framing yes, running the
protocol no. Keep `safety.md` live throughout.

When a session reaches one of these, the move is the same: name it warmly, say plainly that
the full work wants a trained clinician (and why — the therapeutic relationship and skilled
pacing are part of the mechanism), offer the lower-risk CMT step from `core-cmt.md`, and, if
useful, note it as a goal to raise with a therapist. Record it in the log and `CLAUDE.md`.

## 1 · Chairwork (multiple-selves dialogue)

**What it is:** a structured experiential technique using physical chairs to embody and let
the internal "parts" speak to each other — typically the self-critic, the vulnerable /
struggling self, and the compassionate self. The best-known form is the **three-chair
self-criticism** dialogue: voice the criticism aloud from a critic chair; move to a chair and
*receive* it from inside (the "shock of objectification" where self-compassion often becomes
accessible); then move to a compassionate-self chair and address both — not to silence the
critic but to understand its protective fears with warmth. There are also two-chair
self-compassion and broader multiple-selves versions.

**Why it helps:** switching chairs produces *embodied* shifts, not just intellectual
perspective-taking. Hearing one's own self-criticism as if aimed at another person reliably
activates self-compassion in a way that talking about it does not.

**Why it's clinician-only:** it can produce overwhelming distress; people may dissociate or
fragment; when the critic is an internalized *abusive* voice (not ordinary self-criticism)
it must be differentiated and handled with trauma-informed care, not empathy. All of this
needs a trained therapist and in-session stabilization.

**What to offer instead in self-help:** the **Compassionate Self** and **Compassionate
Other** (`core-cmt.md` 4-5) reach much of the same territory safely — building the
compassionate voice and letting it relate to the struggling part — and **Compassionate
Letter Writing** (`core-cmt.md` 8) lets the compassionate self address the struggling self
on paper, a gentle, contained cousin of chairwork. If the self-critic is the core problem,
work `shame-self-criticism.md` and name chairwork as a goal for therapy.

## 2 · Feared-compassion work (FBR intervention)

**What it is:** direct, structured work on the fears, blocks, and resistances to compassion
(FBRs) — when warmth itself reads as a threat. The clinical intervention has four interwoven
parts: a **formulation** of where the FBR came from (when did warmth first become
dangerous?); **psychoeducation** ("your brain learned warmth signals danger — not your
fault"); **graduated experiential exposure** to compassion as a feared stimulus, starting
with the least threatening flow (to others) and moving slowly toward self-compassion via
imagery, chairwork, and letters; and **respecting the wisdom of the FBR** — validating and
de-shaming it rather than trying to eliminate it.

**Why it helps:** for high-shame, complex-trauma, and personality-disorder presentations the
FBR *is* the maintenance factor. A fear of compassion can't be overridden by more
compassionate encouragement — that's the paradox — so it has to be approached as graduated
exposure, at the person's pace.

**Why it's clinician-only:** it deliberately engages the origins of the threat response and
manages "backdraft" (stored grief, fear, or rage surfacing as warmth enters) — exposure-
shaped work that needs a therapist to hold and pace.

**What to offer instead in self-help:** the self-help layer of FBR work is exactly what this
skill already does — **notice, name, normalize, and slow down** (`model.md` 4,
`walkthrough-protocol.md`). When warmth triggers threat, say so kindly, validate the wisdom
in it, and **step down the ladder**, working the least-threatening flow (compassion to
others, `core-cmt.md` 6) before receiving or self-compassion. Don't push. Name the deeper
FBR work as something a CFT clinician can do.

## 3 · Shame-memory imagery rescripting

**What it is:** revisiting a traumatic shame memory and bringing the adult *compassionate
self* into the scene to give the younger self the protection, validation, or rescue that
was missing — a three-stage process (relive from the younger self's view; observe as an
adult; re-enter with the compassionate self present and intervening), sometimes paired with
compassionate dialogue toward the "child self".

**Why it helps:** for memories that keep generating present-day shame and self-blame, this
can change the felt meaning of the memory in a way that talking about it cannot.

**Why it's clinician-only:** it involves deliberate engagement with traumatic material and
requires the compassionate self to be robustly established *first*; if the adult self can't
hold a compassionate stance it can re-traumatize. The evidence base specific to CFT is very
limited (a single published case study as of 2026). This needs a CFT-trained clinician.

**What to offer instead in self-help:** stabilization only — **SRB** and **safe / soothing
place** (`core-cmt.md` 1-2) to build the capacity to settle, plus the de-shaming
psychoeducation (`model.md`, and trauma-specific framing in `trauma-ptsd.md`). Building a
reliable compassionate self (`core-cmt.md` 5) is itself the preparation a clinician would
want in place. Name rescripting as a goal for trauma-focused CFT with a therapist; do not
guide the user into the memory.
