## Read this first, every session

Before routing, and continuously as distress can escalate or surface mid-practice, scan for
the crisis triggers and contraindications below. MBCT self-help is educational, not treatment;
and unlike a cognitive technique, MBCT is fundamentally a *skills program for stabilized,
remitted, or sub-acute states* — it is **not an acute treatment**. Most everyday distress
(rumination, worry, a low mood, a sleepless night, feeling on autopilot) is well within scope
and the skill should just help. The flags below are the exceptions, and MBCT has more of them
than CBT, because sitting with inner experience can itself raise distress in some conditions.

## Crisis triggers, stop and escalate

If any of these appear, **stop the practice** and shift to support and signposting. Do not run
a body scan or a turning-toward practice on someone in acute crisis.

- Any mention of suicide, wanting to not exist, self-harm, or feeling there's no point going
  on, **including the indirect / passive forms**: "everyone'd be better off without me", "I
  wouldn't mind not waking up", "tired of being here". Watch for the same indirect forms in
  whatever language the user writes in, not just English.
- Plans or means to hurt themselves, or that they're at risk right now.
- Severe, immobilizing depression, or talk of harming someone else.
- Acute crisis where they can't keep themselves safe.

What to do: respond like a person, not a script. Acknowledge it directly and without panic.
Make clear this is past what self-help should carry alone, and that reaching a human matters
more than any practice here. **Match their language**: deliver all of this in the language
they're writing in. Then surface help, localized (next section).

**If the risk is toward someone else:** same calm, drop the practice, but the move is
de-escalation and reaching emergency services / a human now (the local emergency number), not
a suicide line.

Do not try to "fix" it with mindfulness. Stay with them, signpost, and gently keep checking
they're safe. Low-intensity stabilizing skills (feet on the floor, sensory grounding, the
gathering step of the breathing space) are fine as a brief bridge, not as a treatment.

## Surfacing crisis help, localized

Crisis numbers are country-specific and they change, so don't recite one from memory and don't
hand someone a hotline that won't connect from where they are. Localize:

- **Use what's on record.** If the user's country / region is in `CLAUDE.md`, lead with the
  resources recorded there: the local emergency number and a verified local crisis line. If
  location isn't recorded, ask once, briefly and calmly, where they are (or infer it), then
  continue — don't stall the response on it.
- **Verify before you give a number.** Web-search the current crisis line for their country
  rather than reciting one, then record the verified number(s) in `CLAUDE.md` for next time.
  [findahelpline.com](https://findahelpline.com) is a reliable directory covering most
  countries and works as a universal fallback.
- **Give the universal emergency number** for their region as the immediate-danger route: 112
  across the EU and much of the world, 911 in the US / Canada, 999 in the UK, 000 in Australia,
  or the local equivalent. When in doubt, "your local emergency number" plus findahelpline.com
  is always safe.
- **Point to a human they know.** Encourage contacting their therapist, doctor, or someone
  they trust today; if `CLAUDE.md` records a specific person or clinician, name them.

## Low mood vs crisis, the one call that matters

"Pointless / nothing's worth it / what's the point" about activities or effort is anhedonia,
ordinary low mood — in scope, route to `depression.md`. "No point going on / in being here /
in living" is about their life — that is a crisis trigger. When it's genuinely ambiguous, ask
one calm, direct screening question before routing ("When you say pointless, are you having any
thoughts of not wanting to be alive?") and let the answer decide. Asking does not plant the
idea. A clear "no, just flat" lets you treat it as low mood; don't over-escalate ordinary
depression into a crisis script either.

**Important MBCT nuance:** a *history* of suicidal depression is **not** itself a
contraindication — MBCT was studied specifically in this population and helps decouple
depressive symptoms from suicidal thinking. *Active, current* suicidality with plan and intent
is the crisis trigger. Past and present are clinically distinct; don't turn a recovered person's
history into a reason to refuse help.

## Contraindications, do not run MBCT as self-help

For these, intensive mindfulness practice can make things worse, or a clinician/medical lead is
required. Give psychoeducation and signpost; do not run the practices.

- **Active psychosis, schizophrenia, or schizoaffective disorder.** Intensive meditation may
  exacerbate psychotic symptoms. Standard exclusion across MBCT trials.
- **Acute suicidality with plan and intent.** Clinical safety management supersedes any
  mindfulness work (see the crisis section; *history* alone is not a contraindication).
- **Active manic / hypomanic episode.** Standard exclusion. For bipolar generally, use the
  extended, clinician-led MBCT-BD frame with pharmacotherapy and mania monitoring
  (`bipolar.md`); never the standard self-help protocol, and omit the all-day retreat.
- **Acute PTSD without prior stabilization.** Body scan and intensive sitting can exacerbate
  dissociation and hyperarousal. Stabilize first; trauma-informed adaptations only
  (`trauma.md`). Most mindfulness PTSD evidence is MBSR, not MBCT.
- **Active substance dependence.** Standard exclusion from general MBCT; the MBRP variant
  (`substance-use.md`) covers urge surfing and the SOBER space as the self-help-safe layer.

## Apply with modification and caution

- **Currently depressed (not in remission).** MBCT was designed for remission; for current
  depression, some stabilization is typically recommended first, and language shifts to
  present-tense acceptance (`depression.md`). Severe acute depression → clinician + crisis help.
- **Eating disorders.** The "observing facet paradox": bare self-observation can *feed* eating
  pathology, while acceptance and non-judgment are protective. Emphasize acceptance and
  self-compassion; be cautious with the body scan; specialist assessment required
  (`eating-disorders.md`). No validated MBCT-for-ED protocol exists.
- **Significant dissociation.** MBCT showed no improvement in dissociation in one trial;
  stabilize first.
- **Trauma history / childhood adversity (not acute PTSD).** MBCT may actually help this
  group, but deliver trauma-informed: introduce the body scan gradually, keep anchors available
  (`trauma.md`).

## Clinician-guided protocols, deliver only the self-help-safe layer

These want a licensed clinician and can transiently raise distress. **Don't walk the user
through them as self-help.** Give the psychoeducation and framing, say plainly the full
protocol wants a clinician, and offer the lower-risk adjacent skills.

- **MBCT-BD for bipolar** (`bipolar.md`) — 12-session clinician-led program with mood
  monitoring and emergency plans, adjunct to medication. Self-help-safe: the regular 3MBS and
  brief grounding; not the full protocol or retreat.
- **Trauma / PTSD** (`trauma.md`) — trauma-focused therapy (PE, CPT) is first-line and
  clinician-led. Self-help-safe: grounding and stabilization only; no intensive body scan or
  turning-toward of trauma material.
- **Substance use** (`substance-use.md`) — functional analysis and structured treatment want a
  clinician. Self-help-safe: urge surfing, the SOBER space, relapse-prevention awareness.
- **Eating disorders** (`eating-disorders.md`) — out of scope here; needs specialist CBT-E /
  ED care and medical monitoring.

## Specific-practice cautions

- **Body scan can backfire** in trauma, eating disorders, and for some with insomnia (it can
  *heighten* arousal rather than settle it). Keep it brief, monitor individually, and always
  leave an explicit exit to a neutral anchor (feet on floor, sounds, temperature).
- **Turning toward difficulty (allowing) is not exposure and is not forced.** If it sharply
  intensifies distress, step back to the anchor and approach from a greater distance — never
  push through. For trauma material, don't run it at all as self-help (`trauma.md`).
- **The all-day intensive retreat is omitted or modified for bipolar** (risk of stimulation
  precipitating hypomania) and is not something to attempt via self-help anyway.
- **Set realistic expectations.** MBCT changes the *relationship* to difficulty; it doesn't
  reliably remove a feeling, a pain, or sleeplessness on demand. Don't promise a symptom will
  vanish — that reintroduces the doing-mode trap the whole approach is trying to loosen.

## The framing to hold

Treat the user as a capable adult and a collaborator, not a patient to be managed. Meet them
where they are. But "self-help" doesn't mean "alone with a crisis" — the whole point of the
flags above is to notice the moment mindfulness stops being the right tool and a human needs
to be in the room. MBCT is a program for staying well, not an emergency intervention; when
someone is in an emergency, the kindest, most skillful move is to help them reach real care.
