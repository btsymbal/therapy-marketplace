## How to use this file

The anxiety layer, covering the four presentations where MBCT has been adapted or studied: GAD,
panic, social anxiety, and health anxiety. It assumes the 15 core practices in
`core-practices.md` and adds the anxiety-specific emphasis. The unifying MBCT move across all of
them: meet anxious thoughts and feared body sensations as **mental and physical events to
observe**, not problems to immediately solve, suppress, or escape. Deliver every practice per
`walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one cue per turn, end on an invitation, wait.** **Note:** for some
anxiety presentations CBT remains first-line (social anxiety especially); MBCT is positioned as
an adjunct or alternative — say so honestly when relevant. If a panic or worry attack is live,
**anchor first** with the responsive 3MBS (1.9) before any other work.

## 1 · Generalized anxiety / worry (MBCT-A)

**Reach for it when:** chronic worry, "what if" spirals, a mind that won't switch off, racing
over a problem. MBCT-A is non-inferior to group CBT for GAD in a recent RCT.

**The frame:** worry is doing mode applied to an uncertain future — the mind trying to *solve*
its way to safety, which spins the spiral tighter. The MBCT alternative is to see worrying as a
**mental activity** ("worrying is happening") rather than as urgent problem-solving that must be
done now.

**Walk it through:**
1. When a worry cycle is detected, run the **responsive 3MBS** (`core-practices.md`, 1.9) as the
   immediate interrupt. Key cue: "Notice the worry as a mental event — not a problem that needs
   solving this second."
2. Apply **turning toward** (1.13) to the worry: observe it as mental activity without
   *suppressing* it (suppression rebounds) and without *acting on it* (which feeds the cycle).
   "Can we watch the worrying, rather than do the worrying?"
3. For the self-critical edge of GAD ("I should be able to handle this"), add brief
   **loving-kindness** (see `depression.md`, 5).
4. Over time, identify personal anxiety warning signs and build them into a relapse-style
   action plan (1.15).

**If it stalls:** the worry is about a *real, solvable* problem → mindfulness is the wrong tool;
name it and signpost problem-solving (`model.md`). MBCT-A isn't moving it and CBT is available →
CBT (worry postponement, decatastrophizing, intolerance-of-uncertainty work) is well-evidenced
as the fallback.

## 2 · Panic

**Reach for it when:** sudden fear surges, pounding heart, breathlessness, catastrophic
interpretations of body sensations ("I'm having a heart attack / losing control"). MBCT has shown
anxiety reduction in panic RCTs, most often alongside medication.

**The frame:** in panic the *body sensations* are the trigger. The MBCT move is to build
tolerance for and curiosity about those sensations — observing them without immediately
catastrophizing — rather than fighting or fleeing them. **Note:** MBCT does *not* use
interoceptive exposure (deliberately provoking panic sensations); its route is gradual approach
through body scan and allowing, working via acceptance and decentering, not habituation.

**Walk it through:**
1. **Responsive 3MBS as panic interrupt** (`core-practices.md`, 1.9): Step 1 names the physical
   sensations *without* catastrophizing ("racing heart, tight chest — that's what's here"); Step
   2 anchors in the breath; Step 3 expands awareness to put the sensation in context.
2. **Body scan (1.2) + allowing (1.13)** for the interoceptive fear, framed explicitly as
   building curiosity about sensations — *not* as relaxation, and not as a trick to stop the
   panic.
3. **Decentering from panic thoughts** (1.5): "this racing heart means I'm dying" treated as a
   thought to observe, a transient mental event, not an emergency signal.

**Important caution:** don't let slow breathing become a **safety behaviour** used *to stop* a
panic attack — that teaches "I only survived because I breathed" and undermines the learning.
Keep the breath a general anchor, not an anti-panic crutch (`safety.md`).

**If it stalls:** panic is treatment-resistant → panic-specific CBT should be considered. Acute
and overwhelming → anchor on sensory contact (feet, chair) first; if the user can't keep safe,
`safety.md`.

## 3 · Social anxiety

**Reach for it when:** anxiety before/during social situations, fear of judgement, replaying
what they said afterwards. **Be honest:** CBT (attention-shifting, video feedback, behavioral
experiments) remains first-line for social anxiety and is equal to or more effective than
mindfulness-based approaches; position MBCT as an adjunct or an option when CBT is unavailable or
not preferred.

**Walk it through:**
1. Apply **sounds and thoughts as mental events** (`core-practices.md`, 1.5) to self-critical
   social cognitions ("they think I'm boring", "I look visibly anxious") — observing them as
   mental events, *not* challenging or disputing them.
2. For post-event rumination (replaying the conversation), treat the replaying itself as a
   mental activity to notice and step out of via the responsive 3MBS (1.9), rather than a review
   that must be completed.

**If it stalls:** CBT is accessible and the user wants the most effective route → signpost it
(or the cbt-toolkit). The self-criticism is the engine → add loving-kindness (`depression.md`, 5).

## 4 · Health anxiety (MBCT-HA)

**Reach for it when:** fixation on body symptoms, catastrophic interpretation ("this pain means
cancer"), checking, reassurance-seeking, health-googling. MBCT-HA has a detailed published
protocol; all core practices are retained.

**The frame:** health anxiety is maintained by a loop — a sensation → catastrophic interpretation
→ anxiety → attention narrows onto the body → the sensation amplifies → checking/reassurance →
brief relief → heightened sensitivity → more symptoms noticed. The MBCT move develops **curiosity
toward** feared sensations rather than avoidance, and separates the raw sensation from the
catastrophic story.

**Walk it through:**
1. Map the maintenance loop with them (psychoeducation), using one recent episode.
2. Frame **body scan (1.2) and sitting (1.4)** explicitly as developing curiosity toward feared
   sensations rather than scanning-for-danger.
3. The core skill, when a distressing sensation arises: pause → **responsive 3MBS** (1.9) → in
   the expanded awareness of Step 3, gently bring attention to the sensation *itself* (the raw
   pressure, ache, tingling) **and** notice the story that follows it. Hold both at once: "Notice
   the sensation, and notice the thought *about* it — and that they're two different things."
4. Treat checking and reassurance-seeking not via forced response-prevention (that's CBT) but
   through **mindful noticing of the urge to check** and practising sitting with uncertainty.

**If it stalls:** the urge to check is overpowering → don't force prevention; notice the urge as
an urge (like urge surfing, `substance-use.md`) and let it crest. Persistent and impairing →
signpost formal CBT for health anxiety.
