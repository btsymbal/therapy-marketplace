## How to use this file

This is the conceptual core of MBCT, and in MBCT the stance *is* the intervention — without
it the practices are just relaxation exercises. Read it at the start of every session. You
rarely teach it as a block; more often you use it as a lens to name what's happening in the
moment ("notice — that's the mind in fixing mode", "that's a thought, not a fact, can we
watch it instead of follow it?"). Keep any explicit framing short and conversational, two or
three sentences, then return to practice. The model precedes and enables every technique in
`core-practices.md`.

## Automatic pilot

Most mental life runs on **automatic pilot** — habitual, mindless processing without
deliberate awareness. We eat, walk, and react while absorbed in thought, missing what's
actually happening. Autopilot is where rumination and worry run unchecked, and where a small
mood dip can trigger a cascade of old depressive thinking before we even notice. Disrupting
autopilot — *noticing* that we've been somewhere else — is the explicit first target of MBCT
and the foundation everything else rests on. Each moment of "oh, I'd drifted" is a moment of
waking up, and the whole skill is built on repeating it.

## Being mode vs doing mode

MBCT distinguishes two modes of mind (Barnard & Teasdale's Interacting Cognitive Subsystems
model):

- **Doing mode** is goal-directed, driven by the gap between how things are and how they
  "should" be. It's indispensable for practical problems — but turned on inner states it
  becomes the engine of suffering: trying to *fix* low mood by analyzing its causes and
  imagining better states is rumination, which maintains and deepens the very mood it's
  trying to solve. The harder the mind works the problem, the tighter the trap.
- **Being mode** is allowing and observing present experience without evaluation or the
  pressure to change it. It is not passive resignation — it's a different *quality* of
  attention: open, curious, non-striving, present.

Every MBCT practice either **builds the capacity for being mode** (the meditations) or
**applies being mode to difficult internal states** (allowing, turning toward). The 3-Minute
Breathing Space is the portable bridge between the two. The clinical skill is spotting when a
user is stuck in doing mode about something doing mode can't solve (a feeling, a worry, a
sleepless night) and inviting the shift.

## Decentering: thoughts are not facts

**Decentering** (or metacognitive insight) is the ability to experience thoughts, feelings,
and sensations as **transient mental events** passing through awareness — not as facts, not
as truths about reality, not as self-definitions. The key distinction (Teasdale): it's one
thing to *know* intellectually that a thought might be inaccurate; it's another to *experience*
the thought as just a thought, arising and passing like a sound or a cloud. MBCT trains the
second. "I'm a failure" stops being a verdict to argue with and becomes an event to observe:
*"there's the thought 'I'm a failure' — present, painful, and not a fact."* Decentering has
three strands: non-identification with thoughts, non-habitual reacting, and self-compassion.
It is the single most important shift this skill aims for.

## The differential activation hypothesis (why mood matters)

In people who have had multiple depressive episodes, even **mild low mood can reactivate
the full pattern** of depressive thinking — the hopelessness, self-criticism, and rumination
that accompanied past episodes get re-triggered by small dips, automatically, on autopilot.
This is why MBCT targets the *relationship* to thoughts rather than their content: in a low
mood the content will keep regenerating, but a decentered stance breaks the link between a
passing dip and a full relapse. It's also why relapse-prevention work centers on recognizing
one's personal early-warning signs (the relapse signature) before the cascade runs.

## The four mechanisms

MBCT's effects are carried by four well-supported pathways. Knowing which one a practice
builds helps you choose:

| Mechanism | Built by |
|---|---|
| **Decentering** / metacognitive insight | breath & sounds-and-thoughts sitting, choiceless awareness, allowing, inquiry, responsive 3MBS |
| **Self-compassion** | loving-kindness, allowing, the kind/curious stance of inquiry, nourishing activities |
| **Mindfulness capacity** (attentional control) | body scan, mindful movement, all sitting practices, walking |
| **Reduced rumination / worry** | regular 3MBS, nourishing activities, relapse-signature awareness |

The effect is multi-pathway — don't reduce it to "mindfulness lowers rumination" (the
evidence on that single chain is mixed). Self-compassion in particular *decouples* a low mood
from relapse even when reactivity is unchanged.

## Why the sequencing works

The order of practices is deliberate (see SKILL.md "The sequence"):

- The **body scan is taught first** because it needs no prior experience, can be done lying
  down, and builds basic attentional capacity without asking the person to sit with difficult
  thoughts yet.
- **Breath and movement** extend the anchor; **sounds and thoughts** then introduce
  decentering explicitly (watch a thought arise and pass like a sound); **choiceless
  awareness** is the most open practice and needs the most prior stability.
- **Mood work** (allowing, turning toward difficulty, "thoughts are not facts") is placed
  *after* the attention-training phase, because decentering from hard material requires a
  stable attentional base first. Don't skip a distressed user straight to turning-toward
  before an anchor is reliable.
- The **3-Minute Breathing Space** is introduced once foundational practices exist, because
  it compresses them into a portable three-minute bridge.

## How MBCT differs from CBT

Standard CBT examines and restructures the *content* of thoughts (is this thought accurate?
what's the evidence?). MBCT deliberately does **not** dispute content — it changes the
*relationship* to thoughts (can I see this as a passing mental event rather than a fact?).
If you find yourself building an evidence-for/evidence-against case, you've drifted into CBT;
in this skill, the move is to *observe* the thought, not litigate it. (When a thought reflects
a real, solvable problem rather than a mood-driven distortion, mindfulness isn't the tool —
say so and signpost problem-solving or the cbt-toolkit.) MBCT also borrows one behavioral
element from CBT — activity scheduling — but reframes it as bringing mindful awareness to what
actually nourishes vs depletes (`core-practices.md`, 1.14).

## The framing to hold

Treat the user as a capable adult and a collaborator. The teacher in MBCT models the same
non-judgmental curiosity they're inviting — so embody it: unhurried, accepting, not fixing.
"Self-help" doesn't mean "alone with a crisis": the flags in `safety.md` mark where mindfulness
stops being the right tool and a human needs to be in the room. And mindfulness is not a
relaxation technique or a way to empty the mind — it's a way of relating to a mind that will
keep producing thoughts. Set that expectation early and it saves the user a lot of "I'm bad
at this."
