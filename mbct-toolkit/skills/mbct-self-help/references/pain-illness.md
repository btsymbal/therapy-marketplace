## How to use this file

The pain-and-illness layer: chronic pain and the fear of cancer recurrence. Both share MBCT's
defining move for somatic distress — **changing the relationship to the experience rather than
trying to control or eliminate it.** It assumes the 15 core practices in `core-practices.md`.
Deliver per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one cue per turn, end on an invitation, wait.**
**Realistic expectations matter here:** MBCT for pain helps mood, disability, and catastrophizing
more than raw pain intensity; for cancer it targets fear and rumination. Never promise a sensation
will vanish — that reinstates the doing-mode struggle the approach is loosening (`safety.md`).

## 1 · Chronic pain (MBCT-CP)

**Reach for it when:** persistent pain is dragging mood down, driving boom-and-bust activity, or
fuelling catastrophizing about the body. Small effects on pain intensity, medium effects on
quality of life; pain catastrophizing is the mediator.

**The frame (state it plainly):** unlike CBT for pain (which reappraises pain cognitions), MBCT-CP
aims to change the *relationship* to the experience. The body scan here is explicitly **not a
relaxation exercise** and **not an attempt to reduce the pain** — it's observing what's present.

**Walk it through:**
1. **Body scan (1.2) for pain:** use alternative positions as needed; guide observing the painful
   area with curiosity *from a respectful distance* rather than forcing attention into it. The
   cue: "You're not trying to change the pain — just noticing what's actually here, including the
   areas that feel fine."
2. **Allowing (1.13) applied to pain:** turn toward the pain sensations, observe their qualities
   and edges, notice whether they shift as attended to, practice non-fighting — "not 'I accept
   this is permanent', just 'allowing this sensation to be here right now'."
3. **Decentering from pain thoughts** (1.5): "this will never get better", "I can't cope" treated
   as mental events, not facts.
4. **Nourishing vs depleting activities (1.14)** adapted: identify activities that nourish
   *within current pain limits*, countering boom-and-bust.

**If it stalls:** turning toward the pain intensifies distress → step back to a neutral anchor
(breath, a pain-free body region) and approach from further away. The user wants pain *gone* →
gently reset expectations toward mood, movement, and relationship-to-pain, not elimination.

## 2 · Cancer / fear of recurrence

**Reach for it when:** fear that cancer will return (FCR), rumination about recurrence,
catastrophizing about every new sensation. Small-to-medium effects on FCR; self-compassion is
strongly relevant (it's inversely related to FCR, with catastrophizing as the mediator).

**The frame:** FCR is fear of an uncertain future plus catastrophic reading of body sensations —
held not just with decentering but with **kindness toward the self doing the fearing.**
Self-compassion is the central emphasis here, more than in other applications.

**Walk it through:**
1. **Allowing (1.13)** applied to the fear: turning toward it, finding where it sits in the body,
   allowing it to be present rather than fighting or suppressing it.
2. **Decentering FCR thoughts** (1.5), with a precise cue: "Try saying it as — 'I'm having the
   thought that the cancer will return. This thought is here. It's painful. It is not the
   future.' Notice the difference between the thought and a fact."
3. **Self-compassion / loving-kindness** (see `depression.md`, 5) directed at the part of them
   that's frightened — kindness toward the fearing, not just observation of it.
4. For body sensations read as recurrence signs, use the health-anxiety skill of holding sensation
   and story separately (`anxiety.md`, 4).

**If it stalls:** the fear is about a *real* medical decision or symptom needing assessment →
that's not a thought to decenter; signpost the relevant medical follow-up. Self-compassion stirs
grief or resistance → that's expected; return to the breath, go gently, don't force the warmth.
