## How to use this file

The trauma layer. **MBCT for PTSD is clinician-guided and cautious**, and most mindfulness PTSD
evidence is actually MBSR, not MBCT — there's no adequately powered MBCT-specific PTSD trial.
Read `safety.md`: **acute PTSD without prior stabilization is a contraindication** — body scan
and intensive sitting can exacerbate dissociation and hyperarousal. This file's job is honest
signposting plus the narrow self-help-safe layer (grounding/stabilization only). Do **not** run
the body scan or turning-toward of trauma material as self-help. Deliver any safe practice per
`walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one cue per turn, end on an invitation, wait.**

---

## The clinician-only boundary

Say it plainly: evidence-based first-line treatments for PTSD — Prolonged Exposure, Cognitive
Processing Therapy — have substantially stronger evidence than mindfulness, and trauma work
belongs with a trained clinician. MBCT, if used at all for trauma, is secondary/adjunctive and
needs trauma-informed delivery after a stabilization phase. What this skill offers is **grounding
and stabilization** as a bridge, and a clear steer toward trauma-focused professional care. If
dissociation, flooding, or a flashback occurs at any point, drop everything and follow `safety.md`.

## 1 · The evidence caveat (be honest)

**Reach for it when:** the user asks whether mindfulness will help their trauma.

**Convey, briefly and honestly:** most of the mindfulness-for-PTSD evidence is from MBSR, not
MBCT; there's no strong MBCT-specific trial; and intensive practices can worsen dissociation and
hyperarousal in acute, unprocessed trauma. The responsible recommendation is trauma-focused
therapy with a clinician first. Mindfulness may have an adjunctive role *after* stabilization, but
not as a self-help substitute for trauma treatment.

## 2 · Grounding and stabilization (the self-help-safe layer)

**Reach for it when:** the user is activated or anxious and needs to come back to the present —
this is the one place this skill can safely help with trauma.

**Walk it through (present-moment grounding, not trauma processing):**
1. **Sensory grounding (5-4-3-2-1):** "Name five things you can see… four you can hear… three you
   can feel… two you can smell… one you can taste." One sense at a time, slowly.
2. **Feet on the floor / contact anchor:** "Feel your feet on the ground, your back against the
   chair, the temperature of the air. You're here, now, and safe in this moment." 
3. A brief, **eyes-open** breathing space if tolerated — but keep it short, oriented to the
   external present, with explicit permission to stop.

The whole aim is orienting to present-moment safety, *not* turning toward the trauma.

**If it stalls / activates:** grounding doesn't settle them, or dissociation/flooding rises →
stop, follow `safety.md`, and steer to professional support; don't push.

## 3 · Trauma-informed adaptations (for understanding)

**Reach for it when:** explaining how MBCT *would* be adapted in a clinician's hands — for
understanding and signposting, **not** for self-administration.

**The adaptations (clinician-delivered):**
- **Titration:** approach any difficult material in small, tolerable doses, keeping arousal low.
- **Pendulation:** alternate between regulated, grounded states and only *mildly* dysregulated
  ones — never holding prolonged dysregulation.
- **Body scan modifications:** very brief, with explicit permission to return to present-moment
  sensory anchors (feet, temperature) the moment it becomes triggering.
- **Grounding before any meditation:** brief orienting first, to establish a safe present-moment
  base.

These are described so the user understands what trauma-informed MBCT involves and why it needs a
clinician — not as a protocol to run alone.

**If it stalls:** the user wants to do trauma work now → reiterate the boundary warmly and
concretely: this needs a trauma therapist; the most caring move is helping them get there.
