## How to use this file

These 15 are the universal MBCT toolkit, used across every condition. The condition files
(`depression.md`, `anxiety.md`, etc.) assume these and add only the specialized layer, so they
point back here by number. Deliver every one per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one cue per turn,
end on an invitation or question, wait.** When a practice is a guided meditation (1.2, 1.4–1.7,
1.13), you are the live voice — give one instruction, let them follow it, check what arose,
then the next; never paste the whole script. They build in a sequence (`model.md`, "why the
sequencing works"): anchor practices first, decentering next, mood work last. The 3-Minute
Breathing Space (1.8/1.9) is the portable bridge you'll reach for most.

## 1.1 · Automatic-pilot awareness / mindful eating (raisin)

**Reach for it when:** the very start, or any time the user is running on autopilot and the
concept of "being present" is still abstract. It makes autopilot *felt* rather than explained,
and introduces beginner's mind. Any single sensory object works — a raisin, a sip of tea, the
hands under warm water.

**Walk it through (one sense at a time, live):**
1. Pick one small object (or a bite of food). Invite: "Hold it as if you'd never seen one
   before — what do you actually notice?" Wait.
2. **See** it — highlights, folds, colour. Then **touch** — texture, turning it slowly. Then
   **smell** — any aroma, any change with each breath. One sense per turn; ask what's there.
3. **Place** it on the lips without biting; notice anticipation. Then **taste** — one slow
   chew, the waves of flavour, the texture changing. Then **swallow** — the urge, then the act.
4. Close: "What did you notice that you'd normally miss on autopilot?" That noticing-the-gap
   *is* the point.

**If it stalls:** if the raisin feels contrived, swap to any routine activity (washing hands,
walking, the first sip of coffee) — same skill, one object, full attention. Once the concept
lands, move to the body scan (1.2).

## 1.2 · Body scan

**Reach for it when:** the foundational anchor practice — first-line for building sustained
attention and non-judgmental observation, and the entry meditation for almost everyone. Also
the base for pain, insomnia, and health-anxiety work. **Caution** in trauma and eating
disorders (`safety.md`, `trauma.md`, `eating-disorders.md`): keep it brief, with explicit
permission to return to a neutral anchor.

**Walk it through (live, region by region — don't list the whole body at once):**
1. Settle: "Lie down or sit comfortably, eyes closed or soft. Spend a moment feeling the
   whole body resting, and the breath." Wait.
2. Begin at the left toes: "Bring attention there — what's actually present? Tingling,
   warmth, pressure, or nothing at all? You're not changing it, just noticing." Wait.
3. Move slowly upward, **one region per turn or small cluster**: foot, lower leg, knee,
   thigh; the other leg; pelvis, back, belly, chest; hands, arms, shoulders; neck, face;
   crown. At each: notice sensation (or its absence), without judgment.
4. Name the core instruction whenever the mind wanders: "When you notice you've drifted —
   that noticing is the practice. Gently come back to where you were." 
5. End: "Widen to the whole body for a moment, then a few deeper breaths." Check what shifted.

**If it stalls:** pain makes a region hard → observe it with curiosity from a respectful
distance, or use the breath as the anchor instead. Drowsiness → sit up or open the eyes
slightly. The body feels threatening (trauma, ED) → stop, return to feet-on-floor or sound,
and consult `trauma.md` / `eating-disorders.md`. Too restless to lie still → breath sitting
(1.4) or walking (1.7).

## 1.3 · Mindful movement

**Reach for it when:** the user finds sitting difficult, or you want to broaden the anchor
beyond the breath. Useful for those who associate movement with releasing tension. Slower and
gentler than exercise — the point is attention, not flexibility.

**Walk it through (one movement at a time):**
1. Stand (or sit) comfortably, feet hip-width. Invite one slow movement — e.g. "Raise your
   arms above your head on the in-breath, lower on the out-breath, slowly enough to feel
   every part of it. What do you notice?" Wait.
2. Add gentle sequences one at a time: shoulder rolls, slow neck movements, a forward fold.
   Stay **at or below the edge of discomfort** — never push through.
3. When judgment shows up ("I'm not flexible enough"), invite return to the raw sensation.
4. Keep movements slow enough that attention can follow them in real time.

**If it stalls:** limited by pain or disability → adapt to whatever range is comfortable, or
use the body scan (1.2) from lying down. Walking meditation (1.7) is a related movement
practice needing less flexibility.

## 1.4 · Sitting meditation — breath awareness

**Reach for it when:** the foundational sitting practice and the anchor you'll return to most.
Works across all conditions; the bridge from physical anchors (body, movement) to mental ones
(sounds, thoughts). First-line for building concentration and the capacity to return.

**Walk it through (live):**
1. "Sit upright, feet flat or cross-legged, hands resting. Eyes closed or downcast. Notice
   the contact of the body with the chair, the room, any sounds." Wait.
2. "Now find the breath where you feel it most clearly — the nostrils, the chest, or the
   belly rising and falling. Not controlling it, just observing. Rest your attention there."
   Wait.
3. Name the core instruction: "When the mind wanders — and it will — just notice where it
   went, and gently bring it back to the breath. The wandering isn't failure; the returning
   *is* the practice." 
4. Let it run a few minutes, checking in lightly. Capture what the mind did and how it felt
   to come back.

**If it stalls:** the breath provokes anxiety (panic, trauma) → switch to a neutral anchor —
feet on the floor, sounds in the room (`anxiety.md`, `panic`). Drowsiness → more upright, eyes
open. Stable enough to open up → sounds and thoughts (1.5).

## 1.5 · Sitting meditation — sounds and thoughts as mental events

**Reach for it when:** the user is fused to thoughts, ruminating, or treating thoughts as
facts — and has a stable breath anchor. This is where **decentering becomes explicit**: you
learn to watch thoughts arise and pass the way you watch sounds. The most direct practice for
"my thoughts feel like facts" and rumination.

**Walk it through (live, in two stages):**

*Sounds first (the gentler training):*
1. "Start with the breath. Then let your attention open to sounds — whatever's here, near or
   far." Wait.
2. "Notice each sound arising, lasting, and passing — without naming it 'traffic' or judging
   it 'annoying'. Just the bare hearing. Notice the spaces between sounds too." Wait.

*Then thoughts:*
3. "Now let thoughts into the field the same way — watch each one like a cloud crossing the
   sky, or a leaf on a stream. Present a moment, then gone." Wait.
4. The key cue: "You're not pushing thoughts away, not analyzing them, not arguing with them
   — and not *inside* them. You're the space they appear in. When you notice you've been
   swept into a thought, that noticing is a moment of stepping back. Just return to watching."
5. When a hot thought arises ("I'm a failure", "something's wrong"): "Same instruction — it's
   a mental event. Watch it, don't follow it, let it pass." Check whether it loosened.

**If it stalls:** thoughts too absorbing or distressing to watch → return to the breath (1.4)
or body scan (1.2) as a steadier base. Intrusive trauma thoughts → don't force observing,
see `trauma.md`. Charged emotional material → the allowing practice (1.13) is the direct
extension of this skill.

## 1.6 · Sitting meditation — choiceless awareness (open monitoring)

**Reach for it when:** the user already has some stability in breath and sounds/thoughts
practice and you want the most open, being-mode-cultivating form. No chosen object — whatever
is most prominent moment to moment (sensation, sound, thought, emotion) is simply met.

**Walk it through (live):**
1. "Settle with a few minutes of the breath as an anchor." Wait.
2. "Now let go of the anchor and let awareness become open and spacious — not on anything in
   particular, just available to whatever arises." Wait.
3. "Whatever shows up — a sensation, a sound, a thought, a feeling — let it arise, be known,
   and pass. Nothing to make happen, nothing to achieve." 
4. "If you get lost, come back to the breath for a moment, then open again."

**If it stalls:** open practice feels destabilizing or scattered → return to focused breath
(1.4); choiceless awareness needs a stable focused base first. If unsettling emotionally,
step down the sequence.

## 1.7 · Walking meditation

**Reach for it when:** the user can't sit still, or you want a formal practice that carries
into daily life. A good alternative for restlessness or agitation, and a bridge to mindful
everyday walking.

**Walk it through:**
1. "Find a clear stretch, 10–20 feet, where you can walk back and forth. Stand at one end and
   feel the ground under your feet, the body upright." Wait.
2. "Walk very slowly — maybe half normal pace. Put full attention into the feet and legs: the
   heel lifting, the leg swinging, the foot placing, the weight shifting." Wait.
3. Optional anchor: "You can quietly note 'lifting… moving… placing…' — not to analyze, just
   to stay with the sensation."
4. "At the end of the path, pause, turn slowly, and begin again. When the mind wanders, note
   it and return to the feet."

**If it stalls:** self-conscious about slow walking → do it privately, or attend to the
sensations of simply standing still. Mindful movement (1.3) is a related option.

## 1.8 · Three-Minute Breathing Space — regular

**Reach for it when:** the single most portable MBCT practice — a scheduled three-times-daily
habit of stepping out of autopilot. Use it to build the muscle that the responsive version
(1.9) then deploys in distress. Applicable to everyone.

**Walk it through (three steps, an hourglass — wide, narrow, wide):**
1. **Awareness (wide, ~1 min):** "Sit or stand upright. Ask: what's here right now? Notice the
   thoughts present — you don't have to change them. Then the feelings. Then the body
   sensations. Just acknowledge what's there." Wait.
2. **Gathering (narrow, ~30–60s):** "Now narrow to a single focus — the breath. Feel it in the
   nostrils, chest, or belly. Let the breath be an anchor, riding each in-breath and
   out-breath." Wait.
3. **Expanding (wide, ~1 min):** "Widen again — from the breath to the whole body, your
   posture, your face. Then wider still to the room, the sounds, the space around you. Arrive
   here, with an open awareness." Check what shifted.

**Schedule:** suggest three preset times (waking, midday, before bed) or anchor it to existing
routines (with coffee, before a meeting, on getting home). The habit is the point.

**If it stalls:** preset times feel contrived → tie it to routines. Step 2 feels rushed →
lengthen it. Once the habit's there, teach the responsive version (1.9).

## 1.9 · Three-Minute Breathing Space — responsive (crisis)

**Reach for it when:** **live distress** — a worry spiral mid-flight, a wave of low mood or
shame, a panic surge, the 2am dread. This is MBCT's primary emotional first-aid tool: a
mindful interrupt that shifts the person from automatic reactivity into a position of choice.
Reach for it first whenever someone is too caught to think.

**Walk it through:**
1. "First — just notice something difficult is here. A feeling, a thought pulling you, a body
   sensation. That noticing is already stepping out of autopilot." Wait.
2. "Pause where you are, take a dignified posture if you can." Then run the three 3MBS steps
   (1.8): wide awareness of what's present → gather onto the breath → expand to the whole body.
3. The crucial difference: "Now, in that wider, more spacious awareness, *hold* the difficult
   feeling rather than narrowing back into it. From here, does it feel a little less
   overwhelming?" 
4. "Given the awareness you have now — what, if anything, does this situation actually call
   for?" (A skillful response, not an automatic reaction.)

**The key difference from regular:** triggered by distress, not a clock. It's the interrupt
between stimulus and reaction.

**If it stalls:** distress too acute to start → first attend to sensory contact (feet on the
floor, the chair, the air temperature) before step 1. Then, once steadier, the allowing
practice (1.13) is the longer extension of this skill.

## 1.10 · Pleasant events calendar

**Reach for it when:** between sessions, to bridge formal practice and daily life — and to
counter the attentional narrowing of low mood. Shows how much positive experience autopilot
misses, and how mood tracks the quality of attention.

**Walk it through (set it up as homework, four columns):**
1. "Once a day, catch something pleasant *as it happens* — however small: warm water, a good
   coffee, a moment of sun. Pause and actually feel it."
2. "That evening, note four things: the event; the thoughts during it; the body sensations
   during it; the feelings." 
3. Bring it back next session for inquiry (1.12).

**If it stalls:** "nothing pleasant happened" is itself important data — it may reflect
anhedonia or attentional narrowing (`depression.md`). Even a tiny sensory moment counts. The
unpleasant events calendar (1.11) is the companion.

## 1.11 · Unpleasant events calendar

**Reach for it when:** the companion to 1.10, now turning mindful attention *toward* difficulty
rather than away. Reveals each person's characteristic thought–body–feeling pattern under
stress — the beginning of relapse-signature awareness (1.15).

**Walk it through (four columns, like 1.10):**
1. "When something unpleasant happens, gently bring awareness to it instead of suppressing,
   distracting, or ruminating."
2. "Note: the event; the thoughts; the body sensations; the feelings."
3. Teaching point: the same event produces different patterns for different people — and each
   person has a *characteristic* one they can learn to recognize.

**If it stalls:** the assignment generates avoidance → scale right back to noticing one body
sensation in one unpleasant moment. Process what surfaced with inquiry (1.12).

## 1.12 · Inquiry

**Reach for it when:** after every practice — this is *how* you debrief any meditation, and the
main way you embody the decentered, curious stance. Unlike CBT's Socratic questioning (aimed
at a more balanced thought), inquiry is **not** advice, problem-solving, or fixing — it models
*being with* experience.

**Walk it through (three widening circles, one question at a time):**
1. **Experiential:** "What did you notice? What was actually happening for you?" — direct
   experience, not concepts *about* it.
2. **Contextual:** "How was that different from how your mind usually runs? What happened when
   your attention moved?"
3. **Condition-linked:** "How might this connect to what happens when your mood dips / the
   worry starts / the craving hits?"

**What you do NOT do:** reassure, interpret, problem-solve, or steer toward a "correct"
insight. You model the same non-judgmental curiosity you're asking them to bring.

**If it stalls:** they give a factual summary instead of experience → gently refocus: "And in
those few minutes, what did you actually notice in your body or mind?"

## 1.13 · Allowing / turning toward difficulty

**Reach for it when:** a difficult emotion, sensation, or thought is present and the impulse
is to avoid, suppress, or analyze it. The direct application of being mode to hard material —
central to depression (sadness, self-criticism), anxiety and health anxiety (body sensations),
and pain. **Requires a stable anchor first** (`model.md`, sequencing). Watch for over-intensity.

**Walk it through (live):**
1. "Start in breath sitting, with a steady anchor." Wait.
2. "Now bring to mind — or wait for — the difficult thing. A feeling, a sensation, a thought."
3. "Instead of turning away or trying to fix it, deliberately turn toward it. Where exactly in
   the body does it live? What are its edges, its qualities? Is it one thing or several?" Wait.
4. "Breathe *into* that area. On the in-breath, move awareness gently toward it; on the
   out-breath, let the body soften *around* it — not to push the feeling out, just to make
   more room for it." Wait.
5. "Watch what it does — intensifies, eases, shifts, stays. Whatever it does is okay. You're
   not making it leave and not resigning to it forever — just allowing it to be here right
   now." 
6. "When you're ready, widen back to the breath and the whole body."

**Metaphors that help (esp. depression):** the Chinese finger trap (pull away and it grips
tighter), the Black Dog (depression as something that comes and goes, not your identity),
*Suffering = Pain × Resistance* (the struggle against the pain is the extra suffering).

**If it stalls:** turning toward sharply *increases* distress → return to the breath (1.4) and
approach from a greater distance, observing rather than moving in. Too acute → the responsive
3MBS (1.9) is the shorter version. Trauma material → don't force it, see `trauma.md`.

## 1.14 · Nourishing and depleting activities

**Reach for it when:** mood is being dragged by an unbalanced life — too many draining
obligations, too few replenishing things — especially in low mood. MBCT's behavioral-activation
component, reframed through mindful awareness: reconnect with what *actually* nourishes, not
perform wellness.

**Walk it through:**
1. "Over the past while, which activities felt **nourishing** — energizing, fulfilling — and
   which felt **depleting** — draining, joyless, merely obligatory? Let's make two lists. Be
   specific and personal; what nourishes one person depletes another." 
2. "What's the current balance? Are you over-invested in the depleting side?"
3. "Which depleting things are genuinely necessary, and which are optional? Which nourishing
   things have you let slide?"
4. "Pick one concrete, schedulable shift for the coming week — not a performance, an act of
   care informed by what actually nourishes *you*."
5. Crucial cue for low mood: "When mood's low, the pull is *away* from nourishing activities.
   That's exactly when doing them — even reluctantly — matters most. Act first; the mood
   follows, it doesn't lead."

**If it stalls:** the nourishing/depleting distinction isn't obvious → the pleasant (1.10) and
unpleasant (1.11) calendars build exactly this awareness. Initiating feels impossible in low
mood → run a responsive 3MBS (1.9) first to step back from the automatic avoidance, then act.

## 1.15 · Relapse-prevention / early-warning-signs plan

**Reach for it when:** the user has a history of recurrent depression (or anxiety, etc.) and
you're consolidating — turning what's been learned into a personalized, written action plan.
The clinical culmination of MBCT for relapse prevention. Adaptable to any condition with a
relapse/deterioration risk.

**Walk it through (build it with them, one part at a time):**
1. **Personal early-warning signs.** "What are the specific thought patterns, mood shifts,
   body sensations, and behaviour changes that have come *before* your past difficult episodes?
   Your signature — different from anyone else's. Examples: waking early with dread, isolating,
   replaying conversations, losing interest in food, a particular self-critical tone." List
   them earliest-appearing to most-advanced.
2. **Characteristic automatic thoughts.** "Which thoughts tend to show up as it begins? ('I'll
   never get better', 'I'm a burden', 'what's the point?')" Write them specifically — they're
   the early-warning system.
3. **A staged action plan.** For each stage:
   - *First signs:* responsive 3MBS (1.9); increase formal practice; review nourishing
     activities (1.14).
   - *Moderate signs:* tell a trusted person what's happening; use the 3MBS at every routine
     transition of the day.
   - *Serious signs:* contact GP / mental-health professional; revisit MBCT materials;
     consider booster sessions.
4. **Involve a trusted other** who can notice the signs the user misses once they're inside it.
5. **Make it accessible** — a card or document kept where they'll find it when they need it.

**If it stalls:** identifying signs is hard → revisit the unpleasant events calendar (1.11) and
any inquiry notes. The key is **specificity**: "I'll be more mindful" doesn't protect; a
concrete, behavioral plan does.
