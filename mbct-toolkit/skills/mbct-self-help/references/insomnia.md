## How to use this file

The insomnia layer (MBCT-I): standard MBCT practices adapted for sleep. It assumes the 15 core
practices in `core-practices.md`. The defining MBCT move for insomnia: the "doing mode" of
*trying to force sleep* is directly analogous to the doing mode of trying to force a mood to
change — both backfire. The work is **acceptance of wakefulness and decentering from sleep
catastrophizing**, not a new technique for controlling sleep. Deliver per
`walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one cue per turn, end on an invitation, wait.** **Caveat to hold
throughout:** mindfulness practices, including the body scan, can *heighten* arousal for some
people at night — monitor individually and drop anything that winds them up.

## 1 · Decentering from sleep worry

**Reach for it when:** the user is kept awake (or dreading the night) by sleep-related thoughts —
"if I don't sleep 8 hours I can't function", "I'll be wrecked tomorrow", "what's wrong with me".

**Walk it through:** apply **sounds and thoughts as mental events** (`core-practices.md`, 1.5) to
the sleep thoughts — watching "I'll be useless tomorrow" arise and pass as a mental event rather
than a fact or a prediction to act on. The cue: "That's a thought about tomorrow, not tomorrow
itself — can we let it be there without following it?"

**If it stalls:** the catastrophizing is relentless → drop to a simpler anchor (breath, sounds in
the room) and stop trying to "handle" the thoughts at all; the trying is part of the arousal.

## 2 · Acceptance vs forcing sleep

**Reach for it when:** the user is fighting to fall asleep, watching the clock, escalating effort
— and the effort is itself keeping them awake.

**Walk it through:**
1. Name the trap plainly: trying to force sleep is doing mode, and sleep is one of the things
   effort pushes away. "What if, for now, we stop trying to sleep and just allow being awake?"
2. Invite **allowing** (1.13) toward the wakefulness and any frustration — turning toward the
   restless, wired feeling rather than battling it, letting it be here without the fight.
3. The reframe: the aim of practice tonight is *not* to produce sleep (that would be doing mode
   again) — it's to rest in a more allowing, less effortful awareness, from which sleep is more
   likely to come on its own, but isn't the goal.

**If it stalls:** the user keeps checking whether "it's working" (i.e. whether they're falling
asleep) → name that the checking is the effort returning; gently return to allowing without a
target.

## 3 · Pre-sleep practices (with caveats)

**Reach for it when:** building a wind-down routine, in a *planning* conversation rather than at
2am.

**Walk it through:**
- **Body scan (1.2) pre-sleep** — framed as present-moment, non-striving attention, *not* as a
  relaxation/sleep technique. **Monitor individually:** for some it settles, for others it
  heightens arousal — if it winds them up, drop it.
- **Regular 3MBS (1.8) before bed** as a transition from daytime doing mode into a more allowing
  quality of awareness.
- **"Thoughts as mental events" (1.5)** rehearsed earlier in the day so it's available at night.

**If it stalls:** the body scan increases arousal → switch to breath or a brief 3MBS, or drop
formal practice at bedtime entirely and rely on the acceptance framing (2).

---

## 4 · The acute 2am situation

**Reach for it when:** the user is awake *now*, in the middle of the night, distressed.

**Walk it through:** don't open a multi-week practice plan. In the moment:
1. **Decenter the "wrecked tomorrow" catastrophizing** (1) — it's a thought, not a forecast.
2. **Allow the wakefulness** (2) rather than fighting it; drop the effort to sleep.
3. A brief responsive 3MBS (1.9) to step out of the spiral. Reserve routine-building for a
   daytime planning session.

**If it stalls:** lying there fuelling frustration → standard sleep-hygiene advice (getting out of
bed if wakefulness persists) is reasonable, but the MBCT core here is loosening the *struggle*,
not adding another thing to do correctly.
