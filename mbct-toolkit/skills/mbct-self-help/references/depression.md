## How to use this file

The depression layer — MBCT's strongest and most thoroughly evidenced indication. It assumes
the 15 core practices in `core-practices.md` and adds the depression-specific framing and
sequencing. MBCT for depression is primarily about **relapse prevention** for people in
remission with recurrent depression (NICE NG222 recommends it as an alternative to maintenance
antidepressants), but it adapts for current depression with care. Deliver every practice per
`walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one cue per turn, end on an invitation, wait.** **Safety:** severe
or current acute depression, and any suicidal thoughts, need a clinician and crisis help, not
this skill — drop the practice and follow `safety.md`. A *history* of suicidal depression is not
itself a contraindication.

## 1 · Relapse prevention: the core framing

**Reach for it when:** the user has recovered from depression and wants to stay well, or feels
an early dip and fears the slide back. This is MBCT's home ground.

**The frame to convey (briefly, via `model.md`):** in recurrent depression, even a *mild* low
mood can reactivate the whole old pattern of depressive thinking automatically — the
differential activation hypothesis. So the work isn't to argue with the dark thoughts (in a low
mood they'll keep regenerating); it's to **recognize the dip early** and meet the reactivated
thinking with a decentered stance, so a passing low mood doesn't snowball into an episode.
Practice dose matters: people practising formally ~3+ days a week have roughly half the relapse
risk.

**Walk it through:** establish the core practices as a stable base (`core-practices.md`,
sequence), then build the relapse-signature plan (6 / 1.15) as the consolidation. The 3-Minute
Breathing Space (1.8 regular, 1.9 responsive) is the everyday tool.

**If it stalls:** the user is currently *in* an episode, not remitted → see section 7 and
`safety.md` first.

## 2 · The depression maintenance cycle (psychoeducation)

**Reach for it when:** the user is caught in the doing-mode trap of trying to think their way
out of low mood, and doesn't yet see how that *feeds* it.

**Walk it through (use their own experience, not an abstract lecture):**
1. Draw the loop with them: low mood → negative thinking (rumination, self-criticism,
   hopelessness) → mood drops further → behaviour narrows (withdrawal, doing less) → fewer
   rewards → mood drops further. Ask: "Does any part of that loop feel familiar from this week?"
2. Land the key insight: the "doing mode" of analyzing the low mood, trying to fix it, replaying
   what's wrong — *is part of the loop*, not the way out. Ask: "When you try to think your way
   out of feeling low, what actually happens to the mood?"
3. Offer the alternative: mindfulness lets you step out of the loop by changing the relationship
   to the thoughts rather than wrestling their content.

**If it stalls:** too abstract → build it bottom-up from one concrete recent dip. Cognition too
heavy to engage → start with behavioral activation via nourishing activities (5) and come back.

## 3 · Allowing depression-linked experience

**Reach for it when:** hopelessness, sadness, self-criticism, or heaviness is present and the
reflex is to avoid it or analyze it. The first explicit turn toward difficult mood (MBCT session
5, "Allowing"). Needs a stable anchor first.

**Walk it through:** run the **allowing practice** (`core-practices.md`, 1.13) on the depressive
feeling — turning toward the sadness or self-criticism, finding where it lives in the body,
breathing into and softening around it, watching what it does without trying to make it leave.

**Metaphors that help depression specifically:**
- **Chinese finger trap** — the harder you pull away from the feeling, the tighter it grips.
- **Black Dog** — depression as something that visits and leaves, not as who you are.
- **Suffering = Pain × Resistance** — the extra suffering is the struggle against the pain, not
  the pain itself.

**If it stalls:** turning toward intensifies the heaviness sharply → step back to the breath
(1.4) and observe from a greater distance. Self-attack dominates → bring in loving-kindness (5).

## 4 · "Thoughts are not facts" for depression

**Reach for it when:** depressive thoughts ("I'm worthless", "it'll never get better", "I'm a
burden") are being treated as truths. The decentering core (MBCT session 6).

**Walk it through:** run **sounds and thoughts as mental events** (`core-practices.md`, 1.5) on
the depressive thoughts — watching "I'm worthless" arise and pass like a sound, *not* arguing
its content. The depression-specific addition: notice that these particular thoughts are the
ones that have characterized past low periods — so when they show up, they're **early-warning
signs, not verdicts.** That recognition feeds the relapse-signature plan (6).

**If it stalls:** the thought feels too true to watch as "just a thought" → don't switch to
disputing its content (that's CBT, not MBCT); instead drop to a steadier anchor and try again
smaller, or name that this thought belongs on the relapse-signature list. If it's actually a
real, solvable problem → that's not a distortion to decenter; signpost problem-solving
(`model.md`).

## 5 · Kindness in action: nourishing activities + loving-kindness

**Reach for it when:** withdrawal, anhedonia, an unbalanced life dragging mood down, and harsh
self-criticism (MBCT session 7, "Kindness in Action"). MBCT's behavioral-activation component
plus self-compassion.

**Walk it through:**
1. Run **nourishing vs depleting activities** (`core-practices.md`, 1.14) — the two lists, the
   balance check, one concrete schedulable shift, with the crucial cue that in low mood the pull
   is *away* from nourishing things and that's exactly when to do them (act first, mood follows).
2. Add brief **loving-kindness** for the self-critical dimension: a few quiet phrases of
   well-wishing directed first toward someone easy to care about, then gently toward oneself
   ("may I be kind to myself, may I be at ease"). Keep it light; watch for the warmth activating
   resistance, and if it does, return to the breath and don't force it.

**If it stalls:** can't initiate any activity → go smaller (a five-minute version), lean on the
responsive 3MBS (1.9) first to step out of the automatic avoidance. Loving-kindness feels fake
or stirs distress → drop it, stay with the breath; self-compassion can't be forced.

## 6 · The relapse-signature plan

**Reach for it when:** consolidating relapse-prevention work (MBCT sessions 7–8). The structured
culmination.

**Walk it through:** build the **early-warning-signs plan** (`core-practices.md`, 1.15) — the
personal signs earliest-to-most-advanced, the characteristic automatic thoughts, the staged
action plan, a trusted other, kept accessible. Record the accumulating signature in the
working-directory `CLAUDE.md` (`logging.md`) so it grows across sessions and is there when a dip
actually comes.

**If it stalls:** signs are hard to identify → mine the unpleasant events calendar (1.11) and
any past inquiry notes; specificity is everything ("waking at 4am with dread", not "feeling
off").

## 7 · Adaptations for current depression

**Reach for it when:** the user is *currently* depressed, not in remission. The standard protocol
was built for remission; current depression needs adaptation, and severe acute depression needs
a clinician first (`safety.md`). Evidence supports MBCT for current depression, but deliver it
carefully.

**Adaptations:**
- **Language shift:** drop the prevention framing; use present-tense **acceptance** framing —
  allowing what's here now, not preventing a future relapse.
- **Stabilization first:** for severe acute depression, some stabilization is typically
  recommended before mindfulness work; don't push intensive practice on someone who can barely
  function — and screen for suicidality (`safety.md`).
- **Emphasize the mechanisms gently:** decreased rumination, self-compassion, acceptance — over
  any striving to "get better" (which is itself doing mode).
- **Lean on the metaphors** (3) and on very small, kind steps; keep meditations shorter if long
  sits feel unbearable.

**If it stalls:** the user can't engage practice at all, or distress is severe → this is past
self-help; follow `safety.md` and signpost professional care. Cognition too heavy for any
sitting → tiny behavioral steps (5) before any formal practice.
