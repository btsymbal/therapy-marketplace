## When SFBT fits anxiety

General, mild-to-moderate anxiety — worried, on edge, can't settle. Moderate evidence support; the same primary-care RCT that helped depression also reduced general anxiety symptoms in 3 short sessions. SFBT here works by building a picture of an anxiety-reduced life and finding the times the client already manages, rather than analyzing the worry.

Assumes the core sequence in `core-techniques.md`; this adds the anxiety-specific emphasis.

## Technique emphasis

- **Exception questions** (`core-techniques.md` §6) — the lead move: "When have you been less anxious lately? What were you doing? Who was around?" Anxiety almost always has exceptions; surface and amplify them.
- **Scaling** (§8) — anxiety level, *confidence*, and hope. Confidence-scaling is especially useful: "How confident are you, 0 to 10, that you could take that one small step this week?"
- **Coping questions** (§7) — "How have you been managing the anxiety day to day?" Surfaces the coping moves they're already making.
- **Miracle question** (§5) — establish a concrete anxiety-reduced preferred future: "When you wake up and the worry has less grip, what's the first thing you'd notice?"
- **Relationship questions** (§9) — "What would [someone close] notice is different when you're less anxious?" Gathers external, less-catastrophic feedback.

## Adaptations

- **Keep it concrete and present-tense.** Anxiety pulls toward "what if" futures; SFBT's preferred-future questions are concrete and observational ("what would you *notice*?"), which is a different, steadier kind of future-talk. Hold that distinction.
- **Build on small managed moments.** A morning they got out the door despite dread is a deliberate exception — "how did you do that?" and "how could you do more of it?"

## The limitation to be honest about

SFBT does **not** provide the graduated exposure that is first-line for specific phobia, social anxiety, panic disorder, and OCD. For those presentations it can serve as goal-framing or a motivational complement, but it is not sufficient as a standalone treatment — and there's no evidence base for manualized SFBT targeting them specifically. If the anxiety is a specific phobia, panic disorder, social phobia, or OCD, say so and signpost exposure-based CBT (`safety.md`). SFBT fits *general* anxiety and the goal-setting around it, not the structured fear-extinction work.

## If it stalls

Worry keeps pulling the session into "what if" analysis → acknowledge, then turn: "Given that worry — what would you want instead?" or an exception question. Can't find a less-anxious time → coping (§7), then an observe-for-exceptions task (§11). Looks like a specific phobia / panic / OCD → re-read `safety.md` and signpost; don't try to run SFBT as the treatment.
