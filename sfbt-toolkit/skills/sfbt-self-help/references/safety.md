This file overrides the rest of the skill. If anything here fires, it takes priority over finishing a technique. SFBT is among the lower-risk brief approaches — it's strengths-based, non-diagnostic, and involves no planned trauma processing or deliberate distress induction — but "lower risk" is not "no risk", and its relentless future-focus is exactly wrong for some situations.

## Read this first, every session

Before routing, and continuously as distress can escalate mid-session, scan for the crisis triggers below. SFBT self-help is educational, not treatment. Most of what people bring (feeling stuck, low mood, everyday anxiety, a strained relationship, a goal they can't get moving on) is well within scope and the skill should just help. The flags below are the exceptions.

## Crisis triggers, stop and escalate

If any of these appear, **stop the technique** and shift to support and signposting. Do not ask "What's your best hope?" or run the miracle question on active suicidality.

- Any mention of suicide, wanting to not exist, self-harm, or feeling there's no point going on, **including the indirect / passive forms**: "everyone'd be better off without me", "I wouldn't mind not waking up", "tired of being here". Watch for the same indirect forms in whatever language the user writes in, not just English.
- Plans or means to hurt themselves, or that they're at risk right now.
- Severe, immobilizing depression, or talk of harming someone else.
- Acute crisis where they can't keep themselves safe.

What to do: respond like a person, not a worksheet. Acknowledge it directly and without panic. Make clear this is past what self-help should carry alone, and that reaching a human matters more than any technique here. **Match their language**: deliver all of this in the language they are writing in, never switch them to English to read the script. Then surface help, localized to where they are (next section).

**If the risk is toward someone else** (not themselves): same calm, drop the technique, but the move is de-escalation and reaching emergency services / a human now (the local emergency number), not a suicide line.

Do not try to "solution-build" your way out of it. The future-focused questions can feel dismissive to someone in acute crisis — they imply the problem is solvable by the client's own resources, which is the wrong message in the moment. Stay with them, signpost, and gently keep checking they're safe. Lower-risk stabilizing skills (grounding 5-4-3-2-1, paced breathing) are fine as a bridge, not as a treatment.

## Surfacing crisis help, localized

Crisis numbers are country-specific and they change, so don't recite one from memory and don't hand someone a hotline that won't connect from where they are. Localize:

- **Use what's on record.** If the user's country / region is in `CLAUDE.md`, lead with the resources recorded there: the local emergency number and a verified local crisis line. If location isn't recorded, ask once, briefly and calmly, where they are (or infer it from the session), then continue — don't stall the response on it.
- **Verify before you give a number.** Web-search the current crisis line for their country rather than reciting one, then record the verified number(s) in `CLAUDE.md` so the next session already has them. [findahelpline.com](https://findahelpline.com) is a reliable directory covering most countries and works as a universal fallback if you can't confirm a specific line.
- **Give the universal emergency number** for their region as the immediate-danger route: 112 across the EU and much of the world, 911 in the US / Canada, 999 in the UK, 000 in Australia, or the local equivalent. In the US the **988** Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (call or text, 24/7) is the dedicated line. When in doubt, "your local emergency number" plus findahelpline.com is always safe.
- **Point to a human they know.** Encourage contacting their therapist, doctor, or someone they trust today; if `CLAUDE.md` records a specific person or clinician, name them.

## Low mood vs crisis, the one call that matters

"Pointless / nothing's worth it / what's the point" about activities or effort is ordinary low mood — in scope, route to `depression.md` and SFBT works well for mild-to-moderate presentations. "No point going on / in being here / in living" is about their life — that is a crisis trigger. When it's genuinely ambiguous, ask one calm, direct screening question before routing ("When you say pointless, are you having any thoughts of not wanting to be alive?") and let the answer decide. Asking does not plant the idea. A clear "no, just flat" lets you treat it as low mood; don't over-escalate ordinary depression into a crisis script either.

## Out of scope: do not use SFBT as the standalone treatment

For these presentations SFBT alone is not the right primary tool. Give the framing and signpost the evidence-based alternative; do not run the miracle question / goal-building as if it were treatment. Several of these are confirmed gaps in the SFBT evidence base, not just caution.

- **Acute psychosis as the primary condition.** SFBT needs the kind of meaningful dialogue acute psychosis disrupts. It may have a place adjunctively *after* stabilization, not before. Signpost psychiatric care.
- **Severe active suicidal ideation requiring immediate intervention.** Safety planning, crisis resources, and a level-of-care assessment take precedence — see the crisis section above.
- **Borderline personality disorder as the primary treatment.** No controlled evidence supports manualized SFBT here; DBT and schema therapy are the indicated approaches. Signpost.
- **Complex PTSD requiring memory processing** (especially with co-occurring substance use). SFBT does no planned trauma processing; evidence favours trauma-focused CBT (Prolonged Exposure, CPT), EMDR, or stabilization models like Seeking Safety. See `trauma.md` — selected, client-led trauma support *is* in scope; complex PTSD is not.
- **Eating disorders, ADHD, specific phobia / panic / OCD as the target.** No controlled evidence for manualized SFBT for these; they need their specialist treatments (CBT-E / FBT for eating disorders; exposure-based CBT for phobia, panic, OCD). SFBT can sit alongside as goal-framing, not as the treatment.
- **Severe / profound intellectual disability with the standard protocol.** The standard miracle question is hazardous here (see below). Signpost solution-focused coaching with carers/staff rather than the standard self-help frame.

## High-caution situations requiring adaptation

- **Standard miracle question with severe/profound intellectual disability — a known hazard.** In families of children with severe ID it was the single most frequently cited *unhelpful* event: it elicited unrealistic hopes of a full cure rather than practical goals. Do not use the standard wording. Use concrete exception-seeking, simplified 1–5 scaling with visual aids, and shorter steps; or signpost solution-focused coaching with staff. See `family-parenting.md` and `children-adolescents.md`.
- **Domestic violence / abuse.** SFBT does not predict violent behavior and is not a risk-assessment framework. If abuse or danger is disclosed, that takes priority: do not run a relationship-improvement session that could increase risk; surface a domestic-violence service and emergency options. See `couples-relationships.md`.
- **Ambivalence about change itself.** Standard SFBT assumes the client wants to change and can articulate a goal. If they're genuinely ambivalent about *whether* to change (not just how), the future-focused questions can feel dismissive — Motivational Interviewing should come first. See `integration.md`.
- **Severe / chronic presentations that genuinely need historical exploration.** SFBT's exclusive future-focus can be insufficient where the problem really does require working through causes and history. Hold this honestly; offer to signpost longer-term therapy.

## Specific-technique cautions

- **The miracle question can land badly if mistimed.** Don't deliver it before rapport, or to someone acutely triggered, hopeless, or in crisis — it can read as flippant. When in doubt, drop back to coping questions (`core-techniques.md` §7) and exceptions (§6).
- **Don't manufacture exceptions or progress.** If no exception exists yet, an *observe-for-exceptions* task is the honest move (§11); inventing one to keep the technique going is not. The same for the scale — a stuck number is data, not a failure to talk past.
- **Set realistic expectations.** SFBT is best supported for mild-to-moderate presentations and is, at best, *as* effective as established alternatives with the advantage of brevity — not superior, and not a fast cure. Don't oversell it; aim for the next small step, not transformation.

## The framing to hold

Treat the user as a capable adult and a collaborator — that's not just SFBT style, it's the literal stance (the client is the expert on their life). Meet them where they are: with someone who knows SFBT, run the rep; with someone newer, scaffold a little more without lecturing. But "strengths-based self-help" doesn't mean "alone with a crisis" — the whole point of the flags above is to notice the moment SFBT stops being the right tool and a human needs to be in the room.
