## When SFBT fits relationship distress

Couples and relationship strain — the same fights, distance, "we keep going in circles", a relationship one or both want better. SFBT here builds a shared picture of the relationship working and finds the recent good times to build on, rather than litigating who's at fault. In self-help (one partner present) the work is on what *this* person can notice, do, and ask for differently.

Assumes the core sequence in `core-techniques.md`; this adds the relationship-specific emphasis.

## Technique emphasis

- **Miracle question for the relationship** (`core-techniques.md` §5) — "Suppose overnight things were the way you want them between you — what's the first thing you'd each notice was different?" Get concrete, mutual, observable detail.
- **Relationship questions** (§9) — the engine here: "What would your partner say is already a bit better?" · "What would they notice you doing differently when things are good?" · "What would *they* do back?" Builds perspective-taking and breaks self-focused negativity.
- **Scaling** (§8) — relationship satisfaction, hope, commitment. "Where's the relationship on 0 to 10, and what's it at that and not lower?"
- **Exception questions** (§6) — "Tell me about a good patch recently — even a day. What was different then? What were you each doing?"

## Adaptations

- **One partner, not the couple.** In self-help you usually have one person. Keep the goal on what *they* can influence and notice; relationship questions let them imagine the other's view without the other in the room.
- **Stay off blame and history.** SFBT does not analyze who caused what. When the session pulls into grievance, acknowledge, then turn to the preferred future or a recent exception.

## The evidence caveat (read before quoting any effect)

You may see a very large effect size cited for SFBT with couples. Do **not** present it as a strong general claim. It rests on a handful of studies with extreme heterogeneity, heavy geographic concentration (predominantly Middle Eastern samples), a gender-composition anomaly (only the female partner participated in several), a group-therapy confound, and significant publication bias — and an independent replication was **not** statistically significant. The honest position: SFBT shows *promising signals* for couples, especially in some populations, but the evidence is too narrow and heterogeneous to support a strong generalized claim. Say "promising, not proven" if asked.

## Safety

If abuse, coercive control, or violence is disclosed or suspected, this overrides the relationship work: SFBT is not a risk-assessment framework and a relationship-improvement session can *increase* risk. Drop the technique, surface a domestic-violence service and emergency options, and follow `safety.md`. Longer-term intervention is preferred where children are at risk.

## If it stalls

The other partner is "the whole problem" → relationship questions (§9) to shift to what *this* person could notice and do; scale their own hope and effort, not the partner's. No good patch findable → coping (§7) — "how have you kept the relationship going through this?" — then observe-for-exceptions. Pulls into blame/history → acknowledge once, then turn to the preferred future. Abuse disclosed → `safety.md`.
