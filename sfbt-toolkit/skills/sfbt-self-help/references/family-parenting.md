## When SFBT fits family & parenting

Parenting strain, a child's behavior, family tension. Reasonable evidence support across controlled studies of SFBT with children and families — parenting support, child behavior problems, child welfare. The work surfaces family strengths and the times the child *does* do the wanted thing, and builds on those, rather than cataloguing what's wrong.

Assumes the core sequence in `core-techniques.md`; this adds the family-specific emphasis. Works with the parent in self-help (the parent is the client and the agent of change).

## Technique emphasis

- **Problem-free talk** (`core-techniques.md` §2) — surface family strengths and the child's good qualities before the problem; it reframes a demoralized parent and a "problem child".
- **Exception questions for parent–child interaction** (§6) — the lead move: "When *does* [child] listen / settle / cooperate? What's different about those times? What are *you* doing differently then?" Build on the deliberate exceptions.
- **Miracle question for the family** (§5) — "If the miracle happened overnight for your family, what's the first thing you'd notice tomorrow morning?"
- **Scaling** (§8) — parenting confidence, the child's behavior, hope. Upward-from-0 surfaces what the parent is already doing that works.
- **Relationship questions** (§9) — "What does [child] notice when you're parenting at your best? What would they say is different?"
- **Compliments and tasks** (§10–11) — compliment the parent's existing competence; set "do more of what works."

## Adaptation: parenting a child with intellectual disability (important)

The **standard miracle question is a known hazard** with families of children with severe/profound intellectual disability — it was the single most frequently cited *unhelpful* event by mothers in this group, because it elicited unrealistic hopes of a full cure rather than practical goals. **Do not use the standard wording here.** Instead:
- Concrete exception-seeking ("when do things go a bit more smoothly?") rather than an open miracle image.
- Simplified 1–5 scaling with visual aids.
- Shorter sessions with more processing time.
- For severe/profound ID, the better-supported route is solution-focused coaching with staff/carers rather than the standard self-help frame — signpost it. See `safety.md`.

For *mild* intellectual disability, standard SFBT with modifications (simplified scale, concrete language, visual aids) is feasible.

## If it stalls

"The child never does the wanted thing" → there is almost always a partial exception; go smaller ("a time it was even slightly less bad?"), or set an observe-for-exceptions task. Parent is demoralized and self-blaming → coping (§7) and problem-free talk to surface competence first. Severe/profound ID and the miracle question lands badly → switch to concrete exceptions + simplified scaling, per above; consider signposting staff coaching.
