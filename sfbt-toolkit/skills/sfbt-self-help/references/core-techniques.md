## How to use this file

These thirteen are the transdiagnostic core of SFBT, used across almost every situation. The application files (`depression.md`, `couples-relationships.md`, etc.) assume these and add only the population-specific layer, so they point back here by number. They sit in roughly first-session order; EARS (12) opens every *later* session. Deliver every one per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one question per turn, end on the question, wait, then amplify what surfaces.** The quoted wordings are English templates — translate them naturally (`SKILL.md` Language section); keep the structure and the 0–10 scale.

The single operating principle underneath all of them: **co-construct a preferred future, find the exceptions where it's already happening, amplify them, and build small steps toward them — without requiring any explanation of the problem's causes.** Solutions need not be related to the problem.

## 1 · Not-knowing stance (lead from one step behind)

**Reach for it when:** always — it's the posture, not a step. Without it the other twelve become a checklist. Appropriate across every presentation and population.

**Walk it through (it's an attitude, run it continuously):**
1. Approach each answer as if you genuinely don't yet know what they mean. Curiosity, not expertise.
2. Steer the *direction* (toward what they want, toward exceptions); let the client set the *pace and content*. "Leading from one step behind."
3. Respect their understanding of their own situation; treat them as the expert on their life; build solutions from inside their own experience and language.
4. In practice: avoid interpretation, reflect their own words back rather than translating to clinical terms, ask "What do you mean by that?" rather than defining it, and don't suggest what *should* be different.

**If it stalls:** if the client is frustrated by the lack of advice ("just tell me what to do"), validate the wish for direction, then ask what *they* think might help — that question is itself a not-knowing move.

## 2 · Problem-free talk

**Reach for it when:** the opening of a first session, before the problem is on the table. Builds rapport, surfaces strengths and resources invisible to problem-focused questioning, and positions the client as competent. Especially useful with reluctant clients, adolescents, and anyone demoralized by problem-talk.

**Walk it through:**
1. Ask about parts of their life unrelated to the problem: work, interests, relationships, what they're good at. "Before we get into what's hard — tell me a bit about what you're into / what a good day looks like."
2. Listen and reflect; keep it specific, ask for concrete examples.
3. Quietly note the competencies and resources that emerge — you'll return to them later as exceptions or existing solutions.
4. When they're ready, turn toward the work: "What would be most helpful to talk about?"

**If it stalls:** if they're distressed and want to go straight to the problem, follow them — problem-free talk is an opening orientation, not mandatory preamble. With very goal-focused clients, go straight to best hopes (4).

## 3 · Pre-session change inquiry

**Reach for it when:** early in the first session, after a little rapport. A real share of people improve between deciding to act and actually doing the work — naming it surfaces existing momentum and reframes them as already changing.

**Walk it through:**
1. Ask: "Sometimes between deciding to do something about a problem and actually starting, people notice things shifting, even a little. Has anything been any better since you decided to work on this?"
2. If yes: amplify. "What changed? How did you make that happen? What does that tell you about what you can do?"
3. If no, or worse: acknowledge and validate, then move to goal-setting (4) or coping (7). Treat "no" as neutral information, not a failure.
4. Use any positive change as early evidence that change is already underway.

**If it stalls:** no change reported → move to best hopes (4) or the miracle question (5). Things have worsened → coping questions (7) are usually the better next step.

## 4 · Best-hopes goal-setting

**Reach for it when:** core to every session — without a clearly negotiated goal the other techniques have no direction. Right after a brief acknowledgment of the problem in the first session; re-opened each later session alongside EARS (12). Especially important with involuntary or mandated situations, where the client's goal may differ from a referrer's.

**Walk it through:**
1. Ask: "What are your best hopes from doing this?" or "What would you like to be different after we've worked on this?"
2. Turn it **positive and concrete** — what will be *present*, not just what's absent. "What will you be doing *instead*?" rather than "What will have stopped?"
3. Check it's realistic and within their power to work toward.
4. If they name a problem ("I want to stop feeling so low"), translate it: "When you're not feeling so low, what will you be doing that you're not doing now?"
5. With involuntary / mandated situations: "What would be the smallest sign to [the person who's worried] that things were better enough that they'd worry less?"
6. Keep returning to this goal across sessions as the north star.

Variants: "What needs to happen here today for this to feel like a useful conversation?" (session goal). "What would [partner / child / boss] notice is different when things are better?"

**If it stalls:** can't name any hope → the miracle question (5) uses imagery to bypass goal-blocking hopelessness. Unrealistic goal → explore what it would *get* them ("If that happened, what would be better for you?") and work toward that.

## 5 · The miracle question (and variants)

**Reach for it when:** the signature SFBT move. First session, after goal-setting, to generate a rich preferred-future picture when the client can't yet name a concrete goal, feels stuck in problem-talk, or needs a vision to aim at. Best supported for depression and general mental health. **Not as standardly worded** for severe intellectual disability (see `safety.md`), and not for someone acutely triggered or in crisis.

**Walk it through:**
1. Set it up: "I'd like to ask you a slightly unusual question — is that OK?"
2. Deliver it, slowly: "Suppose tonight while you're asleep, a miracle happens, and the problem that brought you here is solved. But because you were asleep, you don't know it's happened. When you wake up tomorrow — what's the first thing you'd notice that tells you something is different?"
3. **Slow down and invite elaboration.** Let them take time; don't rush past the first answer.
4. Fill in detail, one question at a time: "What else would be different?" · "How would [partner/family] notice?" · "What would they do differently back?" · "What's the very first tiny sign — maybe before you're even out of bed?" · "What would be different at work / with the kids?"
5. Identify which pieces of the miracle they've already experienced, even briefly — that's the bridge to exceptions (6).
6. Use the miracle's details to build the scale (8): "If 10 is that morning, where are you now?"

**Common pitfalls (avoid):** asking too early before rapport; rushing the follow-up; accepting one or two words instead of a detailed picture; not asking about the relational ripple effects; framing it as a fantasy rather than a concrete *observational* question ("What would you *notice*?").

Variants: **good-day framing** for children or anyone who finds "miracle" too fantastical — "Imagine you wake up tomorrow and things are just a bit better — what's different?"; **video/film metaphor** — "If we filmed you on a day it was going well, what would we see?"; **near-future framing** — "What would need to happen in the next two weeks for you to feel you were heading the right way?" (more concrete, less aspirational).

**If it stalls:** overwhelmed / hopeless / can't answer → (a) good-day framing; (b) drop to exceptions (6): "When was the last time even a little of that was already happening?"; (c) coping questions (7); (d) relationship questions (9): "What would your best friend say they've already noticed?" Unrealistic answer → gently redirect: "And if that weren't possible — what else would be different?"

## 6 · Exception questions

**Reach for it when:** across all sessions, to find existing solutions — times the problem was absent, milder, or better handled. A first-line move in most SFBT sessions; particularly strong for depression, anxiety, and internalizing problems. The form *presupposes* exceptions exist.

**Walk it through:**
1. Ask a presuppositional exception question: "When was the last time even a little of [the miracle / your goal] was already happening?" · "Tell me about a recent time you felt even a bit more like yourself." · "What's different about the times the problem has less grip?"
2. Explore it in detail: "What was different about that time?" · "What were you doing differently?" · "Who else was there, and what were they doing?" · "How did you manage to make that happen?"
3. Sort it:
   - **Random exception** (they don't know how it happened) → plant a seed: "Interesting — watch for when that happens again and we'll look at it next time."
   - **Deliberate exception** (they did something on purpose) → amplify: "That's exactly the kind of thing to know more about. How could you do more of that?" Deliberate exceptions are the stronger foundation for progress.
4. Connect it forward: "Is that something like what your miracle looks like?"

When none can be found: try potential-exception questions ("Are there situations where it's a bit less intense?"); set an observe-for-exceptions task (11); or revert to the miracle question (5) to build a future-vision first; or use coping (7) if they're overwhelmed.

**If it stalls:** if exceptions stay impossible after several tries, consider whether the client first needs the problem genuinely *acknowledged* before they can look for exceptions. Spend more time on empathic listening and coping (7) before returning here.

## 7 · Coping questions

**Reach for it when:** the client is overwhelmed, scores 0 on the scale, can't find any exception, or is deeply demoralized. This is the **re-entry technique for crisis or deep pessimism** — it surfaces competence and resilience by asking how they've kept functioning despite the problem. Also called resilience or competence questions. Strong in medical settings and with chronic difficulty.

**Walk it through:**
1. Acknowledge the difficulty genuinely *first*: "It sounds like this has been really hard."
2. Ask the coping question: "How have you managed to keep going?" · "How have you stopped it getting even worse?" · "What's kept you going through all this?" · "A lot of people in your situation might have given up — how have you not?"
3. Listen closely — the answer holds existing resources, relationships, values, strengths.
4. Amplify: "That's not nothing. Where did you find that?"
5. Use what emerges as raw material for exceptions (6) and goal-setting (4).
6. Follow with scaling (8): "On 0 to 10, where 0 is the very worst it's been, where are things right now?"

Coping is the doorway back to exceptions: once some competence is named, return — "So in that moment when you managed to cope, what were you doing?"

**If it stalls:** if they reject the premise ("I'm *not* managing — it's all terrible"), don't push. Accept the frame: "You're right, that sounds completely overwhelming." Stay in empathic listening, then try a smaller version later: "What helped even a tiny bit?"

## 8 · Scaling questions

**Reach for it when:** one of the most versatile tools — used across all problems, sessions, and populations. Scaling concretizes the subjective, counters all-or-nothing thinking, measures progress, and builds the bridge from preferred future to the next action step. It generates more specific behavioral next-steps than the miracle or exception questions do. A first-line move every session.

**Standard construction:** "On a scale of 0 to 10, where 10 is [the miracle / your goal fully there / life exactly how you want it] and 0 is [the worst it's been], where would you say you are right now?"

**Walk it through:**
1. Establish both anchors clearly (what is 10? what is 0?).
2. Get their current position.
3. Explore *upward from 0*: "How come you're at a [4] and not at 0?" — this presupposes they've already climbed off the bottom and asks them to name the competence that did it.
4. Find the next small step: "What would a [5] look like? What one small thing would you be doing differently?"
5. Scale confidence if useful: "On that same scale, how confident are you that you could take that one small step this week?"
6. Return next session: "Last time you were at a [4]. Where are you now? What moved it?"

What else can be scaled: safety, self-liking / confidence, motivation / readiness, hope, quality of life in a domain, relationship satisfaction. Pick the one that matters for this session.

**Adaptation:** for children or intellectual disability, use 1–5 with a visual (faces, a ladder).

**If it stalls:** won't accept a number → descriptive scaling ("better, worse, or about the same as last week?"). Scores 0 → go straight to coping (7). Scores 10 → move to consolidation (13); therapy may be near its end.

## 9 · Relationship questions

**Reach for it when:** throughout, to help the client take another perspective, widen change beyond themselves, and find relational resources. Especially useful in couples and family work, and with clients stuck in self-focused negative thinking.

**Walk it through:**
1. Identify a significant other whose view would be informative (partner, parent, child, colleague, friend).
2. Ask: "What would your partner notice is different about you when you're at a [5]?" · "If your best friend were here, what would they say is already a bit better?" · "Who'd be the first to notice you were doing better — and what would they spot?"
3. Explore further: "What would they do differently back? How would that land for you?"
4. Bring it home: "Does that ring true? Is any of it already happening?"

Variant (competence framing): "What would [person] say you've managed to do well?"

**If it stalls:** no significant relationships, or all negative → go abstract: "If someone who knew you well and cared about you were watching over the next month, what would they hope to see?" or "What would the best version of you notice?"

## 10 · Compliments and the end-of-session message

**Reach for it when:** throughout the session as affirmations, and as the formal structured message that closes every SFBT session. Compliments are one of the four hallmarks of the approach; the structured end message is a primary *intervention*, not just a kind closing.

Two kinds of in-session compliment:
- **Direct:** "That took real nerve."
- **Indirect / competence:** "How did you manage that?" — usually stronger, because the client has to find and say the answer themselves.

**The end-of-session message (walk it through):**
1. **The break.** Take a short pause to formulate the message — a deliberate ritual that signals what follows was considered. ("Let me take a moment to pull together what I've heard.")
2. **Compliment.** Acknowledge something specific that actually surfaced — genuine and concrete, not generic. "I was struck by how clearly you described what you want to be different, and how much you're already doing despite how hard it's been."
3. **Bridge.** A linking sentence that connects the compliment to the task and says *why*. "Given how well you already notice what helps, it makes sense to pay even closer attention to those moments…"
4. **Task.** One concrete between-session task (11).

**If it stalls:** if they dismiss compliments ("anyone could do that"), switch to an indirect competence question rather than a direct statement. If the structured message seems to land flat, check the task is actually aligned with their goal — adjust if not.

## 11 · Between-session tasks

**Reach for it when:** as part of the end-of-session message, every session. Tasks extend the work past the session and operationalize change. Match the type to the client's readiness.

**Type 1 — Observational** (for the stuck or early-change client; builds awareness without requiring action):
- The **Formula First Session Task**: "Between now and next time, notice — so you can tell me about it — what happens in your [life / relationship / work] that you'd like to keep happening."
- Observe-for-exceptions: "Watch for any times things are even a little better this week, and notice what you're doing differently."

**Type 2 — Behavioral** (for the client who has a deliberate exception and knows what helps):
- "Do more of what's already working." Specify exactly what.
- Increase the frequency or widen the context of a specific exception behavior.

**Type 3 — Experimental** (for the client ready to try something new):
- "Pick one day this week and act *as if* the miracle had already happened — and notice what's different." (Operationalizes the miracle in behavior.)
- "Try something different from usual, however small."

**Matching:** very stuck / demoralized → observational (FFST); has some exceptions → behavioral; ready to experiment → experimental.

**If it stalls:** if a task wasn't done, never frame it as failure. Ask: "What got in the way? What does that tell us about what'd be more useful?" Adjust complexity, specificity, or timing. When nothing clear emerged, a purely observational task is always available.

## 12 · EARS — the follow-up structure

**Reach for it when:** opening the second and every later session. EARS replaces the first-session arc and is the backbone of ongoing SFBT. It operationalizes the signature follow-up question, "What's better?"

**Walk it through:**
- **E — Elicit:** "What's better since we last met?" (NOT "How are you?" or "What went wrong this week?") The presupposing form assumes some improvement; if there's none, they'll say so and you move to coping (7).
- **A — Amplify:** "What exactly is better? Tell me more." · "How did that happen?" · "What did you do differently?"
- **R — Reinforce:** "How did you manage that? What does it tell you about yourself?" Compliment the change specifically; help them attribute it to their own agency; re-scale: "If you were at a [4] last time, where are you now?"
- **S — Start again:** "What else is better?" Loop back to E until all the improvements are out.

Handling "Nothing's better": move to coping (7) — "How have you kept it from getting worse?"; revisit the scale (even half a point is data); if things have actually worsened, validate fully, then gently: "Given how hard it's been, how have you kept coming to this at all?"

**If it stalls:** if they want the session for a new problem or crisis rather than what's better, **follow the client** — acknowledge it, use coping if needed, then orient back: "We'll hold that. With everything that's happened, what, if anything, has been even a little better?"

## 13 · Consolidation questions (termination)

**Reach for it when:** the final session(s), when the client is at or near their goal (scaling 8–10). Consolidation summarizes the work, names what made change possible, and plans maintenance.

**Walk it through:**
1. "What do you need to keep doing to stay on track?"
2. "What's been most useful in our work together?"
3. "If things slipped back a bit, what would be the first sign — and what would you do?"
4. "What have you learned about yourself in this that you'll take with you?"
5. "If we met again in six months, what would you hope was different from today?"
6. Leave the door open: "If it gets hard again, you can come back — and you know what helps now."

**If it stalls:** if they're anxious about ending, scale their confidence about holding the gains, and offer a booster session at a longer interval rather than stopping abruptly — consistent with client-determined brevity.
