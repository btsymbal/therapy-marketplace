## How to use this file

These nine are the transdiagnostic core, used across almost every problem. The condition files (`panic.md`, `depression.md`, etc.) assume these and add only the specialized layer, so they point back here by name. Deliver every one of them per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.** The steps below already carry the question to ask at each step, ask that, don't paraphrase the whole list.

---

## 1 · Cognitive-model psychoeducation (the CBT triangle)

**Reach for it when:** the start of almost any piece of work, or when the user is treating a feeling as a fact about the world rather than a product of how they read the situation. Often you don't need to teach it explicitly, use it as a lens to pull a vague distress apart.

**Walk it through:**
1. Take one specific recent upsetting moment (not "this week", a moment). Ask: "Walk me to the exact moment, what was happening?"
2. Pull it into four parts. Ask for each in turn: the **situation** (just the facts), the **thought** that went through them, the **emotion + body** sensation, the **behavior** (what they did / wanted to do).
3. Lay out the loop back to them: thought → feeling → behavior → confirms the thought. ("'I'll botch this' → anxiety → you put it off → which 'proves' you'll botch it.")
4. Land the lever: because the parts are linked, changing one moves the others. Ask which corner feels most workable right now, the thought (restructuring), or the behavior (experiment / activation / exposure).

**If it stalls:** if the abstract triangle doesn't land, build it bottom-up from their own concrete example, or skip straight to a **behavioral experiment** (5) so they *feel* the link instead of hearing it.

## 2 · Cognitive restructuring + the 10 distortions

**Reach for it when:** a specific automatic negative thought is driving the feeling, depression, anxiety, self-criticism, anger. The flagship cognitive move; the **thought record** (3) is its worksheet form.

**The 10 distortions** (name them by name): (1) all-or-nothing; (2) overgeneralization; (3) mental filter; (4) discounting the positive; (5) jumping to conclusions, mind-reading or fortune-telling; (6) magnification / catastrophizing & minimization; (7) emotional reasoning; (8) should / must statements; (9) labeling; (10) personalization / blame.

**Naming them in the user's language.** When the session runs in a language other than English, name the distortion in that language so the label is stable across sessions, then map it back to the canonical English **log tag** below for the `distortions:` field (`logging.md`) so the folder stays greppable regardless of session language.

| # | Distortion | Log tag |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | all-or-nothing (black-and-white) | all-or-nothing |
| 2 | overgeneralization | overgeneralization |
| 3 | mental filter (focus on the negative) | mental-filter |
| 4 | discounting the positive | discounting-positive |
| 5 | jumping to conclusions (mind-reading / fortune-telling) | jumping-to-conclusions |
| 6 | catastrophizing / minimization | catastrophizing |
| 7 | emotional reasoning | emotional-reasoning |
| 8 | should / must statements | should-statements |
| 9 | labeling | labeling |
| 10 | personalization / self-blame | personalization |

**Walk it through:**
1. Catch the automatic thought tied to the mood shift; get it verbatim. Ask: "What exactly went through your head, in your words?" Rate belief 0-100%.
2. Name the distortion(s) in it. Ask: "Which of these is it doing?" (offer the two or three likely ones if they're stuck).
3. Evidence for the thought. Ask: "What genuinely supports it?" Take it seriously, don't rush past.
4. Evidence against. Ask: "What doesn't fit? Has there ever been a time this wasn't true?"
5. Build a balanced thought that fits **all** the evidence, in their words. Ask: "So what's the version that accounts for both columns?"
6. Re-rate belief and mood.

**If it stalls:** belief won't drop → run a **behavioral experiment** (5) or hunt for an underlying core belief (`self-esteem-motivation.md`). "I know it's not true but I still feel it" → **third-wave** acceptance / defusion (`third-wave.md`). Too distressed to think → **relaxation** (9) or grounding first. Thought may be **true** → stop disputing, go to **problem-solving** (8).

## 3 · Thought record

**Reach for it when:** you want the full structured restructuring on paper, across depression, anxiety, anger, self-esteem. It is technique 2 in worksheet form; use it when the thought is tangled enough to need columns.

**Walk it through (7-column, Mind Over Mood), one column per turn:**
1. **Situation**, "Where were you, what was happening, who was there?"
2. **Moods**, name each, rate 0-100. "What did you feel, and how strong?"
3. **Automatic thoughts**, all of them, belief 0-100% each; circle the **hot** one (the one that stings most). "Which of these is the one that really lands?"
4. **Evidence supporting** the hot thought. "What's the case *for* it?"
5. **Evidence not supporting** it. "What's the case against, including things you'd normally wave away?"
6. **Balanced thought** weighing all of it, rate belief. "Holding both columns, what's the truer read?"
7. **Re-rate moods.** "Where's the feeling now?"

**If it stalls:** weak evidence search → **Socratic questioning** (4) or the **double-standard** move (`self-esteem-motivation.md`). Balanced thought known-but-not-felt → **behavioral experiment** (5). Belief won't move → underlying core belief. In depression, if cognition is too heavy → do **behavioral activation** (6) first and come back.

## 4 · Socratic questioning

**Reach for it when:** inside restructuring or a thought record, to help the user reach the balanced view by their own reasoning rather than being told it. The most underused lever, and the one that actually changes belief.

**Walk it through (one question per turn, follow their answer):**
1. Pin the specific belief. "What's the exact claim we're testing?"
2. Evidence for. "What facts support it?"
3. Evidence against. "What facts don't fit? Was there a time you didn't believe it?"
4. Alternatives. "Another way to read this? What would you tell a friend you cared about if they said it?"
5. Decatastrophize. "Worst case? Best case? Most realistic case? If the worst happened, could you cope, how?"
6. Synthesize. "So pulling that together, where does it leave the original thought?"

**If it stalls:** **behavioral experiment** (5); the **double-standard** technique (`self-esteem-motivation.md`); or if the thought might be true, pivot to **problem-solving** (8).

## 5 · Behavioral experiment

**Reach for it when:** a thought "feels" true no matter how much you dispute it. You stop arguing and gather real-world evidence. One of the most powerful moves in the whole toolkit, especially for anxiety.

**Walk it through:**
1. State the prediction concretely and testably; rate belief 0-100%. "If you did X, what exactly do you predict happens?"
2. Design the test. "What's the smallest real action that would test that? Where, when?" Decide which safety behaviors to drop.
3. Lock the prediction. "What specifically are you betting will happen, and how would you know?"
4. (They run it, in-session if possible, or as homework.) Observe and record what *actually* happened.
5. Compare outcome vs prediction. "How did it line up with the bet?"
6. Draw the conclusion; re-rate the belief.

**If it stalls:** result discounted → repeat with a tighter prediction and no safety behaviors. Too anxiety-provoking → build a **graded exposure** ladder (7). Feared thing actually happened → use it as data for **problem-solving** (8) and coping ("it was survivable").

## 6 · Behavioral activation

**Reach for it when:** low mood, withdrawal, "can't be bothered", anhedonia, the depression spiral of doing less → fewer rewards → lower mood. First-line for depression. Full depression-specific form (pleasure/mastery ratings) in `depression.md`.

**Walk it through:**
1. Brief monitoring. "Roughly what does a day look like right now, hour to hour, and where's the mood in it?" (Look for the withdrawal pattern.)
2. Values check. "What matters to you that's gone quiet, that you've stopped doing?"
3. Pick activities mixing **pleasure** and **mastery** (accomplishment); start small. "What's one small thing, doable today, in each bucket?"
4. Schedule it concretely, what / when / where, easiest first. "When exactly, today?"
5. The rule: act first, mood follows, do it regardless of motivation. Optionally re-rate mastery/pleasure 0-10 after.

**If it stalls:** anhedonia → go smaller (5-minute tasks), lean on mastery/values, use the **pleasure-predicting sheet** (`depression.md`). Procrastination → **anti-procrastination sheet** / 5-minute rule (`self-esteem-motivation.md`). Blocking thoughts dominate → add a **thought record** (3).

## 7 · Graded exposure

**Reach for it when:** any fear maintained by avoidance, phobia, panic, social anxiety, health anxiety. Avoidance keeps fear alive; repeated, prolonged confrontation without safety behaviors produces new learning. Specialized forms: interoceptive (`panic.md`), social (`social-anxiety.md`), in-vivo phobia (`phobia.md`), ERP (`ocd.md`, clinician-guided).

**Walk it through:**
1. Define the fear concretely; downward-arrow to the core feared outcome. "If that happened, what would it mean / what then?" (repeat to the bottom).
2. Brainstorm 10-15 situations from mild to worst.
3. Rate each SUDS 0-100 (0 none; 30 low; 50-75 significant; 100 extreme). Order lowest → highest with manageable gaps.
4. Start around SUDS 30.
5. Enter and **stay** until anxiety drops noticeably (about half the peak), no escape, no safety behaviors. Log start / peak / end SUDS and duration.
6. Repeat the same rung until it reliably barely registers, then move up.

**If it stalls:** add smaller intermediate rungs; use imaginal or virtual before in-vivo; hunt and drop hidden safety behaviors; lengthen or repeat; shift to the inhibitory-learning frame (the point is violating the feared expectation, not just waiting out anxiety). If an exposure is set as homework, capture the result in the session's `cbt-log` Follow-up (`logging.md`).

## 8 · Problem-solving

**Reach for it when:** the distress is driven by a real, solvable problem, not a distortion. If the thought is **true**, don't restructure it, solve it.

**Walk it through:**
1. Positive orientation. Reframe from "why is this happening to me" to "how can I…". "What's the 'how can I' version of this?"
2. Define the problem concretely; separate facts from assumptions. "What's the actual problem, in one sentence of facts?"
3. Set a realistic, specific goal.
4. Brainstorm options without judging, quantity first. "Give me five, even bad ones."
5. Weigh pros/cons; pick the best or least-worst.
6. Make an action plan, concrete steps and when.
7. (Later) review; if it failed, loop back to step 4.

**If it stalls:** genuinely unsolvable → shift to emotion-focused coping (acceptance, distress tolerance, **relaxation** 9) and restructure the demand that it *must* be solved. Brainstorm stalls → lower the bar or get an outside view.

## 9 · Relaxation

**Reach for it when:** physiological arousal in anxiety and stress, as a coping / down-regulation skill. **Caution (panic):** breathing can become a safety behavior that undermines interoceptive exposure, keep it general wellbeing, not an anti-panic crutch (`safety.md`, `panic.md`).

**Walk it through:**

**Diaphragmatic breathing**, guide it live, one cue at a time, don't just describe it: one hand on chest, one on belly → slow inhale through the nose, belly rising → slower exhale, longer than the inhale → steady rhythm, about in-for-4, out-for-6 → keep it ~5 minutes. Check in: "What's the body doing now?"

**Progressive muscle relaxation**, for each group: tense on the inhale ~5s → release all at once on the exhale → notice the softening ~10-20s. Work up the body: forehead → eyes → jaw → neck/shoulders → arms/hands → chest/stomach → lower back → thighs → calves/feet.

**If it stalls:** if relaxation *increases* anxiety, stop forcing it, switch to **grounding** (`trauma.md`) or brief mindfulness. For panic, prioritize **interoceptive exposure** (`panic.md`). If breathing is becoming a safety behavior, reframe it as general wellbeing. The structured 7-skill **applied relaxation** course is in `anxiety-worry.md`.
