## How to use this file

The anger layer: self-monitoring, de-escalation, and the rational-emotive disputing that targets the "should"/"must" demands driving anger. Assumes the nine in `core-toolkit.md` (restructuring, thought record, problem-solving, relaxation) and adds the anger-specific framing on top. Deliver every technique per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.** The model here is the Aggression Cycle (buildup → explosion at "10" → aftermath); every skill aims to interrupt during buildup.

---

## 1 · Anger meter (1-10 self-monitoring)

**Reach for it when:** the baseline for any anger work; catch anger as it climbs so a coping skill lands before "10" (loss of control, consequences they'll regret).

**Walk it through:**
1. Calibrate the scale to the user. Ask: "If 1 is complete calm and 10 is full loss of control with consequences, where are you right now?"
2. Find their mid-scale tell. Ask: "When you're at a 5 or 6, what's the first cue, the body, the thought, the urge?" (the personal early-warning sign).
3. Map the just-passed episode onto an anger-awareness record. Ask one at a time: the **event**, the **cues**, the **anger-meter number** it hit, the **behavior**, the **consequences**.
4. Plant the mid-scale decision point. Ask: "At your number now, what's the one anger-control move you'll reach for before it climbs?"

**If it stalls:** can't read a number → use a felt-sense word ("simmering", "boiling") and proceed. Already high (8+) and can't think → **anger relaxation** (4) or DBT **TIPP** (`third-wave.md`) first, monitor after. Cue-spotting is blank → walk one concrete recent episode frame by frame via the **CBT triangle** (`core-toolkit.md`).

## 2 · Time-out

**Reach for it when:** they're climbing past the point where they can engage well (roughly 7+), or an argument is escalating toward "10". Leaving the situation beats winning it.

**Walk it through:**
1. Decide informal vs full. Ask: "Can you just disengage quietly here, or do you need to tell them you're stepping out?"
2. If full, script the exit so it doesn't read as storming off. Ask: "What's the one line that says you need a break and will come back to it calm?"
3. Pair it with a down-regulator. Ask: "While you're out, what's the move, a walk, the breathing? How long before you're ready to return?"
4. Set the re-entry. Ask: "What's the signal you're back under the threshold and can pick it up again?"

**If it stalls:** can't physically leave → **anger relaxation** (4) in place. Guilt about walking out reads as abandonment → **A-B-C-D** (3) on the belief that stepping away is failing. Conflict still unresolved on return → **assertiveness** (5) or **problem-solving** (`core-toolkit.md`).

## 3 · A-B-C-D cognitive restructuring (rational-emotive)

**Reach for it when:** the anger is driven by a "should" or "must" demand on the world or other people. The interpretation, not the event, produces the anger. This is restructuring (`core-toolkit.md`) in rational-emotive form, built for anger.

**Walk it through:**
1. **A, activating event.** Ask: "What's the red-flag event, just the facts, that set it off?"
2. **B, belief / self-talk.** Ask: "What were you telling yourself, the 'should' or 'must' in it?" (e.g. "I must always be in control", "everyone should follow the rules", "life should be fair", "I must be respected by everyone").
3. **C, consequence.** Ask: "Given that self-talk, what did you feel, and rate the anger 0-100?" (Make the belief → feeling link explicit, the C follows from B, not from A.)
4. **D, dispute.** Ask: "What's the rational version that fits reality?" Draw it out, don't hand it over (e.g. "I must always be in control" → "I have no power over what I cannot control; I have to accept what I can't change").
5. Re-rate the anger against the new belief.

**If it stalls:** too aroused to think → **anger relaxation** (4) first, then return. Belief feels true → run the full 7-column **thought record** (`core-toolkit.md`) or test it as a **behavioral experiment**. Demand is "this shouldn't have to be solved but it does" → **cost-benefit** (6) on holding the demand.

## 4 · Anger relaxation / deep-breathing

**Reach for it when:** physiological anger arousal, the stress response and relaxation can't co-occur, so down-regulating the body buys back the capacity to choose. Applies to anxiety/trauma arousal too.

**Walk it through (guide it live, one cue at a time, don't just describe it):**
1. Scan for tension from the feet upward. Ask: "Starting at your feet, where's the tension sitting?"
2. Bring attention to the breath. One slow deep breath filling the lungs and chest → hold a second → release and exhale slowly. Cue it, then ask: "What's the body doing now?"
3. Continue the rhythm about 2 minutes. (Escalating fast? Shorten to three deep breaths.)
4. Re-read the anger meter. Ask: "Where's the number now versus when we started?"

**If it stalls:** breathing isn't touching it → **time-out** (2) to remove themselves, or **urge surfing** (`habits.md`) for the anger urge itself. Arousal is very high → DBT **TIPP** (`third-wave.md`). Tension-focused work needed → progressive muscle relaxation (relaxation, `core-toolkit.md`).

## 5 · Assertiveness & conflict resolution

**Reach for it when:** they need to express the anger and get the need met without harm, the line between assertive (state issue + need), aggressive (dominate, intimidate), and passive (swallow it).

**Walk it through:**
1. Locate where they usually land. Ask: "On this one, are you tending aggressive, passive, or assertive?"
2. Separate the issue from the attack. Ask: "What's the actual issue and what do you need, said without a jab in it?"
3. Build the assertive line in their words. Ask: "How would you state that so it names the need, not their character?"
4. Anticipate the response. Ask: "If they push back, what's the assertive hold, not escalating, not folding?"

**If it stalls:** the script keeps tipping into attack → **A-B-C-D** (3) on the belief fueling the aggression (often a "should" about respect). The conflict has a real solvable core → **problem-solving** (`core-toolkit.md`). They freeze into passivity → check for an underlying belief (`self-esteem-motivation.md`).

## 6 · Cost-benefit of anger ("payoffs and consequences")

**Reach for it when:** anger has become a maintained habit, kept alive by short-term payoffs (feels powerful, ends the argument, gets compliance) at long-term cost.

**Walk it through:**
1. Name the apparent payoffs honestly. Ask: "What does the anger actually get you in the moment?"
2. Name the long-term consequences. Ask: "And what does it cost you over weeks and months, the relationships, the aftermath?"
3. Weigh the two columns side by side. Ask: "Laid out together, does the trade still look worth it?"
4. Land the alternative. Ask: "If the payoff is X, what's a move that gets X without the cost?"

**If it stalls:** wants the full structured version → Burns **cost-benefit analysis** (`self-esteem-motivation.md`). The payoff is tied to a demand → **A-B-C-D** (3) on that demand. If anger and a substance habit feed each other for this user, that's a recurring theme, note the link in `CLAUDE.md` (`logging.md`) and flag it when it surfaces.
