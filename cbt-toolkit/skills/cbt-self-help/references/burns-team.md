## How to use this file

These are Burns / TEAM-CBT methods. What sets TEAM apart from Beckian CBT is that it reduces **resistance** before applying any change method, so reach for the resistance tools (1, 2, 6) whenever the user "knows what to do but can't get going", or has mixed feelings about changing. Assumes `core-toolkit.md`; deliver per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.** The TEAM frame: outcome resistance (mixed feelings about recovery itself, hidden benefits of staying stuck) vs process resistance (unwillingness to do the work), melt that first. The Daily Mood Log, examine-the-evidence, survey, double-standard, anti-procrastination, and pleasure-predicting are Burns methods too but live in `depression.md` / `self-esteem-motivation.md`; this file is the home for the resistance-focused and flagship-change methods. The core nine live in `core-toolkit.md`, reference them by name, never re-document.

## 1 · Positive reframing

**Reach for it when:** they know the move but won't start, or are ambivalent about giving up the feeling. Paradoxical agenda-setting done **before** any change technique, to surface subconscious resistance. *(Steps below are from a secondary summary; verbatim Burns wording not obtained, so hold them loosely.)*

**Walk it through:**
1. Take one negative thought or feeling they're carrying (ideally already on a Daily Mood Log, `depression.md`). Ask: "Take that feeling, what's actually good, healthy, or adaptive about it?"
2. Connect it to a value. Ask: "What does feeling this show about what you care about?" (anxiety can show conscientiousness; shame can reflect respect for their own standards).
3. Name the bind out loud: the feeling has a real upside, so part of them has a reason to keep it. Ask: "Given that, do you still want to turn it all the way down?"

**If it stalls:** if the upside won't surface, run the **magic button / magic dial** (2); if they're weighing whether to change at all, **cost-benefit analysis** (6).

## 2 · Magic button & magic dial

**Reach for it when:** straight after positive reframing, to convert the surfaced upside into a calibrated goal rather than "make it all go away". *(Steps from secondary summaries; verbatim Burns steps not obtained.)*

**Walk it through:**
1. The button. Ask: "Imagine a button: press it and every negative feeling vanishes instantly, no effort. Would you press it?" (Most say yes.)
2. Expose the cost. If yes, name what the button also deletes, the upsides from reframing (1). Ask: "If pressing it also wiped out the conscientiousness / the caring underneath, still press it?"
3. Swap to the dial. Ask: "Instead of a button, a dial for each feeling. Where would you set it, low enough to function, high enough to keep its signal?" Set a target number per emotion.

**If it stalls:** if they won't release the button fantasy, **cost-benefit analysis** (6); once a dial target is set, proceed to the truth-based or uncovering methods below.

## 3 · Externalization of voices (+ self-defense + acceptance paradox + counterattack)

**Reach for it when:** intellectual insight isn't reaching gut-level, for self-criticism, low self-esteem, depression. Burns calls it among the most powerful CBT techniques; the engine is a two-voice role-play run **with** them, not described to them.

**Walk it through:**
1. Set up the two voices. Take one self-attacking thought verbatim. Explain: one voice attacks (their negative thought), one defends (the realistic reply), and you'll trade roles when either gets stuck.
2. Pick the grammar. Ask: "Which do you want, 'I-I' (both voices first-person, gentler, safer) or 'You-I' (the attack speaks as 'You', the defense as 'I', more intimidating but far more healing power)?" Offer You-I if they can take it.
3. Run it. You voice the attack in the chosen form ("You're a fraud and everyone can tell"); they answer as the defending voice. Ask after each round: "What does the defending voice say back?" Reverse roles when they stall.
4. Layer in **self-defense**: the reply explains *why* the attack is distorted ("that's overgeneralizing from one talk"). Ask: "Where's the distortion in what the attack just said?"
5. Layer in the **acceptance paradox**: find the kernel of truth and accept the shortcoming with tranquility ("It's true I have real inadequacies, and I can live with that"). Burns: self-defense + acceptance paradox combined is by far the most effective, so push for both. Ask: "What's the grain of truth here you could just accept?"
6. Optionally **counterattack**: the defending voice turns and names the real problem as the act of listening to the relentless self-attack itself. Ask: "What would you say back to the part of you that keeps running this attack?"

**If it stalls:** the **double-standard** move (`self-esteem-motivation.md`); **examine-the-evidence** (`self-esteem-motivation.md` / `depression.md`); the **survey technique** (`self-esteem-motivation.md`). Known-but-not-felt persists, try **third-wave** defusion (`third-wave.md`).

## 4 · Hidden emotion technique

**Reach for it when:** anxiety, especially when other methods stall. The theory: anxious "nice" people suppress a negative feeling that resurfaces as the anxiety. *(The "70-80% of anxiety is suppressed emotion" figure is illustrative only, not verified, don't quote it as fact.)*

**Walk it through:**
1. Reframe the anxiety as a cover. Ask: "Is it possible you're focusing on the anxiety to avoid something more upsetting? What's the real problem you're not looking at?"
2. Go after the feeling underneath. Ask directly: "Do you secretly resent someone or something here, a person, the work? How do you actually feel about it?"
3. Bring it into the open and let them express it (in words now, in action later). Often the anxiety drops fast once the real feeling is named.

**If it stalls:** the **what-if technique** (5) to find the feared fantasy instead; standard **interoceptive / in-vivo exposure** (`panic.md`, `core-toolkit.md`); **assertiveness** (`self-esteem-motivation.md`) to express the surfaced feeling.

## 5 · What-if technique

**Reach for it when:** anxiety, to uncover the deepest feared fantasy under a worry (Uncovering method, attributed to Ellis).

**Walk it through:**
1. Take the anxious thought and drop a downward arrow. Ask: "If that were true, what's the worst that could happen? What do you fear most?"
2. Write the new fear under the arrow, then repeat. Ask the same question of each answer until you hit bottom, the most terrifying fantasy.
3. At the bottom, reality-test it. Ask: "How likely is that, actually? And if it did happen, could you live with it, how?"

**If it stalls:** **decatastrophizing** / Socratic worst-best-realistic (`core-toolkit.md`); **examine-the-evidence** (`depression.md`); **acceptance paradox** (3) applied to the feared fantasy.

## 6 · Cost-benefit analysis

**Reach for it when:** ambivalence or resistance about a thought, belief, feeling, or habit, the "I know I should but" state. The home resistance tool when motivation, not logic, is the block.

**Walk it through:**
**Straightforward:**
1. Name the target (a belief, feeling, or habit). Ask: "What does holding onto this actually get you, the advantages?"
2. Ask: "And what does it cost you, the disadvantages?"
3. Weigh them. Ask: "Out of 100 split between the two columns, where does it land?" If costs win, motivation to change is now real.

**Paradoxical** (when straightforward stalls or they're defensive): list **only** the advantages, then ask: "Given all these advantages, why would you change at all?" Let them argue themselves toward change by feeling the forces keeping them stuck.

**If it stalls:** **positive reframing** (1); **magic button / dial** (2); once they're motivated, the **anti-procrastination sheet** / 5-minute rule (`self-esteem-motivation.md`).

## 7 · Paradoxical magnification

**Reach for it when:** a negative thought resists every refutation. Instead of arguing it, you buy in and exaggerate it to absurdity (Semantic / Humor-based).

**Walk it through:**
1. Take the stubborn thought. Don't dispute it, amplify it. Ask: "Let's go all the way, what's the most extreme version? 'I'm probably the single most inferior person in California'?"
2. Push it past plausibility together until the absurdity is felt, then check the charge. Ask: "Saying it that big, what happened to how true it feels?"

**If it stalls:** Burns's own built-in caution, verbatim: "if you're really upset, this technique may have the unintended effect of making you feel even worse. If so, try another method." If it backfires, switch to the **acceptance paradox** (3) or **examine-the-evidence** (`depression.md`).

## 8 · Other truth-based methods

**Reach for it when:** a depressive overgeneralization or an anxious prediction needs plain reality-testing. Four quick moves, run any one per the loop:
- **Examine the evidence** ("What are the facts?"), documented in `depression.md` / `self-esteem-motivation.md`.
- **Experimental technique** (design a real-world test like a scientist), this is the **behavioral experiment** in `core-toolkit.md`; Burns notes it enables extremely rapid treatment of panic.
- **Survey technique** (ask others whether the belief is realistic), documented in `self-esteem-motivation.md`.
- **Reattribution** (list **all** contributing factors instead of self-blame). Ask: "What did you actually contribute here, and what did others or circumstances contribute?"

**If it stalls:** rotate to another of the four, or to **externalization of voices** (3).
