## How to use this file

Self-help-safe to deliver: the psychoeducation (1), the cognitive framing (2), and small self-chosen response-prevention / urge-tolerance dosing (the safe entry inside 3). NOT to self-run: the full ERP hierarchy and imaginal exposure (3, 4). Present those as what a clinician-guided program does and why, never as a protocol to walk the user through alone. The core nine (downward-arrow, SUDS, graded exposure) live in `core-toolkit.md`, reference by name. For crisis-level distress, DBT TIPP is in `third-wave.md`.

ERP is first-line / treatment of choice for OCD with large effects (pooled g=0.74). Honest caveat to hold: it is not proven superior to all active comparators (Reid 2021 found it no more effective than other active psychotherapies, effect tied to researcher allegiance), and metacognitive therapy is an emerging non-inferior alternative. So: first-line, not magic.

## 1 · OCD psychoeducation

**Reach for it when:** the user is caught in an obsession-compulsion loop, an intrusive thought / image / urge drives anxiety, the compulsion (overt or mental) buys brief relief, and the relief teaches the loop to repeat. Teach the mechanism before anything else.

**Walk it through:**
1. Name the two halves: the **obsession** (the intrusive thought, image, object, or situation that spikes anxiety) and the **compulsion** (what they do, or avoid, to neutralize it).
2. Land the trap: the compulsion works, briefly, and that short-term relief is exactly what keeps the obsession powerful. Each ritual teaches "I only coped because I did the thing."
3. Frame the exit: relief has to come from *not* doing the ritual and letting the anxiety fall on its own. That is what ERP trains, and why it wants a clinician at first.

**If it stalls:** if intrusive thoughts themselves are the distress (not visible rituals), go to the cognitive framing (2) on thought-action fusion before any exposure talk.

## 2 · OCD cognitive framing (inflated responsibility, thought-action fusion)

**Reach for it when:** the obsession is belief-driven, "if I think it, it's as bad as doing it" or "if I don't check / prevent this, the harm is on me." This is the self-help-safe cognitive layer that sits alongside ERP. Clinician-guided in full, but the framing itself is safe to teach.

**The two belief targets** (name them, they like that):
- **Inflated responsibility:** an exaggerated sense of duty to prevent harm, as if they alone are the firewall and any bad outcome is their fault.
- **Thought-action fusion:** treating a thought as morally equivalent to the act, or as making the act more likely, just by having occurred.

**Walk it through:**
1. Surface the belief verbatim. "What does having this thought *mean* about you, in your words?" Rate belief 0-100%.
2. Name which it is, inflated responsibility, thought-action fusion, or both. Add the thought-suppression trap: trying not to think it makes it louder.
3. Test it gently with the standard cognitive moves in `core-toolkit.md` (evidence for / against, the double-standard, "would you hold a friend to this?"), without arguing the intrusive content itself.

**If it stalls:** belief won't move with cognition alone, that is expected, the durable change comes from behavioral ERP (3), which is clinician-guided. Flag it and hand the full work to the clinician. Metacognitive therapy is an alternative the clinician may consider.

## 3 · Exposure & Response Prevention (ERP)

> [!warning] Clinician-guided, not to be self-run alone. The full hierarchy below is **educational**: this is what a clinician-led program does, not a protocol to walk the user through. Offer it so they understand the treatment, then point to the safe entry at the bottom.

**Reach for it when:** they ask what actual OCD treatment looks like, or want to understand why their rituals persist. Two parts: **Exposure** (confronting the feared thought / image / object / situation) and **Response Prevention** (choosing not to perform the compulsion). Clinician-guided at first, then self-run once trained.

**What a clinician-guided program does (educational, do not self-run):**
1. With the clinician, downward-arrow ("What would that mean? Why would that bother you?") to pinpoint the core fear; map the avoided situations.
2. Choose the exposure type (imaginal / in-vivo / virtual / interoceptive).
3. Rate scenarios SUDS 0-100 (0 calm, 100 worst imaginable).
4. Build a hierarchy of 10-15 items, ascending, starting around SUDS 30.
5. Enter the feared situation and **stay** until SUDS drops ~50%, **preventing the ritual throughout**.
6. Vary proximity / duration, repeat each rung, then move up. (Complete response prevention beats partial; therapist-guided beats self-guided.)

**The safe entry (self-help-safe):** a small, self-chosen piece of response prevention, picking one **low-stakes** compulsion and delaying or dropping it in a deliberate, manageable dose, then tolerating the urge and watching it crest and fall rather than acting on it. Low-stakes means **no real-world safety stake**: not gas / stove, locks, electrics, driving, or medical checks, where the feared harm has a genuine dimension, those stay with the clinician and get psychoeducation + cognitive framing (2) only. One compulsion, one short delay, their choice; stop if distress climbs and won't settle. The full hierarchy stays with the clinician.

**If it stalls:** if direct confrontation isn't appropriate (e.g. harm fears), a clinician adds **imaginal exposure** (4); add the **cognitive work** (2); the clinician may consider metacognitive therapy or ERP plus SSRI. None of these are self-run.

## 4 · Imaginal exposure (within ERP)

> [!warning] Clinician-guided, not to be self-run alone. Educational framing only.

**Reach for it when:** explaining how OCD fears that can't be safely confronted in real life (e.g. fear of harming someone) are treated. Used inside a clinician-led ERP program, not as a standalone self-help exercise.

**What a clinician-guided program does (educational, do not self-run):** the person narrates a vivid description of the most-feared outcome and continues until anxiety reduces by at least half. Combining imaginal with in-vivo exposure outperforms in-vivo alone.

**If it stalls:** the clinician combines it with in-vivo **ERP** (3) and adds **cognitive work** (2) on responsibility and thought-action fusion. The safe layer the user can engage solo remains the psychoeducation (1) and cognitive framing (2).
