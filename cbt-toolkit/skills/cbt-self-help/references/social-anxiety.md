## How to use this file

The specialized layer for **social anxiety**. It assumes the transdiagnostic core in `core-toolkit.md`, especially **graded exposure** (technique 7) and **behavioral experiment** (technique 5), and adds the Clark & Wells CT-SAD pieces: exposure with safety behaviors dropped, attention shifted outward, video feedback, and shame-attacking. Build any exposure steps fresh with them in the moment. Deliver every step per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.**

**Post-event rumination (the event is already over).** If the complaint is replaying something done ("I'm sure everyone thought I sounded stupid"), the live problem is the post-mortem and the mind-reading in it, not a future situation. Don't open a forward exposure: restructure the mind-read (`core-toolkit.md` 2) and name the post-event processing itself as a safety behavior to drop, it feels productive but only deepens the distorted self-image. Move to forward exposure only once the rumination loop is addressed.

## 1 · Exposure + dropping safety behaviors

**Reach for it when:** anxiety about being judged in a social situation. The standard active treatment. It is graded exposure (`core-toolkit.md` technique 7) framed as a **behavioral experiment** (technique 5): the specialized layer is that the work is dropping safety behaviors and shifting attention outward, not just staying in the room.

**Walk it through:**
1. Pick a feared situation and pin the specific prediction. Ask: "What exactly do you predict will happen, in detail, 'I'll blush and they'll laugh'?"
2. List the safety behaviors they use there, rehearsing lines, gripping a glass, avoiding eye contact, over-preparing. Ask: "What are you doing in that situation to feel safer? Name them."
3. Enter the situation and deliberately **drop** them. Ask: "Which of those can you go in without this time?"
4. Shift attention **outward** onto the conversation and the other people, not onto themselves (see 2). Ask: "Where was your attention, on you, or on them?"
5. Afterwards, check what *actually* happened against the prediction. Ask: "How did the real outcome line up with the bet you made in step 1?"
6. Repeat across situations.

**If it stalls:** self-focus won't release → **external-attention training** (2). Distorted self-image persists ("I looked awful") → **video/audio feedback** (3). Fear is specifically of disapproval → **shame-attacking** (4). Need outside data on how they came across → **surveys** (3.7 of the broader anxiety set). Deep-rooted → full CT-SAD with imagery/memory rescripting.

## 2 · External-attention training

**Reach for it when:** self-monitoring during social situations is feeding the anxiety. Self-focus distorts perception and amplifies anxiety; moving attention outward gives accurate social data. Use it standalone and inside the exposures in (1).

**Walk it through:**
1. Catch the self-monitoring. Ask: "When you're anxious socially, how much of your attention is on watching yourself, how you sound, look, come across?"
2. Deliberately move attention to the external task, what others are saying, the room, sounds, details. Ask: "Right now, name three concrete things outside yourself you can attend to instead?"
3. Rehearse it in low-stakes settings first. Ask: "Where this week could you practice this with low stakes, a shop, a casual chat?"
4. Then use it during exposures to gather real evidence. Ask: "Next exposure, can you run attention outward the whole time and report what you actually noticed about *them*?"

**If it stalls:** attention keeps snapping back inward → shorten the task, practice lower-stakes first. Self-image stays negative despite outward focus → **video/audio feedback** (3).

## 3 · Video/audio feedback

**Reach for it when:** they believe their anxiety is far more visible than it is ("I was shaking visibly", "bright red"). People with social anxiety overestimate how visible their anxiety looks; recorded feedback corrects the distorted self-image and has a strong effect on self-appraisal.

**Walk it through:**
1. Predict in detail how they'll look on video, specific and ratable. Ask: "Before we record, exactly how do you predict you'll look, what will be visible?"
2. Do a recorded social task (a short talk, a conversation).
3. Before watching, decide to view it as a stranger would, not as themselves. Ask: "Can you commit to watching this the way you'd watch a stranger, not hunting for flaws?"
4. Watch it.
5. Compare to the prediction (usually far less visible than feared). Ask: "How much of what you predicted is actually there on screen?"
6. Update the belief.

**If it stalls:** they discount the video ("I was just having a good day") → repeat on another task; pair with **surveys** (3.7). Belief won't move → look for an underlying self-defeating belief (`self-esteem-motivation.md`).

## 4 · Shame-attacking exercises

**Reach for it when:** the core fear is disapproval itself, that others thinking badly of them would be unbearable. Ellis's REBT move: deliberately do something mildly embarrassing in public to learn that disapproval is bearable.

**Walk it through:**
1. Pick a harmless shame-triggering act, no harm, no law-breaking (loudly calling out bus stops, singing on a corner, repeatedly asking strangers the time). Ask: "What's a harmless but embarrassing thing you could do in public?"
2. Start with the least scary one. Ask: "Which of those is the mildest place to start?"
3. Do it deliberately in public.
4. Dispute the irrational belief underneath. Ask: "What were you telling yourself, 'it would be awful if they disapproved'? Is that true, or just uncomfortable?"
5. Notice they survived and the disapproval was tolerable, then step up. Ask: "What actually happened, and how bad was it really? Ready for a harder one?"

**If it stalls:** too steep to start → smaller, more private acts first. Belief that disapproval is catastrophic persists → address the **self-defeating beliefs** directly (`self-esteem-motivation.md`); combine with **surveys** (3.7) and full CT-SAD imagery work.
