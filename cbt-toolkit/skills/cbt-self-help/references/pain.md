## How to use this file

This is the chronic-pain layer. It assumes the nine in `core-toolkit.md` and is delivered per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.** Set expectations honestly up front: CBT has the largest evidence base of any psychological approach to chronic pain, but its effect on **raw pain intensity is negligible**, the robust gains are in disability, distress, mood, and catastrophizing. Don't promise the pain will shrink or vanish; the target is a fuller, less pain-governed life. Cognitive reappraisal here is the core **thought record / decatastrophizing** (`core-toolkit.md` techniques 3 and 4) pointed at pain thoughts, referenced by name. Because the effect on raw pain is limited, **acceptance** (`third-wave.md`) is a load-bearing partner, not a fallback.

## 1 · Activity pacing & graded activity

**Reach for it when:** the boom-and-bust cycle, good day -> overdo it -> flare -> crash and rest -> overdo it again. Replaces pain-contingent activity with steady, quota-based, gradually rising activity.

**Walk it through:**
1. Surface the boom-and-bust pattern with an activity diary. Ask: "On a good day, what do you push through, and what does the crash afterward cost you?"
2. Find baseline tolerance: the amount of an activity doable on a **bad** day without triggering a flare. Ask: "For [the activity], how much could you do on a rough day and still be okay, not your best day, your bad one?"
3. Break tasks into smaller chunks with scheduled rest breaks built in *before* the pain forces them. Ask: "How would you slice [task] into chunks, and where do the breaks go?"
4. Act by time or quota, not by pain, stop at the quota even on a good day ("stop before it hurts more"). Ask: "What's the quota you'll hold whether the day is good or bad?"
5. Increase quotas in small, planned increments on a schedule, regardless of day-to-day pain. Ask: "What's the next small step up, and when do you add it?"

**If it stalls:** fear of movement blocks pacing → **cognitive reappraisal** (2) on the catastrophic thought, plus graded **in-vivo exposure** (`core-toolkit.md` technique 7) for fear-avoided movements. Arousal/tension high → **relaxation & activity scheduling** (3). Quota keeps getting broken on good days → shrink the quota, the discipline is holding it when you feel able.

## 2 · Cognitive reappraisal of pain catastrophizing

**Reach for it when:** catastrophic pain appraisals are driving disability and distress, "this pain means something is seriously wrong", "I can't cope with this", "it's only going to get worse". Catastrophizing is a key driver of pain-related disability, more than intensity itself.

**Walk it through:** run the core **thought record** (`core-toolkit.md` technique 3) and **decatastrophizing** within **Socratic questioning** (`core-toolkit.md` technique 4) on the catastrophic pain thought, one step per turn. Catch it verbatim ("What does the pain *mean* to you in that moment?"), rate belief, examine evidence (has the feared catastrophe actually materialized across past flares?), then decatastrophize: worst case, most realistic case, and crucially "if it flared, how have you actually coped before?" Build a **coping-focused** alternative, not a "there's nothing wrong" denial, in their words. Re-rate.

**If it stalls:** belief won't move with evidence → **activity pacing** (1) as lived disconfirmation that they can do more than the thought predicts. Arousal too high to think → **relaxation** (3) first. "Know it's catastrophizing but still feel it", or the thought is partly true (real condition) → **acceptance** (`third-wave.md`), making room for pain while acting on values, given the limited effect on raw intensity.

## 3 · Relaxation & activity scheduling

**Reach for it when:** high arousal/muscle tension amplifying the pain, and a life that has narrowed to the pain, rewarding activities dropped off. Reduces arousal and rebuilds reward.

**Walk it through:**
1. Relaxation: use diaphragmatic breathing and PMR from `core-toolkit.md` (technique 9), guided live one cue at a time. Check in after: "What's the body and the pain doing now versus when we started?"
2. Activity scheduling: this is **behavioral activation** (`core-toolkit.md` technique 6) in a pain context, schedule pleasant, valued activities back in, paced and small. Ask: "What's one rewarding thing pain has crowded out that you could schedule, in a paced-down form, this week?"

**If it stalls:** scheduled activity triggers flares → re-pace via **activity pacing** (1). Catastrophic thoughts block scheduling ("no point, it'll just hurt") → **cognitive reappraisal** (2). Beyond self-help scope → refer to a structured pain-management program (e.g. VA CBT-CP).
