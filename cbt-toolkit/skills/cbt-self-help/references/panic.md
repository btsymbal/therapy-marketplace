## How to use this file

Panic disorder: the specialized layer on top of the core nine in `core-toolkit.md` (graded exposure, behavioral experiment, cognitive restructuring, relaxation), which this file references by name and never re-documents. Deliver every technique per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.**

**Safety framing, keep it live the whole session.** In panic, slow breathing and relaxation used *to stop* an attack become a safety behavior that prevents disconfirmation and undermines interoceptive exposure. Keep breathing as general wellbeing, never an anti-panic crutch (`safety.md`, and the panic caution in `core-toolkit.md` 9). Before suggesting any physical induction, check for relevant medical conditions (cardiac, respiratory, pregnancy, epilepsy); if any are in play, don't run inductions, route to `safety.md`.

## 1 · Interoceptive exposure

**Reach for it when:** panic is maintained by catastrophic misinterpretation of bodily arousal (racing heart, dizziness, breathlessness) and avoidance of the sensations themselves. The single most crucial active component of panic CBT.

**Walk it through:**
1. Find the feared sensation. Ask: "When panic starts, which body sensation is the one you most want to make stop, the racing heart, the dizziness, the breathlessness, the unreality?"
2. **Medical gate (hard precondition, do not skip):** before any physical induction, confirm no cardiac or respiratory condition, pregnancy, or epilepsy. If any apply, or it's unknown, don't run inductions, work the cognitive arm (2) and panic surfing (3) instead. Then pick the induction that reproduces *their* sensation and run it with them. Offer the matching one and ask them to confirm before starting: hyperventilate ~60s (dizziness/tingling); breathe through a thin straw, nose pinched (smothering); spin in a chair (dizziness/derealization); head between legs then sit up fast (lightheadedness); shake the head side to side ~30s (dizziness); run on the spot / step-ups (racing heart, breathlessness); hold the breath (chest tightness); tense the whole body (trembling); stare at a spot or light (derealization). Ask: "Which of these matches it closest, want to start there?"
3. Run the induction long enough to bring the sensation on (~30-60s). Cue it live and stay present. Ask: "Tell me when it's coming on, what are you noticing?"
4. Rate it. Ask: "Anxiety 0-100, and how close does this feel to a real attack, 0-100?"
5. Stay with it or re-induce rather than escaping, no safety behaviors (no slow breathing to dampen it). Ask: "Can you let the sensation sit there without doing anything to fix it, what happens to the anxiety if you just wait?"
6. Repeat daily until the sensation stops triggering fear, then move to harder inductions and natural settings. Ask: "What's the version of this you could rep on your own this week?"

**If it stalls:** belief that it's dangerous won't drop → **decatastrophizing bodily sensations** (2). They keep damping the sensation → **dropping safety behaviors** (4). Sensation won't come on → tighter / longer induction, or pick the one that better matches their feared symptom. Too flooded to start → down-regulate first via grounding (`trauma.md`), then return; build a **graded exposure** ladder (`core-toolkit.md` 7) if even the mild induction is too much.

## 2 · Decatastrophizing bodily sensations

**Reach for it when:** the cognitive arm of panic, a specific catastrophic prediction ("this is a heart attack", "I'm going crazy", "I'll faint") is firing on the sensation. Pair it with interoceptive exposure as the experiment that tests it.

**Walk it through:**
1. Catch the catastrophic prediction during a sensation, verbatim, belief 0-100%. Ask: "When the heart races, what's the exact thing you predict is about to happen?"
2. Examine the track record. Ask: "How many times have you had this sensation, and how many times did the catastrophe actually happen, the heart attack, the faint, the going crazy?"
3. Generate the benign alternative in their words. Ask: "What's the non-catastrophic explanation for a pounding heart and tingling hands, what is the body actually doing?"
4. Test it with an **interoceptive-exposure** experiment (1), bet stated up front. Ask: "If we bring the sensation on right now, what does the catastrophe theory predict, and what does the benign one predict?"
5. Re-rate the belief. Ask: "After that, how much do you buy the catastrophe now, 0-100?"

**If it stalls:** belief holds despite the evidence → run interoceptive exposure (1) as the behavioral experiment, logic alone won't shift it. Needs an in-the-moment response, not analysis → **panic surfing** (3). Residual crutches blunting the test → **dropping safety behaviors** (4). For the underlying disputation mechanics, see cognitive restructuring and Socratic questioning (`core-toolkit.md` 2, 4).

## 3 · Panic surfing / riding the wave

**Reach for it when:** acceptance-based response *during* an attack, replacing fighting and avoiding with letting the symptoms rise and fall. Use it for in-the-moment coping; it is not a substitute for interoceptive exposure between attacks.

**Walk it through:**
1. Notice and name it rather than suppressing. Ask: "Can you say to yourself, out loud if you can, 'this is a panic attack, it's uncomfortable but not dangerous', what shifts when you name it?"
2. Reframe the threat. Ask: "The symptoms are unpleasant but not a medical emergency, what's the evidence they won't harm you?"
3. Allow the wave to rise and crest and fall instead of resisting, the peak usually passes within ~10 minutes. Ask: "Instead of bracing against it, can you let it build and watch it, where is it on the wave right now?"

**Safety note on breathing:** if you use a paced-breathing step here (in 3s, out 3s), watch that it stays a way of riding the wave, not a lever to *abort* the attack. The moment it becomes "do this to make it stop", it's a safety behavior undermining exposure (`safety.md`). Ask explicitly: "Are you breathing to ride this out, or to switch it off?"

**If it stalls:** the wave is still feared → **interoceptive exposure** (1) so it's no longer dangerous. Breathing turned into a stop-switch → **dropping safety behaviors** (4). Catastrophic thought reignites mid-wave → **decatastrophizing sensations** (2).

## 4 · Dropping safety behaviors

**Reach for it when:** response prevention for panic, subtle crutches (carrying meds or water, sitting near exits, checking the pulse, gripping a rail, having someone present) are preventing disconfirmation and keeping the disorder alive. Often the hidden reason exposure isn't landing.

**Walk it through:**
1. List all of them, including avoidance and the subtle ones. Ask: "What do you carry, check, or do to feel safe when panic might hit, including the small reflexes?"
2. Pick one to deliberately drop during the next exposure or attack. Ask: "Which one do we drop first, don't check the pulse, don't sit near the exit, don't sip the water?"
3. Predict what they fear happens without the crutch, belief 0-100%. Ask: "Without it, what do you predict goes wrong?"
4. Experience the situation or sensation fully, crutch dropped. Ask: "Can you stay in it without reaching for the backup, what's coming up?"
5. Observe that the catastrophe doesn't occur and re-rate the belief. Ask: "It didn't happen, so where's the belief now, 0-100?"

**If it stalls:** the catastrophe seemed averted *because* of a residual crutch → hunt the subtle safety behaviors and drop those too. Avoidance is situational (agoraphobic) → add in-vivo **graded exposure** (`core-toolkit.md` 7). Sensations still feared once crutches are gone → loop back to **interoceptive exposure** (1). Combine drops with interoceptive exposure rather than running them in isolation.
