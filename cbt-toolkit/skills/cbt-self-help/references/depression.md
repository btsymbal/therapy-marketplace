## How to use this file

The depression layer: low mood, anhedonia, withdrawal, do-nothingism, and the heavy depressive thoughts that block action. It assumes the nine in `core-toolkit.md` (behavioral activation, thought record, restructuring) and adds the depression-specific worksheet forms. Procrastination and motivation work lives in `self-esteem-motivation.md`. Deliver every technique per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.** **Safety:** severe depression or any suicidal thoughts need a clinician and crisis help, not this skill, drop the technique and follow `safety.md`.

## 1 · Behavioral activation with pleasure & mastery ratings

**Reach for it when:** depressed inertia, anhedonia, "nothing's worth it", the doing-less → fewer-rewards spiral. First-line for depression. The general form is **behavioral activation** (`core-toolkit.md`, technique 6), run that; this adds only the pleasure/mastery scoring and the predicted-vs-actual-mood layer that disproves "activity won't help".

**Walk it through:**
1. (Assumes you've done the BA core: monitored a day, checked values, picked activities.) Add the two-axis lens. Ask: "For each thing you're scheduling, is it for **pleasure** (enjoyable) or **mastery** (a sense of accomplishment), or both?" They don't always overlap; you want a mix.
2. Predict before doing. Ask: "Before you do it, what mood do you predict you'll be in after, 0 to 100?" Capture the prediction.
3. (They do the activity.) Score it after. Ask: "Now that it's done, rate Mastery 0 to 10 and Pleasure 0 to 10, and where's your mood actually, 0 to 100?"
4. Compare predicted vs actual mood. Ask: "How does the actual lift compare to what you predicted?" Depressed people consistently underestimate this; name the gap if it's there.
5. Tie it back. Ask: "Which of these connects to something you actually value?"

**If it stalls:** can't get started → **anti-procrastination sheet** (`self-esteem-motivation.md`) for 5-minute steps. "Nothing will be enjoyable" dominates → run the **pleasure-predicting sheet** (2) to test it experimentally. Blocking thoughts dominate → **Daily Mood Log** (3) + **examine the evidence** (4) first. Perfectionism blocks starting → **anti-perfectionism** / **TIC-TOC** (`self-esteem-motivation.md`).

## 2 · Pleasure-predicting sheet

**Reach for it when:** anhedonia and the belief "nothing is enjoyable / worth it". It is a behavioral experiment in sheet form: predict satisfaction, do the thing, compare. Part of BA.

**Walk it through (five columns, one at a time, capture predicted vs actual):**
1. **Activity.** Ask: "Name one activity with potential for pleasure, learning, growth, or helping others, not overly burdensome. What is it?"
2. **Date / when.** Ask: "When will you do it?"
3. **Who with.** Ask: "Who are you doing this with?" (If alone, log "self", you can be your own companion, that counts.)
4. **Predicted satisfaction (before).** Ask: "Before doing it, how satisfying do you predict it'll be, 0 to 100%?" Lock the number.
5. (They do it.) **Actual satisfaction (after).** Ask: "Now it's done, how satisfying was it actually, 0 to 100%?"
6. Compare the pair. Ask: "Predicted versus actual, what does the gap say about 'nothing is worth it'?"

**If it stalls:** can't get started → **anti-procrastination sheet** (`self-esteem-motivation.md`). "I won't enjoy it anyway" keeps winning even after the data → run that thought through the **Daily Mood Log** (3) + **examine the evidence** (4).

## 3 · Daily Mood Log (Burns six-step)

**Reach for it when:** a specific upsetting moment in depression (also anxiety). This is the depression-flavored cousin of the **thought record** (`core-toolkit.md`, technique 3): same restructuring logic, Burns's six-step framing. Use whichever they know better; the columns map onto each other.

**Walk it through (six steps, one per turn):**
1. **The event.** Ask: "What's the specific upsetting moment, concretely, who/what/when?" (e.g. "boss criticized my report at the 2 PM meeting", not "work").
2. **Emotions, before.** Ask: "Name each emotion in specific words and rate it 0 to 100%."
3. **Automatic negative thoughts.** Ask: "Write them uncensored, every one, and rate how much you believe each 0 to 100%."
4. **Distortions.** Ask: "Which of the 10 distortions is each thought doing?" (offer the likely two or three if they're stuck; list is in `core-toolkit.md`, technique 2).
5. **Balanced replacement.** Ask: "For each, what's a more truthful, balanced version, in your words? Rate your belief in it 0 to 100%."
6. **Re-rate.** Ask: "Now re-rate your belief in the original thoughts, and re-rate the emotions, 0 to 100%. Where did they land?"

**If it stalls:** belief in the negative thought won't drop on step 5 → run **examine the evidence** (4) on that one thought. Self-attack is the engine → **double-standard technique** or **externalization of voices** (`self-esteem-motivation.md`, `burns-team.md`). Belief still won't move → there's an underlying **self-defeating belief**, target it (`self-esteem-motivation.md`). Cognition too heavy to do at all → **behavioral activation** (1) first, come back.

## 4 · Examine the evidence

**Reach for it when:** one negative thought needs a clean, truth-based test, either standalone or as the deepening of Daily Mood Log step 5. Strips it to evidence rather than reframing by feel.

**Walk it through:**
1. Isolate the thought. Ask: "What's the single specific negative thought we're testing, in one sentence?"
2. Evidence **for**. Ask: "What genuinely supports it?" Take it seriously, don't rush past.
3. Evidence **against**. Ask: "What doesn't fit, including things you'd normally wave away?"
4. Weigh and conclude. Ask: "Holding both columns, what's the balanced, evidence-based read?" Re-rate belief.

**If it stalls:** evidence search comes up thin → **Socratic questioning** or the **double-standard technique** (`core-toolkit.md`, `self-esteem-motivation.md`). The belief is a real, solvable problem not a distortion → stop disputing, go to **problem-solving** (`core-toolkit.md`). Belief won't drop even against the evidence → underlying **self-defeating belief** (`self-esteem-motivation.md`).
