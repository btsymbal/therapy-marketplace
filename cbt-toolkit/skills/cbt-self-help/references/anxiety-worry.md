## How to use this file

The specialized layer for GAD / chronic worry and general stress. It assumes the nine transdiagnostic moves in `core-toolkit.md` (psychoeducation, restructuring + the 10 distortions, thought record, Socratic questioning, behavioral experiment, behavioral activation, graded exposure, problem-solving, relaxation), references them by name, and never re-documents them. Deliver every technique below per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.**

---

## 1 · Worry postponement (scheduled worry time)

**Reach for it when:** chronic, uncontrollable worry, especially the meta-belief "I can't control it." The point isn't to stop worrying, it's to prove the worry can be *delayed*, which breaks the uncontrollability story.

**Walk it through:**
1. Set the slot. Ask: "What's a consistent 30-minute window you can give worry each day, not in bed or the bedroom?"
2. The day rule. Explain it: when a worry hits, jot one line and promise to attend to it at worry time, then return attention to what they were doing. Ask: "What will you jot it in, and can you actually park it till then?"
3. Run the slot (in-session or as homework). Work through the listed worries in writing; if none come, keep writing the last one until the time is up. Then change place or activity.
4. Sort. Ask: "Going down the list, which are actionable problems and which are hypothetical what-ifs?"

**If it stalls:** worry won't park → pair with the **worry tree** (2) in the moment. Actionable pile dominates → **problem-solving** (`core-toolkit.md`). Hypothetical pile dominates → **decatastrophizing** (3) or the **worry outcome diary** (4). Arousal too high to refocus → **applied relaxation** (6).

## 2 · The worry tree

**Reach for it when:** a worry is spinning and they can't tell whether to act or drop it. A fast in-the-moment sort: real-and-actionable goes to a plan, hypothetical goes to refocus.

**Walk it through:**
1. Name it. Ask: "What exactly are you worried about, in one line?"
2. The fork. Ask: "Can you actually do something about this, yes or no?"
3a. If **hypothetical** (no): the move is to let it go and shift attention, not to solve it. Ask: "What activity can you turn to right now to refocus?"
3b. If **real and actionable** (yes): build the plan, who / what / when / where / how. Ask: "What's the action, and is it do-now or schedule-for-later?" Then let go and refocus.

**If it stalls:** keeps re-arriving after "let it go" → contain it with **worry postponement** (1). Actionable branch needs depth → **problem-solving** (`core-toolkit.md`). Hypothetical branch still grips → **decatastrophizing** (3).

## 3 · Decatastrophizing / what-if downward-arrow

**Reach for it when:** a catastrophic prediction is driving the anxiety, "what if X, then Y, then disaster." Challenges the forecast and, as much, builds confidence they could cope even if it landed.

**Walk it through:**
1. Find the catastrophe. Downward-arrow it. Ask: "If that were true, what would happen next?" and repeat until the core feared outcome surfaces.
2. Worst case, spelled out. Ask: "So what's the absolute worst case here?"
3. Probability. Ask: "Realistically, how likely is that, 0-100%?"
4. The other two scenarios. Ask: "Best case? And the most *realistic* case?"
5. The coping question (the real lever). Ask: "If the worst did happen, how would you actually cope, what would you have to draw on?"

**If it stalls:** probability estimate stays inflated → gather real data with a **worry outcome diary** (4). "I know it's unlikely but I still feel it" → **IU behavioral experiments** (5) or imaginal exposure to a worry script. Belief won't move on logic alone → **behavioral experiment** (`core-toolkit.md`).

## 4 · Worry outcome diary

**Reach for it when:** they believe their worries reliably come true, or that worrying helps. The diary collects what *actually* happens versus what they predicted, which usually undercuts both beliefs. (The "most worries turn out neutral" idea is a teaching point, not a hard statistic, present it as something to test, not a fact.)

**Walk it through (one column per entry, logged when a worry fires and again later):**
1. **Situation.** Ask: "Where were you, what set it off?"
2. **The worry + prediction.** Ask: "What's the 'what if', and what specifically did you predict would happen?"
3. **Intensity** 0-10. Ask: "How strong, 0 to 10?"
4. **Type.** Ask: "Problem, fear, or hypothetical?"
5. **Outcome (later).** Ask: "What actually happened, versus what you predicted?"

**If it stalls:** patterns aren't landing → run **IU behavioral experiments** (5) or **decatastrophizing** (3) on a live entry. Wants the formal cognitive workup → the **thought record** or **examine-the-evidence** move (`core-toolkit.md`).

## 5 · Intolerance-of-uncertainty behavioral experiments

**Reach for it when:** uncertainty itself is the trigger, and the worry is propped up by certainty-seeking, over-checking, over-preparing, reassurance-seeking, refusing to delegate. IU is the engine under GAD; these experiments are the strongest move against it.

**Walk it through:**
1. Spot the safety behavior. Ask: "What do you do to make the uncertainty feel manageable, checking, over-preparing, asking for reassurance?"
2. Design a small dose. Act "as if" tolerant. Ask: "What's a small thing you could do *without* that, send the email unread, go somewhere unplanned?"
3. Predict. Ask: "What do you expect will go wrong?"
4. (They run it.) Record what *actually* happened versus the prediction.
5. Build the dose. Ask: "What's the next size up?"

**If it stalls:** too anxiety-provoking → shrink the dose, or build a **graded exposure** ladder (`core-toolkit.md`); contain spillover worry with **worry postponement** (1) and the **worry tree** (2). Catastrophic images keep intruding → imaginal worry-script exposure. Arousal too high → **applied relaxation** (6).

## 6 · Applied relaxation (Öst 7-skill course)

**Reach for it when:** general stress and tension you want a portable down-regulation skill for. This is a *trained course*, not a one-off, each skill is rehearsed until the next is added, ending in a sub-30-second relax they can deploy anywhere. Frame it as general wellbeing and stress fitness, not an anti-panic tool (for panic, breathing/relaxation can become a safety behavior, see `safety.md`, `panic.md`).

**Walk it through (one skill per session, only advance once the current one is solid):**
1. **PMR.** Tense-and-release through the body (the full progression is in `core-toolkit.md` technique 9). Ask: "Where did the tension actually drop?"
2. **Release-only I.** Relax each group without tensing first. Ask: "Can you find the let-go without the tense step?"
3. **Release-only II.** Same, smoother, faster.
4. **Cue-controlled.** Think "in" on the inhale, "relax" on the exhale, releasing tension; aim to relax in 2-3 min. Ask: "What happens when you pair 'relax' to the out-breath?"
5. **Differential I.** Stay relaxed while seated, eyes open, moving eyes / head / arms. Ask: "Can you hold the calm while doing something?"
6. **Differential II.** Same while standing or walking.
7. **Rapid relaxation.** A few deep breaths, think "relax" before a slow exhale; aim for under 30 seconds. Then **apply** it in real stressful situations until they stop frightening the user. Ask: "Where this week will you deploy the rapid version?"

**If it stalls:** relaxation *increases* anxiety → stop forcing it, switch to grounding (`trauma.md`) or brief mindfulness. Becoming avoidance → name it and reframe as wellbeing. Stress is appraisal-driven → **decatastrophizing** (3) or restructuring (`core-toolkit.md`); controllable stressor → **problem-solving** (`core-toolkit.md`). Worry-driven → the GAD techniques above (1-5).

## 7 · Breathing retraining

**Reach for it when:** general stress and acute anxiety, to counter shallow over-breathing. **Caveat:** keep it general wellbeing, not an anti-panic crutch, in panic it can become a safety behavior that undermines interoceptive exposure (`safety.md`, `panic.md`).

**Walk it through (guide it live, one cue at a time, don't just describe it):**
1. Low. Breathe down into the abdomen, not the chest. Ask: "Which hand is moving, chest or belly?"
2. Slow. About 3-4s in, 3-4s out, or a longer out-breath. Don't gulp or over-breathe.
3. Settle into the rhythm for a few minutes. Ask: "What's the body doing now?"
4. Make it automatic. Practice daily when calm so it's there to deploy under stress.

**If it stalls:** breathing ramps anxiety → stop, switch to grounding (`trauma.md`). Becoming a panic safety behavior → drop it and prioritize interoceptive exposure (`panic.md`). Wants a fuller trained skill → **applied relaxation** (6).
