## How to use this file

The self-esteem and motivation layer: harsh self-criticism, low self-worth, entrenched core beliefs, procrastination, perfectionism, and do-nothingism. It assumes the nine in `core-toolkit.md` (restructuring, thought record, behavioral experiment, behavioral activation) and adds the self-attack and motivation worksheets. Depression-specific forms (Daily Mood Log, pleasure-predicting) are in `depression.md`. Deliver every technique per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.**

---

## 1 · Double-standard technique

**Reach for it when:** they're far harsher to themselves than they'd ever be to a friend, self-criticism, perfectionism, low self-esteem.

**Walk it through:**
1. Catch the self-attack verbatim. Ask: "What's the exact harsh thing you're saying to yourself?"
2. Flip it. Ask: "Would you say that, in those words, to a friend with the same problem?"
3. Name the gap. Ask: "So what's the double standard here, why the kinder voice for them?"
4. Rewrite. Ask: "Say it to yourself in that same kind, realistic voice, what's the rewritten version?"

**If it stalls:** they'd be harsh to the friend too → run **examine the evidence** (`depression.md`) on the standard itself. Shame or self-loathing runs deeper → externalization of voices / feared-fantasy (`burns-team.md`), and start a **positive data log** (5).

## 2 · Survey technique

**Reach for it when:** a belief about being inferior, inadequate, or uniquely flawed ("everyone thinks I'm boring") that can be checked against real-world data.

**Walk it through:**
1. Pin the belief. Ask: "What's the exact belief we're testing, as a claim about what others think?"
2. Design the question. Ask: "What could you actually ask real people that would test it?"
3. Collect. (They survey trusted others / gather the data.) Ask: "Who will you ask, and when?"
4. Compare. Ask: "How does what they said line up with what you predicted?"

**If it stalls:** combine with **examine the evidence** (`depression.md`) plus a **positive data log** (5). If perfectionism is underneath → target the **self-defeating belief** (6).

## 3 · Anti-procrastination sheet

**Reach for it when:** procrastination, overwhelm, depression-linked do-nothingism. Core principle: action first, motivation follows.

**Walk it through (one column at a time, capture predicted vs actual):**
1. Break it down. Ask: "Split the task into tiny numbered steps, each finishable in 30 seconds to a couple of minutes, just outline the first 4 or 5. What's step one?"
2. **Predicted difficulty + satisfaction (before this step).** Ask: "Before you do it, predict difficulty 0 to 100% and satisfaction 0 to 100%."
3. Do it. (They do the single step.)
4. **Actual difficulty + satisfaction (after).** Ask: "Done, now rate the actual difficulty and satisfaction, 0 to 100% each."
5. Compare. Ask: "Predicted versus actual, people over-predict difficulty and under-predict satisfaction, did that hold?" Then move to the next step and repeat.

**If it stalls:** blocking thoughts in the way → **TIC-TOC** (8). Hidden resistance ("part of me doesn't want to") → **cost-benefit analysis** (9). Perfectionism freezing the start → **anti-perfectionism** (4).

## 4 · Anti-perfectionism (deliberate-imperfection experiment)

**Reach for it when:** perfectionism driving procrastination, depression, or low self-worth, the 100%-or-nothing bar that stops things getting finished. A behavioral experiment with the bar deliberately lowered.

**Walk it through:**
1. Pick the task. Ask: "What's a task you'd normally insist on doing at 100%?"
2. Define good-enough. Ask: "What would an 80% / good-enough version actually look like?"
3. Predict the disaster. Ask: "If you did it at 80% on purpose, what do you predict goes wrong, and how do you think others react?"
4. (They do it imperfectly, on purpose.) Observe. Ask: "What actually happened, did the predicted disaster occur, how did people respond?"
5. Land it. Ask: "What did lowering the bar do to your actual output and satisfaction?" (Usually both rise.)

**If it stalls:** the perfectionist standard itself is the problem → apply the **double-standard technique** (1) to it, and a **cost-benefit analysis** (9) of holding the bar that high. They discount the good outcome ("got lucky") → generate fresh evidence with more **behavioral experiments** (`core-toolkit.md`) and a **positive data log** (5).

## 5 · Positive data log / core-belief record + continuum

**Reach for it when:** an entrenched negative core belief ("I'm worthless / unlovable") that surface-thought work doesn't touch. Slow, cumulative; you strengthen the new belief rather than just arguing the old one.

**Walk it through:**
1. Name the new belief. Ask: "What's the adaptive belief you want to strengthen, stated positively, e.g. 'I am worthwhile'?"
2. Start the log. Ask: "What's one piece of day-to-day evidence, however small, that supports it?" (The core-belief record holds ~25 items; this is ongoing, not one sitting.)
3. Catch the discounted ones. Ask: "What's a small positive from today you'd normally brush off, write it down anyway?"
4. Scale it. Ask: "On a 0 to 100 continuum, how much do you believe the old belief versus the new one right now?" Re-rate over time.
5. Use the continuum against all-or-nothing. Ask: "Instead of worthless-or-worthy, where on the line does the evidence actually put you?"

**If it stalls:** the underlying rule keeps reasserting → name and target the **self-defeating belief** (6) with a Daily Mood Log (`depression.md`) and **cost-benefit analysis** (9). They won't credit the positives → run them through **examine the evidence** (`depression.md`) one at a time.

## 6 · Identifying & challenging self-defeating beliefs (downward arrow)

**Reach for it when:** a surface thought keeps regenerating, the work only sticks once you reach the underlying value-rule (e.g. Perfectionism = "I must never fail or make a mistake"). Target this **after** surface-thought work, not before.

**Walk it through:**
1. Downward-arrow from the surface thought. Ask: "If that thought were true, what would it mean about you?" Repeat the question on each answer until you hit bedrock (or use a Daily Mood Log, `depression.md`, to trace it).
2. Name the belief. Ask: "So the rule underneath is, what, in one sentence?"
3. Weigh it. Ask: "What does living by that rule actually cost you, and what does it get you?" (a **cost-benefit analysis**, 9).
4. Replace it. Ask: "What's a more flexible, self-accepting version of the rule you could live by instead?"

**If it stalls:** the new belief feels hollow → build evidence with a **positive data log** (5); test the old rule via **survey** (2) or **examine the evidence** (`depression.md`); apply the **double-standard technique** (1) to the rule.

## 7 · The action-first rule (the motivation myth)

**Reach for it when:** they're waiting to "feel motivated" before acting, depressive inertia and procrastination. The myth: motivation precedes action. The truth: action precedes motivation.

**Walk it through:**
1. Name the trap. Ask: "Are you waiting until you feel like it before you start, is that the bargain?"
2. Reverse it. Ask: "What if action comes first and the motivation follows it, what's the smallest action you could take right now without feeling ready?"
3. Make it concrete. Ask: "Pick one small doable step, not a 'should', what is it and when?" (run it through the **anti-procrastination sheet**, 3).
4. Drop the coercion. Ask: "Can we state it without a single 'I should', just the next small move?"

**If it stalls:** the step still feels too big → shrink it (the 5-minute rule). A blocking thought is in the way → rewrite it with **TIC-TOC** (8). Genuine ambivalence underneath → surface it with **cost-benefit analysis** (9).

## 8 · TIC-TOC

**Reach for it when:** procrastination driven by specific self-defeating thoughts about the task itself ("five minutes won't help", "I'm not in the mood"). TIC = Task-Interfering Cognition, TOC = Task-Oriented Cognition.

**Walk it through:**
1. Surface the TICs. Ask: "What are the thoughts / excuses telling you not to do this, every one?"
2. Name the distortion. Ask: "Which of the 10 distortions is each TIC doing?" (list in `core-toolkit.md`, technique 2).
3. Substitute a TOC. Ask: "What's the task-oriented version of each, the more objective thought?" (e.g. "five minutes won't help" → "I can do a bit today and a bit tomorrow"; "I'm not in the mood" → "I'll start, then I'll feel more like it").

**If it stalls:** the TOCs are written but action still doesn't start → pair with the **anti-procrastination sheet** (3). Resistance persists → **cost-benefit analysis** (9). Perfectionism underneath → **anti-perfectionism** (4).

---

## 9 · Cost-benefit analysis

**Reach for it when:** ambivalence, resistance, or any self-defeating belief / habit you suspect is being kept for a reason. Surfaces the hidden payoff, don't push for change before you've seen it.

**Walk it through:**
1. State the target. Ask: "What's the thought, belief, habit, or feeling we're putting on the scale?"
2. Advantages of keeping it. Ask: "What does holding onto this actually get you, all of it?" Take it seriously.
3. Disadvantages of keeping it. Ask: "And what does it cost you?"
4. Weigh. Ask: "Put a rough balance on it, say 40/60, which way does it tip, is changing worth it?"

**If it stalls:** the advantages still win → that's real ambivalence, not stubbornness; positively reframe the resistance (`burns-team.md`) before pushing. Once they've tipped toward change → move to the **anti-procrastination sheet** (3) or **TIC-TOC** (8).
