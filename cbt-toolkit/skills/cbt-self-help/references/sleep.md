## How to use this file

This is the insomnia layer (CBT-I). It assumes the nine in `core-toolkit.md` and is delivered per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.** Multicomponent CBT-I (stimulus control + sleep restriction + restructuring + relaxation) is the first-line treatment; **sleep hygiene alone is recommended against** as a standalone therapy, so don't deliver it as the fix, fold it into stimulus control. Restructuring sleep beliefs is the core **thought record** (`core-toolkit.md` technique 3) pointed at sleep-specific thoughts, referenced below by name, not re-documented.

**Acute night vs chronic pattern, triage first.** Most of CBT-I below (stimulus control, sleep restriction, the diary) is structural work that needs days to weeks and **cannot be run at 2am**. If they come in mid-night ("it's 2am, I'm wired, dreading tomorrow"), don't open a multi-week protocol: restructure the "I'll be wrecked tomorrow" catastrophizing (4), use the get-out-of-bed 15-20 minute rule (1, step 3), and paradoxical intention (3) in the moment. Set the diary / sleep-restriction up later as a planning session, delivered as homework. Reserve the full package for an ongoing, chronic pattern.

## 1 · Stimulus control (bed for sleep only)

**Reach for it when:** chronic insomnia where the bed has become a cue for wakefulness (lying awake, frustrated, "wired in bed"). Extinguishes the bed-wakefulness association and rebuilds bed = sleep.

**Walk it through:**
1. Fix a regular morning rise time first, the anchor for everything else. Ask: "What's a wake time you can hold every single day, weekends included?"
2. Go to bed only when **sleepy**, not merely tired. Ask: "How do you tell sleepy from just fatigued, what's the actual signal for you?"
3. The 15-20 minute rule: if sleep won't come (at lights-out or mid-night), get up, go to another room, lights low, quiet low-stimulation activity, no screens/work/eating; return only when sleepy again. Ask: "What's the quiet thing you'd do out of bed, and where?" (Stress: don't watch the clock.)
4. Cut daytime naps that bleed off sleep pressure. Ask: "Are you napping, and when?"
5. Bed and bedroom for sleep or intimacy only. Ask: "What currently happens in bed that isn't one of those two?"

**If it stalls:** still consolidating poorly → add **sleep restriction** (2). Effort/anxiety is the block → **paradoxical intention** (3). "But I won't function" beliefs surface → **restructuring sleep beliefs** (4). Bedtime arousal too high → **relaxation at bedtime** (5).

## 2 · Sleep restriction therapy

**Reach for it when:** fragmented, broken sleep with lots of time in bed awake. Compresses time in bed toward actual sleep time to consolidate it; deliberately builds sleep pressure. (The sleep-efficiency formula below is from the protocol, not computed live.)

**Walk it through (run the diary -> efficiency -> window -> titration loop with them):**
1. Sleep diary, 1-2 weeks: bedtime, estimated time to fall asleep, awakenings, final get-up time. Ask: "Can you start logging tonight, bedtime, rough sleep onset, wake-ups, get-up time?"
2. From the diary, average total sleep time and average time in bed. Sleep efficiency = (avg total sleep time / avg time in bed) x 100. Ask: "Over the week, roughly how many hours did you actually sleep versus how long were you in bed?"
3. Set the sleep window near average **sleep** time, with a safety floor of about 5 hours (never below). Ask: "If your real sleep is about X hours, the window is X, does that floor of 5h feel survivable?"
4. Pick a fixed wake time, count backward to a fixed bedtime; hold the window every night, weekends included. Ask: "Fixed wake time is ___; that puts bedtime at ___, can you commit to both nightly?"
5. Weekly titration on the diary's efficiency: SE >=90% -> add ~15 min time in bed; SE 85-89% -> hold; SE <85% -> cut ~15 min. Ask: "What was this week's efficiency, so we know whether to add, hold, or trim?"

**If it stalls:** can't hold the window → tighten **stimulus control** (1). The *effort* of it is the problem → **paradoxical intention** (3). Daytime-dysfunction fears spike → **restructuring sleep beliefs** (4). Flag if daytime sleepiness becomes a safety issue (driving), and ease the floor up.

## 3 · Paradoxical intention

**Reach for it when:** sleep-onset (initial) insomnia driven by **trying too hard**, the harder they chase sleep, the more it flees. Targets sleep-onset performance anxiety by removing the effort.

**Walk it through:**
1. Name the trap: chasing sleep is itself the arousal keeping them awake. Ask: "When you can't sleep, what are you actively doing to *make* it happen?"
2. Flip the instruction: lie in bed, lights off, and gently try to stay **passively awake**, no effort to sleep, no fighting to stay up either. Ask: "Tonight, can you let go of trying to sleep and just rest with eyes closed, allowing wakefulness?"
3. The point isn't a trick to fall asleep faster; it's dropping the performance demand. Ask: "What happens to the pressure when sleep stops being the goal?"

**If it stalls:** → **stimulus control** (1) if they're still bed-bound and wired. Catastrophic beliefs drive the effort → **restructuring sleep beliefs** (4). Body still tense → **relaxation at bedtime** (5), watching it doesn't become its own effortful chase.

## 4 · Cognitive restructuring of unhelpful sleep beliefs

**Reach for it when:** catastrophic or rigid beliefs about sleep are fuelling the arousal, "if I don't get 8 hours I can't function", "one bad night ruins tomorrow", "I need to sleep *now*". The cognitive component of CBT-I.

**Walk it through:** run the core **thought record** (`core-toolkit.md` technique 3) on the sleep-specific thought, one column per turn. Catch the hot thought verbatim ("What exactly goes through your head at 3am?"), rate belief, examine evidence for and against (often there's history of functioning on poor sleep), build a balanced alternative in their words, re-rate. Name the distortions, usually catastrophizing and all-or-nothing about sleep need.

**If it stalls:** belief won't drop with logic → use **stimulus control** (1) + **sleep restriction** (2) as a **behavioral experiment** (`core-toolkit.md` technique 5) to gather disconfirming sleep data. Effort/anxiety cycle dominates → **paradoxical intention** (3). "Know it's not true but still feel it" → acceptance/defusion (`third-wave.md`).

---

## 5 · Relaxation at bedtime

**Reach for it when:** somatic or cognitive arousal at lights-out, racing mind, tense body. A down-regulation skill for the bedtime window.

**Walk it through:** use the **relaxation** menu in `core-toolkit.md` (technique 9), guided live one cue at a time, not described in a block: abdominal/diaphragmatic breathing, PMR, autogenic training, guided imagery, or meditation. Pick one and run it. Check in after: "What's the body doing now versus when we started?"

**If it stalls:** relaxation becomes its own effortful task or *increases* arousal → stop forcing it, switch to **paradoxical intention** (3) or drop it. Single components not enough → assemble the full multicomponent CBT-I package (1, 2, 4, 5 together) rather than leaning on relaxation alone.
