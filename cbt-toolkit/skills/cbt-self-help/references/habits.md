## How to use this file

The habits layer: riding out urges, cutting the cues, replacing the behavior, and holding the change. Covers BFRBs (trichotillomania, skin picking, nail biting), other habits, and the self-help-safe parts of substance work. Assumes the nine in `core-toolkit.md` and is delivered per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.**

**Scope of safety:** urge surfing (1), stimulus control (2), and relapse prevention (4) are self-help-safe. Structured substance-use treatment and functional analysis (5) want a clinician, route per `safety.md`, don't run them solo. Where cost-benefit, relapse prevention, and stimulus control touch a substance the user is cutting down, treat it as a recurring theme and note it in `CLAUDE.md` (`logging.md`).

**Withdrawal caution (drinking).** Daily or heavy drinking is the one case where abrupt cessation can be medically dangerous (withdrawal seizures). If they're drinking every night and want to stop hard, flag that a medical consult should come first, the self-help skills here support *reduction*, not an unsupervised cold-stop detox.

## 1 · Urge surfing

**Reach for it when:** an urge is present, substance use, anger, gambling, overeating, a BFRB. The frame: an urge is a wave (trigger → rise → peak → fall), it peaks and passes (~30 min) on its own if not fed. A lower-risk self-help skill.

**Walk it through:**
1. Name it as an urge. Ask: "Can you say it plainly, 'I'm having an urge to X' right now?"
2. Turn toward the sensation without fighting it. Ask: "Where do you feel it in the body, and can you watch it without trying to change it?"
3. Hold the reminder. Ask: "Can you tell yourself, 'this is a feeling, not a must; it's temporary and it will pass'?"
4. Surf with the breath as the board. Slow breathing, attention on the body, ride it. Ask: "What's the wave doing now, still rising or starting to fall?"
5. Pair with delay. Ask: "Every minute you delay weakens it, what's the next minute look like?"

**If it stalls:** the cue keeps re-triggering → **stimulus control** (2) to thin the cues. Urge is very high → DBT **TIPP** (`third-wave.md`). Pattern keeps recurring → **relapse prevention** (4) so each known trigger has a prepared move.

## 2 · Stimulus control

**Reach for it when:** a habit or BFRB is cued by the environment, the behavior rides on triggers more than on willpower. Change the cues, not just the resolve.

**Walk it through:**
1. Map the triggering cues. Ask: "What's reliably around when it happens, the place, the object, the routine, the state?"
2. Pick the lever per cue: remove, rearrange, or avoid. Ask: "Which of those can you take out, move, or design around?" (BFRB examples: gloves or Band-Aids, cover mirrors, dispose of tweezers, a hat over pull-prone areas.)
3. Swap the routine the cue sits in. Ask: "What's the small consistent change to the routine that beats relying on willpower?"
4. Set one to test this week. Ask: "Which single adjustment do you actually run starting today?"

**If it stalls:** cues removed but the urge still fires → **habit reversal** (3) for a competing response, or **urge surfing** (1). Slips keep happening at the same cue → **relapse prevention** (4). If drinking is the target, anchor the cue changes to the user's reduction plan recorded in `CLAUDE.md`.

## 3 · Habit reversal training (HRT)

**Reach for it when:** tics, trichotillomania, skin picking, nail biting, the most empirically supported trichotillomania treatment. Often clinician-delivered; for self-help, run awareness + competing response and flag the rest.

**Walk it through:**
1. **Awareness training.** Ask: "When and where does the behavior (and the urge before it) actually show up, can you catch it earlier each time?" (self-monitoring is the engine).
2. **Competing response.** Build a benign, physically incompatible action. Ask: "When the urge hits, what's a move you can hold for a few minutes that makes the behavior impossible right then?" (e.g. clenched fists for pulling).
3. **Social support.** Ask: "Who could notice and acknowledge when you use the competing response?"
4. **Relaxation.** Fold in breathing/PMR (relaxation, `core-toolkit.md`) to lower the baseline arousal that feeds the urge.
5. **Generalization.** Ask: "Which other settings does this need to hold in, and what changes there?"

**If it stalls:** competing response keeps losing to the urge → strengthen **stimulus control** (2) and add **urge surfing** (1). Severe or entrenched (esp. trichotillomania) → this is often clinician-delivered, see `safety.md`. Gains fade over time → **relapse prevention** (4).

## 4 · Relapse prevention

**Reach for it when:** the behavior is changing and the job now is holding it, substance use or any habit. Anticipate the high-risk situations rather than meet them unprepared.

**Walk it through:**
1. List the high-risk situations and triggers. Ask: "Where and when are you most likely to slip, the people, places, states?"
2. Assign a coping skill to each. Ask: "For that situation, what's the prepared move, urge surfing, leaving, a call?"
3. Wire in the two anchors. Ask: "How does **urge surfing** (1) handle the moment and **stimulus control** (2) thin the setup?"
4. Separate a lapse from a relapse. Ask: "If you slip once, what's the plan that stops it becoming a full return?"

**If it stalls:** the chain from trigger to behavior is opaque → **functional analysis** belongs in clinician-supported work (5, `safety.md`). Slips are frequent and the target is drinking → consider professional treatment and update the user's reduction plan in `CLAUDE.md`. Payoff keeps winning → **cost-benefit of anger/habit** framing (`anger.md`).

## 5 · Substance-use CBT (clinician-needed)

**Reach for it when:** the work is structured substance-use treatment. **This is a clinician-supported technique, not self-help.** Functional analysis (mapping antecedents/triggers → use → consequences) and the formal protocol belong in supervised treatment, route per `safety.md`.

**Walk it through (what to do here):**
1. Name it as out of scope for solo work. State plainly that functional analysis and structured substance treatment want a clinician.
2. Offer the self-help-safe pieces that *do* belong here: **urge surfing** (1), distraction, **stimulus control** (2), **relapse prevention** (4).
3. Point to `safety.md` for the referral path, and connect ongoing reduction work to the user's reduction plan recorded in `CLAUDE.md`.

**If it stalls:** any sign this is beyond self-help (escalating use, withdrawal, risk) → stop and follow `safety.md` to formal addiction treatment. For maintaining gains around the edges → **relapse prevention** (4).
