## How to use this file

Reach for these when cognitive restructuring stalls ("I know it's not true but I still feel it", logic isn't moving the feeling), when thoughts are sticky / fused / intrusive, or when distress is too high for any cognitive work at all (run **TIPP** (4) or grounding first, think later). This is the in-the-moment CBT-side pointer to these skills, not a course: keep these brief and practical and don't re-teach the model. Assumes the nine in `core-toolkit.md`; deliver every one per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.**

---

## 1 · ACT cognitive defusion

**Reach for it when:** a thought is sticky, fused, or intrusive and disputation hasn't loosened it (worry, anxiety, self-criticism, rumination). The aim is distance, hold the thought as a passing mental event, not change its content or argue it's false.

**Walk it through (pick one move, run it live):**
1. Get the sticky thought verbatim. Ask: "What's the exact phrase your mind keeps handing you?"
2. **"I'm having the thought that…"** Have the user say it prefixed, then "I notice I'm having the thought that…". Ask: "Say it both ways, does the second one sit at any more distance?"
3. **Leaves on a stream.** Guide it: sitting by a stream, place each thought on a leaf, watch it float off, including the meta-thought "this isn't working", that goes on a leaf too. Ask: "What happens to the thought when you stop holding it and just watch it pass?"
4. **Silly / cartoon voice.** Have the user repeat the thought in a cartoon voice (or sung) for ~20-30s, not to mock it, to drain its literalness. Ask: "How true does it feel said in that voice?"

**If it stalls:** the thought is about something real and solvable → it may not need defusing, go to **problem-solving** (`core-toolkit.md`). If literal disputation is genuinely what's needed → **cognitive restructuring** / **thought record** (`core-toolkit.md`). If the feeling underneath needs room rather than distance → **acceptance / willingness** (3).

## 2 · ACT values + committed action

**Reach for it when:** avoidance and inertia, not a single hot thought, "can't be bothered", stuck, drifting. The action-oriented counterpart to defusion: move toward what matters even while the difficult feeling is still present.

**Walk it through:**
1. Find the live domain. Ask: "Which part of life feels most stuck or off-course right now, work, relationships, health, something else?"
2. Clarify the value under it (the direction, not a goal). Ask: "In that area, what kind of person do you want to be, what actually matters to you there?"
3. Pick one small committed action in its service. Ask: "What's the smallest step you could take toward that today, even with the feeling still here?"
4. Name what will show up and commit anyway. Ask: "What difficult feeling will probably come along, and are you willing to take the step with it riding shotgun?"

**If it stalls:** low mood / withdrawal dominates → **behavioral activation** (`core-toolkit.md`), its structured cousin. The step keeps getting blocked by a real obstacle → **problem-solving** (`core-toolkit.md`). The feelings that show up are the blocker → **acceptance / willingness** (3). Don't rebuild a full values inventory here; build on any values work the user has already done (recorded in `CLAUDE.md`).

## 3 · ACT acceptance / willingness

**Reach for it when:** the distress is largely unavoidable (grief, chronic pain, an outcome already fixed) and the struggle against the feeling is adding a second layer of suffering. Make room for the experience instead of fighting it.

**Walk it through:**
1. Locate the control move. Ask: "What are you doing to push this feeling away or get rid of it, and is it working?"
2. Drop the rope. Ask: "What if you let the feeling be here, no fixing, just allowing it, where do you feel it in the body?"
3. Make room. Have the user breathe into the sensation and let it be its actual size. Ask: "Can you give it space rather than bracing against it?"
4. Move alongside it. Ask: "With the feeling still present, what valued thing can you do anyway?"

**If it stalls:** the thought, not the feeling, is what's stuck → **defusion** (1). The distress is overwhelming, not just uncomfortable → **TIPP** (4). It's an unchangeable loss that needs full acceptance → DBT **radical acceptance** (5).

## 4 · DBT distress tolerance: TIPP

**Reach for it when:** crisis survival, distress is too high for any cognitive skill (flooded, panicking, urge to act on impulse / self-harm). This is the fast down-regulator that `safety.md` and other files point to: it changes body chemistry in minutes. Temporary by design, it buys the window to think, it doesn't fix the problem.

**Walk it through (give the concrete move, do one at a time):**
1. **T - Temperature.** "Splash cold water on your face, or hold something cold (ice, a cold can) against your cheeks / eyes, and hold your breath a few seconds. Do that now and tell me when you've done it." (Triggers the dive reflex, drops arousal fast.)
2. **I - Intense exercise.** "Do a short hard burst, jumping jacks, run on the spot, push-ups, 1-3 minutes, until you're a bit out of breath. Go, then come back." (Burns off the stress hormones.)
3. **P - Paced breathing.** Guide it live: slow the breath, exhale longer than the inhale, in for 4, out for 6, for about a minute. Ask: "What's the arousal doing now, lower at all?"
4. **P - Paired / progressive muscle relaxation.** "Tense a muscle group on the in-breath, release it on the slow out-breath, work up the body." Ask: "Where's the distress on 0-100 now versus when we started?"

Run the temperature step first if they're truly spiking; the others can follow. Get a before and after 0-100 reading, that delta is the data.

**If it stalls:** still too activated → **grounding 5-4-3-2-1** (`trauma.md`). Once distress is back in the workable window → return to cognitive work or **radical acceptance** (5). If a safety flag is live, drop everything and follow `safety.md`, including immediate support if they're at risk of self-harm.

## 5 · DBT radical acceptance & wise mind

**Reach for it when:** they're fighting a reality that can't be changed (radical acceptance), or about to make an impulsive call while flooded (wise mind). Acceptance here means stopping the fight with what is, not approving of it.

**Walk it through:**

**Radical acceptance** (unchangeable situation):
1. Name what's being refused. Ask: "What's the fact you keep bumping against that you can't actually change?"
2. Notice the fight and its cost. Ask: "What does refusing to accept it cost you, on top of the thing itself?"
3. Allow it, fully, without endorsing it. Ask: "Can you let it be true, just as it is, even though you hate it?"

**Wise mind** (impulsive decision):
1. Name emotion mind's pull. Ask: "What does the feeling want you to do right now?"
2. Name reasonable mind. Ask: "What does the cool, logical read say?"
3. Find the overlap. Ask: "Where do those two meet, what does the wise-mind version, both at once, tell you to do?"

**If it stalls:** emotion is too high to accept or decide anything → **TIPP** (4) first. It's the experience rather than the fact that needs room → **ACT acceptance** (3). Part of the situation is actually changeable → **problem-solving** (`core-toolkit.md`) on that part.

## 6 · MBCT decentering & the 3-minute breathing space

**Reach for it when:** rumination is building, or as a brief reset that steps back from the thought-stream (relapse-prevention flavored, useful when the low mood is starting its familiar loop). Decentering = relating to thoughts as passing mental events rather than facts; the breathing space is its portable drill.

**Walk it through (guide the three steps live, one per turn):**
1. **Becoming aware.** "Stop and notice what's here right now, thoughts, feeling, body, without changing any of it. What's actually present?"
2. **Gathering / redirecting.** "Now narrow attention down to the breath, just the sensation of breathing, as an anchor. Rest there for a few breaths." Ask: "Where did the mind go, and can you bring it back to the breath?"
3. **Expanding.** "Widen out from the breath to the whole body and posture, hold the breath and the body together. What's the overall state now?"
4. Land the decentering point. Ask: "Notice those thoughts were events passing through, not facts, does any of them feel less solid now?"

**If it stalls:** a specific thought is still gripping → **ACT defusion** (1), the close cousin. The content genuinely needs examining → **thought record** (`core-toolkit.md`). Repeated low-mood loops → revisit the depression layer (`depression.md`).
