## How to use this file

Acceptance / willingness means making **room** for a difficult feeling, urge, or sensation rather than fighting, suppressing, or avoiding it. These techniques work on the **secondary** struggle — the extra suffering added by the fight — not on the primary pain itself, which they don't promise to remove. Two things hold the whole file together. First, willingness is a **chosen stance, not a feeling**: you don't have to feel willing to be willing. Second, acceptance **always serves a value** — you make room for the discomfort *in order to* do something that matters, never as resignation for its own sake; if it starts to look like giving up or grim endurance, stop and check the values rationale (`safety.md`). Assumes the model in `hexaflex.md`; deliver every one per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.** Run guided pieces in small chunks the user actually does and reports back on, and take a 0–100 reading of the discomfort before and after.

## 1 · Expansion (making room)

**Reach for it when:** a strong aversive emotion — anxiety, grief, shame, anger, craving — has been chronically fought, and the fight is what's exhausting. Use it once the user can see that the control strategies haven't worked. The aim is to let the feeling be its actual size, not to shrink it.

**Walk it through:**
1. Settle them: eyes closed or a soft downward gaze, and get a 0–100 reading. Ask: "Bring the difficult situation to mind just enough to stir the feeling — where in the body is it strongest, and what's it at on 0–100?"
2. Observe its properties, not its name. Ask: "What's its shape, its depth, its temperature — is it moving or still?"
3. Breathe space *around* it. Have them take a few slow breaths into and around the area. Ask: "Can you let the breath open up some room around it, rather than push it out?"
4. The willingness line. Say it plainly: "You don't have to like this feeling or want it here. Your task right now is just to allow it — to give it permission to exist." Ask: "Can you let it be there, exactly as it is?"
5. Re-observe and re-rate. Ask: "Has the sensation changed in quality at all — and where's it on 0–100 now?"
6. Ground and step out. Bring full attention back to the five senses, then ask: "What happened when you made room for it instead of fighting it?"

**If it stalls:** if they're using the breath to *get rid of* the feeling (the core failure mode), drop the breathing, slow right down, and return to pure curious observation. If the belief that the feeling *must* be controlled is in the way → **the struggle switch** (3). If a sticky **thought**, not the feeling, is what's stuck → `defusion.md` 1. If the feeling now needs to go somewhere → name the value and pivot to `committed-action.md` 1.

## 2 · Dropping the rope (tug-of-war)

**Reach for it when:** the user is worn out by years of struggling with a chronic difficulty — depression, chronic pain, addiction, grief — and the futility of the fight is already palpable. This makes that futility concrete and frees their hands. **It must pivot immediately to a values action**, or it lands as defeat.

**Walk it through:**
1. Set the scene. "Picture a tug-of-war with a huge, frightening monster, over the edge of a pit. You've been pulling as hard as you can." Ask: "What do you notice — what's it costing you to keep pulling?"
2. Name the fear. Ask: "What are you trying to prevent by pulling?"
3. The key move. "The harder you pull, the harder the monster pulls." Ask: "So what would happen if you dropped the rope?"
4. Hold the result. "The monster's still there — but powerless without the tug. You're not fighting it, and you've got your hands back." Ask: "What's it like to have your hands free, even with the monster still in the room?"
5. Pivot now — don't linger. Ask: "With your hands back, what's one thing you've been wanting to do? One step in a direction that matters?"

**If it stalls:** if they pick the rope straight back up, the **thought** is probably hooking them → `defusion.md` 9 (Passengers on the bus) or 2. If they can't stay near the monster without grabbing the rope → **Expansion** (1) to build willingness. If they can't find a step, the value isn't clear yet → `values.md` 2 (Bull's-eye).

## 3 · The struggle switch (+ two control buttons)

**Reach for it when:** the user doesn't yet see *why* fighting a feeling makes things worse — good psychoeducation at the front end of acceptance work. This one is mostly explanation, run interactively so they reach the insight themselves rather than being lectured.

**Walk it through:**
1. Set up the switch. "Imagine a switch on your back labelled 'struggle.' When it's ON, any uncomfortable thought or feeling gets treated as something that must not be here." Ask: "When yours is ON and anxiety shows up, what happens next — what do you start fighting?"
2. Name the second layer. "With the switch ON, you get anxiety *about* the anxiety, anger *about* the sadness — new things to struggle with." Ask: "Does that match how it tends to snowball for you?"
3. Switch OFF. "When it's OFF, the original pain is still there — but the struggling layer dissolves." Ask: "What might be different if the original feeling were here but the fight against it weren't?"
4. The crucial distinction. "The switch controls the *struggle*, not the feeling. It doesn't make the primary emotion disappear." Ask: "Does that change what 'turning it off' would actually ask of you?"
5. **Two control buttons** variant, if useful. "Picture two dials: one sets how much distress you feel — and it's jammed, you don't really control it. The other sets your willingness to *have* the distress, and that one you can turn." Ask: "If the distress dial is stuck but the willingness dial turns, which one's worth your effort?"

**If it stalls:** if they take the switch as a literal off-button for feelings, make the distinction explicit and slow down — that misread is the failure mode. Once they see the struggle is optional but the feeling isn't → **Expansion** (1) or **Dropping the rope** (2) to put it into practice. If the fused thought is driving the struggle → `defusion.md` 6 (Naming the story).

## 4 · Urge surfing

**Reach for it when:** a craving or urge is cresting — substance use, eating-disorder urges, self-harm impulses, OCD compulsions. The frame is that urges are waves: they rise, peak, and subside on their own, usually within 20–30 minutes, if you don't act on them. You're the surfer, not the wave.

**Walk it through:**
1. Name it and rate it. Ask: "Can you say it as 'there's an urge to [X] arising right now' — and where's it on 0–100?"
2. Ground briefly. One slow breath, feet on the floor, a quick look around the room. Ask: "Feet down, one slow breath — done?"
3. See the wave. "Picture the urge as a wave: rising, cresting, then falling. It will peak and pass on its own." Ask: "Where is your wave right now — rising, at the peak, or starting to drop?"
4. Stay with the body of it. Ask: "What are the physical sensations of the urge, watched from the outside rather than fused with?"
5. Ride it. "Let it rise, be at its peak, and subside — you don't have to push it down, just stay on the board." Ask: "Can you ride it without acting, and tell me what it's at on 0–100 now?"
6. Reconnect to a value. Ask: "While you ride this one out, what matters to you right here?"

**If it stalls:** if they're trying to make the wave break faster, that's still struggling — name it and return to riding. If the urge is overwhelming and they can't get on the board → `present-moment.md` 4 (Dropping anchor) first, then surf. If a craving-*thought* is the hook → `defusion.md` 1 (Labelling). For the wider relapse picture → `substance-use.md`.

## 5 · Physicalising emotions (making room)

**Reach for it when:** the user is stuck in their head and disconnected from the body, or pure verbal expansion isn't landing — turning the feeling into an *object* gives them something to observe. Also useful in chronic pain, where bodily awareness is already switched on.

**Walk it through:**
1. Locate it. Ask: "Where does this feeling live in the body — and what's it at on 0–100 right now?"
2. Make it an object. Ask: "If it had a shape, colour, texture, temperature, and weight, what would they be — and does it move or pulsate?"
3. Set the observer stance. "You're *describing* it, not being it — you're the observer of this sensation, not the sensation." Ask: "From that observer spot, what else do you notice about it?"
4. Carry it toward what matters. Ask: "Could this object come along with you while you do something that counts today?"
5. Land it and re-rate. Ask: "What would it mean to *carry* this rather than fight it — and where's it on 0–100 now?"

**If it stalls:** if they're dissociated or avoidant → `present-moment.md` 4 (Dropping anchor) to ground first. If very analytical and stuck in description → **Urge surfing** (4) for a movement-based alternative. If a sticky thought rides along with the feeling → `defusion.md` 8 (Physicalising thoughts), its mirror move. To turn the carrying into action → `committed-action.md` 1.
