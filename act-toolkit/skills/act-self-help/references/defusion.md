## How to use this file

Defusion changes your RELATIONSHIP to a thought, not its content. The move is to hold a thought as a passing mental event you are looking AT, rather than a truth you are looking FROM. You are not trying to disprove it, argue with it, or get rid of it, the aim is distance, not disproof. A thought can be word-for-word accurate and still not worth obeying; what matters is workability, whether buying it moves you toward or away from a life you want. Assumes the model in `hexaflex.md`. Deliver every technique per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.** Defusion is a muscle, not a switch, most of these need repeated reps over days, not one clean go, so set that expectation rather than chasing a single big shift.

## 1 · Labelling ("I'm having the thought that…")

**Reach for it when:** a self-critical thought, anxious prediction, or ruminative loop keeps presenting itself as a flat fact ("I'm a failure", "this will go wrong"). The simplest, lowest-risk first move, usable almost anywhere and easy to carry between sessions. Good opener before reaching for anything heavier.

**Walk it through:**
1. Get the thought verbatim, the exact phrase the mind keeps handing over. Ask: "What's the precise sentence, in its own words?"
2. Have them add a prefix and say it aloud: "I'm having the thought that [thought]." Ask: "Say it that way, does anything shift in how it lands?"
3. Add the next layer: "I notice I'm having the thought that [thought]." Ask: "And that version, does it sit at any more distance?"
4. Optional further layer for judgments: "I notice my mind is handing me another judgment." Ask: "When you frame it as your mind doing a thing, rather than a fact about you, what happens to its pull?"
5. Set the rep: pick two or three frequent thoughts and prefix them across the week. Ask: "Which couple of thoughts will you try this on before we next talk?"

**If it stalls:** believability barely moves → `defusion.md 3` Milk-milk-milk for harder semantic distance. The thought belongs to a bigger recurring narrative → `defusion.md 6` Naming the story. It needs a more embodied handle → `defusion.md 8` Physicalising. The thought is welded to a feeling being fought → `acceptance.md 1` Expansion.

## 2 · Leaves on a stream

**Reach for it when:** general rumination and repetitive thought traffic, especially for people who picture things easily. A spacious way to practise watching thoughts come and go rather than being carried off by each one. **Note:** the "let it float away" framing can quietly turn into trying to make thoughts LEAVE, which is suppression. The move is to WATCH thoughts pass, not push them out, drift not eviction.

**Walk it through:**
1. Settle them, eyes closed if comfortable, and set the scene: sitting by a slow stream, leaves drifting on the surface. Ask: "Got the stream, the leaves moving past?"
2. As each thought arrives, place it on a leaf and let the current carry it, at its own pace, no shoving. Ask: "Try a few, what's it like to set a thought down and just watch it travel?"
3. Stress the catch: when a thought hooks you and you realise you've drifted off with it, that's the rep, note "got caught", come back to the bank. Ask: "When you notice you've been pulled in, can you return to watching without scolding yourself?"
4. The sneaky one: the thought "this isn't working" goes on a leaf too. Ask: "Can you set that one on a leaf as well, rather than acting on it?"
5. Debrief. Ask: "Which thoughts were stickiest, and what was it like to watch them instead of following them?"

**If it stalls:** the stream doesn't land → swap the imagery, clouds crossing the sky, cars on a road, luggage on a belt. It tips into shoving thoughts away → drop the imagery and use `defusion.md 1` Labelling, then revisit `acceptance.md 1` Expansion for the feeling underneath. Too flooded to watch anything → `present-moment.md 4` Dropping anchor (ACE) first.

## 3 · Milk-milk-milk (word repetition)

**Reach for it when:** a single high-charge self-label is fused on hard, "worthless", "stupid", "fraud", "broken", and gentler distancing gives only brief relief. The most studied defusion move, it drains a word's meaning by sheer repetition until you hear it as a sound, not a verdict.

**Walk it through:**
1. Demo with a neutral word first. Have them say "milk" out loud, fast, for 30-45 seconds, until it goes strange and loses its meaning. Ask: "What happened to the word, did it turn into just a noise?"
2. Now pick the charged word, the one-word or short-phrase label that bites. Ask: "What's the single word your mind uses?"
3. Take two baseline ratings BEFORE repeating: how believable the word feels 0-10, and how uncomfortable it feels 0-10. Ask: "Right now, believability out of 10, and discomfort out of 10?"
4. Have them repeat that word rapidly aloud for about 45 seconds. Ask: "Keep it going, fast, tell me when the 45 seconds are up."
5. Re-rate immediately. Ask: "Now, believability out of 10, and discomfort out of 10?"
6. Read the gap, and keep going if needed. Discomfort usually drops first; the target is BELIEVABILITY coming down, so run another round if the word still feels true even when it feels less raw. Ask: "Is the word any less convincing, or just less loud, shall we run it again?"
7. Debrief the mechanism. Ask: "What does it tell you that a word you've believed for years can dissolve into noise in 45 seconds?"

**If it stalls:** feeling silly blocks it → name that the silliness IS part of how it works, not a side effect. The word is trauma-charged and repeating it floods them → stop, don't push satiation, go to `acceptance.md 1` Expansion and `safety.md` first, approach later via `defusion.md 1` Labelling. Discomfort drops but believability won't → it may be a whole self-story, not one word → `defusion.md 7` Defusion from a self-story.

## 4 · Playful voices (singing the thought / silly voice)

**Reach for it when:** a repetitive intrusive thought you've been locked in combat with, where you can already hold it at some distance and want to drain its authority through humour.

**Walk it through:**
**Caution first, check before using:** this is **contraindicated for trauma-related intrusions, OCD obsessions, and shame-rooted thoughts**, where playfulness can read as mockery or invalidation and make things worse. It needs a solid sense of safety and is **not a first move**. If there's any doubt, see `safety.md` and use `defusion.md 1` Labelling instead.
1. Confirm the thought is a fair candidate, not trauma, OCD, or shame content, and that they feel steady. Ask: "Does this thought feel safe to play with, or is it too raw for that?"
2. Get it as a short sentence. Ask: "What's the thought in one line?"
3. Pick the route. Singing: a goofy familiar tune ("Happy Birthday", a nursery song). Silly voice: a cartoon character, sports announcer, slow-motion, super-fast. Ask: "Which do you fancy, sing it or say it in a daft voice?"
4. Have them do it aloud, two or three times through. Ask: "Run it a few times, then tell me, how authoritative does the thought sound now?"
5. Return to a normal voice and re-check. Ask: "Back in your own voice, does it still carry the same weight?"

**If it stalls:** humour doesn't land or feels forced → `defusion.md 3` Milk-milk-milk for a non-humour route to the same draining. The thought belongs to a wider narrative → `defusion.md 6` Naming the story. It starts to feel invalidating → stop, switch to `acceptance.md 1` Expansion and treat the feeling with respect, not jokes.

## 5 · Thank you, Mind

**Reach for it when:** repetitive, predictable self-commentary, the inner critic, the worry loop, the same warning on a loop. A light way to acknowledge the mind's output without either agreeing with it or wrestling it. Works best once there's some willingness in place, so it doesn't feel like a brush-off.

**Walk it through:**
1. Notice the thought as it arrives, without arguing. Ask: "Can you catch it the moment it shows up?"
2. Offer a small acknowledgement, aloud or inside: "Thank you, Mind." Ask: "Try it, what's it like to thank the thought instead of fighting it?"
3. Make the distinction clear: this isn't agreeing it's true OR dismissing it, just noticing the mind generated it, the way a phone pushes a notification. Ask: "Can you hold it as the mind doing its thing, neither true nor an enemy?"
4. If gratitude feels fake, swap the phrase. Variants: "Noted, Mind" or "There's that thought again." Ask: "Which wording feels honest rather than forced?"
5. Return to whatever you were doing. Ask: "Can you let the comment sit there and carry on with what you were up to?"

**If it stalls:** it feels superficial or inauthentic → the willingness groundwork may be missing → `acceptance.md 3` The struggle switch. Want more structure → `defusion.md 1` Labelling. There's a bigger recurring theme behind it → `defusion.md 6` Naming the story.

## 6 · Naming the story

**Reach for it when:** the same cluster of thoughts keeps showing up as a theme, a failure narrative, a "not good enough" story, a "nobody really wants me" story. Giving the whole bundle a title turns a swarm of separate convincing thoughts into one familiar, recognisable guest.

**Walk it through:**
1. Map the cluster, the connected thoughts and feelings that travel together. Ask: "When this mood hits, what's the set of thoughts that tends to arrive as a package?"
2. Find the central claim they all keep making. Ask: "What's the one thing this whole bundle is basically insisting?"
3. Title it together, lightly, ideally with a touch of warmth. Ask: "What shall we call it, the failure story, the not-good-enough story, your own name for it?"
4. Practise greeting it by name when it shows. Ask: "Next time it turns up, can you say 'ah, here's the [name] story again, I know this one'?"
5. Keep using the name. Ask: "Is the [name] story around today, and how much is it running the show?"

**If it stalls:** the story is fused to identity, "I AM this" not "here's a story" → `defusion.md 7` Defusion from a self-story. They can name it but can't find any distance from it → `self-as-context.md 1` The observer exercise to access the you who watches the story.

## 7 · Defusion from a self-story (Sticky Notes)

**Reach for it when:** a rigid, fused self-label has hardened into identity, "I am broken", "I am a failure", "I am a burden." Common in low mood, chronic illness, and old wounds. The goal is not to argue the label is false but to hold it as a story you HAVE, not a fact you ARE.

**Walk it through:**
1. Get the core self-description, the bald "I am ___". Ask: "What's the label your mind has welded to you?"
2. Re-house it as a story with Labelling: "I'm having the story that I am broken." Ask: "Said as a story you're having, rather than a fact, does the floor shift at all?"
3. Trace its history, gently. Ask: "When did your mind first start telling you this one?"
4. Run the Sticky Notes exercise. Have them write each self-label on a separate sticky note and physically stick the notes on their body, arm, chest, wherever. Ask: "Got the labels written and stuck on, how does it feel to wear them?"
5. Now make the observer move: the notes are ON you, you are the one they're stuck to, you are not the notes. Ask: "Who's the one wearing these labels, the notes, or the person they're attached to?"
6. Let them choose what to do with the notes, keep wearing them, peel them off, without that being the point, the point is who's observing. Ask: "Whether the notes stay or come off, who's been here watching them the whole time?"

**If it stalls:** they can't locate an observer separate from the story → `self-as-context.md 1` The observer exercise to build that platform first. The label feels permanent and total → `self-as-context.md 4` Continuity of self, to surface the part of you that's stayed constant while the stories changed.

## 8 · Physicalising thoughts

**Reach for it when:** distancing-by-words hasn't given enough felt impact, and the person responds to sensory, embodied imagery (often a fit for chronic pain too). Turning a thought into an object you can see and hold makes "I am the observer of this" concrete rather than abstract.

**Walk it through:**
1. Get the recurring thought. Ask: "Which thought shall we work with?"
2. Have them imagine it as a physical object and describe its properties, without touching the content. Ask: "If this thought had a shape, size, colour, texture, temperature, would it have any of those, does it move, make a sound?"
3. Let them fill in the detail. Ask: "Tell me more about the object itself, what else do you notice about it?"
4. Mark the split. Ask: "You're the one looking at this object, so you and the thought aren't the same thing, can you feel that gap?"
5. Extend to room-making. Ask: "Could you let this object be here, in your hands or beside you, while you go do something that matters?"

**If it stalls:** the thought has a strong emotional/bodily charge more than a cognitive one → `acceptance.md 1` Expansion, or `acceptance.md 5` Physicalising emotions for the feeling itself. They need plainer cognitive distance first → `defusion.md 1` Labelling.

## 9 · Passengers on the bus

**Reach for it when:** thoughts and feelings are bossing the direction of life, and the person needs to SEE the "thoughts driving behaviour" mechanism rather than just hear about it. Works best once there's at least a rough sense of what they want their life to be about. **Note:** can spike anxiety if the thoughts feel like genuine threats or values aren't clear yet, in which case do acceptance work first.

**Walk it through:**
1. Set the scene: they're the driver of a bus, and every passenger is a thought, memory, or feeling, including the loud difficult ones. Ask: "Can you picture yourself at the wheel, the passengers behind you?"
2. Voice the passengers, shouting to turn around, stop, take a different road, threatening. Ask: "What are yours yelling at you right now?"
3. Surface the old strategy. Ask: "Up to now, what have you done to manage them, argued, negotiated, pulled over and stopped?"
4. Make the key distinction. Ask: "Here's the thing, none of the passengers has a steering wheel. You do. So who actually chooses the road?"
5. Reconnect to direction. Ask: "What road were you trying to drive, what matters to you, before the shouting started?"
6. Land the move: you drive toward it WITH the passengers aboard, still noisy, rather than waiting for them to get off first. Ask: "Could you keep driving that way with them still shouting in the back?"

**If it stalls:** they want something more visceral → `acceptance.md 2` Dropping the rope, same insight in the body. Passengers feel genuinely threatening → pause and build room first with `acceptance.md 1` Expansion and `acceptance.md 3` The struggle switch. No clear road to drive toward → values work before coming back here.

## 10 · Computer screen / radio

**Reach for it when:** a thought's AUTHORITY is the problem, the tone of voice, the sense of an official broadcast, more than the words. Two routes here: on-screen text for a single fused statement, and the radio metaphor for constant background self-criticism. **Note:** either can slide into trying to switch the thought OFF, which is suppression; the aim is to strip its authority while it stays present.

**Walk it through:**
**Computer screen** (a single sticky statement):
1. Have them see the thought as plain text on a screen. Ask: "Can you put the exact words up on a screen in your mind?"
2. Mess with the display, font (Comic Sans, tiny, enormous), colour, orientation, a bouncing karaoke ball over the words. Ask: "Change the font and colour, play with it, what happens to the words as graphics?"
3. Note the words become arbitrary shapes, authority drains, thought still there. Ask: "Same words, different look, does the voice of authority hold up?"

**Bad-news radio / "I'm Not Good Enough FM"** (constant background commentary):
1. Have them name the station. Ask: "If your mind's been broadcasting on a channel, what's it called, 'Doom and Gloom FM', 'I'm Not Good Enough FM'?"
2. Tune in and report. Ask: "When that station's playing, what's it actually saying?"
3. Make the shift: a radio can play in the background while you drive, cook, work, on without steering you. Ask: "Could you let the station keep playing while you get on with something that matters?"

**If it stalls:** trying to mute the station makes it louder → stop fighting it, go to `defusion.md 5` Thank you, Mind for a lighter touch. The broadcast is really one theme → `defusion.md 6` Naming the story to title it. Too flooded to visualise → `present-moment.md 4` Dropping anchor (ACE).

## 11 · "What's the hook?" (noticing fusion)

**Reach for it when:** the person gets defusion in theory but can't yet catch, in real time, the moment a thought takes the wheel. Best once a couple of basic defusion tools are already in hand, this is the awareness skill that decides WHEN to use them.

**Walk it through:**
1. Explain "hooked", a thought quietly starts steering what you do before you've noticed. Ask: "Can you think of a recent time a thought ran you without your say-so?"
2. Install the prompt: a quiet internal "What's the hook?" Ask: "Could you keep that question in your back pocket as a check?"
3. Set the trigger, the moment something pulls you off a valued action or you act against what matters. Ask: "What's a signal you could use, a moment you'd know to ask it?"
4. Name the hook, just spot the thought that grabbed you, don't solve it. Ask: "When you catch one, can you name the thought that hooked you without fixing it?"
5. Apply a quick defusion move (Labelling, Naming the story) and steer back. Ask: "Once it's named, which quick move gets you back on your road?"

**If it stalls:** they can't catch fusion mentally → go body-first, "where do you feel the hook physically?", and use the somatic cue as the alarm, then `defusion.md 1` Labelling. Awareness keeps arriving too late → `present-moment.md 4` Dropping anchor (ACE) to build present-moment contact as the base layer.

## 12 · Rules and reasons ("Get off your buts")

**Reach for it when:** behaviour is run by rigid rules, "I must", "I have to", "I should", "I can't because", or by elaborate reason-giving that stands in for action. Common in perfectionism and worry. The point isn't to debate whether the rule is true; it's to loosen its grip on what you actually do.

**Walk it through:**
1. Listen for the rule language, should / must / have-to / ought / always / never / can't-because. Ask: "Say the thing again, can you hear the rule word in there?"
2. Name it as a rule, not a law of physics. Ask: "That sounds like a rule your mind's applying, what exactly does the rule say?"
3. Run "Get off your buts": rephrase "I want to X BUT I feel Y" as "I want to X AND I notice I'm feeling Y." The "but" blocks; the "and" lets both be true at once. Ask: "Try the sentence with 'and' instead of 'but', what changes when both can be present?"
4. Ask the workability question, the heart of it. Ask: "Has following this rule been moving you toward what matters, or away from it?"
5. Make the key point: a rule can be literally true and still cost you, so workability beats accuracy. Ask: "Even if the rule's technically right, is obeying it serving the life you want?"

**If it stalls:** they accept the rule isn't working but can't act past it → committed action, prove that movement is possible without resolving the rule first. The "but" hides a feeling they're unwilling to carry → `acceptance.md 1` Expansion to make room for the Y so the AND becomes livable.
