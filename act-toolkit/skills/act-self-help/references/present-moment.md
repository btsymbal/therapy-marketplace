## How to use this file

Present-moment contact means flexible, nonjudgmental attention to what's happening **now** — inside and outside — so awareness is free for valued action instead of being consumed by planning, ruminating, or rehearsing. These are functional skills, not a meditation tradition: the point isn't calm or a blank mind, it's contact. **Mind-wandering isn't failure** — each time attention drifts and you bring it back is one good rep of the skill; if it wanders a hundred times, that's a hundred chances to practise returning. For acute distress or flooding, don't start with a long sit — go straight to **Dropping anchor** (4), the fast structured grounder. Assumes the model in `hexaflex.md`; deliver every one per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.** Run guided pieces in small chunks the user actually does and reports back on, and take a 0–100 reading before and after where distress is in play.

## 1 · Mindful breathing

**Reach for it when:** you want a plain anchor for attention — opening a session, introducing the observing self, or a between-times practice. The intention is to **notice and allow**, *not* to relax or to stop thinking; setting that expectation up front is half the technique.

**Walk it through:**
1. Settle and set the intention. "Comfortable position. We're just going to notice and allow — not relax, not empty the mind." Ask: "Set up that way — what are you noticing as you start?"
2. Find the breath as it is. "Don't control it — just feel its rhythm, the air at the nostrils or the rise in the chest." Ask: "Where's the breath easiest to feel for you?"
3. Catch the wander. "When the mind drifts — and it will — just note it: 'ah, thinking.'" Ask: "Where did it go, and could you notice it without scolding yourself?"
4. Return, and count that as the win. "Bring attention back to the breath. *That* return is the rep — the wandering isn't the failure, the noticing is the skill." Ask: "What's it like to keep coming back rather than getting it 'right'?"
5. Step out after a few minutes. Ask: "What did you notice — did the mind wander, and what was it like to keep returning?"

**If it stalls:** if they read the wandering as failure, correct it explicitly — the return *is* the practice. If they can't hold an internal focus → **Five senses grounding** (3) for an outward anchor. If they're using the breath to suppress a difficult feeling → add acceptance framing, or go to `acceptance.md` 1 (Expansion). If they're in acute distress → **Dropping anchor** (4). If a specific thought keeps gripping → `defusion.md` 2 (Leaves on a stream).

## 2 · Body scan (acceptance framing)

**Reach for it when:** chronic pain or bodily tension is in the picture, or you want a longer formal practice. Note the ACT twist: the goal is **acceptance** of whatever's noticed, discomfort included — *not* relaxation. Say that out loud or the exercise misfires. **Caution for trauma: pace it slowly, check in often, and let eyes stay open if needed — see `safety.md` and `trauma.md`.**

**Walk it through:**
1. Settle and set the frame. "Lie down or sit comfortably. This is a *noticing* exercise, not a relaxation one — we're making room for whatever's there." Ask: "Set up that way, eyes open or closed as you prefer — ready to begin at the feet?"
2. Start at the feet. "Soles of the feet — notice any sensation: warmth, pressure, tingling, numbness — without trying to change it." Ask: "What's actually there in the feet right now?"
3. Move up in small sections. Go a region at a time — ankles, calves, knees, thighs, hips, belly, chest, hands, arms, shoulders, neck, face — pausing at each. Ask: "What does this part feel like — not what it *should* feel, what it *does*?"
4. Meet pain with room, not repair. At a sore or tight area: "You don't need to fix this — just notice it, and breathe around it." Ask: "Can you stay with this spot rather than skip past it — what's it at on 0–100?"
5. Debrief. Ask: "What did you notice — any areas you wanted to skip, and what was it like to stay with discomfort instead of fighting it?"

**If it stalls:** if they're frustrated that discomfort didn't lift, name the expectation explicitly — this isn't a relaxation drill. For a less internally focused option → **Five senses grounding** (3). If discomfort is really the main target → `acceptance.md` 1 (Expansion). If the scan stirs trauma material, slow or stop and follow `trauma.md`. If they need a steadier anchor → **Dropping anchor** (4).

## 3 · Five senses grounding (5-4-3-2-1)

**Reach for it when:** acute distress, dissociation, or overwhelming emotion needs a quick way back into the room. It's a fast outward anchor — sensory contact, not internal focus — and works almost anywhere.

**Walk it through:**
1. Land in the present and rate. Ask: "Bring attention to right now — where's the distress on 0–100 before we start?"
2. **5 — see.** Ask: "Name five things you can see right now."
3. **4 — feel.** Ask: "Four things you can physically feel — chair, feet on the floor, clothing, the air's temperature?"
4. **3 — hear.** Ask: "Three things you can hear, near or far?"
5. **2 — smell.** Ask: "Two things you can smell — or could smell if you focused?"
6. **1 — taste.** Ask: "One thing you can taste?"
7. Pause and re-rate. Ask: "What are you aware of now — and where's the distress on 0–100?"

**If it stalls:** if they rush through mechanically, slow the pace — three to five seconds of genuine contact per item, not a checklist. If they need body-grounding as well as sensory contact → **Dropping anchor** (4), which adds it. If they've come back down and can tolerate internal focus → **Mindful breathing** (1). If distress stays at crisis level, follow `safety.md`.

## 4 · Dropping anchor (ACE: Acknowledge, Come back, Expand)

**Reach for it when:** an emotional flood, panic, grief surge, runaway rumination, or an impulse about to tip into action. **This is the structured go-to for acute distress** — a substitute for a long sit. Best practised first while calm, so it's actually available when the storm hits.

**Walk it through:**
1. **A — Acknowledge** (not optional). Name the inner experience without judgement: "I notice I'm feeling overwhelmed; I notice a lot of difficult thoughts are here." Ask: "Can you name what's showing up inside right now — and what's it at on 0–100?"
2. **C — Come back to the body.** Three physical points of contact: press the feet into the floor, straighten the posture, take one slow deliberate breath. Ask: "Feet down, sit tall, one slow breath — done all three?"
3. **E — Expand to the world.** Engage the senses: "Name a few specific things you can see, hear, and touch right now." Ask: "What's around you — give me a few specifics?"
4. Check in and re-rate. Ask: "A little more present, a bit steadier — where's it on 0–100 now?"

**If it stalls:** skipping step 1 and doing only Expand turns this into *distraction*, i.e. avoidance — the acknowledge step is what keeps it grounding, so don't drop it. If they need a stripped-down version → **Five senses grounding** (3). If they need a stronger physical jolt, cold water or a strong sensory stimulus. Once steady, if a feeling now needs room → `acceptance.md` 1; if a thought is still gripping → `defusion.md`. If a safety flag is live, follow `safety.md`.

## 5 · Mindful daily activities

**Reach for it when:** the user resists formal meditation, or you want present-moment practice woven into ordinary life. Homework-style: one routine activity done with full five-sense attention. Keep it small — one activity, or it becomes a burden.

**Walk it through:**
1. Pick one predictable activity. Washing dishes, the morning coffee, the walk to work, brushing teeth. Ask: "Which single ordinary thing will you do mindfully — just one?"
2. Engage all five senses during it. Ask: "While you do it, what does it look, sound, feel, smell, and taste like — can you stay with those details?"
3. Plan the return. "When the mind drifts to planning or reviewing, just bring it back to the sensory details." Ask: "When you notice you've drifted, can you come back without making it a problem?"
4. Keep the scope tiny. Ask: "One activity a day to start — which one, and when?"
5. Debrief next time. Ask: "What did you notice — what was different about doing it mindfully?"

**If it stalls:** if they pile on too many activities it gets burdensome — start with one specific, predictable one. For a more structured formal practice → **Body scan** (2). If they need a defined focus point → **Mindful breathing** (1). If drifting keeps pulling them into a particular sticky thought → `defusion.md` 5 (Thank you Mind). To tie the practice to something that matters → `values.md` 2 (Bull's-eye).
