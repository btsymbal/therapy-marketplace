## How to use this file

Self-as-context is noticing from the stable observer perspective — the "you that notices," distinct from any thought, feeling, role, or self-story. The move you are pointing at is: "You are not your thoughts; you are the one noticing them." It is the most central process in ACT, and the one that gives every other process somewhere to stand. Assumes the model in `hexaflex.md`; deliver each technique per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait** — the experiential drop only happens if the user does each piece live and reports back before you continue. **Caution:** body-focused and historically-focused versions can destabilise dissociative or severely traumatised users. Assess first (`safety.md`/`trauma.md`), keep eyes open, check in often, and be ready to switch to Dropping anchor (`present-moment.md` 4) the moment they float off or flood.

## 1 · The observer exercise ("Who is noticing?")

**Reach for it when:** the user is fused with a thought, feeling, or role as if it *were* them, and you want them to contact the perspective from which all of it is observed. This is the primary, ~15-minute guided self-as-context exercise, best run after some defusion and acceptance groundwork. It activates several ACT processes at once, so go slow and let each noticing land.

**Walk it through:**
1. Settle them: eyes closed if comfortable, a few unforced breaths. Ask: "Let me know when you're settled and ready to just notice."
2. Breathing: "There's breathing happening, and there's a 'you' noticing it. If you can notice the breath, you're not only the breath — you're also the one noticing." Ask: "Can you find the part of you that's watching the breath?"
3. Body: "You've had this body since childhood; it has changed completely. What stayed constant is the 'you' noticing it." Ask: "Notice the body in the chair — and notice who's noticing it. Still there?"
4. Thoughts: "Thoughts are arriving. Notice you're observing them, not being them." Ask: "Watch a thought come and go — are you the thought, or the one watching it pass?"
5. Feelings: "You've had countless feelings across your life; they came and went. The 'you' noticing remained." Ask: "What's here now, and who's aware of it?"
6. Land it: "This observing you has no size, no shape, no history. It can't be harmed by anything it observes. It's simply always there, noticing." Ask: "What's it like to rest as that for a moment?"
7. Debrief gently. Ask: "What was it like to notice the part of you that does the noticing?"

**Brief version (30 seconds):** "For thirty seconds, just silently observe your own mind — and notice who's doing the noticing." Use it as an in-the-day re-entry once the full exercise has landed once.

**If it stalls:** too abstract or "I can't find it" → drop to **Sky and weather** (2) or **Chessboard** (3) as concrete images. If a self-*story* ("I'm broken") is gripping and won't let the observer through → unhook it first with `defusion.md` 7 (Defusion from a self-story). If they dissociate or flood → stop and run `present-moment.md` 4 (Dropping anchor), eyes open.

## 2 · Sky and weather

**Reach for it when:** the user grasped (or needs) the observer stance but wants something portable to carry between sessions — a memorable image for "thoughts and feelings pass; I remain." Works well alongside the observer exercise and in anxiety; use with care in depression.

**Walk it through:**
1. Offer the image: "Your thoughts and feelings are like weather — always changing. Sunny, stormy, clear, then changed again." Ask: "What's the weather like in there right now?"
2. "You're not the weather. You're the sky. Weather moves through the sky — even a violent storm — and the sky stays." Ask: "Can you try on being the sky rather than the storm?"
3. "No weather has ever permanently damaged the sky. The sky just holds all of it." Ask: "What shifts when you let the feeling be weather you're holding, not weather that's wrecking you?"
4. Hand them the practice: when something difficult arrives, internally: "I am the sky; this is weather passing through." Ask: "Will you try that line next time a storm rolls in?"

**Modification for severe depression:** if they identify with the storm and can't find the sky, don't argue them out of it. Offer: "Even in the middle of the storm, the sky is still there — just not visible right now." That keeps the observer available without demanding they feel it.

**If it stalls:** wants something more active than an image → **Chessboard** (3). Wants the felt experience, not a metaphor → **Observer exercise** (1). Storm is overwhelming rather than just present → make room with `acceptance.md` 1 (Expansion), or ground first with `present-moment.md` 4.

## 3 · Chessboard (/ stage)

**Reach for it when:** the user is caught in a war between competing internal states — parts of them fighting parts of them — and identifying with one side. The board image relocates them from combatant to context.

**Walk it through:**
1. Set it up: "Picture a chessboard. White pieces, black pieces — all your different thoughts, feelings, memories, urges." Ask: "What are the main pieces at war in you right now?"
2. "The white and black pieces are always at war; the battle feels urgent and winnable if only the right side wins." Ask: "Which side have you been trying to make win?"
3. Turn it: "You assumed you were one of the pieces. What if you're the board?" Ask: "Try that on — you, the board the whole game sits on."
4. "The board touches every piece but isn't threatened by any. It can't lose. The battle happens *on* it, not *to* it." Ask: "What changes when the fight is happening on you rather than to you?"

**Substitute for non-chess players:** run it as a **stage / theatre** — the thoughts and feelings are actors playing out a loud drama; you are the stage they perform on, in contact with every scene but not damaged by any of it.

**If it stalls:** wants a gentler image → **Sky and weather** (2). Wants the experiential version → **Observer exercise** (1). The pieces are really a fused self-story → `defusion.md` 6 (Naming the story) or 7 (Defusion from a self-story).

## 4 · Continuity of self across time

**Reach for it when:** the user is lost in a present identity crisis, or has been dramatically changed by illness, loss, or circumstance and feels they've lost themselves. Walking the timeline shows the observer has persisted through an entire life of change.

**Walk it through:**
1. Anchor now: "Notice that you're here, right now, aware." Ask: "Can you feel the simple fact of being present and noticing?"
2. Go back: "Picture yourself at about age ten — different body, different thoughts, different worries. And yet there was a 'you' there noticing all of it." Ask: "Can you sense that the one noticing back then is the one noticing now?"
3. Step forward through a few ages: mid-teens, early adulthood, a hard period they choose. Ask each time: "Everything was different then — but was the noticer there too?"
4. Draw the line: "Your body, thoughts, circumstances all changed completely. One thing stayed constant — the observer." Ask: "What's it like to realise that part of you was never lost?"
5. Land it: "That observing self is exactly what you're touching right now."

**Caution:** for traumatic histories, "the same you through all of it" can be distressing rather than steadying. Move slowly, watch their face/voice, and if resistance rises, drop the timeline and return to `present-moment.md` 4 (Dropping anchor). Skip the harder periods unless they're stable.

**If it stalls:** the time-travel framing destabilises → stay only in the present with **Observer exercise** (1) or **Sky and weather** (2). Trauma surfaces → `trauma.md` and `present-moment.md` 4.

## 5 · "I am noticing X"

**Reach for it when:** you want a brief, in-the-moment move that needs no setup and quietly trains both defusion and self-as-context at once. Good for the user's most frequent sticky thoughts and for naming feelings on the fly.

**Walk it through:**
1. Lay out the three-level ladder with one of their own thoughts:
   - Level 1 (fused): "I'm a failure."
   - Level 2 (defusion): "I'm having the thought that I'm a failure."
   - Level 3 (self-as-context): "I'm noticing that I'm having the thought that I'm a failure."
   Ask: "Say all three out loud — does each rung put a little more space between you and the thought?"
2. Run it on two or three of their most frequent distressing thoughts. Ask: "Which of these does the third rung loosen most?"
3. Extend to feelings: "I'm noticing that I'm feeling anxious right now." Ask: "Does naming it as something you're *noticing* change how much it owns you?"
4. Hand it over as ongoing language practice. Ask: "Will you catch one fused thought a day and walk it up the ladder?"

**If it stalls:** it becomes mechanical repetition with no real shift → re-deepen the underlying experience with **Observer exercise** (1) or **Chessboard** (3). Wants something more embodied or narrative → `defusion.md` 8 (Physicalising thoughts) or `defusion.md` 6 (Naming the story).
