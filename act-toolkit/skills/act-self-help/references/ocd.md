## How to use this file

This is the OCD layer, and it is **gated**. ERP (exposure & response prevention) remains the first-line, evidence-based treatment; ACT for OCD is adjunctive and is meant to be run by a clinician trained in both ACT and exposure. As self-help, deliver **only** psychoeducation and the relationship-shift toward obsessions — the change from *fighting/neutralising* thoughts to *holding* them. Do **not** design or run exposures or response-prevention; that is the clinician's territory — signpost it, don't walk it. And never apply Playful voices / silly-voice defusion (`defusion.md` 4) to obsessional content; it can backfire on OCD and trauma. Keep it short and careful. Assumes the model in `hexaflex.md`; deliver per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.**

## 1 · OCD psychoeducation + the relationship-shift

**Reach for it when:** the user is caught in the obsession–compulsion loop — an intrusive thought, image, or urge spikes anxiety, and a compulsion (overt or mental) buys brief relief that teaches the loop to repeat. Teach the mechanism, then the ACT reframe — nothing operational.

**Walk it through:**
1. Name the two halves: the **obsession** (the intrusive mental event that spikes distress) and the **compulsion** (what's done, or avoided, to neutralise it). Ask: "Which thought, and what do you do to make it quieter?"
2. The ACT shift: the goal is not to *eliminate* the thought or prove it false, but to change the **relationship** to it — to hold an obsession as a mental event the mind produces, **not a command and not a truth that has to be acted on**. Ask: "What if the thought could be there without being an instruction?"
3. Hold the line on what's safe to do solo: the actual work of tolerating the discomfort *without* doing the compulsion is exposure/response-prevention, and that is clinician-guided. Ask: "Would you be open to taking that part to a clinician trained in ERP and ACT?"

**If it stalls:** if the relief-seeking is intense and the user wants a method to stop a ritual → do **not** improvise one; signpost ERP and stay with psychoeducation and the safe defusion layer (2).

## 2 · Safe defusion layer (holding obsessions as mental events)

**Reach for it when:** the obsession is treated as a threat that must be neutralised, and the user can engage gently with the idea that thoughts aren't threats. Defusion is the primary ACT mechanism here — but kept to the safe moves only.

**Walk it through:** use **Labelling** (`defusion.md` 1) — "I'm having the thought that…" placed in front of the obsession — and **Leaves on a stream** (`defusion.md` 2), letting obsessions float past rather than be grabbed and wrestled. The aim is distance and a changed relationship, not reassurance, not disproving the content, and not getting rid of the thought. Note clearly: defusion here is **not** a covert compulsion — if the user starts using "leaves on a stream" to *make the thought go away*, that's the loop again, and it's a cue to involve a clinician. **Never** use Playful voices (`defusion.md` 4) on this content.

**If it stalls:** if defusion is quietly being run as a neutralising ritual → flag it and stop; the durable work is clinician-guided ERP plus ACT. If acceptance of the discomfort *without* the compulsion is what's needed → that is the clinician's territory; signpost it, don't run it.
