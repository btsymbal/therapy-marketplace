## How to use this file

This is the conceptual hub the six process files (`defusion.md`, `acceptance.md`, `present-moment.md`, `self-as-context.md`, `values.md`, `committed-action.md`) all point back to. It is **not** a technique to walk through step by step — it's the model you hold in your head so you can route well, explain a move in one sentence, and know where a technique sits in the larger arc. Don't lecture the user from it; metabolize it and use it as a lens. When you do need to teach a sliver (what a value is, why fighting a feeling backfires), keep it to a sentence or two and then run the relevant technique per `walkthrough-protocol.md`.

## What ACT is doing

ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy) targets **psychological flexibility**: the ability to stay in contact with the present moment as a conscious human being, and to change or persist in behavior in the service of chosen values. Its central claim is that much suffering comes not from painful thoughts and feelings themselves, but from the **struggle against them** — and from how that struggle narrows life away from what matters. So ACT doesn't try to make a thought less true or a feeling less intense. It changes the person's **relationship** to those inner experiences, so they no longer dictate behavior, and then points the freed-up behavior toward what the person actually cares about.

## The six processes (the hexaflex)

Six interlocking processes define psychological flexibility. Each is the healthy pole of a corresponding rigidity:

1. **Cognitive defusion** (`defusion.md`) — relating to thoughts as passing mental events ("looking *at* a thought") rather than literal truths to obey ("looking *from* a thought"). The aim is distance, not disproof.
2. **Acceptance / willingness** (`acceptance.md`) — opening up and making room for difficult feelings, urges, and sensations instead of fighting or avoiding them. Willingness is a chosen stance, not a feeling: "you don't have to feel willing to be willing."
3. **Present-moment contact** (`present-moment.md`) — flexible, nonjudgmental attention to what's happening now, inside and out, instead of being lost in planning, ruminating, or rehearsing.
4. **Self-as-context** (`self-as-context.md`) — noticing from the stable observer perspective ("the you that notices"), distinct from any thought, feeling, role, or self-story. The most central node in the flexibility network.
5. **Values** (`values.md`) — chosen directions for living ("be a loving parent", "create", "contribute") — compass headings, not goals to complete.
6. **Committed action** (`committed-action.md`) — building larger patterns of values-linked behavior, adjusting methods while holding direction, with discomfort allowed to come along.

These six are interdependent; cleanly separating one from the next (defusion vs acceptance, present-moment vs self-as-context) matters less in practice than keeping the whole system moving.

## The triflex: Open · Aware · Engaged

The six group into three functional pillars — the most useful working map:

- **Open** = acceptance + defusion. Change your relationship to inner experience: drop the struggle with feelings, unhook from thoughts.
- **Aware** = present-moment contact + self-as-context. Know what's happening, from the vantage point of the observer, here and now.
- **Engaged** = values + committed action. Move in a chosen direction.

The usual arc is **Open → Aware → Engaged**: you generally have to loosen the grip of thoughts and feelings (Open), from a grounded observer stance (Aware), before values and action (Engaged) can actually land. When Engaged work stalls or triggers avoidance, back up to Open. Use the triflex when you can't tell which fine-grained process is live — see `routing.md`.

## Inflexibility → flexibility, at a glance

| Inflexibility (the problem) | Flexibility (the target process) |
|---|---|
| Cognitive fusion (thoughts taken literally) | Cognitive defusion |
| Experiential avoidance (fighting/fleeing feelings) | Acceptance / willingness |
| Loss of present-moment contact (lost in time) | Present-moment contact |
| Attachment to the self-story ("I am ___") | Self-as-context |
| Unclear or avoidant values | Values clarification |
| Inaction / avoidant persistence | Committed action |

Routing is just reading which row is loudest right now and reaching for the technique on the right.

## The workability lens

The question ACT asks of any thought, feeling-strategy, or behavior is **not "is it true?"** but **"is it working — does holding it this way move you toward the life you want, or away from it?"**. A thought can be perfectly accurate and still be a terrible boss to take orders from. A control strategy ("just don't think about it") can feel sensible and still be costing the user their life. Keep returning to workability; it's the spine of every process. Tie acceptance and defusion to *what the user is willing to have the discomfort in service of* — workability is always measured against their values.

## How ACT differs from CBT

CBT (see the sibling `cbt-toolkit` plugin) evaluates and modifies the **content** of thoughts — testing whether a thought is accurate and building a more balanced one. ACT changes the **context and function** of thoughts — how literally they're held and how much they govern behavior — regardless of whether they're true. Both work, and meta-analytically they're roughly equivalent; at the mechanism level the line is blurrier than the theory suggests (defusion and reappraisal may share a decentering core). Practically: if the user wants to *test* a thought, that's CBT restructuring; if the thought "feels true no matter what" or testing it just feeds the loop, that's ACT's cue to stop arguing and **defuse** instead.

## Creative hopelessness: "is the struggle working?"

ACT often opens by gently surfacing that the control agenda has failed — not to induce despair, but to make room for a different move. The question: "Everything you've tried to get rid of this feeling / thought — pushing it down, avoiding, distracting, arguing — has it worked, in the long run? What has the struggle cost you?". When the honest answer is "no, and a lot", the user becomes willing to try the counterintuitive alternative: drop the rope (`acceptance.md`), unhook (`defusion.md`), and put that energy toward what matters (`values.md`, `committed-action.md`). This is the hinge the whole model turns on.
