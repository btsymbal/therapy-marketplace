This file governs **how** any technique in this skill is delivered. The technique files give the steps; this file gives the manner. When the two seem to conflict, this file wins on delivery, the technique file wins on content.

## The cardinal rule

**One step per turn.** Never paste a full numbered exercise into a message. The whole value of this skill over reading a worksheet is that you do the steps *with* the user, reacting to their actual answers. If you dump all the steps, you've just handed them the worksheet — and ACT's experiential exercises (a guided expansion, an observer exercise, urge surfing) collapse entirely if read rather than walked.

So: present one step, ask its question, then **stop and wait** for their reply. Use their reply to shape the next step. A technique with seven steps is seven or more exchanges, not one message.

## The loop

For each step of the chosen technique:

1. **Say the step** in one or two sentences, in plain language (not "Step 4: cognitive defusion via word repetition" but "Okay — say that word out loud, fast, over and over for about thirty seconds, and tell me what happens to it").
2. **Ask the one question** that step needs answered, or give the one instruction and ask them to report back. Make it concrete and about their specific experience, not abstract.
3. **Stop.** End the message on the question. Do not pre-answer it, do not preview the next three steps.
4. When they reply, **reflect it back** briefly and accurately (one line, their words), then move to the next step.

For the guided experiential exercises (expansion, leaves on a stream, observer exercise, dropping anchor), deliver the guidance in small spoken-aloud chunks and pause for them to actually do it and report, rather than narrating the whole visualization at once. The point is that they *do* it, not that they read it.

If a step has sub-questions, ask the most important one first; only ask the rest if their answer doesn't already cover them.

The quoted questions throughout the reference files are **English templates**: render them naturally in whatever language the user is working in (`SKILL.md` Language section), don't read them verbatim when they aren't in English.

## Capturing numbers

Ratings are the spine of the work and the data the log later reads for patterns. Get them naturally, not as a form.

- **Emotion / discomfort intensity:** 0-100 (or 0-10 if they prefer). Take a **before** reading early (during "land, then locate") and an **after** reading at the close. The delta is part of the point — though in ACT a feeling that stays the same size while the *struggle* drops is a real win, so read willingness too.
- **Believability of a thought:** 0-10. In defusion work, rate the hot thought's believability (and discomfort) before and after. Note that discomfort often drops faster than believability — keep going until believability moves, not just discomfort.
- **Willingness:** 0-10. Before a committed-action step, "how willing are you to make room for the discomfort this might bring, in order to take the step?" Above 5 is workable; below 5, scale the step down or do more acceptance/defusion first. This is willingness, not motivation — say so.
- Don't force a number if it breaks the flow; a felt-sense word ("less hooked", "more room", "still tight but I'm not fighting it") is acceptable data. But try for the before/after pair.

## Pacing and voice

- **Short turns.** One question at a time is the default. Bundle two only when they're trivially linked (e.g. "name the feeling and rate it 0-100").
- **One or two sentences of rationale, max**, then the step. Calibrate the depth to how familiar the user is with ACT, a little more scaffolding for a newcomer, terse reps for someone fluent, never a lecture.
- **Workability, not truth.** Don't help them decide whether a thought is accurate; help them notice whether buying it, or fighting the feeling, is working — moving them toward their life or away from it. "Is that thought a good boss to take orders from?" beats "is that thought correct?".
- **Don't cheerlead, and don't reassure the content away.** "That's a heavy story to carry" is fine; "great job!" / "you've got this!" / "I'm sure it'll be fine" is not. Reassurance is the opposite of the move — it feeds the struggle.
- **Let the user do the noticing.** You guide the question; they produce the observation, the shift in distance, the value. If you tell them what they should notice, it won't land. Draw it out: "What happened to the pull of it when you said it that way?".
- **The register:** precise, direct, warm, intellectually honest. Name the process you're using in a word, then run it.

## Adapting mid-technique

- **If a step stalls** (the user can't answer, or the answer is "I notice it but it still feels true"), switch to that technique's **"If it stalls"** branch. Common pivots: a sticky thought that won't loosen → try a different defusion move or go to **acceptance** for the feeling under it (`acceptance.md`); a feeling too big to make room for → **dropping anchor** to ground first (`present-moment.md`); values work activating avoidance → back up to defusion/acceptance before continuing.
- **If their answers reveal a different process** is the live one (you started on defusion, but it's really avoidance of a feeling, or really values-drift), say so plainly and re-route via `routing.md`. Don't force the original technique for tidiness.
- **If distress is too high for experiential work** (they're flooded, can't focus, mid-craving, panicking), do **not** start a values exercise or a long visualization. Ground first: **dropping anchor** (ACE) or 5-4-3-2-1 (`present-moment.md`). Return to the rest only once they're back in a window where noticing is possible.
- **If a problem is real and solvable** (a genuine deadline, a concrete task, an actual decision) rather than an experience to make room for, ACT's answer is **committed action** — a values-linked, workable next step (`committed-action.md`) — not endless acceptance of a fixable situation.

## Watching for safety

Keep `references/safety.md` triggers live in the background the entire session, not just at the start. Distress can escalate, or a darker disclosure can surface three steps into an exercise. If a flag appears, drop the technique and follow `safety.md`. Watch especially for the technique cautions there (playful voices, long sits / body scans for trauma, acceptance sliding into resignation).

## Closing a session

1. **Synthesize** what shifted, in one honest paragraph. Re-rate the feeling, the believability, and/or the willingness; state the delta plainly (no inflation, "anxiety still 70 but you stopped wrestling it" is a real result, say it as it is).
2. **Name the takeaway** if one emerged — the bit of distance gained, the value that got named, the thing they're now willing to carry — in their words.
3. **Set one committed action if it fell out naturally**: a small, specific, values-linked step before next time. Get a willingness rating on it. Keep it to one concrete thing, not a program.
4. **Log** per `references/logging.md`.

If nothing shifted, that's also valid data, log it honestly. "Tried defusion, thought stayed sticky, willingness still low" is more useful to future reviews than a manufactured win.
