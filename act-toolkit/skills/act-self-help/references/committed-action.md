## How to use this file

Committed action means building **larger and larger patterns of values-linked behaviour** — and adjusting the *method* while holding the *direction* fixed. That is **flexible persistence**: when a step fails you change how you go, not where you're going. The difficult thoughts and feelings are allowed to **come along for the ride**; they are not obstacles to be cleared first. Two things govern this whole file. First, committed action needs an **acceptance / defusion repertoire as a precursor** — without it, every step collides with the discomfort and stalls, so if the user can't yet make room for what shows up, pause here and build that first (`acceptance.md`, `defusion.md`). Second, **values govern the direction, not mood**: the question is never "do I feel like it?" but "is this the way I want to be living?" Assumes the model in `hexaflex.md`; deliver every one per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.**

## 1 · SMART-ACT goal-setting

**Reach for it when:** a value is clear but hasn't yet become anything you could actually *do* — the user knows what matters but is vague, stuck, or overwhelmed about where to start. The aim is to translate a chosen value into a concrete, time-framed step you take inside the next 24 hours, not a wish-list.

**Walk it through:**
1. Anchor in the value first. Ask: "Which value are we serving here — what is it you want to stand for in this part of life?"
2. Draft a candidate action and test it against SMART-ACT — **S**pecific, **M**eaningful (genuinely tied to that value), **A**daptive (it actually improves your life, it's *not* avoidance dressed up as a goal), **R**ealistic, **T**ime-framed. Ask: "If we hold your action up to those five, which one does it fall short on?"
3. Build the temporal hierarchy — immediate (within 24h), short (days–weeks), medium (weeks–months), long (months–years) — and pick the **immediate** rung to start. Ask: "What's the one small thing you could do in the next 24 hours?"
4. Make the values link explicit and out loud. Ask: "Can you finish this: 'I'm doing this because I value ___'?"
5. **Micro-commitment** if motivation is low or depression is heavy: shrink the step until it's almost too small to refuse — one text, five minutes, one page. The rule is **do before you feel — motivation follows action, it doesn't precede it.** Ask: "What's the smallest version of this you'd be willing to do even with no motivation at all?"

**If it stalls:** if barriers are looming, map them with **HARD barriers** (4); if the willingness isn't there, take a **Willingness-to-act rating** (5); if the value itself feels thin or borrowed, go back to `values.md` 2 (Bull's-eye) or 6 (Values vs goals).

## 2 · Behavioural activation with values

**Reach for it when:** low mood, withdrawal, or chronic inertia has shrunk life down — activities that once mattered have quietly dropped away. Like CBT behavioural activation, but every activity gets an explicit **values rationale**, so it's not mood-repair, it's living.

**Walk it through:**
1. Find what's been lost. Ask: "What did you used to do that mattered, that has fallen away since things got hard?"
2. Attach the value, not the mood. Be plain about the reframe: "We're not scheduling this walk to fix how you feel — we're scheduling it because you value your health and being outdoors." Ask: "What value does putting this back in your week actually serve?"
3. Anticipate the discomfort and choose anyway. Ask: "You may well not feel like it when the time comes — are you willing to do it with the not-feeling-like-it riding along?"
4. Start small and concrete; use the SMART-ACT immediate rung (1). Ask: "What's the smallest version you could schedule this week?"
5. Debrief afterwards with **acceptance framing**, not a mood-score verdict. Ask: "What showed up while you did it — what thoughts and feelings came, and how did you handle them?"

**If it stalls:** if a fused thought ("pointless", "I can't") is blocking activation → `defusion.md` 1 (Labelling) or 11 (What's the hook?); if the felt cost of the discomfort is what's stopping you → `acceptance.md` 1 (Expansion) before continuing; if low mood plus pain is the picture → `pain.md`.

## 3 · FEAR / DARE

**Reach for it when:** a committed action has already **stalled** and you need to diagnose *why* before trying again. **FEAR** names the four usual culprits; **DARE** is the matching antidote. Use it as a quick differential, not a lecture.

**Walk it through:**
1. Run the **FEAR** diagnostic with the user and find the most prominent one: **F**usion with unhelpful thoughts · **E**xcessive goals (bigger than your time, skill, or health allows) · **A**voidance of discomfort · **R**emoteness from values (the goal drifted from what matters). Ask: "Of those four — fusion, too-big a goal, dodging discomfort, or the goal not really mattering — which one was loudest when it stalled?"
2. Apply the matching **DARE** antidote: **D**efusion · **A**cceptance · **R**ealistic goals (scale it down) · **E**mbracing values (reconnect to the why). Ask: "Given that's the one, here's the move — are you willing to try it on this step?"
3. Re-set the step with the antidote built in. Ask: "What does the step look like now that we've addressed that?"

**If it stalls:** **F** → `defusion.md` 12 (Rules and reasons) or 11 (What's the hook?); **E** → scale down via **SMART-ACT** (1); **A** → `acceptance.md` 1 (Expansion) or 2 (Dropping the rope); **R** → `values.md` 1 (Values card sort) or 6 (Values vs goals). For a more granular barrier breakdown go to **HARD barriers** (4).

## 4 · HARD barriers (+ contingency plans)

**Reach for it when:** a step is *planned but not yet taken* and you want to anticipate what will trip it — especially with a long avoidance history. The job is to surface obstacles **before** they hit and to write the if-then plan now, while it's cheap.

**Walk it through:**
1. For each planned step, ask the barrier question. Ask: "What could realistically get in the way of this one?"
2. Sort each barrier with **HARD** — **H**ooked (fused with a thought), **A**voiding discomfort, **R**emoteness from values, **D**oubtful about ability (self-doubt as its own thing). Ask: "Is this an *internal* one — a thought, feeling, urge, doubt — or an *external* one, like time, money, other people?"
3. For **internal** barriers, apply **DARE** (3). Ask: "Which DARE move fits this internal barrier?"
4. For **external** barriers, write a concrete **if-then contingency plan**. Ask: "Finish this out loud: 'If ___ happens, then I will ___.'"
5. Guard the failure mode: a named obstacle without a written if-then plan is just a worry. Ask: "Have we turned every obstacle into either a DARE move or an if-then plan?"

**If it stalls:** if internal barriers dominate and won't shift → `acceptance.md` 1 (Expansion) and `defusion.md` 11 (What's the hook?); if the doubt is really about whether the value is yours → `values.md` 5 (Values statement); if commitment keeps breaking even after planning → add **Public commitment** (6).

## 5 · Willingness-to-act rating (+ daily willingness diary)

**Reach for it when:** right before a step that you know will bring discomfort, and you need to make the **willingness vs motivation** distinction concrete. Willingness is a stance you can choose now; motivation is a feeling you can't summon to order — this rating measures the first, never the second.

**Walk it through:**
1. Take the rating before the step. Ask: "On 0–10, how willing are you to make room for the uncomfortable thoughts and feelings this step might bring, *in order to* take it?"
2. Hold the line on what you're measuring. Say it plainly: "I'm not asking how motivated you feel — I'm asking whether you'll have the discomfort and act anyway." Ask: "Does that change your number?"
3. Read the threshold. **Above 5 is workable** — proceed. **Below 5** — either scale the step down or do more acceptance first, don't push through on willpower. Ask: "Where did you land, and does that mean we go, or shrink the step?"
4. **Daily Willingness Diary** for ongoing tracking — log three numbers each day: **UPSET** (0–10, how intense the difficult feeling was), **STRUGGLE** (0–10, how hard you fought to suppress it), **WORKABILITY** (0–10, how well today's approach served living well). Ask: "Are you willing to jot those three numbers each evening so we can see the pattern?"

**If it stalls:** if the number stays below 5, the felt cost is too high → `acceptance.md` 1 (Expansion) or 2 (Dropping the rope) to lower it; if you can't even take the rating because you're swamped in the moment → `present-moment.md` 4 (Dropping anchor) first; to diagnose *which* barrier is sinking willingness → **FEAR / DARE** (3).

## 6 · Public commitment / accountability

**Reach for it when:** commitments keep quietly evaporating and some **social weight** would help them hold — or the user simply wants to make a step real by saying it out loud to someone. Stating it aloud changes its standing.

**Walk it through:**
1. State the **valued direction**. Ask: "Say it out loud — 'What I'm moving toward is ___.' What goes in the blank?"
2. Name the **anticipated thought-obstacles**. Ask: "What thoughts do you expect will show up to talk you out of it?"
3. Name **how you usually handle** those thoughts. Ask: "When they've shown up before, what have you typically done?"
4. Test the **workability** of that. Ask: "Has that way of handling them actually moved you toward what matters — or away?"
5. Commit to **one step today**, out loud, to a chosen witness. Ask: "What is the one committed step you'll take today — and who will you say it to?"
6. Pick an **accountability format** if there's no one to tell: a written commitment card carried and reread daily, the willingness diary (5), or a check-in next session. Ask: "Which of those will hold you to it?"

**If it stalls:** if the commitment breaks again, run **HARD barriers** (4) and **FEAR / DARE** (3) before the next one; if the obstacle thoughts are too gripping to say aloud → `defusion.md` 2 (Leaves on a stream); if the direction itself wobbles when stated → `values.md` 3 (Tombstone / eulogy) or 4 (80th birthday) to firm it up.
