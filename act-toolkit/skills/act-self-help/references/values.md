## How to use this file

Values in ACT are chosen *directions* for living — compass headings like "be a loving parent," "create," "contribute" — never goals to complete or achieve. A direction can be lived in this moment and the next; it can't be finished or failed, only moved toward or away from. Values work lands best *after* some defusion and acceptance groundwork; doing it cold tends to trigger the very avoidance you're trying to outflank. Throughout, watch for **pliance** — living by what "should" matter, or to please others, rather than from genuine choice — and use the diagnostic question whenever a value sounds borrowed: "Would you still pursue this if nobody ever knew?" Assumes the model in `hexaflex.md`; deliver each technique per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a question, wait.** Values that surface here flow naturally into `committed-action.md` 1 (SMART-ACT).

## 1 · Values card sort

**Reach for it when:** the user can't name what matters, or names only vague abstractions. The card sort is the most concrete entry point — sorting and ranking does the clarifying that open questions can't. Suitable for almost everyone; usually the first formal values exercise.

**Walk it through:**
1. Offer a set of value words — adventure, integrity, connection, creativity, achievement, health, service, and so on (use a 60+ card list, or generate one). Ask: "Ready to sort these into three piles — very important, somewhat, not important right now?"
2. Have them sort, then set the "very important" pile aside. Ask: "Which made the 'very important' pile — read them to me?"
3. Narrow that pile to ten. Ask: "If you could only keep ten, which go and which stay?"
4. From the ten, rank a top five. Ask: "Which five sit at the very top, in order?"
5. Explore each top value experientially. Ask: "Tell me about a time this one really mattered — what did you do, and what did it cost you?"
6. Name the gap between valued and lived. Ask: "Which of these are you actively living right now, and which have gone quiet?"

**If it stalls:** wants a more visual, domain-structured map → **Bull's-eye** (2). Lists socially acceptable words with no felt contact → switch to a narrative entry: **Tombstone** (3) or **80th birthday** (4). Once a value is clear but stuck → `committed-action.md` 1 (SMART-ACT).

## 2 · Bull's-eye / life compass

**Reach for it when:** the user wants a single visual map of where their living sits relative to what matters, across the main areas of life — and a built-in read on the gap. Doubles as a between-session homework sheet they can re-run.

**Walk it through:**
1. Draw a dartboard split into four quadrants: Work/Education, Leisure, Relationships, Personal Growth/Health. Ask: "Which of these four do you want to start with?"
2. In each, write a brief *direction*, not a goal — e.g. "In relationships, be warm, honest, and present." Ask: "What direction do you want to be heading in this area — what kind of person, not what achievement?"
3. Place an X in each quadrant for how close they're currently living to that direction: centre = living it fully, outer ring = far from it. Ask: "Where does your X land in this one, honestly?"
4. Note what supports and what blocks each domain. Ask: "What's pulling you toward the centre here, and what's pushing you out?"
5. For each domain, one specific action step. Ask: "What's one concrete thing this week that nudges your X inward?"

**Failure mode:** blaming distance from centre entirely on uncontrollable external factors. Gently bring it back to workability — "of the things in your control, what moves the X?" — rather than debating the externals.

**If it stalls:** the four domains feel constraining → fall back to the per-value units of **Card sort** (1). A direction is clear but won't convert to movement → `committed-action.md` 2 (BA with values) or `committed-action.md` 1. Avoidance flares as you name the gap → step back to `defusion.md`/`acceptance.md` before pressing on.

## 3 · Tombstone / eulogy

**Reach for it when:** the user's values stay abstract or distant and you want emotional contact with what actually matters. Imagining the end of a long life cuts through the socially-acceptable answers fast.

**Walk it through:**
1. Invite it: "In your mind's eye, imagine you're at your own funeral, at the end of a long, full life." Ask: "Can you picture the room — who's there?"
2. "Those people meant something to you." Ask: "Who's present, and what did they mean to you?"
3. Someone significant stands to speak. Ask: "What would you most want them to say about the kind of person you were?"
4. The memorial itself. Ask: "What would you want your tombstone to say, in just a few words?"
5. Debrief toward direction. Ask: "What does that tell you about what actually matters — and how close are you to living it now?"

**Failure mode:** the gap produces shame ("I'm nowhere near this"). Reframe it explicitly and out loud: "This isn't evidence of failure — it's a compass reading. It just tells you where north is." A compass reading, not a verdict.

**If it stalls:** death-themed framing is too heavy → the warmer **80th birthday** (4). Can't engage imaginally at all → concrete **Card sort** (1). The shame hardens into a fused self-story → `defusion.md` 7 (Defusion from a self-story).

## 4 · 80th birthday party

**Reach for it when:** the user responds better to celebration than to mortality — same contact with what matters, warmer doorway. Good for those who found the eulogy exercise too sombre.

**Walk it through:**
1. Settle, eyes closed, a few breaths. Ask: "Let me know when you're settled."
2. "Picture your 80th birthday, surrounded by whoever matters most to you." Ask: "Who's in the room?"
3. Someone who knows and loves you well stands to give a toast. Ask: "What do you hope they say — what impact do you hope you had on their life, what do they say mattered most to you?"
4. Pull values from the toast. Ask: "What three values come through in what you hoped they'd say?"
5. One near-term action per value. Ask: "For each, what's one small thing you could do in the next day or two in that direction?"

**Alternative for non-visualisers:** instead of the scene, imagine receiving a birthday card or voicemail from someone important. Ask: "What do you hope the message says?"

**If it stalls:** needs sharper emotional impact → **Tombstone** (3). Can't engage with imaginal work at all → **Card sort** (1). Values emerge but stall at action → `committed-action.md` 1 (SMART-ACT).

## 5 · Writing a values statement

**Reach for it when:** the user has done some clarifying (card sort, bull's-eye, tombstone) and you want to consolidate it into language they can return to — a direction in words, not a destination.

**Walk it through:**
1. Pick one or two high-priority domains. Ask: "Which area do you most want to put into words?"
2. Complete the stems for each: "In this area, the qualities I want to bring to my actions are…" and "The kind of person I want to be here is…". Ask: "How would you finish those two?"
3. Write it first-person, present-tense, as an ongoing quality of action — not an endpoint. Ask: "Can you phrase it as something you *do and keep doing*, rather than something you arrive at?"
4. Pliance check. Ask: "Would you still pursue this if nobody ever knew you'd chosen it?"
5. Connect to action. Ask: "What's one step in this direction you could set this week?"

**If it stalls:** can't generate the language → back to **Card sort** (1). The statement reads flat or borrowed → try the sweet-spot move: recall a moment life felt rich and meaningful, and ask what value was being lived right then. Pliance check keeps failing → it may be someone else's value; revisit with `defusion.md` 7 before rewriting.

## 6 · Values vs goals (+ five failure modes)

**Reach for it when:** the user keeps collapsing values into goals — treating a direction as a box to tick — or when their "values" feel like a to-do list. This is psychoeducation, not a guided exercise, but it's load-bearing: confusing the two is one of the commonest ways values work fails.

**Walk it through:**
1. Draw the distinction plainly:
   - **Values** = ongoing direction (a compass heading); can never be finished; intrinsically reinforcing (the *doing* is the point); never fail — you can always re-engage. Example: "be a loving parent."
   - **Goals** = specific achievable endpoints (way-points on the route); get completed and checked off; extrinsically reinforcing (the *outcome* is the point); can succeed or fail. Example: "attend the play on Friday."
   Ask: "Take something you've been calling a goal — is it the heading, or a way-point on the way there?"
2. Show how they nest: goals serve values; the value is why the goal matters. Ask: "If 'attend the play' is the goal, what's the direction underneath it?"

**Five failure modes to watch for in values work:**
1. **Verbal content without experiential contact** — listing socially acceptable values with no felt connection.
2. **Pliance** — following what "should" matter, or what pleases others, rather than genuine choice.
3. **Premature goal-confusion** — treating values as destinations to reach and tick off.
4. **Metaphor mismatch** — compass, tombstone, birthday party simply don't resonate for this person.
5. **Skipping defusion/acceptance groundwork** — going at values before flexibility work; avoidance then shows up immediately in committed action.

**If it stalls:** can't generate any values to compare → **Card sort** (1). A value is clear but stuck as a heading → convert to way-points with `committed-action.md` 1 (SMART-ACT). Avoidance keeps flaring as goals get named → back to `defusion.md`/`acceptance.md` before pushing into action.
