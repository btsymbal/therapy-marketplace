## How to use this file

Distress Tolerance is the **acceptance**-pole module for getting through pain without making it
worse. It splits in two: **Crisis Survival** skills (1–5) for acute moments — the goal is to get
through without doing something that makes things worse — and **Reality Acceptance** skills (6–9)
for situations that can't currently be changed — the goal is to stop adding suffering on top of
unavoidable pain. **If they're in a crisis and at risk of acting badly, use Crisis Survival
first; you can't accept anything mid-flood.** Assumes `foundations.md`; deliver per
`walkthrough-protocol.md`: one step per turn, end on a cue, wait. Get a before/after SUDS (and
urge rating) — that delta is the measure.

## Crisis Survival

## 1 · TIPP

**Reach for it when:** overwhelming emotional arousal, right now — the first-response skill. It
uses direct physiological mechanisms to drop emotion intensity fast, *before* any cognitive skill
can work. **Safety gate (`safety.md`): do not use the Temperature step with a heart condition, an
eating disorder, or heart-rate medication; scale Intense Exercise to their health.**

**Walk it through (one move at a time; have them actually do it and report back):**
1. **T — Temperature.** "Fill a bowl with cold water (or grab ice / a cold pack). Take a breath,
   hold it, and put your face in the cold water for 15–30 seconds — or hold the ice to your cheeks
   and eyes. Do it now and tell me when it's done." *(Triggers the dive reflex; heart rate and
   arousal drop fast.)* Ask: "What happened to the intensity?"
2. **I — Intense exercise.** "Do a hard burst — jumping jacks, run on the spot, push-ups — for a
   few minutes, until you're a bit out of breath. Go, then come back." *(Burns off the stress
   chemistry.)* Ask: "Where's the SUDS now?"
3. **P — Paced breathing.** Guide it live: "Slow the breath right down, and make the out-breath
   longer than the in — about in for 5, out for 7. Keep it going a minute or two." Ask: "What's
   the arousal doing?"
4. **P — Paired muscle relaxation.** "Tense a muscle group hard as you breathe in, then let it go
   completely on the out-breath. Work up the body — feet, legs, belly, hands, arms, shoulders,
   face." Ask: "Where's the distress, 0–100, now vs when we started?"

Run Temperature first if they're truly spiking; the others follow. The before/after SUDS pair is
the data.

**If it doesn't work, try:** cold too mild → add ice, longer submersion; exercise worsens
agitation → go to paced breathing and PMR instead; arousal still high after → move to **ACCEPTS**
(2) or **Self-Soothe** (3). If an urge to self-harm is in the room, hold `safety.md` live.

## 2 · ACCEPTS

**Reach for it when:** you need to distract from an overwhelming emotion long enough for it to
subside — especially after TIPP, or when TIPP isn't available. This is intentional, time-limited
redirection of attention, not suppression.

**Walk it through (offer one or two letters that fit, don't recite all seven):**
- **A — Activities:** "Do something that takes attention — a puzzle, a chore, cooking, a walk. The
  more absorbing, the better." Ask: "What's available to you right now?"
- **C — Contributing:** "Do something for someone else — text a friend to support *them*, a small
  kindness." (Shifts focus off your own pain.)
- **C — Comparisons:** "Compare to a time you got through something harder, or to others facing
  worse — to broaden perspective, not to minimize your pain."
- **E — Emotions (opposite):** "Generate a different emotion on purpose — a funny clip, uplifting
  music, something warm."
- **P — Pushing away:** "Put the problem in a box on a shelf for now, or build a wall between you
  and it — knowing you'll come back to it when you're calmer."
- **T — Thoughts:** "Crowd out the distressing thought — count objects, recite lyrics or a poem,
  do mental arithmetic."
- **S — Sensations:** "Use a safe, intense sensation to interrupt the momentum — hold ice, a warm
  shower, a strong mint, a rubber band on the wrist." Ask after whichever you pick: "What happens
  to the intensity?"

**If it doesn't work, try:** cycle through several letters instead of relying on one; if
distraction brings guilt, validate it with **Radical Acceptance** (6); combine with **Self-Soothe**
(3).

## 3 · Self-Soothe with the five senses

**Reach for it when:** emotional pain is high and what's needed is comfort and self-care —
especially when loneliness or shame is in the mix.

**Walk it through (pick the sense most available, guide it slowly):**
- **Vision:** "Look at something beautiful or calming — nature, art, a candle flame, photos of
  people you love."
- **Hearing:** "Soothing or meaningful music, nature sounds, or speaking kindly to yourself
  aloud."
- **Smell:** "A scent you like — coffee, a candle, flowers, essential oil."
- **Taste:** "Something mindful — herbal tea sipped slowly, a piece of good chocolate or fruit,
  full attention on the flavor."
- **Touch:** "A warm blanket, a heating pad, a soft fabric, a bath, stroking a pet." Ask: "Which
  sense can you reach right now — and what do you notice as you give it full attention?"

**If it doesn't work, try:** combine several senses at once (a warm bath + music + a candle); if
no single sense lands, move to **IMPROVE the moment** (4).

## 4 · IMPROVE the moment

**Reach for it when:** crisis skills have brought enough calm to try something more cognitive and
meaning-oriented; also good for milder distress.

**Walk it through (pick one or two letters):**
- **I — Imagery:** "Picture a safe, comforting place in full detail — sight, sound, smell,
  temperature — and rest there."
- **M — Meaning:** "Find or make some meaning in this pain — what might it teach, or let you help
  others with later? Connect it to a value."
- **P — Prayer / meditation:** "Use whatever spiritual practice is yours — or simply open to
  something larger than this moment."
- **R — Relaxation:** "Progressive muscle relaxation, deep breathing, a bath, gentle stretching."
- **O — One thing in the moment:** "Bring yourself fully to one present thing — a breath, a sound
  — and refuse to time-travel to past or future."
- **V — Vacation:** "Take a brief, time-limited break — a short walk somewhere pleasant, ten
  minutes outside, a small daydream."
- **E — Encouragement:** "Say something compassionate and true to yourself — 'I've gotten through
  hard things before', 'this will pass'." Ask after whichever you pick: "What shifts?"

**If it doesn't work, try:** combine two or three letters; if meaning-making feels impossible in
acute crisis, drop back to **Self-Soothe** (3) or **TIPP** (1).

## 5 · Pros and Cons

**Reach for it when:** there's an urge to act on a self-destructive behavior — self-harm,
substance use, a rage outburst, ending something impulsively. This weighs the consequences of
tolerating the urge vs acting on it. (If self-harm is involved, hold `safety.md` live.) Best
built at a *calmer* moment so the grid is ready when the urge spikes.

**Walk it through (build the four-quadrant grid with them, one cell at a time):**
1. "Let's name the urge plainly." Ask: "What's the urge, and how strong, 0–100?"
2. "Pros of acting on it — be honest, including the real relief it'd give." Ask: "What does acting
   on it get you?"
3. "Cons of acting on it." Ask: "What does it cost you, short and long term?"
4. "Pros of *not* acting / tolerating it." Ask: "What's good about riding it out?"
5. "Cons of tolerating it." Ask: "What's hard about not acting?"
6. "Now read the whole grid." Ask: "With all four cells in view, which way actually weighs more?"
   Re-rate the urge.

**If it doesn't work, try:** urge too high to think through a grid → **TIPP** (1) first to lower
it; pros of acting feel overwhelming → **ACCEPTS** (2) / **Self-Soothe** (3) to reduce intensity,
then return to the grid. Keep the completed grid somewhere accessible for next time.

## Reality Acceptance

## 6 · Radical Acceptance

**Reach for it when:** a painful situation **cannot currently be changed** — a loss, an illness,
the past, someone else's behavior — and fighting that reality is generating extra suffering on top
of the unavoidable pain. Radical Acceptance is accepting reality completely, with mind and body.
It is **not** approval, resignation, giving up, or saying the situation is okay.

**Walk it through (one move per turn):**
1. "Notice you're fighting reality — catch the 'it shouldn't be this way'." Ask: "Where are you
   refusing what's actually true?"
2. "The reality is what it is, whether or not you accept it. Acceptance is about *you*, not
   approving it." Ask: "Can you say the fact plainly, as a fact?"
3. "All events have causes — your non-acceptance doesn't undo them." Ask: "Even if it's unfair,
   can you see it came from a chain of causes?"
4. "Accept with the whole self, not just the head — let the body soften around it." Ask: "Can you
   unclench, even slightly, around this?"
5. "What would you do differently if you fully accepted it? Then do those things, even before
   acceptance feels complete." Ask: "What's one action that would follow from accepting it?"
6. "Allow the grief that comes with acceptance — grief fits; bitterness is optional. And life can
   still hold worth alongside this." Ask: "Can you let the sadness be here without the
   bitterness?"
7. Note: this is repeated practice, not a one-time event — expect to come back to it.

**If it doesn't work, try:** acceptance keeps slipping → **Turning the Mind** (7); active
resistance / "I won't" → **Willingness vs Willfulness** (8); cognitive acceptance unavailable →
**Half-Smile and Willing Hands** (9) as an embodied entry; prepare for the emotions acceptance
brings with **Cope Ahead** (`emotion-regulation.md`).

---

## 7 · Turning the Mind

**Reach for it when:** Radical Acceptance keeps slipping away — the mind automatically returns to
fighting reality. Acceptance isn't a permanent state; it's a choice you make again and again.

**Walk it through:**
1. "Picture a fork in the road: one path is acceptance, one is fighting reality." Ask: "Which path
   are you on right now?"
2. "Make the choice, consciously, to turn toward acceptance." Ask: "Can you say it — 'I'm choosing
   to accept this'?"
3. "Expect to turn back many times. Each turning *is* the practice — there's no 'done'." Ask: "Can
   you commit to turning again each time you notice you've drifted off the path?"

**If it doesn't work, try:** **Willingness vs Willfulness** (8); use **Half-Smile** (9) as a
physical anchor for the turning; if grief overwhelms, let it move fully and turn the mind again
once the wave passes.

## 8 · Willingness vs Willfulness

**Reach for it when:** stubborn refusal, resentment, or "I won't" — willfulness — is keeping the
suffering in place. Willingness is doing what the situation needs, even when you don't want to.

**Walk it through:**
1. Name it. "Willfulness sounds like 'I won't', 'it's not fair', 'why should I have to'." Ask:
   "Is that the voice that's running right now?"
2. Acknowledge it without judging it. Ask: "Can you say 'I'm being willful right now' without
   beating yourself up for it?"
3. Find the willing alternative. Ask: "What would being *willing* look like here — what would you
   do if you were fully open to responding to reality as it is?"
4. Take one willing action, however small — even a gesture of openness counts. Ask: "What's the
   smallest willing move you could make?"
5. Let the body lead if the mind won't: use **Half-Smile / Willing Hands** (9) to embody
   willingness before you feel it.

**If it doesn't work, try:** **Radical Acceptance** (6) on the emotion underneath the willfulness;
**Turning the Mind** (7); if arousal is too high to shift at all, **TIPP** (1) first.

---

## 9 · Half-Smile and Willing Hands

**Reach for it when:** cognitive acceptance is unavailable and you need an **embodied** entry —
the body can carry willingness before the mind does.

**Walk it through:**
- **Half-Smile:** "Relax your whole face — forehead, jaw, eyes, cheeks. Now lift the corners of
  your mouth just barely, the faintest smile, eyes soft. Hold it a minute or two." Ask: "What do
  you notice as you hold it?"
- **Willing Hands:** "Let your hands rest, palms turned up and open, fingers relaxed, shoulders
  dropped. Hold that posture." Ask: "Does anything ease, even slightly?"

Useful moments to use these: waking before getting up, waiting in traffic or a line, watching
someone you disagree with speak, during physical pain, during practice, facing an unwanted task,
remembering something painful.

**If it doesn't work, try:** pair with paced breathing; if the smile feels forced or mocking, drop
it and use Willing Hands alone, or switch to **Loving Kindness** (`mindfulness.md`).
