## How to use this file

Emotion Regulation is the largest, most **change**-oriented module: understand the emotion, then
change or accept it as appropriate, then build resilience so the crises come less often. Assumes
`foundations.md`; deliver per `walkthrough-protocol.md`: one step per turn, end on a question,
wait. Capture the emotion intensity (and belief in the threatening interpretation) before and
after. **If the user is too flooded to think, down-regulate first** with crisis survival
(`distress-tolerance.md`, TIPP) — none of the cognitive skills here work through a flooded nervous
system.

---

## The decision tree (the spine of this module)

When an emotion is driving the distress, this is the master logic — most "which skill?" questions
resolve here:

1. **Check the Facts** (3) — does the emotion, and its intensity, fit the actual facts?
2. Emotion **doesn't fit** the facts → **Opposite Action** (4).
3. Emotion **fits** the facts AND the situation **can change** → **Problem Solving** (5) — or
   **DEAR MAN** (`interpersonal-effectiveness.md`) if the change is an interpersonal ask.
4. Emotion **fits** the facts but the situation **cannot change** → **Radical Acceptance**
   (`distress-tolerance.md`) and **ride the wave** (6).

This is the acceptance/change dialectic in operational form (`foundations.md`, `routing.md`).

## 1 · Understanding emotions (the DBT emotion model)

**Reach for it when:** the start of emotion work; when the user is ashamed of *having* emotions,
or treats a feeling as a simple fact rather than a process with parts you can intervene on.

Emotions run as a chain — naming the parts both de-shames and shows the levers:
1. **Prompting event** (external or internal — an event, thought, memory, sensation)
2. **Interpretation** (what you make it mean — a threat, a loss, a violation)
3. **Body response** (heart rate, tension, breath, hormones)
4. **Action urge** (flee, attack, freeze, withdraw, cry out)
5. **Emotion name** (fear, anger, sadness, shame, joy, love)
6. **Function** (what it signals — fear: danger; anger: a boundary crossed; shame: a norm
   violation; sadness: loss; love: attachment)

Emotions aren't the enemy: they carry information and motivate adaptive action *when the
interpretation fits the facts*. The whole module hinges on telling apart emotions that fit the
facts (justified) from those that don't.

**Walk it through:** take their specific emotion and pull it apart along the chain, one part per
turn. Ask for each: "What set it off? What did you make it mean? What's the body doing? What's the
urge? What's the emotion, named? What might it be trying to tell you?" Land the lever: because the
parts are linked, you can intervene at the interpretation (Check the Facts), the urge (Opposite
Action), or the situation (Problem Solving).

**If it stalls:** the model feels abstract → build it bottom-up from their moment; too distressed
to map anything → down-regulate first (`distress-tolerance.md`).

## 2 · STOP

**Reach for it when:** an emotion-driven impulse is about to lead to an action that will make
things worse — the moment before you send it, say it, throw it.

**Walk it through:**
1. **S — Stop.** "Freeze. Don't move, don't speak, don't act — just for a second." Ask: "Can you
   hit pause right now?"
2. **T — Take a step back.** "Physically or mentally step back. Take a breath. Make a gap." Ask:
   "What happens if you give it three seconds of space?"
3. **O — Observe.** "Notice what's actually here — the situation, your body, your mind." Ask:
   "What are you feeling, and what does the situation actually contain?"
4. **P — Proceed mindfully.** "Now ask Wise Mind: what do I want to do, and is it effective?" Ask:
   "From that steadier place, what's the move?"

**If it doesn't work, try:** arousal too high for STOP to land → **TIPP** (`distress-tolerance.md`)
first; need temporary distance → **ACCEPTS** (`distress-tolerance.md`); even 30 seconds of STOP
beats none.

## 3 · Check the Facts

**Reach for it when:** an emotion seems out of proportion — the intensity doesn't match what
happened, or a feeling is driving avoidance of something that may not actually be threatening. The
key question: **does the emotion, and its intensity, fit the actual facts?**

**Walk it through (one question per turn):**
1. "Name the emotion and rate it 0–100." Ask: "What is it, and how strong?"
2. "Describe the event that set it off — just the facts, no interpretation." Ask: "What actually
   happened?"
3. "Now your interpretations, assumptions, thoughts about it — be specific." Ask: "What are you
   telling yourself it means?"
4. "Are those facts, or interpretations? What are other ways to read it?" Ask: "What else could be
   true?"
5. "What's the actual probability of the feared outcome?" Ask: "Realistically, how likely is the
   thing you're dreading?"
6. The deciding question: "Does the emotion and its intensity fit the facts?" → **fits + can
   change** = **Problem Solving** (5); **fits + can't change** = **Radical Acceptance**
   (`distress-tolerance.md`); **doesn't fit** = **Opposite Action** (4).

**If it doesn't work, try:** can't slow down enough to check → **Describe** (`mindfulness.md`) or
**TIPP** first; reality-test out loud with someone trusted; if the thought keeps shifting under
the emotion, pin it before checking.

---

## 4 · Opposite Action

**Reach for it when:** Check the Facts (3) found the emotion **doesn't fit** the facts — or it
fits but can't change the situation and is only causing suffering. You act **directly opposite**
to the action urge — and **all the way**.

**Decision tree recap:** emotion justified + changeable → Problem Solving (5); justified +
unchangeable → Radical Acceptance (`distress-tolerance.md`); **not justified → Opposite Action.**

**Emotion-by-emotion guide:**

| Emotion | Action urge | Opposite Action |
|---|---|---|
| Fear | Flee / freeze | Approach gradually; do the feared thing at a manageable pace |
| Anger | Attack, confront, punish | Gently avoid (for now); be kind; act friendly even if you don't feel it |
| Shame | Hide, disappear | Expose yourself (with a safe person if needed); hold your values |
| Guilt (unjustified) | Over-apologize for what isn't your fault | Don't apologize; act on your actual values |
| Guilt (justified) | Avoid the person harmed, shut down | Apologize, make amends, repair, recommit |
| Love (harmful) | Approach, seek contact | Avoid contact; don't act on the urge if the relationship is bad for you |
| Sadness / depression | Withdraw, isolate, slow down | Activate and engage — reach out, move, participate |

**Walk it through:**
1. "Name the urge the emotion is pushing." Ask: "What does it want you to do?"
2. "Now the opposite — and *all the way*: posture, face, tone, thoughts, action all aligned with
   the opposite emotion. Half-hearted won't shift it." Ask: "What's the fully opposite move, and
   can you commit to it completely?"
3. Run it, then re-rate the emotion.

**If it doesn't work, try:** make sure it's *all the way* (a half-done opposite action doesn't
work); arousal too high → **TIPP** first; re-check that the emotion is genuinely unjustified
(**Check the Facts**, 3); if it's actually justified, switch to **Problem Solving** (5) or
**Radical Acceptance** (`distress-tolerance.md`).

---

## 5 · Problem Solving

**Reach for it when:** the emotion **fits the facts** AND the situation **can be changed** — so the
right response is to change the situation.

**Walk it through:**
1. "Define the problem specifically, in observable terms." Ask: "What's the actual problem, in one
   sentence of facts?"
2. "Brainstorm solutions — as many as possible, don't judge them yet." Ask: "Give me several,
   even bad ones."
3. "Evaluate the options — pros, cons, likelihood, what each needs." Ask: "Which look most
   workable?"
4. "Pick the best available one, do it, then check if it worked and adjust." Ask: "What's the
   first concrete step, and when?"

**If it doesn't work, try:** turns out it can't really be changed → **Radical Acceptance**
(`distress-tolerance.md`); the change is an interpersonal ask → **DEAR MAN**
(`interpersonal-effectiveness.md`); the problem keeps shifting under the emotion → **Check the
Facts** (3) to pin it.

## 6 · Ride the wave (Mindfulness of Current Emotion)

**Reach for it when:** the decision tree lands on *neither* Opposite Action nor Problem Solving —
the situation can't be changed and the emotion is a valid response to be **experienced** rather
than escaped. This is the same skill as Mindfulness of Current Emotion (`mindfulness.md`, 9); the
full walk-through lives there.

In short: **name** the emotion → **locate** it in the body → **watch** it as a wave rising,
cresting, falling, without feeding or fighting it → **don't act** on the urge → **wait** through
the crest. Get a before/after intensity reading.

**If it doesn't work, try:** too intense to observe → **TIPP** (`distress-tolerance.md`) first;
needs full acceptance of an unchangeable fact → **Radical Acceptance** (`distress-tolerance.md`);
extreme / breakdown level → **Managing Extreme Emotions** (7).

## 7 · Managing Extreme Emotions (the skills breakdown point)

**Reach for it when:** distress is so extreme that processing capacity is overwhelmed and the
other skills can't be reached — a "skills breakdown point." A bridge back into the skill system.
**See `safety.md`: the self-help-safe layer is the crisis-survival front end plus signposting; the
full protocol wants a clinician, and if self-harm risk is present, that's a crisis, not another
rep.**

**Walk it through:**
- **Pre-step — confirm the breakdown point.** Rate distress 0–100 (SUDS). A very high score
  (80+) confirms it. Ask: "Where's the distress, 0–100?"
- **Step 1 — Crisis survival first.** Use **TIPP**, **ACCEPTS**, **Self-Soothe**, or **IMPROVE**
  (`distress-tolerance.md`) to get arousal down enough to think. Ask after: "Has it come down at
  all?"
- **Step 2 — Ride the wave.** Once arousal eases, **observe and ride** the emotion (6) without
  fighting it.
- **Step 3 — Other ER skills.** If still needed, try **Check the Facts** (3), **Opposite Action**
  (4), or **Problem Solving** (5).
- **Safety caveat:** if nothing works and self-harm risk is present, stop the skill and get
  immediate support (988 in the US / local — `safety.md`).

**If it doesn't work, try:** this is a bridge skill best practiced with a therapist before relying
on it alone in extreme states — signpost that plainly (`safety.md`).

## 8 · Nightmare Protocol

**Reach for it when:** recurring nightmares are disrupting sleep. An imagery-rehearsal procedure
that rewrites the dream before sleep. **See `safety.md`: self-help-safe for low-intensity,
non-trauma recurring dreams; trauma nightmares are clinician-led — offer the relaxation/preparation
steps and signpost, don't run the rewriting on trauma content.**

**Walk it through (one step per turn):**
1. "Get calm first — mindfulness, PMR, Self-Soothe, or a Wise Mind exercise before we start." Ask:
   "Can you settle into a calmer state first?"
2. "Pick a manageable, lower-intensity recurring nightmare — not a trauma one for now." Ask:
   "Which recurring dream feels workable?"
3. "Write it out in full sensory detail — sights, sounds, smells, what you felt and believed in
   it." Ask: "Walk me through how it goes."
4. "Design a change to the story *before* the worst point — fantastical is fine (superpowers, turn
   the monster into a unicorn, become invincible)." Ask: "What new turn do you want it to take?"
5. "Write the full revised version — the usual beginning, your changes, the new ending."
6. "Each night before sleep, rehearse the changed version with the relaxation." Ask: "Can you make
   that a pre-sleep routine?"
7. "During the day, visualize the revised dream and practice the relaxation, as often as you can."

**If it doesn't work, try:** work with a clinician — most effective supported, especially for
trauma; if nightmares are severe and trauma-related, route toward DBT PE / a trauma clinician
(`safety.md`); if relaxation is unavailable at bedtime, use **TIPP** earlier in the evening.

## 9 · ABC PLEASE (building resilience)

**Reach for it when:** building long-term resilience and reducing baseline vulnerability — the
preventive skill, worked upstream of crises ("I keep ending up here").

**Walk it through — ABC (build the positive) then PLEASE (protect the body):**

**A — Accumulate positive emotions.**
- Short-term: "Each day, do one pleasant or meaningful thing — and be fully present in it
  (distracted pleasant events don't accumulate)." Ask: "What's one small pleasant thing you could
  do today, and actually be in?"
- Long-term: "Identify your values across life domains and take one small step toward a long-term
  goal this week — build a life with positives in it, not only crisis management." Ask: "What
  matters that's gone quiet, and what's one step toward it?"

**B — Build mastery.** "Each day do one thing that's genuinely a bit challenging but achievable —
pitched just above comfort, for the felt sense of competence." Ask: "What's a small,
slightly-stretching thing you could pull off?"

**C — Cope ahead.** "For a situation you're dreading: name what you'll feel, write a script of
exactly which skills you'll use and in what order, then rehearse it vividly — including the hard
moment — and end with relaxation." Ask: "What's coming up that we could rehearse, and which skills
go in the script?"

**PLEASE — protect the body that holds the emotions:**
- **PL — treat PhysicaL illness;** **E — balanced Eating;** **A — Avoid mood-altering
  substances;** **S — balanced Sleep;** **E — Exercise.** Ask: "Which of these — illness, eating,
  substances, sleep, exercise — is most out of balance right now, and what's one realistic change?"

**If it doesn't work, try:** in active crisis the resilience work feels impossible → stabilize
first with distress tolerance (`distress-tolerance.md`), then return; pleasant events trigger guilt
→ **Radical Acceptance** (`distress-tolerance.md`) of the right to have positive experiences.
