## How to use this file

The skills in this companion are not a loose toolkit — **they are expressions of this model.** A
little of it, delivered warmly and conversationally, de-shames the user's dysregulation (it's a
system doing its job, shaped by biology and history they didn't pick — not a character flaw) and
makes every skill make sense. You rarely need all of it in one session. Deliver it the way you
deliver everything here (`walkthrough-protocol.md`): not as a lecture, but built from the user's
own example, a piece at a time, checking it lands. Use the parts that explain *their* specific
difficulty.

## 1 · The biosocial theory (where dysregulation comes from)

**Reach for it when:** the user treats their intense emotions as evidence they're broken,
"too much", or defective — rather than as a system that runs hot for understandable reasons.

DBT's account of emotional dysregulation is **transactional**: it grows from the interaction of
two factors over time.

1. **Biological emotional vulnerability** — three features: **high sensitivity** (a lower
   threshold for emotional reactions, so things set you off that wouldn't set off others),
   **high reactivity** (more intense responses when they come), and a **slow return to baseline**
   (emotions take longer to subside). Some people are simply wired this way. (Updated theory adds
   impulsivity as a fourth vulnerability factor.)
2. **A pervasively invalidating environment** — surroundings that, over time, fail to teach three
   things: how to **label and regulate** emotional arousal, how to **tolerate distress**, and
   when to **trust your own emotional responses**. An environment that dismisses, punishes, or
   erratically rewards emotional expression teaches none of these.

Neither alone is the whole story; they shape each other. The point for the user: **the way your
emotions work is a product of biology and experiences you didn't choose** — this is not your
fault. That's not an excuse (you're still the one who works the skills), but it lifts the second
layer of suffering, the shame about *having* big emotions in the first place. The skills exist to
teach exactly the things the environment didn't.

**Walk it through:** map it onto their own example. "When your boss criticised the report, it
sounds like it hit fast and hard and stayed for hours — that's the vulnerability piece. And it
sounds like growing up, big feelings got 'you're overreacting' rather than help naming them —
that's the environment piece. Between them, of course this is hard. None of it is a defect." Land
the relief, then move to a skill.

**If it stalls:** if "not your fault" gets rejected ("but it *is* me"), don't argue it — that
rejection is often the self-criticism the work will address. Note it, stay warm, and route to a
skill rather than debating the model.

## 2 · The acceptance/change dialectic

**Reach for it when:** the user is stuck between "I just have to make this feeling go away" and
"there's nothing I can do" — or whenever you're deciding which skill the moment calls for.

The central idea of DBT: **change happens in the context of acceptance.** Two truths hold at
once — you can accept yourself and your reality exactly as they are *and* work to change what can
be changed. Neither pole alone works: pure change-pushing invalidates the genuine pain and tends
to backfire; pure acceptance abandons problems that could actually be solved.

So for any painful moment there's a real question: **is this a change situation or an acceptance
situation?** The facts decide:
- If the emotion doesn't fit the facts, or the situation can be changed → reach for **change**
  skills (Opposite Action, Problem Solving, DEAR MAN).
- If the emotion fits the facts but the situation cannot be changed right now → reach for
  **acceptance** skills (Radical Acceptance, riding the wave).

This is the engine of the routing decision tree (`routing.md`, `emotion-regulation.md`). Most
"which skill?" confusion dissolves once you locate which pole the situation actually sits on.

## 3 · The four modules and what each targets

Each module addresses a different kind of dysregulation, and the four map onto the two poles of
the dialectic:

| Module | Pole | Targets | File |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mindfulness** | Acceptance (foundational) | Self-dysregulation — scattered attention, being swept away by thoughts/emotions, not knowing your own mind | `mindfulness.md` |
| **Distress Tolerance** | Acceptance | Behavioral dysregulation — crisis behaviors, acting on urges, inability to tolerate pain without making it worse | `distress-tolerance.md` |
| **Emotion Regulation** | Change | Emotional dysregulation — emotions too intense, too frequent, not understood, driving the whole show | `emotion-regulation.md` |
| **Interpersonal Effectiveness** | Change | Interpersonal dysregulation — losing relationships or self-respect, can't ask, can't say no, conflict spirals | `interpersonal-effectiveness.md` |

**Mindfulness is the foundation** — it's the meta-skill that makes the other three accessible
(you can't check the facts or use a relationship skill if you can't first notice and steady your
own mind). That's why a brief mindfulness move often opens the work regardless of which module
the problem lives in.

## 4 · The dialectical stance ("both/and")

**Reach for it when:** the user is caught in all-or-nothing thinking — "you always", "I never",
"it's ruined", "they're all against me" — or splitting a person or situation into all-good /
all-bad.

A dialectical stance holds that reality is rarely all-or-nothing, and that **two apparently
opposing things can both be true at once.** "I did my best *and* I want to do better." "You love
me *and* you set a limit I hate." "This is genuinely unfair *and* I still have to deal with it."
The move is to replace **"but"** (which cancels the first half) with **"and"** (which holds both).

This is both a worldview and a usable in-the-moment skill — especially in conflict, where
all-or-nothing framing escalates things (it's the core of Walking the Middle Path,
`interpersonal-effectiveness.md`).

**Walk it through:** catch an absolute statement, then find the kernel of truth on both sides.
"You said you've 'completely failed' — can we hold both: it's true this went badly, *and* it's
true you've handled hard things before? Both at once?" Watch whether the rigidity loosens.

**If it stalls:** if they can't hold both poles because one emotion is too loud, that's a signal
to down-regulate or work the emotion first (`distress-tolerance.md` / `emotion-regulation.md`),
then come back to the dialectic.
