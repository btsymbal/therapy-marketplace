## How to use this file

Mindfulness is the **foundational** module — the meta-skill that makes the other three
accessible. It teaches the **What skills** (Observe, Describe, Participate — what you do to be
mindful) and the **How skills** (Non-Judgmentally, One-Mindfully, Effectively — how you do it),
anchored by **Wise Mind**. Assumes `foundations.md`; deliver every skill per
`walkthrough-protocol.md`: **one step per turn, end on a cue or question, wait.** These are
practices, not explanations — guide them live, with neutral content first before anything
emotionally charged.

---

## 1 · Wise Mind

**Reach for it when:** Emotion Mind (swept by feeling) and Reasonable Mind (cold logic) are in
conflict — an impulsive urge, an over-intellectualized avoidance, a decision that feels
impossible. Wise Mind is the integration of the two with intuitive knowing; the access point for
every other mindfulness skill.

**Walk it through (pick one accessing exercise, run it live):**
1. Settle them: "Sit or lie comfortably, soften your gaze or close your eyes, let the breath slow."
2. **Breathing Wise Mind:** "On each in-breath, say silently 'Wise'; on each out-breath, 'Mind'.
   Keep going until something settles." Ask: "What do you notice — any sense of a calmer, knowing
   place?"
3. *(or)* **Stone Flake on a Lake:** "Picture a calm, deep lake. Become a small stone or
   snowflake and float gently down, settling on the warm sandy bottom — the centre of Wise Mind.
   What's there at the centre?"
4. *(or)* **Ask a question:** "Take a real question you need guidance on. Breathe it in, breathe
   out, and don't force an answer — just notice what arises, and whether it has the quality of
   *just knowing*, calm rather than desperate."
5. Land what came. Ask: "Reading from that settled place, not the panicked one — what does Wise
   Mind say here?"

**If it stalls:** extend the breathing a few more minutes; slow the imagery down; or have them
briefly note what Emotion Mind and Reasonable Mind are each saying, then look for the third,
integrative read. If strong emotion is blocking access, do **Loving Kindness** (8) or down-
regulate with **TIPP** (`distress-tolerance.md`) first.

## 2 · Observe (What skill)

**Reach for it when:** automatic reactions, thoughts, or urges are pulling toward impulsive
action or avoidance. Observing creates a small gap between stimulus and response.

**Walk it through:**
1. "Bring your attention to what's happening right now — in your body, your mind, the room. Don't
   label it yet, just sense it." Ask: "What's actually here in this moment?"
2. "Let experiences touch your mind and slide off, like water off a Teflon pan — notice a thought
   or feeling arrive without grabbing it or pushing it away." Ask: "Can you watch one come and go
   without engaging it?"
3. "When you notice your mind got hooked by a thought or plan, just notice that, and gently come
   back to the present." Ask: "Where did it wander, and can you bring it back?"

**If it stalls:** pure observation feels impossible → add words with **Describe** (3); narrow to
one sense (just sounds, just the feet on the floor) rather than the whole field; if the emotion
is overwhelming, go to **Mindfulness of Current Emotion** (9).

## 3 · Describe (What skill)

**Reach for it when:** emotions are overwhelming or ambiguous, or there's a tendency to treat
feelings as facts. Putting words to experience lowers intensity and adds clarity.

**Walk it through:**
1. "After observing, put plain words to what you noticed — just the facts." Ask: "What do you
   notice, factually? Try 'I notice a tightening in my chest' rather than 'I feel terrible'."
2. "Use 'I notice…' or 'I observe…' to stay in the observer seat." Ask: "Say it that way — what
   comes?"
3. "Describe a thought *as* a thought." Ask: "Instead of 'I'm going to fail', can you say 'I
   notice the thought that I'll fail'? What shifts?"

**If it stalls:** words are hard to find → slow down and return to **Observe** (2); if judgments
keep slipping in ("it's pathetic"), pair with **Non-Judgmentally** (5).

## 4 · Participate (What skill)

**Reach for it when:** self-consciousness, rumination, or over-monitoring is blocking engagement
— social moments, creative or physical activity, intimacy.

**Walk it through:**
1. "Pick the activity and commit to throwing yourself fully into it." Ask: "What are you going to
   fully enter?"
2. "Drop the self-watching — stop observing yourself doing it and just become one with it." Ask:
   "Can you let go of the part that's grading you?"
3. "Act from instinct and training rather than analysis; when you notice you've stepped back to
   watch, gently re-enter." Ask: "What happens when you stop narrating and just do it?"

**If it stalls:** build up through **Observe** and **Describe** first; use **One-Mindfully** (6)
as a bridge; if performance anxiety is high, lower arousal with **TIPP**
(`distress-tolerance.md`) before starting.

## 5 · Non-Judgmentally (How skill)

**Reach for it when:** self-criticism, shame, or good/bad evaluation is intensifying the emotion
or blocking problem-solving. The skill separates *describing* (factual, observable) from
*evaluating* (right/wrong, good/bad).

**Walk it through:**
1. "Catch the judgment as it appears — 'this is terrible', 'I'm an idiot', 'that was wrong'." Ask:
   "What's the judgment your mind just made?"
2. "Acknowledge it without judging the judging: 'I notice I'm having the judgment that…'." Ask:
   "Can you say it that way?"
3. "Now restate it as pure description — what happened, what you felt, the consequence — with the
   evaluation stripped out." Ask: "What's the just-the-facts version?"
4. Note the distinction: describing a consequence ("that led to X") is allowed; condemning the
   person ("I'm bad") is the part to release.

**If it stalls:** practice on neutral, low-stakes content first; pair with **Describe** (3); if
shame is the engine, go to **Loving Kindness** (8).

## 6 · One-Mindfully (How skill)

**Reach for it when:** divided attention, multitasking, or mental fragmentation is raising
distress or dropping effectiveness. Do one thing at a time.

**Walk it through:**
1. "Choose one task and give it your whole attention." Ask: "What's the one thing, right now?"
2. "When the mind jumps to another task or worry, notice it and return." Ask: "Where did it go —
   and can you bring it back to just this?"
3. "If something else genuinely needs doing, finish or pause this first — don't split." Ask:
   "Can you let the other things wait their turn?"

**If it stalls:** cut external distractors first; if anxiety is driving the scatter, do paced
breathing or **TIPP** (`distress-tolerance.md`) first; pair with **Effectively** (7) when there's
pressure to do everything at once.

## 7 · Effectively (How skill)

**Reach for it when:** being right, being fair, or honoring an ideal is getting in the way of
doing what actually works. Effectiveness means doing what the situation requires, not what *ought*
to be true.

**Walk it through:**
1. "Name your actual goal here — what outcome do you really need?" Ask: "What are you trying to
   get?"
2. "Now what would actually get you there, given reality as it is?" Ask: "What works, regardless
   of fair?"
3. Catch the trap: "Are you prioritizing being right / fair / principled over being effective?"
   Ask the deciding question: "Would you rather be right, or get what you need?"
4. "Act the effective way, even if it feels unfair or imperfect."

**If it stalls:** unclear what situation they're actually in → **Check the Facts**
(`emotion-regulation.md`); the issue is an interpersonal ask → **DEAR MAN**
(`interpersonal-effectiveness.md`); this may be an acceptance moment, not a change one → **Radical
Acceptance** (`distress-tolerance.md`).

## 8 · Loving Kindness

**Reach for it when:** self-criticism, shame, or interpersonal alienation is present; as a warmth
supplement to other mindfulness skills, or to soften toward a difficult person.

**Walk it through:**
1. "Sit comfortably, a few slow breaths." Then start with the self: "Silently repeat: *May I be
   safe. May I be happy. May I be healthy. May I live with ease.* Let them land gently — don't
   force a feeling." Ask: "What happens as you say them?"
2. "Now bring to mind someone you care about easily, and send the same phrases to them." Ask:
   "How does that one feel?"
3. "Then a neutral person — someone you neither like nor dislike. Then, if you're ready, a
   difficult person. Then all beings everywhere." Ask: "Where does it get harder, and where does
   it flow?"

**If it stalls:** start smaller — only the self, or a pet or child, before widening. If
self-directed phrases trigger strong self-criticism, ease off and return to **Observe** (2) and
**Non-Judgmentally** (5); deeper self-compassion blocks are better worked with a clinician.

## 9 · Mindfulness of Current Emotion (riding the wave)

**Reach for it when:** an emotion is intense and there's an urge to suppress it, escape it, or act
on it — and (per the decision tree) the emotion fits the facts but the situation can't be changed,
so the move is to *experience* it rather than fix or flee it. Also reachable from Emotion
Regulation (`emotion-regulation.md`) as "ride the wave".

**Walk it through:**
1. "Name it: 'I'm noticing anger' / 'this is grief'." Ask: "What's the emotion, named?"
2. "Find it in the body — chest, jaw, stomach? Describe its physical qualities." Ask: "Where is
   it, and what does it actually feel like?"
3. "Watch it like a wave — rising, cresting, falling. Don't feed it, don't fight it." Ask: "Can
   you observe it without amplifying or pushing it away?"
4. "Notice the action urge it's creating — to yell, withdraw, escape — without acting on it." Ask:
   "What does it want you to do, and can you not do it, just for now?"
5. "Wait through the crest. Emotions that aren't fed or suppressed tend to peak and pass." Ask:
   "Where's it on the wave now?"

**If it stalls:** too intense to observe → **TIPP** (`distress-tolerance.md`) first to bring
arousal down, then return; tied to an unchangeable situation that needs full acceptance →
**Radical Acceptance** (`distress-tolerance.md`); if it's a skills-breakdown level of extreme →
**Managing Extreme Emotions** (`emotion-regulation.md`, see `safety.md`).
