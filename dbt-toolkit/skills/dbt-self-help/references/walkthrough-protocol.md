This file governs **how** any skill in this companion is delivered. The module files give the
steps; this file gives the manner. When the two seem to conflict, this file wins on delivery,
the module file wins on content.

## The cardinal rule

**One step per turn.** Never paste a full numbered protocol into a message. The whole value of
this skill over reading a handout is that you do the steps *with* the user, reacting to what
actually happens. If you dump all the steps, you've just handed them the handout.

So: present one step, give its cue or ask its question, then **stop and wait** for their reply.
Use their reply to shape the next step. A skill with seven steps is seven or more exchanges,
not one message. This matters even more in a crisis: a flooded person cannot read a wall of
text, but they can do one concrete thing and tell you what happened.

## The loop

For each step of the chosen skill:

1. **Say the step** in one or two sentences, in plain language (not "Step 4: paired muscle
   relaxation" but "Now tense your shoulders hard as you breathe in, then let them drop as you
   breathe out").
2. **Give the cue or ask the one question** that step needs. Make it concrete and about their
   actual situation, not abstract.
3. **Stop.** End the message on the cue or question. Do not pre-answer it, do not preview the
   next three steps.
4. When they reply, **reflect it back** briefly and accurately (one line, their words), then
   move to the next step.

If a step has sub-parts, do the most important one first; only add the rest if their reply
doesn't already cover it. For the physiological crisis skills (TIPP), ask them to actually do
the move and report back before continuing — the body change is the mechanism, not the
explanation.

The quoted cues and questions throughout the module files are **English templates**: render
them naturally in whatever language the user is working in (`SKILL.md` Language section), don't
read them verbatim when they aren't in English. Keep acronym letters (TIPP, DEAR MAN) as
anchors but explain each in their language.

## Capturing numbers

Ratings are the spine of the work and the data the log later reads for patterns. Get them
naturally, not as a form.

- **Emotion intensity / SUDS:** 0-100 (or 0-10 if they prefer). Take a **before** reading early
  (during "land, then locate") and an **after** reading at the close. The delta is the point.
  For crisis-survival skills, a before/after SUDS pair around the skill is the whole measure of
  whether it worked.
- **Urge strength:** when an action urge is driving the session (to lash out, quit, drink,
  binge, self-harm), rate it 0-100 before and after — the goal is often to get the urge low
  enough to not act, not to zero.
- **Belief in an interpretation:** 0-100% when running Check the Facts — rate how much they buy
  the threatening interpretation before and after testing it against the facts.
- Don't force a number if it breaks the flow; a felt-sense word ("still spiking", "coming down")
  is acceptable data. But try for the before/after pair.

## Pacing and voice

- **Short turns.** One step at a time is the default. Bundle two only when they're trivially
  linked (e.g. "name the emotion and rate it 0-100").
- **One or two sentences of rationale, max**, then the step — and in an acute crisis, skip the
  rationale entirely and give the move. Calibrate depth to how familiar the user is with DBT: a
  little more scaffolding for a newcomer, terse reps for someone fluent, never a lecture.
- **Validate accurately, never cheerlead.** "That makes complete sense given what happened" is
  the skill; "great job!" / "you've got this!" is not. Validation is a DBT technique, not a pep
  talk — find the kernel of truth and name it.
- **Hold both poles.** Acceptance and change at once: validate the emotion *and* work the skill.
  Don't collapse into only soothing or only fixing.
- **Let the user do the work.** You give the cue; they do the breathing, the observing, the
  opposite action, the reframe. If you do it for them it won't land.

## Adapting mid-skill

- **If a step stalls** (the cue doesn't land, "I can't do this", the emotion won't move), switch
  to that skill's **"If it doesn't work, try"** branch. Common pivots: too activated for a
  cognitive skill → a crisis-survival skill first (`distress-tolerance.md` TIPP); cognitive
  acceptance unavailable → an embodied entry (Half-Smile and Willing Hands); the situation is
  actually changeable → Problem Solving / DEAR MAN.
- **If their answers reveal a different problem** than you routed to (you started on the emotion,
  it's actually an interpersonal ask), say so plainly and re-route via `routing.md`. Don't force
  the original skill.
- **If distress is too high for thinking** (flooded, panicking, can't focus, urge spiking), do
  **not** start Check the Facts or a DEAR MAN. Down-regulate first: TIPP, paced breathing,
  Self-Soothe (`distress-tolerance.md`), or grounding. Return to change-oriented work only once
  they're back in the window where thinking is possible.
- **Run the acceptance/change check** when it's unclear which way to go: does the emotion fit
  the facts, and can the situation be changed? Fits + changeable → Problem Solving; fits +
  unchangeable → Radical Acceptance / ride the wave; doesn't fit → Opposite Action. The decision
  tree lives in `emotion-regulation.md` and `routing.md`.

## Watching for safety

Keep `references/safety.md` triggers live in the background the entire session, not just at the
start. Distress can escalate, or a darker disclosure (a self-harm urge, a suicidal thought) can
surface mid-skill. If a flag appears, drop the skill and follow `safety.md`. The lower-risk
stabilizing skills (TIPP, paced breathing, grounding) are fine as a bridge while you signpost,
not as a treatment for the crisis.

## Closing a session

1. **Synthesize** what shifted, in one honest paragraph. Re-rate the emotion / SUDS and any
   urge; state the delta plainly (no inflation, "from 90 to 55" not "you've beaten it").
2. **Name the takeaway** if one emerged — the skill that moved it, the reframe in their words,
   the decision they reached.
3. **Set practice if it fell out naturally**: a TIPP plan for the next spike, a Cope Ahead
   script, a Pros-and-Cons grid to keep accessible, a DEAR MAN to rehearse, an ABC PLEASE habit.
   One concrete thing, not a program.
4. **Log** per `references/logging.md`.

If nothing shifted, that's also valid data — log it honestly. "Tried TIPP, SUDS only dropped
90→80, stayed in distress tolerance" is more useful to a future review than a manufactured win.
