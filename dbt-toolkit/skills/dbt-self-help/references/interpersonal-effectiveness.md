## How to use this file

Interpersonal Effectiveness is a **change**-pole module: getting what you need, keeping
relationships, and keeping self-respect — usually all three at once, in tension. The organizing
question is **which goal is primary in this interaction?** Assumes `foundations.md`; deliver per
`walkthrough-protocol.md`: one step per turn, end on a question, wait. These skills are best
rehearsed *before* the conversation — run them as a Cope Ahead (`emotion-regulation.md`) when the
real interaction is still ahead.

---

## Which goal is primary? (the routing question)

Before reaching for a skill, locate the primary goal — it picks the acronym:

- **Get what you want / say no** (objective primary) → **DEAR MAN** (1)
- **Preserve or improve the relationship** (relationship primary) → **GIVE** (2)
- **Act in line with your values / keep self-respect** (self-respect primary) → **FAST** (3)
- **More than one matters** → combine: DEAR MAN + GIVE, DEAR MAN + FAST, or all three.

Ask: "In this conversation, what matters most — getting the outcome, the relationship, or your own
self-respect?" Then build the matching skill, grafting in the others as needed.

## 1 · DEAR MAN (objective effectiveness)

**Reach for it when:** the primary goal is to **get what you want, make a request, or say no**
while keeping self-respect and the relationship (secondary).

**Walk it through (build their actual script, one letter at a time):**
- **D — Describe.** "State the situation in plain facts — no interpretation." Ask: "What's the
  factual situation? e.g. 'Yesterday you said you'd call by 6 and I heard from you at 9.'"
- **E — Express.** "Say how you feel about it, with 'I' statements." Ask: "What's the 'I felt…'?"
- **A — Assert.** "Ask clearly for what you want, or say no clearly — don't make them mind-read."
  Ask: "What's the specific ask?"
- **R — Reinforce.** "Name the benefit to *them* of giving it." Ask: "What's in it for them, or
  why's it good for the relationship?"
- **M — (stay) Mindful.** "Stay on your goal — broken-record it, don't get pulled into attacks or
  tangents." Ask: "If they deflect, what's your one-line return to the ask?"
- **A — Appear confident.** "Confident tone, eye contact, composed posture — even if you don't feel
  it." Ask: "How will you carry yourself?"
- **N — Negotiate.** "Be willing to offer alternatives, give to get, find the middle." Ask:
  "What's your fallback ask if the full one's a no? 'What would work for you?'"

**If it doesn't work, try:** check the timing's right; relationship is strained → add **GIVE** (2);
unsure how hard to push → **The Dime Game** (4); worry thoughts are blocking assertiveness →
**Check the Facts** (`emotion-regulation.md`) on the specific worry.

## 2 · GIVE (relationship effectiveness)

**Reach for it when:** the primary goal is to **preserve or improve the relationship** — even at
the cost of not fully getting your way. Combine with DEAR MAN when both matter.

**Walk it through:**
- **G — (be) Gentle.** "No attacks, threats, judging, or contempt — even if provoked. Describe
  behavior, don't assault character." Ask: "How do you keep it gentle even where it stings?"
- **I — (act) Interested.** "Show genuine interest — listen, eye contact, don't interrupt." Ask:
  "Can you let them feel heard first?"
- **V — Validate.** "Acknowledge their feelings/view make sense given their experience — find the
  kernel of truth even where you disagree." Ask: "What's the true part of their side you can name?"
  (See Validation, 5.)
- **E — (use an) Easy manner.** "Lighten it — warmth, a little humor, persuasion over pressure."
  Ask: "How do you keep it easy rather than heavy?"

**If it doesn't work, try:** self-respect is getting sacrificed for the relationship → add **FAST**
(3); validation feels dismissive → check you're at the right **level** (5); anger is breaking the
gentleness → **Opposite Action** for anger (`emotion-regulation.md`).

## 3 · FAST (self-respect effectiveness)

**Reach for it when:** the primary goal is to **keep self-respect and act on your values** —
regardless of whether you get the outcome or please the other person. Often combined with DEAR MAN
for values-laden asks.

**Walk it through:**
- **F — (be) Fair.** "Be fair to yourself *and* to them — neither over-give nor dismiss their
  valid concerns." Ask: "Where's the fair line here?"
- **A — (no) Apologies.** "Don't apologize for existing, having needs, asking, or disagreeing —
  reserve apologies for genuine wrongdoing." Ask: "Where are you over-apologizing?"
- **S — Stick to values.** "Name your values before the interaction and hold them under pressure —
  if you'd be ashamed of the behavior later, it's probably off-values." Ask: "What value do you
  want to act from here?"
- **T — (be) Truthful.** "No lies, exaggeration, or playing helpless to get out of conflict." Ask:
  "Can you stay honest even in the hard moment?"

**If it doesn't work, try:** pressure is overwhelming FAST → reduce distress first with **ACCEPTS**
/ **TIPP** (`distress-tolerance.md`); values feel fuzzy → name them explicitly first; rehearse the
whole thing with **Cope Ahead** (`emotion-regulation.md`).

## 4 · The Dime Game (intensity calibration)

**Reach for it when:** deciding **how hard** to make a request or say no — calibrating the force on
a 0 (don't ask at all) to 10 (ask with maximum intensity) scale.

**Walk it through:** weigh these ten factors with them — higher answers push the intensity up:
1. How capable is the other person of giving it? 2. Does the situation require an ask? 3. How
important is this to your long-term goals? 4. Is this a relationship where asking is appropriate?
5. Will asking help or damage the relationship? 6. Are you feeling competent/self-respecting enough
to ask? 7. Are you giving fair weight to your own interests? 8. Is the timing right? 9. Have you
earned the right to ask here? 10. Is the ask morally/ethically right?

Ask: "Running through those, does this feel like a low, middling, or high-intensity ask — and so
how firmly do you pitch it?"

**If it doesn't work, try:** when genuinely unsure, default to a moderate 4–6; consult a trusted
person; if the real question is *whether* to ask at all, run **Pros and Cons**
(`distress-tolerance.md`).

## 5 · Validation (levels 1–6)

**Reach for it when:** reducing invalidation in an interaction — validating someone else (inside
GIVE, 2), or understanding what validation actually is (including for yourself). The six levels go
deeper as they rise:

| Level | Name | What it involves |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Being present | Undivided attention, full focus, no distraction |
| 2 | Accurate reflection | Reflecting back what they said, showing you heard |
| 3 | Mind reading | Articulating what they might feel/think but haven't said |
| 4 | In terms of history | "That makes sense given your history / circumstances" |
| 5 | Normalizing | "Anyone would feel that in this situation" |
| 6 | Radical genuineness | Treating them as a capable equal, a full human being |

**Walk it through:** identify who needs validating and pick a level that matches the moment. Ask:
"What's the deepest true thing you can say about why their reaction makes sense?" Higher levels are
more validating; over-using levels 1–2 when 5–6 is called for can feel dismissive. Level 6 is the
most therapeutic and the hardest.

**If it doesn't work, try:** validation getting rejected → check the level matches what they need;
your own emotional state may be blocking genuine validation → **Observe** (`mindfulness.md`) before
trying again.

## 6 · What interferes

**Reach for it when:** DEAR MAN / GIVE / FAST aren't working and you need to find what's blocking
it. Five categories and their fixes:

| Factor | What it looks like | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| Skill deficit | "I don't know how to do this" | Rehearse it — role-play, or with a trusted person |
| Worry thoughts | "If I ask, they'll leave me" / "I don't deserve this" | **Check the Facts** (`emotion-regulation.md`) on the worry |
| Emotions | Too distressed to use the skills | Down-regulate first (`distress-tolerance.md` / `emotion-regulation.md`), then the IE skill |
| Indecision about goals | "I don't know what I want" | **Pros and Cons** (`distress-tolerance.md`) on the goal |
| Environmental constraints | The other person is genuinely abusive or unable to respond | **Problem Solving** (`emotion-regulation.md`) about the relationship/context itself; if abusive, see `safety.md` |

Ask: "Which of these is in the way — not knowing how, worry thoughts, too much emotion, not knowing
what you want, or the situation itself?"

## 7 · Walking the Middle Path (DBT-A)

**Reach for it when:** family or parent–teen conflict marked by extreme polarization —
all-or-nothing thinking, splitting, escalating "you always / you never". Added in the adolescent
adaptation; built on the dialectical stance (`foundations.md`, 4).

**Core moves:**
- **Dialectical thinking ("both/and"):** reality is rarely all-or-nothing; two opposing things can
  both be true. Replace "but" with "and".
- **Validation in the family:** caregivers learn validation (levels 1–6, skill 5) to reduce the
  invalidating responses that worsen dysregulation.
- **Behavioral principles:** both sides learn that behavior follows its consequences (reinforcement,
  extinction, shaping) — fewer power struggles, more shaping.

**Walk the "both/and" through:**
1. "Catch the absolute statement — 'you never trust me', 'you always lie'." Ask: "What's the
   all-or-nothing line?"
2. "Find the kernel of truth on *both* sides." Ask: "What's true on each side at once? e.g. 'I have
   lied before AND I've been honest recently.'"
3. "Replace 'but' with 'and' — 'but' cancels the first half, 'and' holds both." Ask: "Can you say
   it as an 'and'?"
4. Example to land it: "'You love me AND you set limits I hate' — both true at once."

**If it doesn't work, try:** in heated family conflict, individual emotion skills (e.g. **Opposite
Action** for anger, `emotion-regulation.md`) may need to come first; a family therapist trained in
DBT-A is strongly recommended for sustained work.
