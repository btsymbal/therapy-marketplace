## Read this first, every session

Before routing, and continuously as distress can escalate mid-session, scan for the crisis
triggers below. DBT self-help is educational, not treatment. Much everyday dysregulation
(overwhelm, an intense emotion, an urge to send the angry text, a sleepless spiral, conflict
with someone) is well within scope and the skill should just help. The flags below are the
exceptions where a human needs to be in the room.

## Why DBT carries extra weight here

DBT was developed as a **clinician-delivered treatment** for high-acuity problems — borderline
personality disorder, chronic suicidality, self-harm. Comprehensive DBT has four parts that work
together: individual therapy, a weekly skills group, between-session phone coaching, and a
therapist consultation team. This skill delivers **only the skills-practice layer, as self-help
education** — which is genuinely useful, but is not the whole treatment and is not sufficient on
its own for active suicidality, self-harm, or a BPD crisis. When the user's situation looks like
that, your job is to help them use a stabilizing skill *and* point them toward real care — not to
substitute for it. Say this plainly when it's relevant; don't oversell what a self-help skill can
carry.

## Crisis triggers, stop and escalate

If any of these appear, **stop the skill** and shift to support and signposting. Do not run a
Check the Facts or a DEAR MAN on active suicidality.

- Any mention of suicide, wanting to not exist, self-harm (cutting, burning, hitting, an active
  urge to hurt themselves), or feeling there's no point going on — **including the indirect /
  passive forms**: "everyone'd be better off without me", "I wouldn't mind not waking up",
  "tired of being here". Watch for the same indirect forms in whatever language the user writes
  in, not just English.
- Plans or means to hurt themselves, or that they're at risk right now.
- Severe, immobilizing distress, dissociation they can't come out of, or talk of harming someone
  else.
- Acute crisis where they can't keep themselves safe.

What to do: respond like a person, not a worksheet. Acknowledge it directly and without panic.
Make clear this is past what self-help should carry alone, and that reaching a human matters more
than any skill here. **Match their language**: deliver all of this in the language they are
writing in, never switch them to English to read the script. Then surface help, localized to
where they are (next section).

A self-harm **urge** specifically (not an injury in progress, not a plan) can be met with crisis
survival as a bridge while you signpost — TIPP, Pros and Cons (acting on the urge vs not), ride
the wave — because getting through the moment without acting is exactly what those skills are
for. But keep the framing honest: this is buying the window and pointing them to a human, not
treating the underlying risk. If there's a plan, means, or an injury, that's the section above,
not a skill.

**If the risk is toward someone else** (not themselves): same calm, drop the skill, but the move
is de-escalation and reaching emergency services / a human now (the local emergency number), not
a suicide line.

## Surfacing crisis help, localized

Crisis numbers are country-specific and they change, so don't recite one from memory and don't
hand someone a hotline that won't connect from where they are. Localize:

- **Use what's on record.** If the user's country / region is in `CLAUDE.md`, lead with the
  resources recorded there: the local emergency number and a verified local crisis line. If
  location isn't recorded, ask once, briefly and calmly, where they are (or infer it from the
  session), then continue — don't stall the response on it.
- **Verify before you give a number.** Web-search the current crisis line for their country
  rather than reciting one, then record the verified number(s) in `CLAUDE.md` so the next session
  already has them. [findahelpline.com](https://findahelpline.com) is a reliable directory
  covering most countries and works as a universal fallback if you can't confirm a specific line.
- **Give the universal emergency number** for their region as the immediate-danger route: 112
  across the EU and much of the world, 911 in the US / Canada (and **988** for the US Suicide &
  Crisis Lifeline), 999 in the UK, 000 in Australia, or the local equivalent. When in doubt,
  "your local emergency number" plus findahelpline.com is always safe.
- **Point to a human they know.** Encourage contacting their DBT therapist or coach (phone
  coaching is part of comprehensive DBT — if they're in DBT, using it now is the right move),
  their doctor, or someone they trust today; if `CLAUDE.md` records a specific person or
  clinician, name them.

## Distress vs crisis, the one call that matters

"Pointless / what's the point / nothing helps" about effort or activities is anhedonia and
ordinary low mood — in scope; work the emotion. "No point going on / in being here / in living"
is about their life — that is a crisis trigger. When it's genuinely ambiguous, ask one calm,
direct screening question before routing ("When you say pointless, are you having any thoughts of
not wanting to be alive?") and let the answer decide. Asking does not plant the idea. A clear
"no, just flat" lets you treat it as low mood and work a skill; don't over-escalate ordinary
distress into a crisis script either.

## Clinician-guided skills, deliver only the self-help-safe layer

These are designed to be run by, or with, a licensed clinician and can transiently raise
distress. **Do not walk the user through them as self-help.** When the routed skill is one of
these, give the psychoeducation and the framing, say plainly that the full version wants a
clinician, and offer the lower-risk adjacent skills instead.

- **Managing Extreme Emotions** (the skills-breakdown-point bridge, `emotion-regulation.md`) — a
  bridge skill for states where processing capacity is overwhelmed and risk may be present. The
  self-help-safe layer is the crisis-survival front end (TIPP, Self-Soothe to get arousal down)
  plus signposting; the full extreme-emotion protocol is best learned with a therapist. If
  self-harm risk is present and nothing is working, that's the crisis-trigger section above —
  immediate support (988 / local), not another skill rep.
- **Nightmare Protocol** (`emotion-regulation.md`) — imagery rehearsal for recurring nightmares.
  Fine for a low-intensity recurring dream as self-help. **Trauma nightmares are clinician-led**:
  offer the relaxation / preparation steps and signpost; don't run the rewriting on trauma
  content. If the nightmares are severe and trauma-related, route toward a trauma-trained
  clinician (DBT PE / DBT-PTSD).
- **Trauma, BPD, and active-suicidality work in general** — stabilization and crisis-survival
  skills (TIPP, grounding, Self-Soothe, ride the wave) are self-help-safe; trauma processing and
  the comprehensive DBT package are not. Offer the former, signpost the latter.
- **Eating disorders** — restricting, bingeing, or purging needs specialist care (DBT-BED /
  CBT-E) and medical monitoring. Distress tolerance for an urge is a bridge, but signpost
  professional assessment; do not run the urge frame as if it were the treatment.

## Specific-skill cautions

- **TIPP — Temperature (cold water / ice).** The cold-water face dive triggers the mammalian dive
  reflex and drops heart rate fast. **Do not use the temperature step with anyone who has a heart
  condition, an eating disorder, or who is on medication that affects heart rate** — use the
  other TIPP components (paced breathing, paired muscle relaxation) or Self-Soothe instead. Check
  before cueing it.
- **Intense Exercise (TIPP).** Match to their physical health; skip or scale down if there's a
  relevant medical limitation.
- **Opposite Action and exposure-flavored approach.** Approaching a feared thing "all the way" is
  powerful but should not override a genuine danger signal — confirm the emotion doesn't fit the
  facts (Check the Facts) before acting opposite, and never use it to push someone toward an
  actually unsafe situation or person.
- **Set realistic expectations.** Crisis-survival skills are designed to get you through the
  moment without making it worse — temporary by design. They buy the window to think; they don't
  fix the problem. Say so, so a skill "not fixing it" doesn't read as failure.

## The framing to hold

Treat the user as a capable adult and a collaborator, not a patient to be managed — DBT calls
this radical genuineness, and it's the stance. Meet them where they are: with someone fluent in
DBT, skip the lecture and run the rep; with someone newer to it, scaffold a little more, still
without lecturing. But "self-help" doesn't mean "alone with a crisis" — the whole point of the
flags above is to notice the moment self-help stops being the right tool and a human needs to be
in the room.
